### The Craft and Its Structure
Medieval surgery occupied a genuinely strange and stratified position in the era's medical world, and understanding that stratification is the single most useful thing for building this profession into your setting. Medicine was split — often sharply, sometimes contentiously — between **physicians**, who were university-trained, worked from classical texts (Galen, Hippocrates, Avicenna), diagnosed through humoral theory, and treated illness largely through diet, medicine, and observation _without_ touching the patient invasively; and **surgeons**, who performed hands-on, physical, often bloody interventions — setting bones, lancing wounds, amputating limbs, treating battlefield injuries, and performing what operations the era's knowledge allowed. Physicians were considered learned, gentlemanly, and high-status; surgery, by contrast, was **manual labor** in a period that culturally ranked manual labor beneath intellectual work — regardless of how much skill, nerve, and practical knowledge surgery actually demanded.

**The barber-surgeon** is the essential figure to know here. Because surgery involved cutting, and barbers already owned sharp instruments and were comfortable working close to the body with a blade, **barbers and surgeons merged into a single common trade** across much of medieval Europe — the same person who cut your hair might set your broken arm, pull a rotten tooth, lance a boil, or bleed you for humoral balance (bloodletting was a genuinely mainstream, frequently prescribed treatment, not a fringe practice). This is where the traditional **red-and-white barber's pole** comes from — red for blood, white for the bandages, a real piece of medieval trade signage that survived into the modern day long after the two professions split apart. Barber-surgeons organized into guilds distinct from (and looked down upon by) physicians' associations — England's Barber-Surgeons' Company formally combined the two trades under one guild charter in the 1500s, codifying a split-yet-unified structure that had existed informally for a long time before that.

**University-trained surgeons** did exist, particularly in Italy (Bologna and Salerno were genuinely important centers of surgical learning and even produced real advances and written surgical treatises), where surgery achieved more academic respectability than in much of northern Europe. A skilled, literate, learned surgeon working from real anatomical and clinical knowledge could exist alongside — and look down on — the more common barber-surgeon practicing largely through hands-on apprenticeship and rougher empirical experience. This gives you a natural **two-tier structure within surgery itself**: the university-trained, higher-status surgeon-physician, and the guild-trained, lower-status barber-surgeon doing the bulk of everyday practical work.

**What surgery could actually do** — Setting and splinting broken bones, lancing abscesses and boils, basic wound treatment and stitching, tooth-pulling, bloodletting, cauterization (using heat to close wounds and stop bleeding, since it was one of the few reliable ways to prevent fatal blood loss), and amputation (performed without anesthesia beyond alcohol or opiates where available, and without germ theory — infection, not the surgery itself, killed the majority of amputation patients). Surgeons genuinely could and did save lives, but operated under enormous constraints: no understanding of infection, no reliable anesthesia, and real limits on what internal surgery was survivable at all. **Battlefield surgery** was a major, constant source of practical experience and employment — army surgeons treated an endless stream of wound trauma and, out of grim necessity, developed real practical expertise in trauma treatment that exceeded civilian practice in some respects.

**Status and reputation** — Genuinely mixed and context-dependent: a skilled battlefield or urban surgeon could be respected, trusted, and well-paid, especially by nobility or military commanders who needed reliable trauma care; but the trade as a whole carried a whiff of low, bloody, manual-labor disrepute that university physicians actively cultivated to maintain their own superior status. Surgeons were also, not unreasonably, sometimes viewed with the same uneasy fascination/suspicion as smiths — people who cut into the body and handled blood and death directly occupied an uncomfortable, quasi-liminal social space.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Medicine, Investigation (or Survival, for a battlefield/campaign surgeon)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Herbalism Kit, or a custom "Surgeon's Tools" (bone saw, lancet, cautery iron)
- **Feat:** Healer or Tough
- **Equipment:** A surgeon's kit (blades, bone saw, bandages, cautery iron), a barber's razor, a small stock of wine/spirits for treatment, a battlefield or guild letter of reference, 10 gp

This background is an obvious, strong fit for **Clerics (Life domain especially), Druids, or Artificers** mechanically, but it's arguably even more interesting on a **Fighter or Barbarian** who's seen enough battlefield trauma to have picked up genuine surgical skill out of necessity, or a **Rogue** whose anatomical knowledge cuts both ways (literally) — someone equally capable of saving a life or knowing exactly where to end one. The barber-surgeon dual trade is a fun, slightly dark bit of flavor for a character who cuts hair and sets bones with the same steady hand.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Battlefield Surgeon** — Hardened, practical, unsentimental about blood and death, genuinely skilled from years of trauma treatment — a great contact for a party that regularly gets hurt, and a source of grim, matter-of-fact war stories.
- **The Barber-Surgeon** — Runs an ordinary shop doing haircuts, tooth-pulling, and bloodletting in the same afternoon — a good low-key recurring town NPC, a little grim-humored about the trade's dual nature.
- **The University Surgeon Who Looks Down on Everyone** — Learned, literate, genuinely skilled, and insufferably condescending toward barber-surgeons and physicians alike — a great source of both real medical help and social friction.
- **The Surgeon Accused of Overreach** — Facing guild or physician backlash for treating patients "above their station" (performing physician-style diagnosis rather than staying in their manual lane) — a professional-jurisdiction dispute echoing the tailor/seamstress and saddler/loriner tensions.
- **The Field Medic With a Past** — A traveling surgeon following mercenary companies or accompanying the party, whose surgical skill was earned somewhere specific and troubled (a war, a plague, a disaster) they don't discuss directly.
### Game Hooks
- **The Botched Amputation** — A surgeon's patient died or was maimed after a procedure — was it simple period-appropriate medical limitation, negligence, or something more deliberate? A grounded, morally gray investigation hook.
- **The Physician-Surgeon Feud** — A university physician and a skilled surgeon clash over a patient's treatment — one insisting on humoral theory and diet, the other on direct intervention — and the party's ally or patron's life may hang on whose approach wins out.
- **Battlefield Debt** — An NPC (or PC) owes their life to a battlefield surgeon from years past, who now needs a favor returned — a classic "old debt comes due" hook with real emotional stakes.
- **The Anatomist's Notes** — A surgeon has been quietly and controversially studying anatomy beyond what's socially or religiously sanctioned (echoing real period tension around dissection) — their notes are valuable, dangerous, or both, and someone wants them suppressed or stolen.
- **No Surgeon for Miles** — A wilderness, siege, or plague-stricken setting where the party's only access to real medical care is a single overwhelmed surgeon — resource-management tension, triage decisions, and a reason for the party to actually need and value this NPC's skill directly.
