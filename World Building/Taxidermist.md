### A Modern Practice With No Real Medieval Equivalent
Taxidermy as we understand it — preserving and mounting animal specimens for display using tanning, wire armatures, and the specific techniques developed from the 18th century onward — simply didn't exist as a recognized medieval trade. The specific combination of skills, motivations, and techniques that define modern taxidermy developed substantially later, driven partly by natural history collecting traditions that were themselves post-medieval developments.

That said, medieval people did preserve and display animals and animal parts for various purposes — just through different methods and for different reasons than modern taxidermy. Let's cover what actually existed.
### What Medieval People Actually Did With Animal Remains
**Trophy display**: as covered in the hunters and gentlemen/ladies entries, hunting was a significant status activity for medieval nobility, and displaying evidence of successful hunts was a recognized practice
- **Antlers and horns**: mounted directly — the most common form of hunting trophy display, requiring no specialized preservation skill beyond basic cleaning and drying
- **Boar heads**: displayed as trophies, sometimes presented at feasts as ceremonial dishes (the famous "bringing in the boar's head" tradition connecting to the chef and feast entries)
- **Hides and pelts**: dried and displayed or used practically, connecting to the furrier and leatherworker entries

**Heraldic and ceremonial display**:
- **Stuffed animals as heraldic display**: some evidence exists of stuffed or mounted animals used in heraldic and ceremonial contexts — a lord whose heraldic animal was a particular beast might display a preserved specimen
- **Funeral heraldry**: elaborate noble funerals sometimes involved displaying the deceased's armor, weapons, and heraldic achievements — occasionally including preserved animals as part of this ceremonial display

**Curiosity and wonder cabinets** (proto-taxidermy's closest ancestor):
- **Wunderkammern**: the formal "cabinet of curiosities" tradition is more specifically associated with the Renaissance and early modern period, but the underlying impulse — collecting and displaying remarkable natural objects — had medieval roots
- **Church reliquaries and treasuries**: as covered in the church and goldsmith entries, churches maintained treasuries displaying remarkable objects including natural specimens — unusual animals, strange bones, and curiosities treated as evidence of God's wonderful creation
- **Crocodiles and exotic animals**: churches and noble collections sometimes displayed preserved exotic animals (crocodile skins are documented in various medieval church treasuries across Europe) — simple drying and preservation rather than proper taxidermy mounting

**Practical preservation methods**:
- **Drying**: the simplest approach — removing flesh and allowing the remaining structure to dry, as with the antler/horn trophy practice
- **Salting**: applying salt to preserve organic material, connecting to the food preservation techniques covered throughout this list in the butcher and fishermen entries
- **Tanning**: applying tanning techniques (covered in the tanner entry) to preserve hides and skin — not full taxidermy mounting but genuine preservation
- **Natural mummification**: in dry conditions, simply allowing material to desiccate naturally
### Connection to Related Medieval Practices
**Bone carving**: as covered in that entry, working with animal bones and skeletal material — the bone carver's trade involved preserved skeletal material without the full skin preservation taxidermy implies

**Apothecary specimens**: as covered in the apothecary entry, medieval pharmacies sometimes kept unusual natural specimens — dried animals, animal parts with supposed medicinal properties, and natural curiosities as part of their stock and display

**Falconry and hunting bird preservation**: as covered in the animal trainer entry, falconers had genuine practical knowledge of bird anatomy and sometimes preserved notable birds — a prize hunting bird might be preserved after death as a memento, using simple drying techniques
### Why Proper Taxidermy Didn't Develop
A few genuinely interesting reasons why the specific modern practice didn't emerge in the medieval period:

**Natural history motivation**: modern taxidermy's primary driver was scientific natural history — creating accurate study specimens for zoological classification and research. Medieval natural philosophy didn't approach animals this way — they were understood through a combination of practical observation and inherited classical/biblical frameworks (the **bestiary** tradition, treating animals' supposed characteristics as moral allegories, being a distinctively medieval approach) rather than systematic empirical natural history

**Technical limitations**: effective taxidermy required the development of specific preservation chemicals and mounting techniques that came later — arsenic-based preservation (genuinely effective but extremely toxic), wire armature construction, and modern tanning chemistry all postdate the medieval period

**Aesthetic priorities**: medieval aesthetic interest in animals ran toward heraldic representation and allegorical meaning rather than naturalistic display — a heraldic beast stylized for visual impact mattered more than a naturalistically accurate mounted specimen
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A proto-taxidermist NPC as an unusual, slightly ahead-of-their-time character — perhaps a hunter or apothecary who's developed unusual preservation techniques going beyond simple drying, collecting and displaying remarkable specimens in ways their contemporaries find fascinating or unsettling
- A church treasury's collection of preserved exotic animals and curiosities as a specific worldbuilding location detail — a cathedral whose treasury includes a dried crocodile suspended from the ceiling (a genuinely documented real medieval church feature), strange preserved creatures from distant lands, and objects whose origin and nature are uncertain
- Preserved animal remains as plot-relevant objects — a specific mounted or preserved creature that's more than it appears, perhaps containing hidden material, serving as a container for something valuable, or being the preserved form of something that was once alive in a more unusual sense
- The bestiary tradition as worldbuilding texture — your setting's understanding of animals following the medieval allegorical/moral framework rather than modern naturalistic zoology, with creatures understood through their supposed symbolic properties rather than scientific classification
