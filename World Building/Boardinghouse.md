### A Slightly Anachronistic Term, But a Real Concept
"Boardinghouse" as a specific term is more associated with later periods (especially the 18th-19th centuries), but the underlying concept — paying for lodging plus meals on a longer-term or recurring basis, as opposed to a one-night inn stay — absolutely existed in medieval Europe in various forms. It just went by different names and was organized somewhat differently than the classic image of a Victorian boardinghouse with a landlady and a dining table full of regular lodgers.
### The Closest Medieval Equivalents
**Inns**: as covered with bartenders/taverns, inns provided lodging plus food, primarily for travelers — but some inns, especially in cities with significant numbers of merchants, students, or visiting officials passing through repeatedly or staying extended periods, functioned much like a boardinghouse for semi-regular guests.

**Hostels/hospices**: religious institutions (the word "hospital" originally meant a place of hospitality broadly, not specifically medical care) that housed travelers, pilgrims, and the poor, often run by monastic orders along major pilgrimage routes. These weren't commercial boardinghouses, but they served a similar housing-plus-sustenance function for people away from home.

**Student lodgings**: this is actually one of the closest real analogs. As medieval universities grew (Paris, Oxford, Bologna, and others from the 12th century onward), students needed somewhere to live, and:
- Many students initially lodged with **private householders** in university towns, paying for room and board much like a boardinghouse arrangement
- **Colleges** gradually developed partly to formalize and improve on this — providing structured lodging, meals, and oversight for students, evolving from informal boarding arrangements into the more institutional college system recognizable today (Oxford and Cambridge's college structures have roots here)
- **Halls**: intermediate, less formal arrangements where a group of students rented and shared a house, sometimes under a designated "principal" responsible for order and rent collection

**Merchant lodgings/fondaco-type arrangements**: as mentioned with the cosmopolitan bazaar discussion, cities with significant foreign merchant populations (Venice's _fondaco_ system is the clearest example) sometimes maintained dedicated lodging houses for specific merchant communities — a Venetian fondaco might house, feed, and even warehouse goods for German merchants, for instance, functioning as a combined boardinghouse, warehouse, and trading post for a specific group.

**Almshouses**: charitable institutions providing long-term shelter (and sometimes food) for the poor, elderly, or infirm — not commercial boardinghouses, but a long-term residential support structure with some functional overlap.
### What Staying Somewhere Long-Term Looked Like
For someone needing extended lodging in a medieval town — a merchant conducting lengthy business, a student, a traveling craftsman working a temporary job, or a legal petitioner waiting on court business — the arrangement was less standardized than a modern boardinghouse:
- **Renting a room from a private household**: very common — a family with extra space might take in a lodger for a fee, providing a room and sometimes meals, operating informally rather than as a dedicated commercial enterprise
- **Negotiated, personal arrangements**: rates, meal inclusion, and length of stay were likely worked out individually rather than through standardized boardinghouse rules
- **Reputation and connections mattered enormously**: in the absence of formal regulation or rating systems, finding decent long-term lodging likely depended heavily on word-of-mouth, guild connections, or letters of introduction
### Regulation and Oversight
- Inns and hostelries in towns were sometimes subject to municipal oversight similar to other trades — checking that hosts weren't overcharging, weren't harboring criminals or unsavory characters, and maintained some basic standards
- The **innkeeper's liability** for guests' property was a recognized legal concept in some jurisdictions — early forms of the idea that an innkeeper bore some responsibility for goods stolen from guests under their roof
### Worldbuilding Angle
This is a profession/institution where a fantasy setting has real freedom, since the "boardinghouse" concept maps loosely onto several real medieval institutions without a single clean historical template to follow. A few hooks:
- A long-term lodging house catering specifically to adventurers between expeditions — a recurring home base with a proprietor who knows the party, stores their excess gear, and has informal connections to local job/quest sources (functionally similar to many D&D campaign "home base" tropes)
- A university/magical-academy-adjacent boarding house for students, letting you explore academic intrigue, rival student factions, or a wise old landlady who's seen generations of young mages come through her door
- A foreign quarter's dedicated lodging house (echoing the fondaco model) for a specific ethnic, racial, or factional group in a multicultural city — useful for exploring cultural tension, trade relationships, or diplomatic intrigue
- An almshouse-style charitable lodging that becomes relevant to a quest involving the city's poor or displaced population
