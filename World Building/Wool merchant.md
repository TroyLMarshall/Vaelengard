### The Craft and Its Structure
Wool wasn't just a commodity in medieval Europe — it was one of the **economic engines of the continent**, and the merchants who controlled its trade were among the wealthiest, most politically influential non-nobles of the era. This is genuinely different in scale from the artisan trades above: a wool merchant wasn't a craftsman working with their hands so much as a **capitalist, logistician, and financier**, moving raw material across borders and turning sheep pastures into international fortunes.

**England as the wool superpower** — For much of the medieval period, England's economy was staggeringly dependent on wool exports, primarily raw fleece shipped to the great cloth-weaving centers of **Flanders** (Bruges, Ghent, Ypres) and later **Italy**, where it was spun, woven, and dyed into finished cloth and resold at a huge markup. This lopsided arrangement — England grows it, Flanders profits from finishing it — created enormous tension and enormous opportunity, and English wool merchants who found ways to capture more of that value (or who controlled the export licenses) became fabulously rich.

**The Staple system** — England eventually formalized wool export through the **Staple**, a fixed trading post (Calais, for much of the 1300s–1400s) through which _all_ exported wool legally had to pass, taxed and regulated by the crown. The merchants licensed to trade at the Staple — the **Merchants of the Staple** — held an effective legal monopoly, and crown finances leaned on wool taxation so heavily that wool essentially bankrolled English wars (including much of the Hundred Years' War). This is a fantastic worldbuilding mechanism: a chokepoint trade good, a legally privileged merchant class, and a crown whose military campaigns are literally funded by sheep.

**The merchant's actual work** involved: buying raw wool in bulk from monasteries and estates (monasteries, especially Cistercian houses, were massive wool producers), grading and sorting quality, arranging transport (overland to ports, then by ship), securing licenses and paying export duties, and — critically — **extending and managing credit**. Wool merchants were often also moneylenders and financiers by necessity, since buying a season's wool crop upfront required serious capital, and they'd advance loans to monasteries or nobles against future wool yields. Some of the wealthiest wool merchants effectively functioned as bankers to kings.

**Guild and civic power** — Wealthy wool merchants frequently became mayors, aldermen, and members of parliament; wool money built cathedrals, almshouses, and grand houses (the famous "wool churches" of England's Cotswolds were literally funded by wool fortunes). This trade produced real social mobility — a shrewd merchant family could rise from nothing to genuine political power within a couple of generations, which was rare in a rigidly stratified society.

**Risk** — For all the wealth, this was a genuinely risky trade: piracy and shipwreck, war disrupting trade routes (England and Flanders/France were not always on good terms), royal debts that went unpaid, market crashes from oversupply, and the crown periodically seizing merchant wealth or hiking taxes to fund wars. A wool fortune could evaporate fast.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Persuasion, Insight (or History, if leaning into political savvy)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Navigator's Tools or a language, reflecting international trade dealings
- **Feat:** Skilled or Lucky
- **Equipment:** A merchant's ledger, a signet ring or seal, fine traveling clothes, a letter of credit, 50 gp (wool merchants should start with noticeably more starting capital than other trade backgrounds — that's the point of the background)

This is an excellent background for a character with **real starting wealth and connections but not noble blood** — a Bard, Paladin (oath of a self-made family), Rogue (con artist raised around finance), or even a Fighter whose family's fortune bought them the best masters-at-arms money could hire. It's also a great background for someone whose family's fortune has _collapsed_ — a wool merchant's heir now adventuring because the family business was ruined by piracy, war, or royal debt default.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Staple Monopolist** — A merchant holding a crown-granted export monopoly, ruthless about protecting it from smugglers and rivals, but quietly aware the crown could revoke it on a whim — paranoid, powerful, and dangerous to cross.
- **The Merchant-Banker to the Crown** — An NPC who's loaned a king or noble a staggering sum against future wool revenues, and is now watching nervously as the war they funded goes badly — their fortune (and maybe their life) rides on the outcome of events the party might influence.
- **The Self-Made Alderman** — Rose from nothing on wool money, now sits on the town council, and is obsessed with respectability and legacy-building (funding a cathedral, marrying into nobility) — sponsors the party in exchange for discreet services that protect their reputation.
- **The Ruined Heir** — A former wool fortune's last scion, reduced to genteel poverty after a shipwreck, war, or crown seizure took everything — proud, resourceful, and looking for any way to rebuild, legitimate or not.
- **The Smuggler Merchant** — Officially licensed, secretly running wool (or something else) outside the Staple system to dodge crushing export duties — needs discretion, and the party either catches them or gets recruited into the operation.
### Game Hooks
- **The Piracy Problem** — A merchant's wool shipment (and the fortune riding on it) is lost to pirates or a monster at sea; the party is hired to recover it, investigate an inside job, or negotiate with whoever took it.
- **The Royal Default** — The crown owes a merchant house a fortune from war financing and shows no sign of paying — the party gets pulled into a delicate mission to recover the debt without provoking royal anger, or to help the merchant quietly extract themselves from an increasingly dangerous financial entanglement.
- **The Staple War** — Rival cities or trading factions vie for control of a wool chokepoint (a literal Staple-style monopoly port) — political intrigue, sabotage, and smuggling all in one economic conflict the party can tip in someone's favor.
- **The Monastery's Ledger** — A monastery deeply indebted to a wool merchant faces losing its lands/independence if it can't repay — a hook that blends religious politics with hard economics, and gives the party a genuine moral question about who "deserves" to win.
- **Wool Money, Blood Money** — The party discovers a respected, civic-pillar merchant's fortune was built on something considerably darker than sheep (smuggling, extortion, a pact, war profiteering) — a slow-burn "peel back the respectable facade" investigation hook.
