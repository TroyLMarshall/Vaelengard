### The Craft and Its Structure
A **farrier** shod horses — trimming hooves and fitting iron shoes — but in the medieval period the role was broader and blurrier than the modern specialist. Farriers were frequently **blacksmiths who specialized in horse work**, and in many towns "farrier" and "smith" were the same person wearing two hats. The trade only fully split off as its own guild-recognized profession later in the medieval period (England formally chartered the Worshipful Company of Farriers in the 1300s-1600s range, depending on the city).

Farriers didn't just shoe horses. Because they handled animals constantly and understood anatomy from the outside in, they became the **de facto veterinarians** of the medieval world — treating lameness, wounds, colic, and general equine illness. A skilled farrier's knowledge of horse medicine (called "horse leechcraft") could rival or exceed that of human physicians in practical terms, since much of medieval human medicine was theoretical/humoral while farriers worked hands-on with living, injured animals daily. Some farriers wrote or copied treatises on horse care that circulated as valuable technical manuscripts.

**The work itself:**
- **Trimming and balancing hooves** — done properly, this takes real skill; done poorly, it lames a horse.
- **Forging and fitting shoes** — hot-fitting (shaping a heated shoe directly against the hoof) was more advanced and produced a better fit than cold-fitting pre-made shoes, and marked a more skilled smith.
- **Basic veterinary care** — wound treatment, teeth (rasping sharp points), and diagnosing lameness.
- **Making and repairing tack-adjacent hardware** — bits, buckles, and other ironwork tied to horse gear, depending on how much overlap existed with the saddler.

**Social position:** Farriers occupied useful, unglamorous middle ground. Every army, every noble household, every trade caravan needed one, so they had steady work and genuine leverage — a bad farrier could cripple a knight's warhorse or a merchant's whole draft team. Military campaigns depended on farriers to keep cavalry and supply horses functional, so armies employed them directly, often granting them protected status similar to other essential camp trades (like the fletcher or wainwright). At the same time, it was manual labor with animals, so it didn't carry the prestige of a swordsmith's finished, beautiful product — a farrier's work was practical, dirty, and constant rather than a singular masterpiece.

**Regional/practical notes worth stealing:**
- Horseshoes were valuable enough that **lost shoes were sometimes collected and reforged** — nothing wasted.
- Farriers often worked **at crossroads, bridges, or town gates** — high-traffic spots where travelers needed quick repairs, making them natural gossip hubs and info brokers.
- A traveling farrier (as opposed to a settled town one) might function like a circuit rider, moving between villages too small to support their own smith.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Animal Handling, Medicine
- **Tool Proficiency:** Smith's Tools (or a custom "Farrier's Tools" reskin)
- **Feat:** Healer or Tavern Brawler (practical, physical feats fit the "hands-on with large animals" vibe)
- **Equipment:** Smith's tools, a hoof pick and rasp, a set of horseshoes, a satchel of horse-medicine supplies, traveling clothes, 10 gp

This background pairs beautifully with Rangers, Cavaliers, Mounted Fighters, or anyone with a companion/mount — mechanically and narratively it's the background for someone who's always been the one keeping the party's mounts (and by extension, half of society's transportation and warfare infrastructure) actually functioning.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Crossroads Farrier** — Runs a forge at a major crossroads or bridge and knows everyone who's passed through in the last decade. A perfect quiet info-broker NPC who trades gossip for coin without ever leaving their post.
- **The Campaign Farrier** — Attached to a mercenary company or noble's cavalry, having seen multiple battles from the horse-lines rather than the front — an NPC who's witnessed history sideways and has unique intel or trauma from it.
- **The Horse-Leech** — A farrier whose veterinary skill has become locally famous, blurring into folk-healer status; people quietly bring them sick children when the physician's humoral bloodletting hasn't worked, and the farrier has to decide whether to overstep their bounds.
- **The Guild-less Farrier** — Practices without formal guild sanction because they're self-taught, foreign-trained, or excluded (gender, race, species in your setting) — deeply skilled but always one bad customer complaint from being run out of town.
- **The Reforger** — An eccentric who collects lost/discarded horseshoes obsessively, claiming (correctly or not) that iron which has "walked many roads" makes luckier shoes — could be genuine low-key magic, superstition, or a front for something else entirely.
### Game Hooks
- **The Lamed Warhorse** — A noble's prized warhorse goes lame right before a tournament or battle, and suspicion falls on the farrier — sabotage, incompetence, or a curse? The PCs investigate.
- **No Farrier, No Army** — A war effort or caravan is stalled because the local farrier died, fled, or was assassinated — someone needs to escort/protect a replacement farrier or find one fast, tying transportation logistics directly to plot stakes.
- **The Manuscript** — A farrier's handwritten horse-leechcraft treatise turns out to contain something more than horse medicine — coded messages, a partial map, or genuine magical veterinary knowledge someone else wants badly.
- **The Shoe That Wasn't Iron** — A farrier gets asked to work with an unusual material (mithril, cold iron, meteoric metal, a monster's hide/hoof) for a client's exotic mount, and it's beyond their normal expertise — a fun small commission-quest for PCs with an exotic mount or animal companion.
- **Crossroads Witness** — The party needs an alibi, a lead, or a description of a fleeing suspect, and the only witness is the farrier who was shoeing horses at the crossroads that day — a classic "find and question the right NPC" hook, but one who has leverage and will want something in return.
