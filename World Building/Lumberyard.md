### More an Activity Than a Dedicated Institution
Unlike a modern lumberyard — a fixed commercial site stocking standardized cut lumber for sale — medieval timber supply worked somewhat differently, closer to **harvest-to-order or harvest-near-use** than maintaining a standing retail inventory of pre-cut, seasoned lumber.
### How Timber Actually Moved From Forest to Builder
- **Felling near the point of use or transport**: as covered in the logger entry, timber was often felled, roughly processed (squared, sawn into rough planks/beams by sawyers), and moved relatively directly toward its construction destination, rather than passing through a dedicated retail storage yard
- **Riverside and dockside timber storage**: where timber moved via river transport (the cheaper, higher-volume option covered in the docks/wharfs and freight company entries), riverside or dockside storage areas functioned closest to a "lumberyard" — accumulating floated or barge-transported timber awaiting sale, further processing, or distribution
- **Building-site stockpiling**: for major construction projects (connecting to the construction company entry's "fabric" organizations managing long cathedral builds), timber was often stockpiled directly at or near the building site itself, sourced and accumulated specifically for that project rather than purchased from a general-purpose retail yard
- **Seasoning requirements**: timber needs time to properly dry/season before use in fine construction or furniture work (rushing this risks warping and structural problems, similar to the bowyer entry's discussion of yew stave seasoning) — this created genuine need for _some_ storage and waiting period, just not necessarily organized as a dedicated commercial retail yard the way we'd picture today
### Timber Merchants
- **Wood merchants did exist** as a recognizable trade — particularly significant for the larger-scale Baltic timber trade mentioned in the logger entry, where merchants specifically dealt in importing and distributing substantial timber quantities to regions with less abundant local forest
- These merchants likely operated closer to general merchant/trading patterns (covered in the banker, caravan guild, and freight company entries) than a specialized retail "yard" — securing supply, arranging transport, and selling to builders, shipwrights, and other timber-dependent trades at significant volume
### Worldbuilding Angle
Given there's no precise institution to import wholesale, you have real freedom: a riverside timber-storage/sale area near a port or major construction project is a reasonable, grounded "lumberyard"-equivalent location for your setting if you want one — useful as a recognizable spot for PCs to source building material, investigate a construction-related mystery, or encounter timber-trade merchants and laborers, even if it's a slight simplification of the more distributed real historical supply pattern.
