### A Universally Resented but Genuinely Essential Function
We've touched on taxation throughout this list — the government official entry covered royal revenue collection and the sheriff's tax-gathering role, the land office entry mentioned the Domesday Book as a comprehensive property survey, the laborer entry referenced the Statute of Labourers in the context of post-plague economic tension, and the peasant entry covered the burden of overlapping obligations on ordinary people. Tax assessment — determining what people owed — deserves its own treatment, since it was a genuinely significant function with real complexity and real consequences.
### Why Assessment Was Necessary
Medieval taxation wasn't simply a flat rate applied uniformly — it generally required some form of assessment determining what a specific person or community actually owed, based on their property, income, or resources. This assessment function was genuinely complex:
- **Variable wealth**: people's actual ability to pay varied enormously, and taxation systems often attempted (imperfectly) to relate obligation to capacity
- **Complex property relationships**: the overlapping landholding, tenancy, and obligation structures covered in the farm, land office, and noble entries created genuine complexity about who owed what to whom
- **Fraud and evasion incentives**: connecting to the smuggler entry's discussion of tax evasion, people consistently tried to understate their wealth, assets, and income to reduce their tax burden — creating ongoing adversarial dynamics between assessors and taxpayers
### Types of Medieval Taxation
**Royal/national taxes**:
- **Lay subsidies**: periodic royal taxes assessed on moveable property (goods, livestock, stored grain, money) — requiring assessment of what taxpayers actually owned to calculate their liability
- **Poll taxes**: flat per-head taxes requiring identification and counting of taxable individuals rather than property assessment — simpler to administer in principle, but generating real resistance when the flat rate felt disproportionate to ability to pay (the English Poll Tax of 1381 directly contributing to the Peasants' Revolt covered in the laborer entry)
- **The Fifteenth and Tenth**: a standardized English tax levying a fifteenth of rural moveable property and a tenth of urban moveable property — gradually becoming standardized at fixed community quotas rather than fresh individual assessment, reducing assessment burden but also freezing liability regardless of changed circumstances

**Church taxation**:
- **Tithes**: as covered repeatedly throughout this list, a tenth of production owed to the Church — assessed at the local parish level, with genuine disputes about what counted as tithable income and at what rate
- **Clerical taxation**: the Church itself was periodically taxed by the papacy for specific purposes (crusade funding particularly) — requiring assessment of ecclesiastical wealth, a genuinely sensitive and complex task

**Manorial and local dues**:
- As covered in the farm and peasant entries, various manorial obligations (tallage, heriot, merchet, and similar) required assessment of what specific tenants owed based on their holdings and circumstances
### Who Did the Assessing
**Royal commissioners and assessors**: for major national taxes, the crown appointed commissioners (typically local men of substance — knights or prosperous freeholders) to assess their communities
- **Local knowledge as the key resource**: assessors' value lay precisely in their local knowledge — knowing their neighbors' actual wealth, property, and circumstances in ways an outsider couldn't
- **Conflicts of interest**: this same local knowledge came with obvious corruption opportunities — an assessor could undervalue friends and allies, overvalue enemies, or accept bribes to reduce assessments, creating the pervasive fraud concerns documented throughout medieval tax records

**Sworn jurors**: many assessment systems relied on sworn jurors (connecting to the judge entry's discussion of jury development) — local men sworn to honestly report their community's taxable wealth, with the oath's spiritual weight supposed to deter dishonest underreporting

**Sheriffs and local officials**: as covered in the government official entry, sheriffs played significant roles in tax collection alongside assessment — sometimes the same individuals combining both functions
### The Domesday Book as Assessment Model
As covered in the land office entry, the Domesday Book represented the most ambitious medieval English assessment exercise — a comprehensive survey attempting to establish exactly what property existed, who held it, and what it was worth for taxation purposes. Worth expanding here:
- **The sworn inquest method**: information was gathered through sworn testimony from local juries — groups of local landholders sworn to honestly report their area's resources and holdings
- **Cross-checking**: different juries from the same area were sometimes asked the same questions, allowing inconsistencies to be identified and investigated
- **Remarkable comprehensiveness**: the survey's scope was genuinely extraordinary for the period — covering virtually the entire country, recording landholders, tenants, livestock numbers, and valuations in detail that historians still use today
### Assessment Techniques
**Moveable property assessment**: for taxes on goods and livestock, assessors physically visited properties to observe and record assets — creating obvious opportunities for concealment (driving livestock to a neighbor's property before the assessor arrived was apparently a recognized evasion tactic)

**Income assessment**: more difficult to assess than visible property, requiring either self-reporting (subject to obvious understatement) or inference from visible lifestyle and known activities

**Fixed quotas**: as taxation systems matured, some evolved from fresh individual assessment toward fixed community quotas — a town or village owed a set amount regardless of internal distribution, with the community then deciding internally how to divide the burden, reducing the need for individual assessment while shifting the negotiation to the local level
### Corruption and Resistance
This is genuinely central to understanding medieval tax assessment:

**Systematic corruption**: the opportunities for corruption in assessment were so pervasive and well-recognized that they appear constantly in period sources — assessors taking bribes to reduce liability, politically motivated overassessment of enemies, and systematic underassessment of powerful local figures who could make an assessor's life difficult  
**Collective evasion**: communities sometimes collectively understated their wealth to assessors, coordinating their testimony to present a uniformly impoverished picture — a form of collective resistance requiring community solidarity  
**Legal challenges**: taxpayers had some mechanisms for challenging assessments they considered unfair — appealing to higher authorities, presenting counter-evidence, or engaging legal assistance (connecting to the lawyer entry) to contest specific assessments
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A tax assessment as a plot catalyst — a royal commissioner arriving in a settlement to assess its wealth for a new tax, creating immediate community anxiety, political maneuvering, and potential violence if the assessment seems likely to be ruinously high
- A corrupt assessor as a grounded, believable local antagonist — someone using their assessment authority to settle personal scores, enrich themselves through bribes, or serve a powerful noble's interest in driving smaller landholders off desirable territory
- The community evasion dynamic as a morally complex situation — PCs asked to help a village conceal its true wealth from royal assessors, with genuine competing interests (the crown's legitimate revenue needs versus the community's genuine inability to pay a ruinous assessment) making the "right" choice genuinely unclear
- Historical assessment records (connecting to the Domesday Book discussion) as a plot resource — old assessment records preserved in a monastery or lord's archive revealing historical landholding, concealed wealth, or disputed property claims relevant to a current investigation
