### Two Distinct Trades Under One Name
"Chandler" is the historical term (from the French _chandelle_, candle), and it splits into two genuinely different crafts depending on material — a distinction that mattered enough to sometimes warrant separate guilds:
- **Wax chandlers**: worked with beeswax, producing the finer, cleaner-burning, more expensive candles
- **Tallow chandlers**: worked with rendered animal fat (tallow), producing the cheaper, smokier, smellier candles that most ordinary people actually used day to day

This connects directly back to two trades already covered: beekeepers (supplying wax) and butchers (supplying tallow as a byproduct of slaughter) — candlemaking sat right at the consuming end of both those supply chains.
### Wax Candles
- **Material**: pure beeswax, as discussed in the beekeeping section — burned cleanly, brightly, with a pleasant honey-like smell and minimal smoke
- **Primary market**: the Church was the dominant consumer, requiring pure beeswax candles for liturgical use across an enormous number of churches, monasteries, and cathedrals conducting masses and devotional services constantly
- **Secondary market**: wealthy households who could afford the expense, using wax candles as both practical lighting and a status display
- **Cost**: significantly more expensive than tallow, given the labor-intensive and limited supply of beeswax (recall that medieval beekeeping, without moveable frames, was inefficient and often destroyed colonies during harvest)
### Tallow Candles
- **Material**: rendered animal fat — beef and mutton tallow were preferred over pork fat, which burned with an even worse smell
- **The everyday candle**: this is what most medieval households actually used, given the cost difference from wax
- **Significant downsides**: tallow candles smoked more, smelled distinctly unpleasant (a rancid, fatty odor), burned less brightly, and needed more frequent trimming/tending of the wick
- **Affordability**: still not free, but accessible to a much broader portion of the population than wax candles
### The Candlemaking Process
**Dipping method** (most common for tallow, also used for wax):
1. Wicks (typically twisted cotton, flax, or hemp fiber) were repeatedly dipped into a heated vat of melted tallow or wax
2. Each dip added a thin layer, requiring the previous layer to cool slightly before the next dip
3. This was repeated many times, gradually building up the candle to full thickness — genuinely repetitive, time-consuming work

**Molding method** (became more common, especially later in the medieval period):
- Metal or wooden molds allowed multiple candles to be cast simultaneously, a more efficient production method than individual dipping
- Wicks were threaded through the mold before the melted material was poured in

**Rushlights**: worth mentioning as the cheapest lighting alternative below even tallow candles — dried rush plant pith soaked in whatever fat was available (kitchen grease, tallow scraps), used by the poorest households who couldn't afford proper candles at all. Not technically a "candle" in the formal sense, but part of the same basic lighting-fuel economy.
### Other Products Chandlers Made
- **Soap**: tallow chandlers often also made soap, since the rendering and fat-processing skills overlapped significantly — animal fat combined with lye (made from wood ash) produced a basic, harsh soap
- **Sealing wax**: a specific wax preparation for document sealing, sometimes a chandler product, though this could also overlap with other specialized preparation
- **Polish and waterproofing compounds**: wax-based products for various practical household and craft uses
### Guild Structure
- In cities large enough to support formal guilds, **wax chandlers and tallow chandlers sometimes formed separate guilds** reflecting their genuinely different material, market, and status — London's Worshipful Company of Wax Chandlers and Worshipful Company of Tallow Chandlers both exist as distinct historical guilds, underlining this real trade division
- Apprenticeship followed the standard pattern, though the trade's relatively modest capital requirements (compared to, say, armoring) made entry somewhat more accessible than higher-prestige crafts
### Daily Life Without Reliable Light
It's worth sitting with the broader implication here: light was genuinely expensive and limited for most of the medieval population. This shaped daily rhythms significantly — most people's days were organized substantially around natural daylight, with candlelight reserved for necessary tasks after dark rather than the casual all-hours illumination modern life takes for granted. A household's candle/light expenditure was a real, calculated budget item, not an afterthought.
### Fire Risk
Like bakeries, candlemaking and candle use generally carried genuine fire risk in a world of wood and thatch construction — open flames were a constant practical hazard, and various civic regulations existed around candle use, particularly restricting open flame in certain contexts (near hay stores, in crowded wooden tenements) to mitigate the very real danger of catastrophic urban fires.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A wax chandler supplying a cathedral or temple analog with candles enchanted for protection, purification, or detecting undead/evil when lit — a nice mundane-trade-meets-magic-item crossover
- The wax/tallow class divide as a small but textured worldbuilding detail — nobility's halls smelling faintly of honey from wax candles, while a poor tavern reeks of tallow smoke, giving sensory grounding to social distinctions without heavy exposition
- A candlemaker character whose enchanted candles burn with magical light (everburning, color-changing, or light that reveals invisible/illusory things) as a specialty item shop with a clear, grounded craft basis
- Rushlights or cheap tallow candles as a detail signaling genuine poverty in an NPC's home or a dungeon's abandoned hovel — small sensory details that reinforce socioeconomic texture in your settlements
