### A Universal, Underappreciated Trade
Pottery occupies a similar position to basket weaving and broom making in this list — genuinely essential to daily life across all social classes, requiring real skill, but lacking the glamour of metalworking or the prestige of luxury trades. Almost every household in medieval Europe used ceramic vessels daily, making pottery one of the most consistently demanded crafts of the entire period.
### Why Pottery Mattered
Connecting to several entries already covered: before the widespread availability of pewter (covered in that entry), glass (the glassblower entry), and cheap metal vessels, **ceramic pottery was the dominant material for everyday storage, cooking, and eating vessels** across the bulk of the population. Even as pewter and other alternatives became more available, pottery retained enormous importance for specific applications where its properties (heat resistance for cooking, chemical inertness for storage, cheap replaceability) made it superior or simply more practical than alternatives.
### What Potters Made
**Cooking vessels**:
- **Cooking pots**: the fundamental, universal item — used directly over fire or in hearths for the pottage cooking covered in the chef entry, requiring real heat resistance that metal alternatives provided but at much higher cost
- **Pipkins**: smaller cooking pots with a single handle, suited to specific cooking tasks
- **Dripping pans**: collecting fat from roasting meat

**Storage**:
- **Jars and crocks**: for storing preserved foods — salt meat, pickled vegetables, rendered fat — connecting to the food preservation discussion in the fishermen and butcher entries
- **Albarello**: the distinctive cylindrical pharmacy jar mentioned in the apothecary entry — a specific, recognized pottery form with its own design tradition specifically associated with apothecary/medical use
- **Amphorae-style storage vessels**: for liquids including oil and wine

**Tableware**:
- **Bowls and dishes**: for serving and eating, particularly significant at the lower social end where pewter remained out of reach
- **Jugs and pitchers**: for water, ale, and other liquids
- **Cups and mugs**: though pottery cups competed with wood and horn alternatives at the lower end

**Specialized items**:
- **Roof tiles**: connecting to the construction entries — ceramic roof tiles as an alternative to thatch, particularly in regions with good clay but less abundant thatching material, or in towns where fire risk made thatching less desirable
- **Floor tiles**: decorative and practical floor covering, particularly significant in churches and wealthier buildings — some medieval floor tile traditions (like Cistercian tile work) produced genuinely beautiful, sophisticated designs
- **Building bricks**: while stone dominated prestigious construction and timber framing dominated ordinary construction, fired clay bricks were used in some regions and contexts, particularly in areas without good local building stone
- **Drainage pipes**: ceramic pipe sections for basic drainage infrastructure
### Materials and Technique
**Clay sourcing**: pottery required access to suitable clay — not all clay was equally suited to making good ceramics, and finding, preparing, and managing clay supply was a genuine trade concern. Potters typically established themselves near good clay sources rather than importing raw materials from a distance.

**Preparation**: raw clay needed cleaning (removing stones and organic material), sometimes blending different clay types for desired properties, and wedging (working the clay to remove air bubbles and achieve consistent texture)

**Forming methods**:
- **Wheel throwing**: the potter's wheel — one of humanity's genuinely ancient technologies — allowed skilled potters to produce symmetrical, consistent vessel forms much more efficiently than purely hand-building methods, requiring real practice to master but producing superior results at better speed once mastered
- **Hand-building**: coiling, pinching, and slab-building techniques for forms less suited to wheel throwing or in traditions/regions where wheel use was less established
- **Molding**: using pre-formed molds for consistent reproduction of specific shapes, particularly relevant for the more standardized forms like roof and floor tiles

**Firing**: converting formed clay into permanent ceramic through heat — requiring a kiln capable of reaching sufficient temperatures, with fuel consumption (connecting to the charcoal maker and logger entries' fuel supply discussions) representing a genuine ongoing operating cost

**Glazing**: applying a glass-like surface coating (connecting to the glassblower entry's discussion of glass chemistry given the overlapping material science) to improve appearance, reduce porosity, and make vessels easier to clean — medieval glazing traditions developed considerable sophistication, with lead glazes (producing characteristic warm amber/green tones familiar from surviving medieval pottery) being particularly common despite the now-obvious toxicity concerns
### Guild Structure and Regional Variation
- **Potter's guilds**: organized in towns with sufficient demand and concentrated production, following standard apprentice-journeyman-master patterns
- **Regional pottery traditions**: unlike some trades with relatively uniform practice across Europe, pottery developed genuinely distinct regional traditions — particular clay sources, firing techniques, decorative styles, and vessel forms becoming associated with specific production centers
- **Production centers**: some areas became known for quality pottery production serving wider markets — connecting to the freight company and merchant entries' broader discussion of regional specialization and trade
### Rural vs. Urban Production
- **Village potters**: many smaller communities supported a local potter meeting basic everyday needs — a relatively modest, locally-oriented operation
- **Larger production centers**: some areas developed more substantial, commercially-oriented pottery production specifically for wider market distribution, requiring the same kind of organized freight/transport infrastructure covered throughout this list
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A distinctive regional pottery tradition as a worldbuilding detail adding texture to different areas of your setting — the characteristic vessel forms, glazing colors, and decorative motifs of pottery from a specific region giving NPCs' homes and market stalls an immediately recognizable visual identity without heavy description
- A specific albarello or storage jar as a plot-relevant object — perhaps a distinctive apothecary jar from a specific production center helps identify where a poison or remedy originated, connecting the potter's trade to a medical mystery investigation
- Decorated floor tiles in a church or wealthy building as a visual storytelling element — medieval floor tiles sometimes depicted heraldic, narrative, or symbolic imagery, giving a potter's work potential narrative significance beyond pure utility
- A kiln as a location detail — a working pottery kiln, burning continuously during firing cycles, as an atmospheric industrial feature of a settlement, and a practical location for a secret meeting (the noise and heat providing cover) or a dangerous confrontation
