### The Craft and Its Structure
There wasn't really a standalone "dog breeder" trade with its own guild and shopfront in medieval Europe — dog breeding and rearing was almost entirely tied to **specific functional purposes** and organized around **who needed the dogs**, most significantly the noble hunting establishment. The closest real equivalent to a "dog breeder" as a distinct profession is the **kennel-keeper (or "berner"/"varlet de chiens" in some regional terminology) and huntsman** — staff employed within a noble or royal household specifically to breed, raise, train, and maintain hunting hounds, which were themselves a major status investment and practical necessity for medieval aristocratic life.

**Hunting as the driving purpose** — Hunting was simultaneously **practical food-gathering, a major aristocratic pastime, and a genuine display of status and wealth** for medieval nobility, and different hunting purposes required genuinely different, specialized dog types, each demanding real breeding knowledge to maintain and improve: **scenthounds** (like ancestors of bloodhounds) for tracking game by smell over distance, **sighthounds** (greyhound-type dogs) for high-speed chase of fast game like deer or hare, **alaunts and mastiff-type dogs** for bringing down and holding large, dangerous game like boar, and **smaller terrier-type dogs** for flushing game from burrows or dense cover. A noble hunting establishment of any real size maintained **multiple specialized packs**, each requiring dedicated breeding lines, training regimens, and care — a genuinely significant ongoing household expense and area of real expertise.

**The kennel-keeper's role** — Managing a noble household's hounds involved: **breeding decisions** (selecting which dogs to pair based on desired traits — speed, scenting ability, temperament, size — genuine practical animal husbandry knowledge accumulated through experience rather than formal science), **whelping and rearing puppies**, **training** (a long, skilled process turning a young dog into a functional hunting animal, requiring patience and real technique), **feeding and health care** (managing a working kennel of potentially dozens of dogs, recognizing illness, injury, and the practical logistics of keeping a large working-animal population healthy), and **maintaining the hunt itself** — managing the pack's behavior and coordination during an actual hunt, which required real skill in reading and directing groups of trained animals in the field. Senior kennel-keepers/huntsmen occupied a position of genuine trust and expertise within a noble household, often ranking above ordinary household servants due to the specialized skill and the direct, personal involvement of the lord himself in hunting.

**Beyond hunting hounds** — Dogs also served as **guard and working animals** for farms and lower-status households (mongrel-type dogs bred more casually for practical guarding/herding purposes rather than through deliberate refined breeding programs), and, notably, **small lapdogs** became a genuine status accessory for noblewomen by the later medieval period — small, decorative companion dogs kept specifically for affection and display rather than any working purpose, a detail worth knowing if you want a "toy breed as luxury status symbol" angle parallel to fine gloves or embroidered clothing.

**No formal "breed standard" as such** — It's worth flagging that medieval dog "breeding" didn't operate with the rigid, closed-registry breed-standard concept that exists today — dogs were bred and selected for **function and observable traits** (a good scenting dog bred to another good scenting dog) rather than toward a fixed, documented physical standard, and regional/functional "types" (rather than modern "breeds") were the operative category — a "greyhound" or "alaunt" was recognized by function and rough physical type rather than a closed genetic lineage the way a modern kennel club would define it.

**Status** — Genuinely respectable within its niche — a skilled huntsman/kennel-keeper serving a great lord could be a trusted, valued, long-serving household figure with real specialized authority, though the role existed entirely within noble service rather than as an independent tradesperson selling to an open market the way a smith or saddler did. Common people who kept working dogs (farm guard dogs, herding dogs) did so through ordinary practical necessity rather than any specialized "breeder" role — the specialized, skilled version of the trade lived specifically within the hunting establishments of the wealthy.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Animal Handling, Survival (or Nature, for breeding/husbandry knowledge)
- **Tool Proficiency:** None specific, or proficiency reflecting hunting equipment/horn-signaling
- **Feat:** Skilled or Tavern Brawler (physical comfort managing large, sometimes dangerous animals)
- **Equipment:** A hunting horn, leashes and training gear, a favored, well-trained hound (a genuine ongoing companion/asset), practical outdoor clothing, 10 gp

This background is an excellent, natural fit for a **Ranger** (animal companion synergy is almost too on-the-nose, in a good way) — a kennel-keeper background gives a mechanically and narratively grounded reason for a beast-companion-focused character to have deep, practical animal-handling roots. It also suits a **Druid** (working animal husbandry knowledge, comfort with a pack dynamic) or a **Fighter/Paladin** who served in a noble's hunting establishment before taking up arms more directly.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Lord's Master Huntsman** — Runs an entire noble kennel establishment, genuinely trusted and respected within the household, possessing real specialized authority most household staff lack — a good patron or contact for a hunting-themed or wilderness adventure.
- **The Breeding Rival** — Two kennel-keepers (from rival noble households, or competing within one) locked in a prestige competition over whose hounds perform best at the hunt — echoes the guild-rivalry pattern from other trades, but centered on animal husbandry bragging rights.
- **The Lapdog Keeper** — Tends a noblewoman's small decorative companion dogs, a genuinely different, lower-stakes, higher-comfort role than the rough-and-tumble hunting kennel — good contrast NPC, possibly with real insider access to court gossip via the noblewoman's private chambers.
- **The Poacher's Dog-Handler** — Someone using genuine hound-handling skill for illegal hunting on a lord's protected land — combines this profession with real, tangible legal risk and moral complexity around hunting rights.
- **The Devoted Old Huntsman** — Aging, deeply attached to his hounds (some individually named and known across a lifetime of service), facing displacement as a new lord or heir brings in their own kennel staff — a quietly poignant NPC.
### Game Hooks
- **The Poaching Investigation** — Illegal hunting on a lord's protected land traces back to someone using genuinely skilled hound-handling — the party has to track down the poacher(s), possibly sympathizing with their motive (hunger, injustice) rather than treating it as simple villainy.
- **The Prize Hound Theft** — A noble's prized, extensively bred hunting hound (or breeding pair) is stolen — valuable, traceable by very specific traits, and a good "recover the valuable animal" hook with real emotional stakes if the party gets to know the dog.
- **The Hunt Gone Wrong** — A formal noble hunt goes badly (the quarry turns out to be more dangerous than expected — a real boar, or something monstrous in a fantasy setting) and the party has to help manage the chaos, protect the hunting party, or handle a pack of now-panicked hounds.
- **Breeding Line Politics** — Two noble households compete fiercely over hound-breeding prestige, and the party gets pulled into sabotage, theft, or mediating an escalating rivalry that's genuinely more about status than the dogs themselves.
- **The Loyal Hound's Secret** — A hunting dog behaves strangely around a specific person, place, or object — animal instinct picking up on something (a disguised threat, a hidden magical item, an impending danger) before any human notices — using the kennel-keeper's deep animal knowledge as the key to reading the clue correctly.
