### The Craft and Its Structure
Paper was, for most of the medieval European period, the **new technology** — a genuine outsider material competing against the entrenched, expensive, but trusted standard of **parchment/vellum** (prepared animal skin), and understanding that competition is the single most useful thing for bringing this profession to life in your setting.

**Origins and spread** — Papermaking originated in China, spread through the Islamic world (where it was well-established by the 8th-9th centuries, with major production centers like Baghdad, Damascus, and later al-Andalus/Islamic Spain), and reached Christian Europe comparatively late — Islamic Spain had paper mills by the 1100s, and the craft spread into Christian Europe in earnest through Italy (Fabriano, a town that became a legendary European papermaking center) by the 1200s-1300s, then onward into Germany, France, and beyond over the following two centuries. This means paper spent a long stretch of the medieval period as a **regionally uneven, still-proving-itself technology** — well-established and trusted in some places while still novel, foreign, or suspect in others, which is a great asymmetry to build into a fantasy setting (different regions/cultures at very different points on the parchment-to-paper transition).

**How it was made** — European papermaking used **linen and cotton rag** as its raw material (not wood pulp, which is a much later industrial development) — old cloth, worn-out garments, and textile scraps were collected (often by traveling **rag-and-bone collectors**, a whole minor trade of its own worth a mention), sorted, cleaned, and left to **rot and ferment** in water for days or weeks to break the fibers down, then **beaten** (originally by hand or foot-powered stamping mills, later by proper water-powered **stamping mills** using heavy wooden hammers) into a wet pulp slurry. A **vatman** dipped a rectangular wire-mesh **mould** into the vat, lifting out an even, thin layer of pulp fibers, which was then couched (pressed) onto felt, stacked and pressed under heavy weight to squeeze out water, and finally hung to dry. Once dry, the sheet needed **sizing** — treating with gelatin or similar substance so ink wouldn't bleed and feather into the fibers — before it was ready for a scribe's pen. This is a genuinely industrial-feeling process for the period: water-power dependent, multi-stage, and requiring real capital investment in a mill, which meant papermaking, unlike ink making, was organized around dedicated **paper mills** rather than small individual workshops.

**Paper vs. parchment — the real tension** — Parchment was durable, prestigious, and trusted for anything meant to last (legal documents, Bibles, important charters), while paper was **cheaper, faster to produce, and available in much greater volume**, but carried a real reputation problem for a long stretch — seen as flimsy, less permanent, less dignified, even somewhat disreputable for serious or sacred documents. Some legal and ecclesiastical contexts specifically required parchment for important documents well after paper was widely available and used for everyday, lower-stakes writing (student notes, drafts, personal letters, account books). This "cheap new material versus prestigious old one" tension is genuinely rich, real economic and cultural friction you can drop directly into a fantasy setting.

**The printing press connection** — Paper's growing availability through the 1300s-1400s was a direct precondition for Gutenberg's printing press (mid-1400s) to work economically at all — parchment was far too expensive and inconsistent in size/supply to support print runs at scale, while paper mills could produce large, standardized quantities. If your setting includes or is approaching a "printing press" technological beat, papermakers are the unglamorous but essential infrastructure that made it possible, mirroring how wool merchants and Staple ports fed cloth manufacturing, or how hops fed brewing's commercial expansion.

**Guild and mill economics** — Paper mills required real capital (a water-powered site, stamping equipment, vats, skilled vatmen and couchers working in teams) making papermaking more capital-intensive and industrially organized than most crafts discussed so far — closer to milling grain than to a lone artisan's workshop. Mill owners could become genuinely wealthy where paper trade flourished (Fabriano's mills are a real historical example of a whole town's prosperity built on the craft), while the actual skilled laborers — vatmen, couchers, rag-sorters — occupied a more ordinary working-trade position within that larger operation.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Investigation, Persuasion (or Nature, for the fermentation/material-processing angle)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Alchemist's Supplies (a strong fit — the rag fermentation and sizing process is genuinely proto-chemical) or a custom "Papermaker's Tools"
- **Feat:** Skilled or Lucky
- **Equipment:** A paper mould and deckle, a small stock of finished paper sheets, sizing materials, rough working clothes, 10 gp

This background suits a **Wizard or Artificer** particularly well (paper's role as the "new, disruptive information technology" of the era pairs nicely with a caster whose magic likewise represents newer, less traditionally trusted power), or a **Rogue/Bard** from a mercantile mill-owning family, comfortable with capital, labor management, and the social friction between "respectable old ways" and "cheap new ways." It's also a great background for a character who grew up genuinely poor (rag-sorting and vat labor were not glamorous jobs) but has real technical literacy most low-status characters wouldn't.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Mill Owner Chasing Respectability** — Grown genuinely wealthy off paper production but still looked down on by parchment-trade traditionalists and old money — desperate for legitimacy, a good patron or quest-giver NPC with a chip on their shoulder.
- **The Rag Collector** — A traveling gatherer of old cloth and scraps who supplies mills, moving constantly between towns and villages — sees and hears everything, a natural low-key information broker/gossip source, similar in spirit to the crossroads farrier.
- **The Parchment Loyalist** — A scribe, notary, or church official who refuses to accept paper documents as legitimate, causing real bureaucratic friction — a source of comedic or genuinely obstructive conflict when the party needs something recorded or certified.
- **The Vatman Prodigy** — A young, exceptionally skilled sheet-former whose paper is noticeably more even and higher quality than anyone else's — recruited, poached, or protected by rival mill owners.
- **The Foreign-Trained Papermaker** — Learned the craft in Islamic Spain or Italy and brought it somewhere it's still a novelty — treated with suspicion, fascination, or economic opportunism depending on local temperament.
### Game Hooks
- **The Legitimacy Fight** — A crucial document (a will, a treaty, a land claim) was written on paper instead of parchment, and its legal validity is being challenged purely on that basis — party has to navigate a dispute that's genuinely about material culture and trust, not just facts.
- **Mill Sabotage** — A paper mill (a serious capital investment) is sabotaged — flooded, burned, or its water source diverted — by parchment-trade rivals, a jealous competitor, or something else entirely; investigate and help rebuild or catch the culprit.
- **The Rag Shortage** — A sudden shortage of rag material (a war disrupting cloth trade, a plague reducing the population's cast-off clothing, a rival buying up supply) threatens a mill's operation — an economic-logistics hook with a ticking clock.
- **Printing Press Precursor** — If your setting is approaching a "printing press" technological moment, the party could be involved in securing paper supply, protecting an inventor, or navigating the guild/political upheaval a print technology threatens to cause — echoing the hop-beer and windmill "new tech vs. old ways" hooks.
- **The Watermark Clue** — Papermakers often pressed a distinctive **watermark** into their sheets (a real historical practice — wire designs woven into the mould left a faint image visible when held to light) — a document's watermark can be traced back to a specific mill, giving the party a "trace the craftsmanship" investigation thread, this time through paper instead of leather or ink.
