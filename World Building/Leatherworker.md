### A Trade Family We've Touched on Repeatedly, Now Brought Together
We've referenced leatherworking constantly throughout this list — tanners supplying cordwainers (boot makers), leather for armor padding and straps with armorers, the connection to butchers' hide byproducts — so let's bring the full leather production and crafting chain together as its own dedicated entry.
### The Supply Chain — From Hide to Finished Goods
Leatherworking actually involved several genuinely distinct specialized trades, reflecting how complex the hide-to-finished-product process was:
**Tanners**: converted raw animal hides into usable leather through a genuinely unpleasant, labor-intensive chemical process
- **Soaking and dehairing**: hides were soaked, sometimes in lime solutions, to loosen and remove hair and remaining flesh
- **Tanning agents**: the actual tanning process used either **vegetable tanning** (using tannin-rich materials like oak bark or oak galls, the same tannin source mentioned in the dyer entry for ink and black dye production) or, for different leather types, other treatments
- **The genuinely foul process**: as mentioned in the boot maker entry, some tanning methods involved substances like dung or urine in preparation stages, contributing to tanneries' notorious smell and their typical location at town margins
- **Extended processing time**: vegetable tanning particularly was a slow process, sometimes taking many months for hides to be properly tanned, representing genuine ongoing capital tied up in the tanning process

**Curriers**: a further specialization, working tanned leather to improve its flexibility, water-resistance, and finish — essentially a finishing trade between raw tanning and the leather being ready for actual crafting use, treating and dressing the leather with oils or other substances

**Whittawyers/whitetawers**: specialized in a different tanning process (using alum and other substances rather than vegetable tannin) producing a lighter-colored, different-quality leather suited to different applications than oak-bark-tanned leather
### What Got Made From Leather
**Footwear**: as covered extensively in the boot maker (cordwainer) entry — boots, shoes, pattens

**Clothing and accessories**:
- **Belts and girdles**: genuinely universal accessories across social classes, with quality and decoration (buckles, tooling) varying by wealth
- **Gloves**: ranging from simple work gloves to fine, decorated gloves for wealthy wearers — glovers sometimes constituted their own specialized guild given the particular skill required for properly fitted hand-coverings
- **Jerkins and other leather garments**: practical outer clothing, particularly for those doing physical outdoor work

**Military equipment**: connecting to the armorer entry
- **Padding and straps**: leather provided essential padding beneath armor and the strapping holding plate armor pieces together
- **Boiled leather (cuir bouilli)**: a hardening treatment process producing a tough, semi-rigid leather used for some armor components, particularly for those who couldn't afford full metal armor — a genuinely clever low-cost alternative providing real protective value

**Horse equipment**: saddles, bridles, and harness — **saddlers** often constituted their own distinct specialized guild given the particular skill and importance of well-made horse equipment, connecting to the broader importance of horses covered throughout this list (warfare, transport, agriculture)

**Containers and cases**: bags, pouches, and protective cases/sheaths for various items (including sword/knife sheaths, connecting to the blacksmith and bladesmith entries)

**Bookbinding**: leather covers for books represented a genuine specialized application, connecting to the manuscript and scriptoria work covered in the artist/sculptor entry — fine leather bindings, sometimes elaborately tooled and decorated, protected valuable manuscripts

**Bottles and vessels**: leather "bottles" (technically often called **blackjacks** when treated/waxed for liquid-holding) provided a lighter, more portable alternative to the cooper's barrels or ceramic vessels for carrying liquids
### Guild Structure
- Given how many distinct specializations existed within "leatherworking" broadly, guild structure reflected this real differentiation — tanners, curriers, cordwainers, saddlers, and glovers might each maintain their own distinct guild in larger towns with sufficient specialized demand to support separate organization
- **Tanners' marginal social position**: despite leather's universal necessity, tanners specifically (similar to butchers, though perhaps even more pronounced given the smell and unpleasantness involved) occupied a somewhat lower-prestige position within the broader leather trade hierarchy compared to the finer finishing and crafting trades working with the already-processed material
- **Saddlers' relative prestige**: given horses' importance to nobility, warfare, and transport broadly, skilled saddlers serving wealthy clientele could achieve real status and prosperity within the leather trade family
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A tannery's genuinely unpleasant, marginalized location as a believable setting for a back-alley meeting, smuggling operation, or unsavory investigation — connecting to similar sensory-grounding hooks suggested for the dyer entry
- Boiled leather (cuir bouilli) armor as a grounded, lower-cost alternative to full metal armor for poorer PCs or NPCs — useful for differentiating equipment tiers in your setting without needing to invent new materials
- A master saddler crafting horse equipment for a noble's prized warhorse or a PC's special mount — a natural, grounded craft-commission scene parallel to similar hooks suggested for armorers and furniture makers
- Leather bookbinding as a detail connecting to a valuable or magical tome — perhaps a particularly important book's leather binding itself holds some significance (a hidden message tooled into the design, or material with some protective/preservative property)
