The **Merchant Quarter** sits just beyond La Foire — a cluster of counting houses and leased lodgings where the foreign trading communities have, over generations, put down semi-permanent roots.
## **The Flemish**
The largest and longest-established foreign presence, dating back to the early days of the wool trade when Flemish weavers first realized Port Ancelin wool was suited to their cloth. They operate mostly through **factors' agreements** — buying in bulk at La Foire and shipping raw wool home to be woven in Bruges and Ghent, where it commands a far higher price as finished cloth than it ever could as raw fleece. The Flemish keep the most visible foothold in town: a handful of merchant families maintain counting houses year-round rather than just during trading season, and it was a Flemish merchant who quietly confirmed Robin Attwood's wool had been wrongly graded during the Mallow Affair — a sign of how closely they watch the market's integrity, since their own profits depend on it.
## **The Hanseatic Merchants**
Traders from the northern German trading league, drawn by Port Anceline's deep harbour, capable of handling the largest wool cogs of the era. Less rooted in town than the Flemish — they tend to arrive seasonally with the trading fleets, conduct business in concentrated bursts, and leave once their ships are full. Their presence is felt more at the quay and the Tron than in any permanent quarter — they deal in volume and efficiency, less interested in long social entanglements with local families.
## **The Italians**
Smaller in number but disproportionately influential, mostly wool brokers rather than direct buyers — middlemen who deal less in the open market and more through private arrangements, often brokered over quiet conversation at the Fleece & Anchor rather than in the open stalls of La Foire. Their connections run toward the great Italian banking and cloth families, and a good word from an Italian broker can open doors to buyers in Florence or Genoa that no local factor could reach alone.
## **Daily Life & Friction**
It's not unusual to hear three or four languages shouted across La Foire on a market day, and the Merchant Quarter has slowly absorbed touches of each community — a Flemish-style guildhall here, a small chapel favored by Hanseatic sailors there. Tension exists but rarely boils over: the foreign merchants need Port Ancelin's wool and reputation for honest trade as much as the town needs their money, which tends to keep relations civil even when negotiations get heated.
