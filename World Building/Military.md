### Pulling Together a Genuinely Sprawling Topic
We've touched on military pieces throughout this entire list — the fighter class mapping, mercenaries, the armorer and bowyer/fletcher supply chains, the constable's military command authority, knights in the gentlemen/ladies entry, forest law's connection to martial hunting training — so let's bring the actual structure of medieval military organization together as its own dedicated synthesis.
### The Feudal Levy — The Foundational System
As touched on in the mercenary and gentlemen/ladies entries: the core medieval military obligation ran through the **feudal hierarchy** itself
- **Knight service**: a knight holding land from a lord owed military service in exchange — typically a defined term (commonly around 40 days annually in various traditions), bringing himself, his equipment, and often additional men he was responsible for providing
- **Scaling up the hierarchy**: lords owed service to their own overlords, who owed service to the king — meaning a royal summons to war theoretically cascaded down through multiple levels of feudal obligation, with each lord responsible for mustering his own vassals
- **Genuine limitations**: as covered in the mercenary entry, this system's fixed service terms and variable quality created real practical problems for extended campaigns, driving the growing reliance on paid professional soldiers (mercenaries) covered in that entry as the medieval period progressed
### Knights — The Core of Feudal Military Force
- **Heavy cavalry**: knights fought primarily as armored heavy cavalry, representing enormous individual investment — warhorses (the destriers mentioned in the animal trainer entry), full armor (connecting to the armorer entry), weapons, and the years of dedicated training (the page-squire-knight progression touched on in the gentlemen/ladies entry) all required substantial wealth, explaining why knighthood correlated so strongly with landholding nobility
- **The retinue**: a knight typically didn't fight alone — he brought squires, men-at-arms, and other supporting fighters he was responsible for equipping and bringing to muster, meaning a single knight's feudal obligation actually represented a small military unit, not just one armored individual
### Infantry — Often Underappreciated But Essential
- **Levied infantry**: common foot soldiers, sometimes raised through broader feudal obligation extending below the knightly class — ordinary free men owing some lesser military service, equipped far more modestly than knights
- **Archers**: as covered extensively in the bowyer and fletcher entries, archery (particularly the English longbow tradition) became genuinely decisive in various medieval conflicts, with archers drawn from a broader social base than knighthood, given the longbow's lower equipment cost (though it demanded serious lifelong training, as noted in the bowyer entry)
- **Pikemen and spearmen**: infantry formations using polearms could, with proper discipline and formation, genuinely threaten even armored cavalry — the Swiss pike tradition mentioned in the mercenary entry represents a particularly famous and effective example, though this reached its fullest development somewhat later in the period
- **Crossbowmen**: as mentioned in the bowyer and mercenary entries, crossbows required less lifelong training than longbows, making them more accessible to broader recruitment, including significant mercenary crossbow traditions (Genoese crossbowmen particularly)
### Castles and Siege Warfare
Connecting to the armorer, mason, and guard entries:
- **Castles as the war's actual geography**: medieval warfare often centered less on open-field pitched battles and more on **siege warfare** — capturing or holding fortified positions, since controlling castles meant controlling the surrounding territory
- **Siege equipment**: trebuchets, catapults, and battering rams required real engineering knowledge, connecting to the carpenter and mason entries' structural expertise
- **Garrison defense**: as covered in the guard entry, castle garrisons held fortifications against siege, with the constable commanding defense
- **Mining and countermining**: attacking forces sometimes dug tunnels to undermine castle walls, while defenders might dig counter-tunnels to intercept them — genuinely sophisticated underground siege engineering
### Naval Warfare
Connecting to the docks/wharfs and carpenter (shipwright) entries:
- **Less specialized naval combat than later eras**: medieval naval warfare often involved adapting merchant vessels for combat (boarding actions, essentially land battles fought on ships) rather than purpose-built warships in the later sense, though some dedicated war-vessel traditions did exist
- **Coastal raiding and piracy**: as touched on in the docks/wharfs entry, naval threats to trade were a genuine, persistent concern requiring escort and defense
### Military Orders — Religious-Military Synthesis
Connecting to the paladin discussion in the D&D classes entry:
- **The Knights Templar, Knights Hospitaller, Teutonic Knights**, and similar orders combined formal religious vows with dedicated military function — genuinely unique medieval institutions blending monastery and military unit
- **Crusading context**: these orders developed substantially around the Crusades, defending pilgrimage routes and conquered territory in the Holy Land, though the Teutonic Knights particularly also became major military/political powers in the Baltic region
- **Independent wealth and power**: military orders accumulated substantial independent landholding and wealth across Europe (not just in crusading territories), making them genuinely significant political actors in their own right, somewhat independent of normal feudal hierarchy
### Command Structure
Connecting to the constable and government official entries:
- **The Constable and Marshal**: as covered in those entries, these senior household-derived offices held genuine military command authority at the kingdom level
- **Lords commanding their own retinues**: below the highest command, individual lords led their own feudal forces, creating a command structure that was as much about social hierarchy and personal loyalty as formal military rank in the modern sense
### Logistics — The Unglamorous Reality
Connecting to the freight company, farm, and chef entries:
- **Feeding an army**: provisioning significant military forces required real logistical sophistication — purchasing, requisitioning, or looting food, connecting to the agricultural surplus systems covered in the farm entry
- **Equipment supply chains**: as covered extensively throughout this list (armorers, bowyers, fletchers, blacksmiths), maintaining an army's equipment required substantial ongoing craft production and supply coordination
- **Pay**: as covered in the mercenary entry, increasingly significant portions of medieval military forces required actual cash payment rather than pure feudal obligation, connecting to the taxation and banking systems covered throughout this list
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A military order analog (religious-military hybrid) as a major faction in your setting — independently wealthy, semi-autonomous from normal feudal hierarchy, with its own internal culture and goals that might align or conflict with PCs' interests
- The feudal-levy-versus-mercenary tension as a worldbuilding/political detail — a ruler genuinely torn between unreliable-but-traditional feudal obligation and expensive-but-flexible paid professional forces, especially relevant if your setting has its own equivalent of the Hundred Years' War's protracted conflict
- A siege as a multi-session set piece — drawing on the genuine combination of engineering, logistics, and combat siege warfare actually involved, rather than simplifying it to straightforward combat
- Military logistics as an adventure hook in its own right — PCs tasked with securing supply lines, escorting payment to unpaid (and increasingly restless) mercenary forces, or investigating sabotage to an army's equipment supply chain
