### Challenging a Persistent Myth First
There's a remarkably durable popular misconception that medieval Europeans didn't bathe — that personal hygiene was universally neglected and bathing essentially unknown. This is genuinely false, and worth addressing directly before describing what public bathing actually looked like, since the myth has done real damage to how people picture medieval life.

Medieval people **did** bathe, valued cleanliness, and had specific institutions dedicated to public bathing. The decline of Roman-style elaborate bath culture is real, and later medieval/early modern religious and disease-related concerns did reduce public bathing's prominence in some periods and regions — but the flat claim that medieval people never bathed or considered bathing somehow wrong is simply not supported by the historical evidence.
### The Bathhouse — What It Actually Was
**Stews** (in English terminology) or **bagnios**, **étuves** (French), **Badstube** (German) — various regional terms for essentially the same institution: a public bathing establishment providing hot water bathing for a fee, serving urban populations who lacked private bathing facilities at home (as covered in the private homes entry, most medieval households had no dedicated bathing infrastructure).
### Physical Setup
- **Heating system**: the central technical requirement — heating significant quantities of water required substantial fuel consumption, connecting to the charcoal maker and logger entries' fuel supply discussions. Some bathhouses used furnace systems heating water in large vessels, while others used steam/vapor bathing approaches more directly
- **Tubs**: wooden bathtubs (connecting to the cooper entry's barrel/vessel-making tradition) filled with hot water for soaking — sometimes large enough for multiple bathers simultaneously, a social bathing norm quite different from modern individual bathing expectations
- **Steam rooms**: some establishments offered vapor/steam bathing alongside or instead of direct water immersion, similar in concept to a sauna
- **Attendants**: bathhouse staff provided scrubbing, hair washing, and sometimes additional services — connecting to the broader personal grooming traditions of the period
- **Dressing/relaxation areas**: space for undressing, resting, and sometimes eating/drinking before or after bathing — since a trip to the bathhouse was a social occasion rather than a purely functional hygiene task
### Social Function — More Than Just Hygiene
Like taverns and markets covered throughout this list, bathhouses served real social functions beyond their primary stated purpose:
- **Social gathering and conversation**: bathing together was a social activity, particularly significant for urban populations without the private space for social entertaining covered in wealthier homes
- **News and gossip exchange**: the relaxed, social bathing environment was a natural venue for information exchange
- **Business discussions**: merchants and others sometimes used bathhouses as informal meeting spaces, similar to the social functions of inns and taverns covered earlier
### The Notorious "Stews" — Prostitution Overlap
This is the aspect of medieval bathhouses most often referenced in historical popular culture, and worth addressing honestly:
- **Real but probably overstated in modern popular imagination**: many medieval bathhouses were perfectly ordinary bathing establishments without any prostitution involvement — but some, particularly in certain cities and periods, did overlap with or shade into brothel functions
- **The "stews" association**: in London specifically, the Southwark "stews" (bathhouses) across the Thames became so associated with prostitution that "stews" became essentially synonymous with brothel in some period usage — though the connection between bathing and commercial sex was more specifically concentrated in particular times and places than a universal medieval bathhouse characteristic
- **Municipal regulation**: similar to the brothel licensing discussed in that entry, civic authorities in various places attempted to regulate bathhouses — sometimes specifically to manage the prostitution overlap, sometimes purely to regulate the bathing trade itself
### Religious and Moral Attitudes
- **Generally positive toward cleanliness**: medieval Christian theology generally valued physical cleanliness as compatible with spiritual life — bathing before major religious occasions (Christmas, Easter) was encouraged, and cleanliness had genuine religious associations
- **Periodic concern about mixed-sex bathing**: some Church and civic commentary expressed concern about mixed-gender bathing's moral implications, and various regulations attempted to require gender separation — suggesting mixed bathing did occur and was considered a problem worth legislating against by those with moral concerns
- **Later increasing suspicion**: toward the later medieval and early modern period, combination of disease concerns (syphilis's arrival and spread in the late 15th century specifically made shared bathing seem newly dangerous) and intensified religious concern about bodily exposure contributed to declining public bath culture in some regions — but this is a late and somewhat post-medieval development rather than a universal medieval attitude
### Who Used Bathhouses
- **Urban working and middle classes**: the primary market, since wealthy households had more private bathing options and very poor households lacked money for the fee
- **Travelers and pilgrims**: inns and bathhouses near major routes served travelers needing to clean up after the road, connecting to the inn entry's broader discussion of traveler service infrastructure
- **Barber-surgeon overlap**: as covered in the barber entry, some barber-surgeons operated from or adjacent to bathhouses, since the combination of hot water, attendants, and a bodily-service context made the combination natural — bloodletting after a hot bath was considered medically appropriate in humoral theory, connecting the two trades practically
### Decline and Regional Variation
- **Never universal**: public bathhouse culture was always more developed in urban, particularly larger urban, contexts than rural ones
- **Regional variation**: some regions (Germany particularly) maintained stronger and more long-lasting public bath traditions than others
- **Class shift**: as private bathing facilities became more accessible to wealthier households, public bathhouses became increasingly associated with lower social status — a familiar pattern seen with other trades and services throughout this list
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A bathhouse as a social gathering and information-exchange location distinct from taverns — perhaps more intimate, more socially leveling (clothes removed being a great equalizer of visible status markers), and offering different roleplay dynamics than a standard tavern scene
- The bathhouse/prostitution overlap as a worldbuilding texture detail rather than a defining characteristic — a district known for its bathhouses carrying a certain reputation that may or may not be entirely deserved, creating the kind of moral ambiguity and social complexity seen with several other entries in this list
- A barber-surgeon operating from a bathhouse as a natural NPC combination — a single establishment offering bathing, grooming, bloodletting, and minor medical treatment, giving PCs a one-stop shop for several services and a single well-connected NPC running all of them
- The later disease-driven bathhouse decline as a worldbuilding event — perhaps your setting has experienced something analogous (a magical plague, a curse associated with shared water) that's driven a similar social shift away from previously normal public bathing, creating cultural anxiety around an institution that used to be unremarkable
