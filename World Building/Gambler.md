### A Widespread, Genuinely Cross-Class Activity With Mixed Legal Status
Gambling in medieval Europe wasn't a "profession" in the structured guild sense of most entries on this list, but it was a genuinely widespread social practice spanning every social class, with real legal complexity, moral controversy, and — for some — a way to make a living, whether through skill, luck, or outright cheating.
### Games People Actually Played
**Dice**: probably the single most common gambling activity across all social classes
- **Hazard**: a popular medieval dice game (whose name, fittingly, gives us the modern word "hazard" in the sense of risk) involving betting on dice roll outcomes with genuinely complex rules
- Dice were cheap and portable (connecting back to the bone carver entry, which covered dice production), making this the most accessible form of gambling for ordinary people, soldiers, and travelers
- Dice games appear constantly in period sources, court records, and moral condemnations — taverns, markets, and military camps were common venues

**Cards**: arrived in Europe later in the medieval period (roughly 14th century, with origins tracing back through Islamic and ultimately Chinese card traditions transmitted via trade routes), spreading relatively quickly once introduced and becoming an increasingly significant gambling medium toward the end of the medieval period and into the early modern era

**Board games with betting elements**: games like backgammon-precursor games (tables games) sometimes involved wagering, blending strategic skill with gambling stakes

**Wagering on contests**: betting on tournaments, jousts, archery contests, animal contests (including some of the blood sports mentioned with the animal trainer entry, like bear-baiting), and other competitive events was widespread across social classes
### Gambling Across Social Classes
- **Nobility and royal courts**: gambling, particularly dice and later cards, was a genuine, sometimes serious pastime at court — court records and chronicles document significant sums won and lost by nobles and even monarchs, and gambling debts among the elite could carry real political/financial consequences
- **Soldiers and military camps**: gambling was endemic in military contexts — soldiers with pay, time, and genuine mortality risk hanging over them had obvious reasons to gamble, and military camp gambling appears constantly in period sources
- **Common people and taverns**: as covered in the bartender entry, taverns and alehouses were frequent gambling venues, drawing the moral suspicion of religious and civic authorities already noted there
- **Clergy** (officially prohibited, unofficially common): Church law generally prohibited clergy from gambling, but enforcement was inconsistent, and period sources do document clerical gambling despite the prohibition
### Legal and Religious Attitudes
- **Church condemnation**: gambling was viewed with real moral suspicion by religious authorities — associated with greed, idleness, and the irrational, fortune-driven nature of dice/cards potentially conflicting with proper trust in divine providence rather than chance
- **Civic regulation**: various towns and kingdoms passed laws restricting or regulating gambling at different points — sometimes targeting it specifically among certain populations (common people, soldiers, or specific venues) while turning more of a blind eye to elite gambling
- **Inconsistent enforcement**: as with many medieval moral regulations (similar to brothel licensing and dancing restrictions covered earlier), formal prohibition coexisted with widespread practical tolerance, particularly for the wealthy and powerful who faced less real scrutiny
- **Gambling debts**: disputes over unpaid gambling debts could end up in legal proceedings, though the legal enforceability of gambling debts specifically varied by jurisdiction and period — some authorities were reluctant to use court power to enforce what they considered a morally dubious activity in the first place
### Professional and Semi-Professional Gambling
- **Itinerant gamblers**: some individuals made at least part of their living traveling between markets, fairs, and taverns, gambling with locals — overlapping somewhat with the more marginal traveling-entertainer population discussed earlier, and carrying similar social suspicion
- **Cheating and loaded dice**: given the financial stakes, cheating was a genuine, documented concern — weighted or shaved dice, marked cards (once cards became established), and sleight-of-hand techniques all have historical attestation, and "gambler" carried real connotations of potential dishonesty in period attitudes, similar to how the tooth-drawer entry noted showmanship blending with outright fraud
- **Professional cardsharps/dice-sharps**: by the time cards spread widely, dedicated cheats working markets and taverns for a living represented a real, recognized (if disreputable) category of semi-professional gambler
### Gambling and Other Vices — A Connected Moral Category
Medieval moralizing literature frequently grouped gambling with other condemned activities — drunkenness, prostitution, idleness — as interconnected vices often found together in the same disreputable venues (taverns, certain market activities), reflecting a broader medieval moral framework that saw these behaviors as mutually reinforcing rather than isolated individual choices.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A gambling den or back-room dice game as a classic, well-grounded urban adventure location — a good venue for PCs to gather information, get pulled into a debt-related plot hook, or encounter a cheating NPC whose loaded dice (or magically-influenced luck) creates a believable confrontation
- A noble court's gambling culture as a political intrigue element — significant gambling debts among courtiers creating leverage, blackmail material, or genuine financial ruin with political consequences, mirroring real historical court gambling stakes
- A traveling cardsharp or dice-sharp NPC blending genuine skill with outright cheating — a fun, morally ambiguous rogue-adjacent character type for a market or tavern encounter
- Magical luck manipulation as a fantasy-specific complication on top of mundane cheating methods — a gambler whose suspiciously consistent winning streak turns out to involve actual magic rather than simple skill or fraud, giving a mundane vice scene a fantastical twist
