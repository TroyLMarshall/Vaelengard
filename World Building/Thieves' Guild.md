### A Famous Fantasy Convention With Real (But Different) Historical Roots
The "thieves' guild" is one of D&D's most iconic worldbuilding elements — a formal, organized criminal organization controlling underworld activity in a city, with its own hierarchy, training system, and guild-like structure. Let's examine what actually existed in medieval Europe, since the reality is genuinely interesting even where it differs from the fantasy convention.
### Did Actual Thieves' Guilds Exist?
The honest answer: **not in the formal, openly institutionalized way D&D presents them**, but organized criminal networks with genuine internal structure absolutely existed, and some specific historical examples come remarkably close to the fantasy archetype.

**The Coquillards**: a documented 15th-century French criminal organization operating across Burgundy, with recognized internal hierarchy, specialized roles with specific names for different types of criminal activity, their own argot (secret vocabulary), and organizational structure sophisticated enough to be documented in legal records when authorities broke up part of the network — the poet François Villon was apparently connected to this group

**The Beggars' Brotherhood**: various cities documented organized beggar networks with territorial control, internal hierarchy, and enforcement mechanisms — connecting to the beggar entry's discussion of the difference between licensed and unlicensed begging, since controlling who could beg where represented a genuine underground organizational function

**Urban criminal networks more broadly**: organized fencing operations (receiving stolen goods), protection rackets, and coordinated theft rings existed in major medieval cities — recognizable predecessors to modern organized crime even without formal "guild" structure
### Why Fully Formal Thieves' Guilds Didn't Quite Exist
Several genuine historical reasons the D&D thieves' guild archetype doesn't map perfectly onto medieval reality:

**Formalization = vulnerability**: the more formally organized a criminal network, the more visible it became to authorities — genuine criminal organizations had strong incentives to remain informal, compartmentalized, and deniable rather than developing the kind of formal institutional structure that would make them easier to identify and prosecute

**Guild structure required civic legitimacy**: as covered in the craft guilds entry, real guilds existed within a recognized civic framework — they had charters, guildhalls, and legal standing. Criminal organizations obviously couldn't openly claim these legitimizing structures without immediately attracting prosecution

**Alternative organizing principles**: real criminal networks organized around trust networks, kinship, ethnic community ties, and shared criminal enterprise rather than the formal apprenticeship/masterpiece/guild meeting structure of legitimate trades
### What Real Criminal Organization Looked Like
**Fencing networks**: receiving and reselling stolen goods required connections between thieves and legitimate-seeming merchants who could move stolen property without raising suspicion — a genuine, organized criminal function connecting the theft itself to the economic benefit  
**Protection and territorial control**: controlling which beggars, gamblers, and petty criminals operated in a specific urban territory — extracting a share of criminal proceeds in exchange for "permission" and protection from both rivals and sometimes authorities  
**Corrupt official relationships**: as touched on in the government official, constable, and tax assessor entries, corrupt officials were essential to criminal network operation — bribing watchmen to ignore certain activities, corrupting customs officials (connecting to the smuggler entry), or maintaining relationships with lawyers who could manage prosecution risks
### The Secret Language — Cant
One genuinely well-documented aspect of medieval criminal subculture:
- **Thieves' cant**: criminal argot — specialized vocabulary incomprehensible to outsiders, allowing criminal communication in public without being understood
- Documented in various European sources from the medieval and early modern period, with word lists preserved in both legal records (authorities trying to understand captured criminals) and literary sources
- This linguistic separation mirrors the trade-secret protection function of guild mystery knowledge, but applied to criminal rather than craft knowledge

### Building a Thieves' Guild for Your Setting
Since the fantasy convention is useful even if not perfectly historically accurate, here's how to ground it in genuine historical texture:

**Structure it around actual criminal functions rather than guild metaphor**:
- A fencing operation as the economic core — someone who can move stolen goods is the essential figure criminal networks form around
- Territorial control over specific criminal niches (begging in specific areas, protection of certain gambling dens) rather than a comprehensive criminal monopoly
- Corrupt official relationships as essential infrastructure rather than optional extras

**Use the Coquillards model**:
- Specialized roles with distinctive names for different criminal activities
- Internal argot/cant that marks membership
- Genuine hierarchy based on criminal reputation and utility rather than formal apprenticeship
- Vulnerability to law enforcement infiltration and prosecution as a realistic organizational weakness

**Connect to legitimate trades**:
- Perhaps a specific legitimate guild serves as cover — a guild of locksmiths, secondhand dealers, or river boatmen whose legitimate trade provides both cover and genuine criminal utility
- The blurry line between legitimate and criminal activity (a peddler who sometimes fences stolen goods, a tavern keeper who allows gambling and prostitution) more historically realistic than a sharp legitimate/criminal division
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A criminal network organized around a specific legitimate trade as cover — the secondhand dealers' guild that doubles as a fencing operation, the boatmen's association that controls smuggling routes, or the entertainers' guild whose traveling performers gather intelligence for criminal purposes
- The Coquillards' documented argot as inspiration for your thieves' guild's cant — PCs needing to learn or decode specific criminal vocabulary to gain access to or information from this network
- A corrupt official as the thieves' guild's most essential asset — not a shadowy guildmaster in a hidden headquarters but a city watchman captain, a customs official, or a minor judge whose protection makes the network's operations possible, making "taking down the guild" require confronting institutional corruption rather than just defeating criminal fighters
- Internal guild conflict as a plot driver — two competing criminal factions fighting for territorial control, with the PCs caught between them or recruited by one side against the other
