### A Trade Central to Europe's Most Important Industry
We've referenced weaving throughout this list — the dyer entry covered cloth coloring as part of the textile production chain, the merchant guilds entry discussed wool and cloth as the medieval economy's most significant traded commodities, the laborer entry mentioned spinning and textile work as supplementary household income, and the farm entry noted wool production as a major economic driver particularly in England. Let's give the actual cloth production craft its dedicated, full treatment.
### Textile Production's Economic Significance
Before getting into the craft itself, worth establishing just how important the textile industry was:
- **The dominant manufacturing industry of medieval Europe**: cloth production represented the largest organized manufacturing activity in medieval Europe by volume and value — more economically significant than metalworking, construction, or any other manufacturing sector
- **International trade driver**: as covered in the merchant guilds and docks/wharfs entries, English wool and Flemish cloth were among the most significant traded commodities in the entire medieval economy — the Hanseatic League, Italian banking houses, and major merchant organizations all substantially built around cloth trade
- **Employment multiplier**: the full cloth-production chain (shepherd → shearer → wool merchant → spinner → weaver → fuller → dyer → tailor) employed a remarkable proportion of the medieval population in various capacities
### The Production Chain — Where Weaving Fits
Weaving was one stage in a genuinely long production process:

**Sheep rearing**: as covered in the farm entry, wool began on the sheep's back — breed selection, pasture management, and animal care all affecting ultimate wool quality

**Shearing**: the annual harvest of wool fleeces — skilled work requiring both speed and care to avoid damaging the fleece or injuring the animal

**Sorting and grading**: separating wool by quality — different grades suited to different end products

**Washing/scouring**: removing lanolin and dirt from raw fleece — a smelly, labor-intensive process connecting to the soapmaker entry's discussion of wool scouring as a specific soap application

**Carding or combing**: preparing the cleaned wool fibers for spinning — either carding (producing a fluffy, slightly tangled fiber mass suited to woolen yarn) or combing (producing aligned, parallel fibers suited to worsted yarn), producing different final cloth characteristics

**Spinning**: converting prepared fiber into continuous yarn — traditionally with a drop spindle, increasingly with a spinning wheel (a significant medieval technological development improving spinning productivity substantially) as the period progressed

- Predominantly women's work, often performed as supplementary household labor throughout the medieval period

**Weaving**: interlacing warp (lengthwise) and weft (crosswise) threads to produce cloth — the specific stage this entry focuses on

**Fulling**: cleaning and thickening the woven cloth through pounding and washing — sometimes done by foot treading (connecting the term "walker" as a surname to this occupation), increasingly using water-powered fulling mills (connecting to the mills entry) through the medieval period

**Dyeing**: as covered in that entry, coloring the cloth either before or after weaving

**Finishing**: various surface treatments (napping, shearing the raised surface smooth) producing the final cloth's specific texture and appearance
### The Loom — The Weaver's Central Tool
**The horizontal ground loom**: the most basic type, used throughout antiquity and into the medieval period in various contexts

**The warp-weighted loom**: an older type with the warp threads hanging vertically, weighted at the bottom — used particularly in Scandinavian and early medieval Northern European traditions

**The horizontal treadle loom**: the technologically significant medieval development — a more sophisticated loom where foot pedals (treadles) controlled the shed (the opening through which the weft thread passed), freeing both hands for throwing and catching the shuttle carrying the weft thread. This dramatically improved weaving productivity compared to earlier loom types.

- Became widespread through the early medieval period, transforming professional cloth production
- Required a dedicated workshop space rather than being as easily portable as simpler loom types

**The drawloom**: for producing complex patterned weaves (silk brocades and similar elaborate textiles), the drawloom used a more complex mechanism allowing individual warp thread control — requiring either a second operator (a draw-boy controlling pattern threads while the weaver operated the treadles) or very sophisticated mechanical setup
### Types of Cloth Produced
**Woolen cloth**: the dominant medieval textile, ranging from rough homespun for ordinary use to the finest broadcloths of Flanders and England valued across Europe

- **Broadcloth**: a high-quality, densely woven wool cloth produced on wider looms (hence "broad"), fulled and finished to produce a smooth, dense surface — the premium English export product
- **Worsted**: a different texture from broadcloth, using combed rather than carded wool, producing a smoother, lighter fabric suited to different end uses

**Linen**: woven from flax fiber, used for clothing next to the skin (shirts, undergarments), household textiles (sheets, tablecloths), and various practical applications — somewhat simpler to weave than fine wool but requiring its own specific preparation and weaving knowledge

**Silk**: the luxury end of the textile market, requiring different weaving techniques and extremely valuable raw material imported from the East — silk weaving concentrated in specific specialist centers (Lucca in Italy particularly famous for medieval silk production) given the specialized knowledge and equipment required

**Fustian and mixed-fiber cloths**: various combinations of fiber types (wool and linen, cotton and linen) producing specific characteristics — fustian particularly becoming significant later in the medieval period
### Guild Structure — Varied and Sometimes Complex
Textile production's multi-stage character created complex guild relationships:

**Weavers' guilds**: organized specifically around the weaving stage — one of the oldest and most established guild types in many cities  

**The Flemish cloth towns**: cities like Ghent, Bruges, and Ypres built their entire civic identity and economy around organized cloth production — the weavers' and related textile guilds representing genuinely powerful political forces in these cities' governance (connecting to the merchant guilds and guild office entries' discussion of guild political power)  

**The putting-out system**: an alternative to guild workshop production, particularly significant for some textile trades — a merchant "putting out" raw materials (wool or yarn) to cottage workers who processed it at home and returned the processed product, with the merchant coordinating the overall production chain. This bypassed some guild controls while maintaining the merchant's commercial control of the production process.  

**Journeymen's conflicts**: as touched on in the craft guilds entry, textile trades were among the first where journeymen began organizing against master weavers' control — the multi-generational economic squeeze that prevented journeymen from achieving master status creating real labor conflict in major Flemish cloth towns particularly
### Urban vs. Rural Weaving
**Urban guild weaving**: high-quality commercial production concentrated in towns with guild organization, quality standards, and market access — producing the tradeable cloth that drove the medieval economy's largest industry

**Rural cottage weaving**: as covered in the laborer and peasant entries, basic weaving for household use occurred throughout rural communities — simple fabrics produced for family use rather than market sale, using simpler equipment and less specialized skill than professional guild weaving
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A major cloth-producing city as a worldbuilding location where guild politics genuinely dominate civic life — the weavers' guild and cloth merchants effectively being the city's real political power, with labor conflict between journeymen weavers and master weavers providing ongoing social tension that shapes daily life and adventure opportunities
- The putting-out system as an economic model for a specific faction — a merchant organization that controls production not by owning workshops but by controlling raw material supply and finished product markets, making rural cottage workers economically dependent without formal employment or guild protection
- A specific cloth type as a luxury trade good relevant to a quest — perhaps a particular quality of fabric only producible in one location due to specific local wool, water, or knowledge, whose disruption (war, plague, guild collapse) has created economic crisis felt across a wide area
- A weaver's technical knowledge as an unexpected skill set — understanding of pattern and structure, experience with complex mechanical systems (the drawloom's sophisticated mechanics), and the patience and precision required for fine weaving translating into abilities (reading complex patterns, working with intricate mechanisms) that might prove useful in unexpected adventuring contexts
