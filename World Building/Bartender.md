### A Modern Word for an Old Job, With Some Caveats
"Bartender" as a specific term and role is more of a later development, but medieval Europe absolutely had people running establishments where you could get a drink — they just looked a bit different from a modern bar, and the line between "drinking establishment," "inn," and "private home selling surplus ale" was blurrier than today.
### Types of Drinking Establishments
**Alehouses**: the most common and humble option, especially in England — often literally just someone's home where the household brewed more ale than they needed and sold the surplus. Run predominantly by women, called **alewives** or **brewsters**, this was one of the relatively few trades where women had a clear, recognized, and fairly widespread economic role.

**Taverns**: somewhat more formal and often specifically associated with **wine** rather than ale/beer, generally a notch higher in social status and price than alehouses, more common in wine-producing regions (southern Europe especially) or catering to wealthier clientele.

**Inns**: combined lodging with food and drink, serving travelers, merchants, and pilgrims — Chaucer's _Canterbury Tales_ famously kicks off at the Tabard Inn, giving a vivid (if literary) sense of the social mixing that happened in these spaces.
### What the Job Actually Involved
**Brewing or sourcing the drink itself**: especially for alehouses, the alewife often brewed the ale herself — this was genuinely skilled work involving malting grain, mashing, boiling, fermenting (often with wild or reused yeast cultures rather than the cultivated strains we'd recognize today), and timing the whole process without modern measurement tools or refrigeration. Ale spoiled relatively fast, so brewing was a frequent, ongoing task rather than a one-time batch.

**Serving customers**: pouring and selling drinks, obviously, but also managing the social dynamics of a space that mixed locals, travelers, and sometimes troublemakers.

**Food service**: many establishments served simple food alongside drink — bread, cheese, pottage, and whatever was locally available, though the formality of this varied a lot by establishment type.

**Lodging** (for inns specifically): managing rooms, stabling for travelers' horses, and the general logistics of hosting overnight guests.

**Managing a social/information hub**: taverns and alehouses were key gathering points for gossip, news, business deals, and local social life — not unlike a modern bar's social function, but with even more weight given the lack of other public gathering spaces (especially for those outside church-related activities).

### Regulation — The "Ale-Conner" and Quality Control
Like bread, ale was considered too important to leave entirely unregulated:
- **Ale-conners** (or "ale-tasters") were officials, sometimes elected at the local level, responsible for testing ale quality and ensuring it was sold at fair, legally set prices and proper measures.
- Selling watered-down, low-quality, or overpriced ale could result in fines or public punishment, echoing the same kind of regulatory logic seen with bread (the Assize of Bread and Ale,  governed both together in England).
- An **ale stake** (a pole, often with greenery or a bush attached) outside a building signaled that ale was for sale there — an early form of trade signage for an often illiterate customer base.
### Women's Role: The Alewife
This deserves particular emphasis: brewing and selling ale was one of medieval Europe's most accessible trades for women, especially widows or wives supplementing household income. Some scholars connect the later, more sinister "witch" stereotype imagery (pointed hat, cauldron, broomstick, cat) partly to the visual trappings of alewives — the hats helped them stand out as sellers in a crowded market, the cauldron was literally for brewing, and so on — though this connection is debated and shouldn't be overstated as a single clean explanation.
### Reputation and Social Concerns
Taverns and alehouses carried a somewhat mixed reputation:
- Genuinely vital as social and economic hubs, sources of nutrition (ale was safer to drink than often-contaminated water and provided real caloric value) and rest for travelers
- Also viewed with moral suspicion by religious and civic authorities — associated with drunkenness, gambling, prostitution, and disorder, leading to periodic crackdowns, curfews, or moral condemnation from preachers
- Some towns restricted hours of operation or required licensing specifically because of these concerns
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- An alewife whose brews have minor magical properties (courage, truth-telling, prophetic dreams) — a great low-key magic item economy and a reason for adventurers to specifically seek her out
- A tavern as the campaign's central information hub/quest board, run by an NPC who knows everyone's business
- An ale-conner NPC who's corrupt, taking bribes to overlook bad brews — a small but flavorful local intrigue hook
- A tavern at a crossroads serving as neutral ground between factions, with the keeper bound by a personal or magical code of hospitality that even hostile parties must respect
