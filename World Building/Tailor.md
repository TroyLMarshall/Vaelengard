### A Trade Serving Everyone From Peasant to King
Clothing was a universal need, and the tailor — making and altering garments from cut and sewn cloth — occupied a recognized, respected position in the craft guild hierarchy across virtually every medieval town of any size. Unlike some specialized trades serving only wealthy clientele, tailors operated across the full social spectrum, though naturally with enormous variation in material quality, complexity, and price between the peasant's simple tunic and the nobleman's elaborately constructed ceremonial robe.
### Tailor vs. Other Cloth-Working Trades
Worth distinguishing from related trades we've touched on:
- **Weavers**: produced the cloth itself — converting thread into fabric
- **Dyers**: colored the cloth, as covered in that entry
- **Fullers**: cleaned and thickened woven wool cloth
- **Tailors**: cut and assembled the finished cloth into actual garments — the final stage of the production chain connecting raw fiber to wearable clothing
- **Drapers**: merchants selling cloth — distinct from tailors who worked it, though sometimes the same person sold and worked cloth in smaller operations
### What Tailors Actually Made
**Everyday clothing for ordinary people**:
- **Tunics**: the fundamental garment across most of the medieval period for both men and women — a simple T-shaped garment requiring minimal pattern cutting, achievable even by relatively basic sewing skill
- **Braies and hose**: lower-body garments for men
- **Simple cloaks and hoods**: outer garments for warmth and weather protection

**More complex garments for wealthier clients**:
- **Fitted garments**: as the medieval period progressed, particularly from the 14th century onward, fashion shifted toward increasingly fitted, shaped garments — requiring genuine pattern-cutting skill to achieve the desired three-dimensional shape from flat cloth
- **Doublets and pourpoints**: fitted upper-body garments with considerable internal structure
- **Complex layered dress**: wealthy women's elaborate dress constructions with fitted bodices, trained skirts, and decorative elements required real tailoring skill

**Specialized garments**:
- **Ecclesiastical vestments**: Church vestments (chasubles, dalmatics, copes) were sometimes extraordinarily elaborate — heavily embroidered, made from the finest available silk and cloth, representing some of the most prestigious tailoring commissions
- **Heraldic garments and livery**: as covered in the hired servers and noble entries, household livery and heraldic display garments required both technical skill and precise execution of specific heraldic designs
- **Military clothing**: padded garments worn under armor (the gambeson/aketon mentioned in the armorer entry) required specific construction for their protective function
### The Craft Itself
**Pattern cutting**: the tailor's fundamental technical skill — understanding how to cut flat cloth into the shapes that would, when sewn together, produce a three-dimensional garment fitting a human body correctly. This required:
- Genuine geometric understanding of how flat shapes transform into three-dimensional forms
- Accumulated pattern knowledge for standard garment types, sometimes preserved as physical paper or parchment templates
- Measurement and fitting skills for bespoke garments tailored to specific individuals

**Sewing**: assembly of cut pieces using needle and thread
- **Hand sewing throughout this period**: machine sewing didn't exist — every seam, every stitch was placed by hand, making complex garments genuinely labor-intensive to produce
- **Thread types**: linen thread for most sewing, silk thread for fine or luxury work, connecting to the string maker entry's discussion of thread production
- **Stitch types**: different stitches for different purposes — running stitches, backstitch for strength, whip stitches for finishing edges — each requiring practiced skill for consistent, quality results

**Fitting and alteration**: adjusting garments to fit specific bodies — a constant, ongoing aspect of the trade since bodies varied and garments needed to fit well to function properly and look appropriate

**Decorative techniques**:
- **Embroidery**: decorative stitching directly onto garments — sometimes the tailor's own work, sometimes from a dedicated embroiderer (a related but sometimes distinct specialization)
- **Dagging**: decorative cutting of fabric edges into ornamental shapes — a specific later medieval fashion detail
- **Appliqué**: attaching decorative fabric pieces onto a base garment
- **Lining**: fine garments often had separate linings — sometimes of contrasting fabric, sometimes of fur (connecting to the furrier entry) for warmth and display
### Guild Structure
- Tailors organized into guilds in towns with sufficient demand, following standard apprentice-journeyman-master progression
- **The Merchant Taylors**: London's tailors' guild became one of the most significant and prestigious craft guilds in the city — the **Worshipful Company of Merchant Taylors** eventually becoming one of the Great Twelve Livery Companies of London, reflecting how economically significant the cloth-working trade was
- **Tension with cloth merchants/drapers**: similar jurisdictional disputes to those seen between other paired trades throughout this list — questions about who could sell cloth versus who could work it, with tailors and drapers sometimes in conflict over the commercial boundaries between their trades
### Fashion and Social Signaling
Connecting to themes throughout this list — clothing was one of the most immediate, constantly visible status signals in medieval society:
- **Sumptuary law compliance**: as covered with shoes, fur, and color in earlier entries, tailors needed to understand and work within sumptuary restrictions — a tailor making a garment that violated sumptuary law for a client created genuine legal risk
- **Fashion cycles**: particularly in the later medieval period, fashion changed with increasing speed — the shift toward fitted garments, the varying hem lengths, sleeve styles, and decorative details that marked the fashionable versus the old-fashioned were real commercial drivers for tailoring work
- **Visual hierarchy reinforcement**: by making the physical garments that encoded social hierarchy, tailors were literally constructing the visible fabric of medieval social order — a philosophically interesting position for what might seem like a purely practical trade
### Status and Economics
- **Respected, often prosperous trade**: tailors serving wealthy clientele could achieve real prosperity and guild standing
- **Variable demand**: unlike some universal-need trades (bakers, blacksmiths), tailoring demand correlated with disposable income — wealthy clients commissioning multiple elaborate garments, ordinary people buying minimal basic clothing or making their own simple garments, creating genuine economic stratification within the tailoring market
- **Women's involvement**: as with several other trades throughout this list, women participated in sewing and garment production — though formal guild tailoring was predominantly male, women's domestic sewing and seamstress work outside guild structure was substantial and economically significant
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A master tailor as an unexpected intelligence source — someone who measures and fits the wealthy and powerful, whose workshop is a venue for social display and conversation, and who accumulates knowledge of clients' bodies, habits, and social relationships over years of intimate professional contact
- Sumptuary law enforcement as a tailor's professional hazard — a tailor pressured by a powerful but legally ineligible client to make a garment that violates sumptuary restrictions, creating a genuine ethical and legal dilemma
- A specific garment as a plot-relevant object — heraldic livery identifying someone as a specific noble's servant, an ecclesiastical vestment stolen from a church, or an elaborate ceremonial robe whose construction details reveal something about its owner's identity or intentions
- Fashion as a social battlefield in a noble court — tailoring work as part of the political competition covered in the politicians and noble entries, where appearing in the right style at the right moment carries real social stakes
