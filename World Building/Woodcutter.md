### A Foundational Trade Underlying Nearly Everything We've Covered
We've referenced timber and wood supply constantly throughout this list — carpenters needing structural timber, coopers needing stave wood, charcoal burners needing fuel wood, bowyers needing yew and ash, basket weavers and broom makers needing flexible branches — so let's give the actual harvesting of wood its own dedicated treatment, since it's genuinely foundational infrastructure that nearly every other craft in this list ultimately depended on.
### Why Wood Mattered So Much
Connecting to themes throughout this entire list: wood was medieval Europe's dominant material for construction (timber-frame housing, as covered in the carpenter and private homes entries), fuel (alongside charcoal, covered in that entry), tool handles, containers (the cooper's barrels), weapons (bow staves, arrow shafts), and countless other applications. Without consistent wood supply, virtually every other trade covered in this list would have struggled to function.
### Woodland Management — Not Simply "Cutting Down Trees"
This is genuinely important to establish: medieval forestry, where well-managed, involved real sophisticated, sustainable practice rather than simple unrestricted clearing:

**Coppicing**: as mentioned repeatedly throughout this list (basket weavers, broom makers, charcoal burners), this technique involved cutting trees back to the base/stump periodically, encouraging vigorous new growth of multiple straight shoots — a genuinely sustainable system producing a renewable supply of useful wood from the same root system indefinitely, rather than requiring constant new planting
- **Coppice cycles**: different wood applications required different growth periods — basket-weaving withies needed relatively young, flexible growth (shorter cycles), while larger structural or fuel wood required longer growth periods before harvesting
- **Coppice rotation management**: well-managed woodland divided into sections harvested on a rotating schedule, ensuring a continuous, sustainable supply rather than depleting the entire woodland at once

**Pollarding**: a related technique, cutting trees at a higher point (above where grazing animals could reach new growth) rather than at the base — allowing coppice-style renewable harvesting in areas where livestock grazed among the trees

**Standing timber/high forest**: for larger structural timber (major building beams, ship timber) requiring substantial trunk diameter, trees needed to grow to full maturity over many decades rather than the shorter coppice cycle — representing a genuinely different, longer-term management approach and investment horizon
### Who Did the Actual Cutting
- **Woodcutters/loggers**: those specifically engaged in felling and processing timber, ranging from skilled specialists handling valuable structural timber to more general laborers (connecting to the laborer entry) doing rougher harvesting work
- **Connection to charcoal burners**: as covered in that entry, charcoal makers often handled their own wood harvesting as part of their broader trade, working directly within coppiced woodland specifically managed for fuel production
- **Estate/manorial woodcutters**: given that significant woodland was often held by lords (connecting to the forest law and hunting rights discussed in the hunters entry) or monasteries, woodcutting frequently happened as employed labor on someone else's managed land rather than independent harvesting of freely available material
- **Sawyers**: a related but distinct specialization, focused specifically on sawing felled timber into usable planks and beams — working in pairs with a long pit-saw in many traditions (one person above, one in a pit below, working the saw together through a log positioned over the pit), genuinely demanding, physically synchronized labor
### Tools
- **Axes**: the primary felling tool, requiring real skill to use efficiently and safely
- **Saws**: for both felling (in some traditions/periods) and especially for processing felled timber into usable planks and beams
- **Wedges and mauls**: for splitting wood along the grain
- **Carts and pack animals**: for transporting felled timber from the woodland to wherever it would be processed or used — genuinely significant logistics given how heavy and bulky timber is, connecting to the freight company entry's broader discussion of medieval transport challenges
### Forest Rights and Restrictions
Connecting directly to the hunters and farm entries' discussion of forest law and common land rights:
- **Wood-gathering rights**: ordinary peasants often held specific, customary rights to gather certain wood from common or manorial woodland for their own household use — though these rights were frequently restricted by type of wood, method of gathering, or specific allowances (some customs distinguished between gathering fallen deadwood, which might be more freely permitted, versus actively cutting standing timber, which was more restricted)
- **Lordly control over valuable timber**: larger, more valuable standing timber was typically more tightly controlled by whoever held the land, given its significant value for construction or sale
- **Forest law overlap**: as covered in the hunters entry, royal forests' legal restrictions covered not just hunting but often wood-gathering and land use more broadly, creating the same kind of class tension and occasional resentment discussed in that entry
### Regional Significance and Trade
- **Shipbuilding timber**: particularly valuable, requiring specific qualities (size, strength, appropriate curves for hull construction) connecting to the carpenter entry's discussion of shipwrights — certain forests with good shipbuilding timber held genuine strategic and economic significance
- **The yew supply chain**: as covered in the bowyer entry, England's reliance on imported yew for longbow staves represents a specific example of how timber supply could become a genuine strategic/trade concern beyond purely local use
- **Baltic timber trade**: connecting to the Hanseatic League discussions throughout this list, Baltic regions' extensive forests fed significant timber export trade, supplying regions with less abundant local woodland
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- Coppice woodland management as a worldbuilding detail explaining why your setting's forests look a certain way and support certain trades — useful for grounding basket weavers, broom makers, and charcoal burners (all covered earlier) in a coherent, sustainably-managed forest economy rather than treating wood as simply infinitely available
- A dispute over wood-gathering rights (similar to fishing/hunting right disputes covered elsewhere) as a small, grounded local conflict — a lord restricting customary gathering rights, or a community pushing back against new restrictions
- A magically significant or monster-guarded stand of ancient, uncoppiced "high forest" as a contrast to the managed woodland most loggers work in — explaining why this particular grove is different, dangerous, or valuable compared to ordinary timber sources
- A shipbuilding timber shortage as a strategic plot element — echoing the yew-supply hook suggested for bowyers, a kingdom's naval ambitions constrained by genuine timber availability, creating believable economic-military tension
