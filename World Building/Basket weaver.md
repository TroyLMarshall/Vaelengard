### A Humble but Universally Needed Trade
Basket weaving (often called **basketry** or, for the practitioners, sometimes "basket-makers" or regionally specific terms) was one of those background trades that didn't carry much prestige but was absolutely essential to daily medieval life. Nearly every household, farm, and trade depended on baskets and woven goods for storage, transport, and work — this was the era's equivalent of cardboard boxes, plastic bins, and shopping bags all rolled into one craft.
### Materials
- **Willow/withies**: the single most common material across much of Europe — willow grows fast, is flexible when fresh-cut, and was often deliberately cultivated in **withy beds** or pollarded willow groves specifically for basket-making material
- **Reeds and rushes**: used especially in wetland regions, also doubling for thatching and floor coverings
- **Straw**: for certain coarser baskets and particularly for beehives (skeps) and some food storage
- **Split wood/splints**: thin strips of oak, ash, or hazel, split and woven for sturdier baskets
- **Bark fiber**: in some regions, particularly from lime/linden trees
### What Basket Weavers Made
The range was genuinely broad, since baskets served so many functions:
- **Agricultural baskets**: for harvesting grain, fruit, and vegetables; winnowing baskets for separating grain from chaff
- **Fish traps and creels**: woven traps for catching fish and eels, plus baskets for carrying the catch
- **Storage baskets**: for grain, root vegetables, wool, and household goods
- **Transport baskets**: panniers for pack animals, baskets fitted to carts
- **Beehives (skeps)**: the classic dome-shaped woven straw beehive
- **Fencing and wattle**: woven panels for fencing, and critically, **wattle-and-daub** construction — woven wood lattice (wattle) covered with a mud/clay/straw mixture (daub) was one of the most common medieval building techniques for walls, meaning basket-weaving skills overlapped directly with house construction
- **Furniture elements**: woven seating, cradle baskets, and simple chairs
- **Eel traps and weirs**: specialized fishing equipment
- **Sieves and riddles**: for sifting flour, grain, or other materials
### Where Basket Weavers Fit Socially and Economically
Unlike trades requiring expensive tools, long apprenticeships, or guild gatekeeping, basket weaving was:
- **Widely practiced as a rural/cottage craft**: many people, especially in farming communities, knew basic basketry as a useful household skill rather than relying solely on dedicated specialists
- **A trade for the poor or those with limited other options**: it required minimal capital investment (no expensive tools or workshop needed) compared to metalworking or other guild crafts, making it accessible to those without resources to enter more prestigious trades
- **Sometimes practiced by itinerant workers**: traveling basket-makers might move between villages, harvesting local willow and selling their wares as they went
- **Occasionally guild-organized in larger towns**: cities with enough demand and specialized production sometimes had organized basket-makers' guilds, but this was less universal than for trades like baking or smithing
### The Craft Itself
- **Harvesting timing**: willow withies were typically cut in winter when the sap was down, then often stored and soaked again before working to restore flexibility
- **Weaving techniques**: various methods including simple over-under weaving, more complex twining, and randing (weaving single rods over and under stakes) — techniques that have survived largely unchanged into modern traditional basketry
- **Stakes and weavers**: thicker, sturdier pieces formed the structural "stakes" or ribs, while thinner, flexible pieces ("weavers") were woven around them
- **No metal tools required for basic work**: unlike many trades, you could produce functional baskets with little more than a sharp knife and your hands, though specialized tools like bodkins (for piercing) and shaves helped with finer work
### A Trade Tied to the Land
Basket weaving connects closely to **coppicing** — the practice of cutting trees (especially willow and hazel) back to a stump periodically, encouraging vigorous new growth of straight, flexible shoots ideal for weaving. This made basket-making materials a renewable, sustainably managed resource, often tied to common land rights or specific plots dedicated to growing weaving material.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks, given how unglamorous this trade is in real history:
- A humble basket-weaver who turns out to know forest/fey magic, since the craft's deep connection to specific trees and cutting cycles lends itself naturally to druidic or fey associations
- Woven items as the unexpected vector for smuggling or hidden messages (something concealed within a basket's construction itself)
- A basket-weaver's seemingly mundane skill at "binding" and "weaving" reframed as literal magic — basket charms, ward-knots, or woven traps that hold more than fish
- Wattle-and-daub construction tied to a settlement built in a fey-touched forest, where the building material itself carries faint enchantment from the source trees
