### The Craft and Its Structure
A **pardoner** was a licensed church agent authorized to distribute **indulgences** — official Church grants reducing the temporal punishment (time in purgatory) owed for sins already confessed and forgiven — typically in exchange for a donation toward some sanctioned cause: funding a crusade, building or repairing a cathedral, supporting a hospital, or other approved church works. In theory, this was a legitimate and important piece of medieval religious/financial infrastructure: the Church needed to raise money for genuinely large capital projects and charitable works, and indulgences were a real, doctrinally grounded mechanism for doing so, formally authorized through papal or episcopal license. In practice, the trade became one of the most notorious sources of corruption and popular resentment in the medieval Church, and that gap between official theory and lived reality is exactly what makes pardoners such rich material for your setting.

**How the system worked** — A pardoner carried official documentation (a papal bull or episcopal letter) authorizing them to preach and collect on behalf of a specific cause, traveled from town to town, preached to gathered crowds (often skilled, theatrical public speakers by necessity of the job), displayed **relics** — genuine or claimed fragments of saints, holy sites, or sacred objects — as part of the pitch, and sold indulgences (and often access to view/touch the relics) for money that was supposed to flow back to the authorizing cause, with the pardoner typically retaining a commission or percentage as their livelihood.

**The corruption problem** — This system created obvious and well-documented incentive problems: pardoners were paid based on how much they collected, giving every incentive to oversell, exaggerate, use manipulative preaching or emotional pressure, traffic in **fake or dubious relics** (bones of dubious provenance, "splinters of the true cross" sold many times over across many towns), and in the worst cases, pocket money meant for the Church entirely, or sell indulgences without genuine authorization at all. **Chaucer's Pardoner** in _The Canterbury Tales_ is the single most famous literary depiction of this — a character who openly admits, in his own prologue, that his entire trade runs on greed and manipulation, preaching against avarice purely because it's an effective, profitable sermon topic, all while carrying a bag of fake relics (a pillowcase claimed as Our Lady's veil, pig bones sold as saints' relics). This is satire, but satire that lands because it reflected genuine, widely recognized abuse — pardoners had a real and well-earned reputation problem by the later medieval period.

**Church response and reform pressure** — The abuses were significant and visible enough that they became a genuine flashpoint of religious reform pressure — the sale of indulgences, and pardoners' role in it, was a direct and major target of criticism that fed into the Protestant Reformation (Martin Luther's 95 Theses, just past the usual medieval cutoff, was substantially a protest against indulgence-selling practices) — meaning pardoners occupy a real historical position as a symbol of institutional corruption serious enough to help fracture Western Christianity. For a "medieval" fantasy setting, you don't need to go that far, but it tells you the abuse was significant enough to be historically consequential, not just minor local grumbling.

**Not all pardoners were frauds** — It's worth balancing the Chaucer-style caricature with the reality that the _underlying office_ was legitimate and many pardoners likely did honest work funding real church projects — the trade's terrible reputation stemmed from the ease of abuse and the visibility of the worst offenders, not universal corruption. A licensed, honest pardoner doing real fundraising for a hospital or crusade is just as period-accurate as a swindler, and the tension between the two types (and the difficulty of telling them apart) is the actual interesting material.

**Social position** — Pardoners were often outsiders to the communities they visited — itinerant by trade necessity — carrying official Church authority that gave them real, if borrowed, social power, but lacking the settled community trust that a parish priest or local religious figure would have. This combination of institutional authority plus itinerant, transactional relationship with any given town made them simultaneously respected (representing the Church) and viewed with real wariness (a stranger asking for money on the Church's behalf, impossible for an ordinary person to verify).
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Deception, Persuasion (or Religion, if playing an honest pardoner)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Forgery Kit, or Disguise Kit
- **Feat:** Actor or Skilled
- **Equipment:** A letter of authorization (genuine or forged), a case of relics (genuine, dubious, or outright fake — player's choice), traveling clergy-adjacent robes, 15 gp

This background is close to tailor-made for a **Bard or Rogue** — the itinerant, theatrical, persuasion-driven trade maps directly onto both classes' core fantasy, and the built-in ambiguity (is your pardoner honest or a fraud?) gives a player a genuinely interesting alignment/backstory choice baked into character creation. It also works well for a **Cleric** wrestling with institutional corruption they've witnessed or participated in — a former pardoner who saw too much and left, now trying to do honest religious work instead.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Chaucer-Style Fraud** — Openly (if privately) cynical, selling fake relics and manipulative sermons with real theatrical skill, genuinely charming even as the party (or the audience) slowly realizes what they're watching — a fun, morally clear antagonist or trickster ally.
- **The Honest Believer** — A pardoner who genuinely believes in the cause they're fundraising for and does the work sincerely, growing increasingly disturbed by how many of their peers are frauds — a good source of insider information about corruption elsewhere.
- **The Reformed Pardoner** — Left the trade after a crisis of conscience (or after being caught), now trying to atone or make amends — could be a genuinely redemptive NPC or a target for someone from their old life.
- **The Relic Authenticator** — Someone (Church official, rival pardoner, or independent expert) whose job is verifying whether a given relic is genuine — a natural investigative partner or obstacle for any relic-focused plot.
- **The Local Priest Who's Had Enough** — A parish priest fed up with pardoners repeatedly fleecing his congregation, looking for allies (possibly the party) to expose or drive off the next one who shows up.
### Game Hooks
- **The Fake Relic Exposed** — The party discovers (or is hired to prove) that a traveling pardoner's prized relic is fraudulent — a public reckoning that could ruin the pardoner, expose a larger fraud network, or spark a genuine crisis of faith in the town.
- **The Stolen Authorization** — Someone's operating as a pardoner using a forged or stolen letter of church authorization — real fraud with real legal/religious consequences, and the party has to determine legitimacy before deciding how to act.
- **Funding Gone Missing** — Money collected by a pardoner for a specific cause (a hospital, a crusade, a cathedral repair) never arrives at its destination — embezzlement, robbery, or something else? An investigation with genuine moral stakes since real people depended on that money.
- **The Genuine Article** — Subvert expectations: a relic everyone assumes is a pardoner's usual fraud turns out to be authentically, unnervingly magical or holy — a hook that plays against the trope for a memorable reveal.
- **Town on the Edge** — A charismatic, manipulative pardoner is whipping a town into religious fervor (and extracting significant money) right as some other crisis hits — the party has to navigate real religious sentiment, genuine community need, and probable fraud all at once, without an easy clean answer.
