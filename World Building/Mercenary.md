### Connecting to Several Threads Already Covered
We've referenced mercenaries a few times throughout this list — touched on with guards (professional, paid soldiers versus rotated civic obligation), the freight company and caravan guild entries' mention of hired escorts, and the fighter/D&D classes discussion — so let's give this trade its full dedicated treatment, since it's genuinely significant to how medieval warfare actually functioned, especially as the period progressed.
### Why Mercenaries Existed Alongside Feudal Military Obligation
The classic image of medieval warfare often centers on **feudal levy** — knights and men owing military service to their lord in exchange for land, as covered in the gentlemen/ladies and government official entries. But this system had real limitations that created genuine demand for paid professional soldiers instead:
- **Limited service terms**: feudal obligation often came with specific limits on how long a vassal owed military service (commonly 40 days a year in some traditions) — fine for short campaigns, genuinely inadequate for extended warfare or siege operations that might drag on for months or years
- **Variable quality and reliability**: feudal levies brought whoever a lord could muster, not necessarily skilled, experienced, or particularly motivated fighters
- **Specialized skills**: certain military capabilities (skilled crossbowmen, particular cavalry traditions, siege engineering) weren't evenly distributed across feudal society, creating demand for hiring specifically skilled outside specialists
- **Flexibility**: a ruler could hire mercenaries precisely when and where needed, without the complex obligations, loyalties, and limitations tangled up in feudal military service
### Types of Mercenary Forces
**Individual mercenary knights/soldiers**: knights or experienced soldiers without stable feudal land-holding (sometimes younger sons without inheritance prospects, sometimes simply those who found warfare more lucrative or appealing than settled estate management) hiring out their martial skill for pay

**Mercenary companies**: organized groups, sometimes substantial, operating under a recognized captain — genuinely significant military forces in their own right, not just loose collections of individuals
- **Free companies**: a term particularly associated with later medieval mercenary bands (especially prominent during lulls in the Hundred Years' War) — soldiers between formal employment who continued operating as organized military forces, sometimes turning to outright banditry, extortion, or "protection" rackets against the very populations they'd previously been hired to fight for or against
- **Specific famous companies**: various named mercenary companies achieved real historical reputation and notoriety across different regions and periods, with leaders sometimes becoming genuinely significant political/military figures in their own right through successful mercenary careers

**Specialized mercenary traditions**: certain regions developed particular reputations for specific military specialties, becoming go-to sources for rulers seeking that capability
- **Crossbowmen**: certain regions (Genoa particularly) developed strong reputations for skilled mercenary crossbow units, hired across various conflicts
- **Swiss pikemen**: though more prominently a later medieval/early modern phenomenon, Swiss mercenary infantry developed a formidable reputation for disciplined pike formation tactics, becoming sought-after mercenary exports
### Condottieri — Italy's Distinctive Mercenary Tradition
Worth specifically highlighting since Italian city-states developed an unusually thoroughgoing reliance on mercenary forces:
- **Condottieri**: literally meaning "contractors," these were mercenary captains who contracted their military forces to Italian city-states (Florence, Venice, Milan, and others), who frequently preferred hiring outside military leadership over arming and relying on their own citizens
- **Genuine political significance**: successful condottieri could achieve real political power and even rulership — several Italian dynasties trace their origin to successful mercenary captains who parlayed military success into actual territorial control, representing a genuinely significant path to power outside traditional hereditary nobility
- **Contracts (condotta)**: formal agreements specifying terms of service, payment, and obligations — reflecting real, sophisticated commercial/legal arrangement rather than informal hired-muscle relationships
### Payment and Economics
- **Wages**: mercenaries were paid directly for service, in contrast to feudal obligation's land-based exchange — this required genuine cash resources from whoever was hiring, connecting to the banking and taxation systems covered throughout this list
- **Plunder and ransom**: beyond formal wages, mercenaries (like soldiers generally in this era) often profited from looting captured territory and **ransoming** captured enemy nobility — ransom specifically could be extraordinarily lucrative, since capturing a wealthy noble or knight alive for ransom was far more profitable than killing them, creating genuine economic incentive shaping battlefield behavior
- **Risk of non-payment**: rulers and city-states sometimes struggled or simply refused to pay mercenaries what was owed, creating real risk of mercenary forces turning to plunder, extortion, or outright betrayal when payment didn't materialize as promised — the "free companies" turning to banditry during unemployed periods reflects this same underlying economic precariousness
### Social Status and Reputation
- **Genuinely mixed reputation**: mercenaries occupied an ambiguous social position — valued for real military utility, but often viewed with suspicion given their primary loyalty to payment rather than feudal/national loyalty, and the genuine, well-documented tendency for unemployed mercenary forces to become a destructive, predatory presence on the very populations they'd previously served
- **Path for social mobility**: similar to several trades covered throughout this list (lawyers, successful merchants), military mercenary service offered a genuine, if dangerous, path to wealth and status for those without inherited noble position — successful mercenary careers could fund land purchase, noble marriage, or even (as with the condottieri) actual political power
- **Church ambivalence**: similar to several other morally complex trades covered in this list, the Church held genuinely mixed views — warfare conducted for pure pay rather than legitimate cause sat uneasily with just war theory, even as the Church itself sometimes employed or relied on mercenary forces for its own military needs (including Crusading contexts)
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A mercenary company between contracts, genuinely dangerous and morally ambiguous, as a faction PCs might need to negotiate with, hire, or defend against — drawing on the real historical "free companies" turn toward banditry during unemployed periods for a grounded, non-cartoonish threat
- A condottieri-style mercenary captain who's successfully parlayed military success into genuine political power — an NPC ruler or rival whose legitimacy rests on conquest and continued military reputation rather than inherited right, creating interesting political texture distinct from traditional hereditary nobility
- Ransom economics as a worldbuilding/gameplay mechanic — captured nobles or PCs themselves becoming valuable ransom targets rather than simply killed, changing the stakes and incentives around certain combat encounters
- A mercenary PC background drawing on the genuine social-mobility-through-danger narrative — someone without noble birth or guild connection who's built their position purely through martial skill and the willingness to fight for pay, fitting naturally with the Fighter class discussion from the D&D classes entry
