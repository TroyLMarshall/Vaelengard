### Role and Status
Apothecaries occupied an interesting middle position in medieval society — more skilled and respected than a street herb-seller, but below the university-trained physician in the medical hierarchy. They were craftsmen and merchants as much as healers, typically organized into **guilds** (in many cities, alongside or combined with grocers and spice merchants, since the line between "spice" and "medicine" was blurry).

In England, apothecaries were long grouped with the **Worshipful Company of Grocers** before eventually forming their own guild in 1617 (just past the medieval period, but the roots of separation go back further). In places like Florence and Venice, apothecary guilds (the _speziali_) were well-established and influential by the 13th-14th centuries.
### Daily Work
**Running the shop**: An apothecary's shop was part pharmacy, part general store. Shelves held jars of dried herbs, roots, minerals, and increasingly exotic imported ingredients — pepper, cinnamon, sugar, and other goods that traveled the same trade routes as medicines.

**Compounding remedies**: This was the technical heart of the job
- Grinding ingredients with mortar and pestle
- Making **electuaries** (medicine mixed with honey or syrup into a paste)
- Preparing **tinctures** and infusions
- Rolling **pills** and **boluses**
- Creating **plasters** and **ointments** for external use
- Distilling **waters** and **oils** from plants

**Following physicians' prescriptions**: Trained physicians (who diagnosed based on humoral theory — balancing blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile) would write prescriptions, and the apothecary's job was to actually prepare and sell the remedy. This division of labor was a key feature distinguishing the apothecary from a folk healer who did both.

**Selling directly to the public**: Many people, especially those who couldn't afford a physician's consultation fee, went straight to the apothecary for advice and remedies — making apothecaries a sort of medieval first-line healthcare provider, despite this technically being outside their sanctioned role.
### Stock and Sourcing
- **Local herbs**: sage, rue, wormwood, mint, chamomile — often grown in monastery or personal physic gardens
- **Imported materials**: via long trade networks — myrrh, frankincense, camphor, exotic spices, and eventually substances like rhubarb root from Asia
- **Mineral substances**: sulfur, mercury, various salts
- **Animal-derived ingredients**: things like "theriac" (a complex, almost mythical compound originally meant as a universal antidote, sometimes containing dozens of ingredients including viper flesh)
- **Sugar**: apothecaries were major sugar importers since sugar was used both as medicine and as a way to make other medicines palatable — this is part of why apothecaries often evolved into confectioners too
### Training
Apothecaries typically trained through **apprenticeship** — several years (often 7, mirroring other craft guilds) working under a master apothecary, learning to identify ingredients, compound remedies, and run the business side of the trade. This was a far cry from the Latin-and-classical-texts university education of a physician, which created ongoing tension and turf disputes between the two professions throughout the period.
### Tools of the Trade
- Mortar and pestle (the enduring symbol of the profession)
- Scales for precise measurement
- Alembics for distillation
- Jars and **albarellos** (the distinctive cylindrical ceramic jars used for storage, often beautifully decorated)
- Reference texts like herbals and pharmacopoeias listing known remedies and their preparation
### The Blurry Edges
The apothecary's world overlapped messily with:
- **Physicians** (who diagnosed, theoretically leaving compounding to apothecaries)
- **Barber-surgeons** (handling bloodletting, minor surgery, tooth-pulling)
- **Wise women and folk healers** (using traditional remedies outside formal guild structure)
- **Alchemists** (sharing techniques like distillation and some belief in transformative properties of substances)

This overlap meant a lot of professional jealousy and occasional legal battles over who was allowed to diagnose, prescribe, or sell what.
### Worldbuilding Angles
#### Historical Grounding (real medieval practices to reskin)
- **Guild membership** – Apothecaries were often part of the same guild as grocers/spicers (real historical overlap), since both dealt in imported goods measured by weight. Great basis for guild politics and turf wars.
- **The humoral system** – Medieval medicine ran on balancing blood, phlegm, black bile, yellow bile. An apothecary's diagnosis and prescriptions could follow this logic, giving you a distinct in-world "medical theory" that's wrong by our standards but internally consistent and flavorful.
- **Physician vs. apothecary divide** – Physicians diagnosed (expensive, university-trained, rare), apothecaries compounded and sold remedies (more accessible, trade-trained). Common people often skipped the physician entirely and went straight to the apothecary—a good class-based tension.
- **The shop itself** – Jars of dried herbs, powdered minerals, animal parts, imported spices (worth their weight in silver), a mortar and pestle, hanging bundles of herbs, maybe a stuffed crocodile from the "wonder cabinet" tradition to impress customers.
- **Traveling drug peddlers/mountebanks** – Less reputable sellers hawking miracle cures in town squares, sometimes running scams—good rogue NPC fodder or unreliable info source.
#### Fantasy Twists to Layer On
- **Alchemical crossover** – The line between apothecary and alchemist is blurry; a shop might sell both mundane tinctures and low-grade magical elixirs, with the good stuff kept behind the counter.
- **Monster-part pharmacology** – Real medieval medicine used bizarre ingredients (mummy powder, unicorn horn, powdered gems); in your setting these could be _real_ efficacious ingredients—ground manticore spine for pain relief, troll blood for regeneration salves, banshee hair for sleep draughts.
- **Licensed vs. hedge apothecaries** – Guild-sanctioned shops sell "safe" remedies; hedge apothecaries (often folk healers, sometimes half-fey or witch-taught) sell riskier, more powerful, or illegal concoctions.
- **Poison double life** – Anyone who can heal can also harm. Apothecaries make natural suspects in murder mysteries, and a shady one might run a poison side-business under the counter.
- **Plague doctors as evolution** – If your setting has magical plagues or curses, plague-doctor apothecaries wearing beaked masks stuffed with wards and herbs could be a specialized, feared/respected subclass.
- **Fey or planar ingredient trade** – Rare ingredients sourced from the Feywild, Shadowfell, or elemental planes create smuggling routes and exotic supply chains—an apothecary might have a shady contact who "doesn't ask where it's from."
#### Social & Political Hooks
- **Church tension** – Clergy may view apothecary magic-adjacent remedies with suspicion (is this medicine or witchcraft?), especially for female apothecaries, who historically dominated herbal medicine and were disproportionately accused of witchcraft.
- **Guild monopolies and price gouging** – During a plague or crisis, guild-controlled apothecaries could hoard cures or jack up prices, creating hooks for player intervention or black-market alternatives.
- **Noble patronage** – A skilled apothecary serving a noble house has protection and status but is also the first suspect if the lord falls ill or dies.
- **Reputation-based economy** – An apothecary's remedies are only as trusted as their name; a botched cure (or slander from a rival) can ruin a shop overnight.
#### Sensory/Flavor Details
- Shops smell of dried herbs, tallow candles, and something faintly metallic (crushed minerals/gemdust).
- Ledgers written in a mix of Latin-esque trade terms and symbolic shorthand for ingredients.
- Apprentices doing the grinding and drying while the master apothecary handles diagnosis and rare compounding.
- A back room or cellar with the "real" inventory—things not meant for browsing customers.
