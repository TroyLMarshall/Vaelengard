### The Craft and Its Structure
A **currier** occupied the crucial middle step in leather production, between the **tanner** (who converted raw hide into rough tanned leather through a long, foul-smelling process) and the various finished-leather trades — saddlers, shoemakers, glovers, cordwainers, bookbinders — who needed leather in a usable, consistent state to actually work with. Currying was the **dressing, finishing, and conditioning** of tanned leather: taking the stiff, uneven output of the tanning pits and working it into something supple, waterproof, uniformly thick, and properly colored/finished for its intended use. This is a trade most people today have never heard of precisely because it sat invisibly in the supply chain — essential, technical, and rarely glamorous enough to get remembered the way swordsmiths or embroiderers are.

**The actual work** involved a sequence of physically demanding steps: **shaving** the leather to an even thickness with a specialized curved knife, **stretching and working** it (traditionally by hand, working oils and greases — fish oil, tallow, or other fats — into the fibers to make it supple and water-resistant, called "stuffing" the leather), **paring** away irregularities, and often **dyeing or blacking** the surface, especially for shoe leather (black leather dressing has a real, distinct medieval technique lineage). The currier's tools were fairly specialized — the **currier's knife** (a wide, two-handled blade for shaving/paring) is a distinctive enough implement to be a good flavor prop.

**Guild position** — Curriers organized into their own guilds in major cities (London's Worshipful Company of Curriers is real, chartered in the 1400s though the trade obviously predates formal incorporation), often maintaining **tense boundary disputes with tanners on one side and the leather-goods trades on the other** — arguments over exactly where tanning ends and currying begins, and whether shoemakers or saddlers were allowed to do their own currying rather than buying finished leather, were a recurring source of guild friction, not unlike the tailor/seamstress or saddler/loriner tensions covered earlier. This is a genuinely useful worldbuilding pattern across many of these trades: **medieval craft guilds spent enormous energy litigating exactly where one trade's monopoly ended and the next one's began**, and curriers sat in the middle of one of the more contentious of these chains.

**Working conditions and status** — Curriers avoided the very worst of the tanning process (the raw hide soaking, hair removal, and dung/lime treatments that made tanneries notoriously foul-smelling and were usually pushed to a town's outskirts or downwind side), but the trade still involved hard physical labor with strong-smelling oils and dyes, keeping it firmly in the "respectable but unglamorous" tier — skilled, guild-protected, steadily employed, but not prestigious. A currier's work was judged entirely on **consistency and reliability** rather than individual artistry — the goal was leather that behaved predictably for the next craftsman down the line, which made reputation and trust between curriers and their buyers (saddlers, cordwainers, etc.) a real economic asset.

**Economic position** — Because virtually every leather good in medieval society — shoes, saddles, gloves, belts, bookbindings, bottles/jugs (leather "black jacks" and bombards were real drinking vessels), armor components — depended on properly currried leather, a skilled, reliable currier had steady, diversified demand across many other trades, even without individual fame.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Investigation, Persuasion (or Insight, for judging quality and reading buyers)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Leatherworker's Tools
- **Feat:** Skilled or Tough (reflecting the physical labor of the trade)
- **Equipment:** A currier's knife, stuffing oils/tallow, a partly-finished hide, work clothes permanently marked by oil and dye, 8 gp

This background suits a **Fighter or Barbarian (hard physical trade background), Rogue (a trade built on knowing exactly where one guild's rights end and another's begin — a natural skill for someone who later learns to work other kinds of boundaries), or an Artificer** if you want a "supply chain specialist" flavor for a character focused on crafting and logistics rather than glamorous individual masterpieces.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Boundary Litigant** — A currier locked in an ongoing guild dispute with tanners on one side and saddlers/cordwainers on the other over exactly whose work is whose — a source of petty but escalating local trade conflict.
- **The Trusted Middleman** — A currier so reliable that saddlers, shoemakers, and armorers across town all buy exclusively from them — quietly influential, well-connected, and a good source of leads since half the town's craftsmen pass through their shop.
- **The Downwind Apprentice** — Someone who started at the tannery (the genuinely unpleasant, low-status end of leatherwork) and worked their way up to currying, carrying real class-consciousness about the trade's hierarchy and the people stuck at its bottom.
- **The Adulterator** — A currier cutting corners with cheap or spoiled oils to save money, producing leather that looks fine but fails prematurely — echoing the pewterer's alloy-cutting hook, with consequences that show up later (a failed saddle girth, a torn boot) rather than immediately.
- **The Black-Leather Specialist** — Known specifically for fine blacked/dyed leather work, supplying shoemakers and bookbinders who need consistent, high-quality dark leather — a narrow but genuine specialization worth a name and reputation.
### Game Hooks
- **The Guild Chain Dispute** — Escalating tension between tanners, curriers, and a leather-goods guild threatens to shut down a town's entire leather supply right when the party needs gear repaired or made — a logistics-flavored political hook.
- **Faulty Leather, Delayed Consequences** — Cut-rate currying is traced back only after multiple failures (a saddle strap, a boot sole, a scabbard) start happening across town — an investigation that has to work backward from several seemingly unrelated small disasters to one common cause.
- **The Reliable Contact** — The party needs specific, high-quality, or unusual leather (for armor, a magic item component, an exotic creature's hide that needs proper treatment) and the trusted currier is the person who can actually do it right — a good small commission/networking scene.
- **Tracing the Trade** — A piece of evidence (a scrap of distinctively finished leather) needs to be traced back through the supply chain — tannery to currier to the specific craftsman who bought that batch — a multi-step investigation using the trade's structure as the puzzle.
- **The Apprentice's Grudge** — A currier who rose up from tannery work uses their position to quietly sabotage or expose wealthier guild members who look down on tannery workers — party gets pulled in when the sabotage crosses a line into something more serious.
