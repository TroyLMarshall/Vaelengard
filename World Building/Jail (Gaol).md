### A Different Function Than Modern Imprisonment
We touched on this briefly in the guard entry, but it's worth fully unpacking since medieval imprisonment worked on genuinely different logic than the modern system. The crucial starting point: medieval jails (commonly spelled **gaol** in period English usage) were overwhelmingly used for **holding people before trial or punishment**, not as the primary punishment itself. Long-term imprisonment as a standard criminal sentence is much more a development of later centuries.
### Why Imprisonment Wasn't the Main Punishment
Medieval justice relied heavily on other punishment types, mostly because **imprisonment was expensive and produced no useful outcome** for either the authorities or (in some views) the wronged party:
- **Fines**: a huge proportion of medieval offenses, even fairly serious ones, were resolved through monetary penalties — generating revenue for the relevant court/authority rather than costing money to house an offender
- **Corporal punishment**: whipping, branding, mutilation (cutting off an ear or hand for certain offenses) — brutal by modern standards, but quick and didn't require ongoing resource expenditure
- **Public humiliation**: stocks, pillory, being paraded through town, or other shaming punishments — relying on social consequence rather than confinement
- **Execution**: for serious crimes, capital punishment was genuinely common, again resolving the matter without ongoing cost
- **Exile/outlawry**: banishing someone from a town or region, or declaring them a legal outlaw (someone outside the law's protection, who could be killed without legal consequence) — removing the problem from the community entirely
### What Jails Actually Held
- **Pre-trial detainees**: people awaiting their case being heard, particularly relevant given how infrequently courts sometimes sat (royal itinerant justices, as mentioned in the government official entry, might only pass through a region periodically, meaning someone could wait considerably before their case was actually heard)
- **Debtors**: those unable to pay debts could be imprisoned specifically as pressure to compel payment (often by family or associates, since the debtor obviously couldn't earn money while imprisoned) — debtor imprisonment persisted as a genuine practice well beyond the medieval period
- **Those awaiting execution or corporal punishment**: a holding period between sentencing and the punishment's actual carrying-out
- **Political prisoners**: nobles, rivals, or others held for political reasons by a monarch or powerful lord — sometimes held in genuinely comfortable conditions reflecting their status, a sharp contrast to the conditions facing ordinary prisoners
### Conditions
- **Genuinely brutal by modern standards**: overcrowding, poor or absent sanitation, minimal food (sometimes requiring prisoners or their families to pay for basic provisions), and high disease rates were common
- **Pay-to-survive system**: as mentioned in the guard entry, gaolers often charged fees for better treatment, food, removal of restraints, or other basic considerations — a system genuinely prone to extortion and abuse given minimal oversight, where a prisoner's actual experience depended heavily on their ability to pay their gaoler
- **Variation by status**: a wealthy or noble prisoner could expect to pay for considerably better treatment, sometimes even maintaining servants or receiving visitors, while a poor prisoner faced the system's harshest, most neglectful end
### Where Jails Were Located
- **Castle dungeons**: as the popular imagination often pictures, castles frequently incorporated holding cells, though "dungeon" (from French _donjon_, originally referring to a castle's main keep/tower rather than specifically an underground prison) is a slightly misleading term — not every castle had elaborate underground cells, and holding space might simply be a secure room rather than a purpose-built dungeon
- **Town gaols**: substantial towns maintained dedicated jail buildings or rooms, often associated with the town's main administrative building or gatehouse
- **Sheriff's custody**: as covered in the government official and constable entries, the sheriff held responsibility for maintaining order at the county level in England specifically, which included managing local jail facilities
- **Bishop's prisons**: given the Church's own court system (covered in the government official entry), ecclesiastical authorities maintained their own holding facilities for matters under Church jurisdiction
### Gaolers
- As mentioned in the guard entry, the gaoler's position was sometimes held as a kind of **office or franchise** — purchased or granted rather than simply a salaried job, creating direct financial incentive to extract fees from prisoners to recoup investment and turn a profit
- **Minimal oversight**: unlike guild-regulated trades covered throughout this list, gaolers operated with comparatively little formal accountability, contributing to the genuine reputation for corruption and abuse associated with the role
- **Required some security/physical capability**: managing potentially dangerous or desperate prisoners required at least basic competence in restraint and security, even without anything resembling modern professional training
### Sanctuary as an Alternative
Connecting back to the church entry: the right of **sanctuary** (claiming temporary protection within church grounds) represented a genuine alternative to immediate arrest and imprisonment, at least temporarily — someone could claim sanctuary to buy time, negotiate terms, or in some cases arrange exile rather than facing trial and likely worse punishment.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A corrupt gaoler extracting bribes for basic treatment as a classic, grounded "talk or bribe your way to better conditions" obstacle for a captured PC — mirroring the real historical fee-extraction system rather than a purely binary "escape or don't" scenario
- The contrast between noble/wealthy prisoner treatment and ordinary prisoner brutality as a worldbuilding detail revealing your setting's real power structure — useful if a captured noble NPC ally receives suspiciously comfortable treatment while common prisoners suffer nearby
- Debtor's imprisonment as a sympathetic, grounded plot hook — a PC's family member or contact imprisoned for debt, creating urgency to raise funds rather than simply needing a rescue/combat solution
- Sanctuary-seeking as an alternative to a straightforward jailbreak — a pursued PC or NPC racing for consecrated ground rather than fighting their way out, adding a different tactical and roleplay dimension to an escape scenario
