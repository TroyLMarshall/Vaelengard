### A Trade We've Already Partially Covered
Back in the candlemaker entry, we noted that tallow chandlers often made soap alongside candles, given the overlapping skill and raw material base — both trades used rendered animal fat as a primary input, and the processing knowledge transferred between them. Let's give soapmaking its full dedicated treatment now, since it's genuinely interesting in its own right and somewhat more complex than simply "boil fat with lye."
### Why Soap Mattered
Personal and household cleanliness — despite the persistent myth about medieval people never washing (thoroughly debunked in the public baths entry) — was a genuine concern, and soap served real practical purposes beyond the body:
- **Personal hygiene**: washing hands, face, and body, connecting to the public baths entry's discussion of medieval bathing culture
- **Laundry**: cleaning clothing and household linen — the hired servers entry mentioned laundresses as a specific domestic role, and their work required cleaning agents
- **Craft applications**: soap served various practical craft purposes beyond personal hygiene — wool scouring (cleaning raw wool before processing in the textile trades covered with the dyer entry), and various cleaning applications in other trades
### The Chemistry — What Medieval Soapmakers Actually Did
Medieval soapmakers didn't understand the underlying chemistry in modern terms, but they had genuine, empirically-developed practical knowledge of what we'd now call **saponification** — the chemical reaction between fat and a strong alkali producing soap:

**Fat/oil component**:
- **Tallow**: rendered animal fat (cattle/sheep preferred, as covered in the chandler entry), the dominant fat source for soap across much of northern Europe
- **Olive oil**: particularly significant in Mediterranean soapmaking traditions — **Castile soap** (from Castile in Spain) and similar Mediterranean soaps used olive oil as the primary fat, producing a notably different, gentler product than tallow-based soap
- **Other oils**: various plant-derived oils in regions where they were locally available

**Alkali component**:
- **Lye from wood ash**: the primary alkali source — water percolated through wood ash (potassium-rich ash from hardwoods particularly) produced **lye** (a potassium hydroxide solution), the essential alkali component for soap production
- **Quality variation**: the strength and chemical composition of homemade lye varied considerably depending on the wood ash source and preparation method, making consistent soap quality genuinely challenging without modern analytical chemistry
- **Barilla and salt-based alkalis**: in Mediterranean traditions, alkali derived from burned salt-tolerant plants (barilla, as mentioned in the glassblower entry for glass production) provided a different chemical base — sodium-rich rather than potassium-rich — producing harder soap than the softer potassium-based northern European tradition

**The process**:
- Combining fat and lye solution in the correct proportion
- Extended heating and stirring — sometimes many hours — to achieve proper saponification
- **Testing for completion**: experienced soapmakers used practical tests to assess whether saponification was complete — the tongue test (touching the mixture to check for residual caustic lye's distinctive tingle), visual assessment, and accumulated experiential judgment
- **Salting out**: adding salt to the soap mixture to separate soap from the glycerin and water byproducts, particularly for hard soap production
- **Curing**: finished soap needed time to harden and complete the chemical process — freshly made soap being considerably harsher than properly cured product
### Hard vs. Soft Soap
A genuine, practically significant distinction:
- **Hard soap**: produced using sodium-based alkali (or through the salting-out process), producing the solid bar-like soap more familiar to modern users — harder to make but easier to transport, store, and sell
- **Soft soap**: produced using potassium-based lye (from wood ash), producing a softer, paste-like or semi-liquid product — more common in northern European domestic production, used directly from the container rather than in bar form
### Where Soapmaking Happened
**Household/cottage production**: basic soft soap production using wood ash lye and whatever fat was available was accessible enough for household production — a practical domestic skill similar to basic ale brewing, not requiring specialist equipment or guild training

**Commercial soapmakers**: larger-scale, commercial soap production for urban markets required more consistent quality and quantity than household production provided, leading to recognizable trade specialists particularly in larger towns

**Geographic specialization**: certain regions developed particular reputations for soap production, especially where local raw materials (olive oil in Mediterranean regions, specific plant-ash sources) produced distinctive, high-quality products — connecting to the regional specialization pattern seen throughout this list
### Guild Structure
- Soapmakers organized into guilds in towns with sufficient commercial production, sometimes grouped with chandlers (given the overlapping fat-processing skills) or organized independently
- Quality regulation similar to other food/household product trades covered throughout this list — adulterated or poor-quality soap was a recognized problem, with various attempts at guild-level quality enforcement
### Luxury Soap
Connecting to the broader luxury goods theme throughout this list:
- **Scented soaps**: adding aromatic substances (herbs, imported perfumes) to soap produced luxury products for wealthy consumers, connecting to the apothecary and herbalist entries' discussion of aromatic plant knowledge
- **Castile and similar quality soaps**: fine Mediterranean olive-oil soaps became genuine luxury trade goods, imported to northern Europe where they commanded premium prices compared to local tallow soap
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A soapmaker's lye production (requiring large quantities of wood ash) as an unexpected connection to another trade — perhaps a soapmaker and a potter or glassmaker (both needing ash-derived materials) sharing a wood ash supply chain, creating interesting inter-trade economic relationships
- Luxury scented soap as a trade good signaling wealth and access to distant markets — finding imported Castile soap equivalent in a remote location suggesting unexpected wealth or connections worth investigating
- A soapmaker's practical chemistry knowledge (genuine alkali handling, fat processing, and material testing) as a natural bridge to alchemical interests — a soapmaker NPC who's begun experimenting beyond soap into broader material transformation, connecting the mundane trade to your setting's magical/alchemical tradition
- Lye as a dangerous material in its own right — concentrated lye's genuinely caustic properties making a soapmaker's workshop a location where chemical hazards exist in a historically grounded way, useful for a puzzle, trap, or improvised weapon scenario
