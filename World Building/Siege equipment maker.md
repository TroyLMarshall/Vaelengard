### The Craft and Its Structure
Building siege engines — the trebuchets, mangonels, ballistae, siege towers, and battering rams that defined medieval siege warfare — wasn't typically a standalone, guild-organized trade with its own dedicated shopfront the way smithing or saddlery was. Instead, it drew together several existing specialist skills under the direction of a genuinely distinctive medieval professional: the **engineer** (from the Latin _ingenium_, meaning skill, cleverness, or a clever device — the same root giving us both "engineer" and "ingenious," a nice bit of etymological texture connecting the trade directly to the idea of clever mechanical invention).

**The engineer as project director** — A medieval siege engineer combined genuine mathematical and mechanical understanding (calculating counterweight ratios for a trebuchet, structural loads for a siege tower, the geometry of a mine tunnel's approach to a wall) with practical carpentry and construction oversight, typically directing a team of skilled **carpenters, smiths, and laborers** who did the actual hands-on building using timber and iron sourced and shaped much like any other major construction project. This makes the siege engineer conceptually similar to the master mason directing a cathedral or bridge project — someone with real specialized design knowledge overseeing a construction crew drawing on ordinary craft trades, rather than a solitary artisan-inventor building a war machine alone in a workshop.

**Types of siege engines and their requirements** — **Trebuchets** (the most sophisticated and effective medieval siege engine, using a counterweight to fling projectiles with real destructive force and, with skilled calibration, genuine accuracy over distance) demanded the most sophisticated engineering understanding — counterweight mass, arm length ratios, and sling length all needed careful calculation to achieve reliable, effective range and power, and a skilled trebuchet engineer's expertise in getting these ratios right was genuinely rare and valuable. **Mangonels and other torsion/tension engines** (earlier, generally less powerful and less accurate than trebuchets, using twisted rope or similar tension mechanisms) required different but related mechanical knowledge. **Siege towers** (tall wooden structures rolled up to a wall to let attackers cross directly onto the ramparts) demanded structural engineering to support real weight and withstand both movement and defensive attack, plus practical logistics getting such a massive structure across a battlefield. **Battering rams**, **mining operations** (tunneling beneath walls to collapse them, sometimes literally supported by wooden props later set alight to trigger deliberate collapse — a technique requiring real structural/mining knowledge distinct from carpentry alone), and **protective coverings** (mobile shelters — "cats" or "sows" — protecting attackers working close to a wall) rounded out an engineer's practical repertoire.

**On-site construction, not portable equipment** — Crucially, the largest and most powerful siege engines were typically **built on-site at the siege itself**, using locally sourced or hauled timber, rather than constructed elsewhere and transported intact — meaning a besieging army needed to bring (or recruit locally) not a finished trebuchet, but the **engineers and skilled labor force** capable of building one from raw material once they arrived, plus the iron fittings, rope, and specialized components (like a trebuchet's sling or counterweight mechanism) that might genuinely be prefabricated and transported since they required more precise craft than rough on-site carpentry could provide.

**Who employed engineers** — Kings and great lords retained skilled engineers as valuable, sometimes long-serving military specialists — genuinely respected and well-compensated given how directly a siege's success or failure (and therefore a whole campaign's or war's outcome) could depend on their competence. Skilled engineers, like the mercenary captains and specialist military professionals of the period, sometimes moved between different lords' service based on where the pay and opportunity were best, making this a genuinely mobile, marketable specialist skill rather than a fixed local trade tied to one town or lord.

**Countermeasures and defensive engineering** — The same engineering knowledge applied equally to **defense** — engineers designed and improved castle fortifications specifically to resist siege engines (thicker walls, angled bastions to eliminate blind spots, counter-mining to intercept an attacker's tunnel), meaning a skilled engineer's expertise was valuable to besiegers and defenders alike, and the same person might plausibly work either side across a career, or even switch sides within a single conflict if circumstances (capture, better pay, changed loyalty) demanded it.

**Social position** — Genuinely elevated compared to most crafts in this list — a skilled siege engineer possessed rare, high-value expertise directly affecting military outcomes, commanding real respect, pay, and mobility across noble and royal patronage, occupying a position closer to a valued military specialist or even a knight's social proximity than an ordinary craftsman's guild-bound status, even without noble birth themselves.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Investigation, History (or Arcana, if leaning into a setting where siege engineering blends with magical or unusual mechanics)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Carpenter's Tools or Mason's Tools, plus a case for Tinker's Tools if leaning into precision mechanism design
- **Feat:** Skilled or Alert
- **Equipment:** Engineering plans/calculations, carpenter's or mason's tools, a small model or scale demonstration of a siege mechanism, practical working clothes, 15 gp

This background is an outstanding fit for an **Artificer** — genuine applied mechanical/mathematical skill, direct crossover into practical problem-solving and construction, and a plausible reason for real technical expertise beyond ordinary crafting. It also suits a **Fighter** with a tactical/strategic bent (real battlefield engineering knowledge, comfort directing labor under military pressure) or a **Wizard** if your setting blends arcane theory into engineering calculation.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Mercenary Engineer** — Sells siege expertise to whichever lord pays best, genuinely mobile and unattached to any single loyalty, morally pragmatic about working for whoever's paying.
- **The Defensive Specialist** — Focuses specifically on fortification design and countermeasures rather than offensive siege engines, deeply respected by lords anxious about their castle's vulnerabilities.
- **The Engineer Torn Between Sides** — Captured or coerced into working for a besieging force against their own former employer/homeland — genuine moral tension and divided loyalty.
- **The Innovator** — Developing a genuinely new siege engine design or improved calculation method, ambitious and possibly reckless about testing unproven engineering under real battlefield pressure.
- **The Veteran Master** — Decades of experience across many sieges, deeply pragmatic and unsentimental about the brutal realities of siege warfare, a good source of hard-won practical knowledge and grim war stories.
### Game Hooks
- **The Siege Preparation** — The party needs to help construct (or sabotage) siege equipment ahead of an actual assault — a good "logistics and engineering under time pressure" hook distinct from direct combat.
- **The Captured Engineer** — A valuable siege engineer has been captured by an enemy force and is being coerced into working against their own side — a rescue/extraction mission with real strategic stakes.
- **Countering the Siege** — The party's stronghold faces an incoming siege, and they need a defensive engineer's help designing countermeasures (counter-mining, reinforcement, disabling approach paths) before the attackers arrive.
- **The Sabotaged Engine** — A siege engine fails catastrophically during construction or use — sabotage, faulty calculation, or bad material — and the party investigates whether it was incompetence, an enemy agent, or something else.
- **The Engineer's Choice** — A skilled engineer with genuine value to both sides of a conflict has to be persuaded, bribed, or coerced to work for the party's cause instead of a rival's — a social/negotiation hook with real strategic consequences riding on the outcome.
