### The Craft and Its Structure
Medieval poetry existed across several genuinely distinct traditions, ranging from noble amateur composition to professional court entertainment to towering, era-defining literary works — and unlike most crafts covered in this list, poetry's practitioners spanned nearly the entire social hierarchy, from kings and knights composing as a genuine mark of refined nobility down to hired professional performers reciting others' work for coin.

**Troubadours and trouvères — noble composer-poets** — Flourishing especially from the 1100s onward in southern France (troubadours, composing in Occitan/Provençal) and northern France (trouvères, in Old French), with related traditions among German **Minnesänger**, this tradition's defining feature was that its composers were often **nobility themselves** — knights, lords, and even monarchs (Richard the Lionheart is a genuine historical example of a royal troubadour-composer) treating skilled poetic composition as a real, prestigious aristocratic accomplishment, not a trade practiced by social inferiors. Their central subject, **courtly love** (_fin'amor_), codified an elaborate, highly stylized poetic vocabulary: devoted, often anguished worship of an idealized (frequently married, frequently unattainable) noble lady, framed through the language of feudal service and religious devotion transposed onto romantic longing — the poet-lover as his lady's "vassal," his suffering and devotion a mark of refinement rather than simple romantic desire. This poetic tradition shaped medieval aristocratic culture's entire vocabulary for romantic expression, influencing chivalric literature, courtly behavior, and popular conceptions of "romance" for centuries afterward.

**Performance versus composition** — A noble troubadour composed the words and music, but often relied on a **jongleur** or professional performer to actually deliver the piece before an audience — meaning the aristocratic prestige of composition and the more socially precarious work of performance were frequently split between two different people, echoing the same social-status split covered in the musicians entry (composer-nobility versus itinerant performer).

**Epic narrative poetry — the chanson de geste** — A distinct, older tradition of long heroic narrative verse celebrating legendary and historical martial deeds — the _Song of Roland_ (recounting a legendary/exaggerated account of a real Frankish rearguard action, composed and transmitted across generations) is the standout surviving example, and this genre served a genuinely important cultural function: transmitting and reinforcing a society's shared heroic history, values, and identity through memorized, performed verse, kept alive by minstrels reciting it at courts and gatherings across generations before (and alongside) any written transmission.

**Literary giants and the shift toward vernacular prestige** — By the later medieval period, individual poets achieved genuinely towering, era-defining literary reputation in their own right, writing in vernacular languages rather than Latin and thereby elevating those vernacular tongues to genuine literary respectability: **Dante Alighieri** (_Divine Comedy_, early 1300s, Italian), **Geoffrey Chaucer** (_Canterbury Tales_, late 1300s, Middle English — already referenced several times across this list's tradesperson satire), and **Christine de Pizan** (a genuinely remarkable figure — a professional woman writer and poet in early 1400s France, supporting herself and her family through her writing after being widowed, and a significant early voice defending women against misogynistic literary tradition) all represent poets who achieved real, lasting individual literary fame and influence well beyond court entertainment, using poetry to engage directly with philosophy, politics, religion, and social critique.

**Women poets** — Beyond Christine de Pizan's later, exceptional professional career, women did compose within the troubadour tradition itself — the **trobairitz** were female troubadour-composers in southern France, genuinely participating in the same courtly-love poetic tradition as their male counterparts, though in smaller numbers and against real social constraint, offering you a historically grounded template for a noblewoman poet-composer character.

**Social position — genuinely bifurcated, like musicians** — Composition itself, especially within the troubadour/courtly tradition, could be a mark of noble refinement practiced by the highest-status people in society; professional performance of that composed material (or of epic narrative verse) was typically lower-status, itinerant work; and the later medieval shift toward individually-famous literary vernacular poets created a new kind of status — genuine personal literary renown, sometimes achieved from relatively modest origins (Chaucer himself came from a merchant, not noble, family) through sheer talent and patronage.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Persuasion, History (or Insight, for a courtly-love poet reading romantic and political undercurrents)
- **Languages:** One additional language (reflecting the vernacular literary tradition)
- **Feat:** Skilled or Fey Touched (for a more mystically-inclined poetic voice)
- **Equipment:** Writing materials, a collection of original or memorized verse, fine clothes (if noble-composer tradition) or humbler traveling garb (if professional performer/reciter), 10-20 gp depending on tradition

This background is an obvious, excellent fit for a **Bard**, but the specific tradition chosen shapes the character meaningfully: a **troubadour-tradition Bard** carries real noble status and courtly-love thematic material, ideal for a character entangled in romantic and political court intrigue; an **epic-reciter Bard** carries deep historical/legendary knowledge as a living cultural archive; a **Christine de Pizan-style professional writer** offers a compelling, unusual background for a character (of any gender) who built independent standing and livelihood through talent and persistence rather than birth, a strong option for a **Wizard** or **Cleric** wanting a literary/intellectual bent instead.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Noble Troubadour Entangled in Scandal** — Composed devoted courtly-love poetry for a specific lady whose husband or family has taken serious, dangerous offense — real political/romantic stakes from what began as elegant convention.
- **The Trobairitz** — A noblewoman poet-composer navigating real social constraint to participate in a tradition mostly practiced by men, fiercely protective of her own poetic voice and reputation.
- **The Professional Writer** — Echoing Christine de Pizan directly — supports themselves and dependents through genuine literary skill and patronage after losing other means of support, sharp, independent, and unusually socially mobile through talent alone.
- **The Epic Reciter** — Carries a whole chanson de geste in memory, a living cultural archive whose recitation at the right moment could shift a court's mood, remind people of old oaths, or reveal forgotten history.
- **The Rival Poet** — Locked in genuine literary/romantic competition with another troubadour over the same patron's favor or the same lady's attention — echoes court intrigue but through composed verse rather than direct conflict.
### Game Hooks
- **The Scandalous Verse** — A troubadour's courtly-love poem, meant as elegant convention, is read as literal accusation or genuine romantic threat by a jealous husband or rival — the party navigates the fallout of poetry taken too literally (or not literally enough).
- **The Poet's Patronage** — A skilled but currently patron-less poet needs the party's help securing (or surviving the loss of) noble sponsorship — court-politics-adjacent, with real stakes for the poet's livelihood.
- **Reciting the Old Oath** — An epic narrative poem contains a genuine historical/legal claim (an old oath, a boundary, a promise between houses) that becomes suddenly, urgently relevant, and only a skilled reciter's memory can accurately recall and prove it.
- **The Professional Writer's Defense** — Echoing Christine de Pizan's real historical work, a poet is using their writing to publicly defend someone (a woman, a marginalized figure, an unpopular truth) against a hostile establishment, and needs protection or support to keep writing safely.
- **The Trobairitz's Voice** — A noblewoman poet's work is being suppressed, plagiarized, or attributed to a male relative instead of her, and the party helps her reclaim authorship and voice — a grounded justice hook with real historical resonance.
