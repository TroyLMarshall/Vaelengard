### A Trade We Flagged But Didn't Fully Cover
Back in the metalsmiths entry, we specifically mentioned coppersmiths (and braziers — workers in brass, a copper-zinc alloy) as worth dedicated treatment — occupying a middle tier between common blacksmith ironwork and prestigious goldsmith precious metalwork, serving real everyday and luxury needs across multiple sectors of medieval life.
### Copper and Its Alloys — The Material Basis
Understanding what coppersmiths actually worked with requires distinguishing several related but distinct materials:

**Pure copper**: relatively soft, highly malleable, excellent thermal conductivity — these properties making it well-suited to cooking vessels and specific craft applications, though its softness limiting structural applications

**Bronze**: copper-tin alloy — harder and stronger than pure copper, with lower melting point than pure copper, and the material covered extensively in the foundry entry for bell-casting and sculptural work. The overlap between coppersmith and foundry/bell-founder work was real — both working copper-based alloys, though through different techniques (the coppersmith primarily hammering and forming, the founder primarily casting)

**Brass**: copper-zinc alloy — producing a distinctively golden-yellow metal with different properties from bronze, used for decorative fittings, scientific instruments, and various applications where its appearance and workability suited the need. The **brazier** specifically worked brass rather than pure copper or bronze, though the term was sometimes used more loosely.

**Latten**: a medieval term for a brass-like copper alloy used particularly for decorative objects, memorial brasses (connecting to the graveyard entry's discussion of brass memorial plates), and various decorative fittings
### What Coppersmiths Made
**Cooking and kitchen equipment**:
- **Cauldrons and cooking pots**: copper and bronze cooking vessels were genuinely valued kitchen equipment for prosperous households — copper's excellent thermal conductivity making it superior to iron for even heat distribution in cooking, connecting to the chef entry's discussion of kitchen equipment
- **Pots, pans, and specialized cooking vessels**: various forms suited to specific culinary applications
- **Stills and distillation equipment**: copper's workability and chemical properties (relatively non-reactive with many distilled substances) made it the preferred material for distillation alembics — connecting to the alchemist and apothecary entries' discussion of distillation as a key process

**Decorative and household objects**:
- **Candlesticks**: copper and brass candlesticks ranging from simple practical forms to elaborately decorated objects for wealthy households and churches
- **Aquamaniles**: decorative water vessels used for hand-washing at table in wealthy households — often cast in animal forms (lions, knights on horseback) and representing genuine artistic as well as functional objects
- **Ewers and basins**: matched sets for hand-washing, connecting to medieval dining etiquette covered in the chef and hired servers entries
- **Incense burners and censers**: liturgical equipment for church use, connecting to the church entry

**Scientific and precision instruments**:
- **Astrolabes**: as covered in the astrologer and astronomer entries, astrolabes were precision brass instruments — their production requiring both the metalworking skill of a coppersmith/brazier and precision engraving connecting to the goldsmith entry
- **Clocks and mechanical devices**: brass's workability and dimensional stability making it suitable for clock components and other precision mechanical work as mechanical clockmaking developed through the later medieval period
- **Scales and weights**: precision weighing equipment, connecting to the marketplace entry's discussion of weight and measure regulation

**Church and ecclesiastical objects**:
- **Fonts**: baptismal fonts in copper or bronze — significant ecclesiastical commissions connecting to the church entry
- **Lecterns**: eagle lecterns (book-rests in the form of an eagle with spread wings) in brass or bronze were a specific, recognizable form of ecclesiastical furniture
- **Memorial brasses**: as touched on in the graveyard entry, engraved brass memorial plates set into church floors were a significant specialized production — requiring both the material production of appropriate brass sheets and skilled engraving of the memorial imagery and inscription

**Trade and commerce equipment**:
- **Measures**: copper and brass measuring vessels for trade use, connecting to the marketplace entry's discussion of standardized measures
- **Bells**: smaller bells (hand bells, door bells, animal bells) distinct from the large church bells covered in the foundry entry — copper and brass small bell production sometimes falling to coppersmiths rather than dedicated bell-founders

### Techniques

**Raising**: the fundamental coppersmith technique for producing hollow vessels — starting from a flat sheet of metal and working it over a stake (a fixed metal form) with a hammer, gradually raising the walls of a vessel through repeated annealed hammering
- **Annealing**: periodically heating and quenching the metal during raising to restore workability — work-hardening as the metal is hammered makes it increasingly brittle until annealing restores its malleability
- This technique required real skill and a developed sense of the metal's behavior — feeling when it needed annealing, when it was ready for the next stage of shaping

**Sinking**: an alternative forming approach — working metal down into a depression rather than raising it over a form, suited to specific vessel shapes

**Planishing**: finishing hammering with a planishing hammer to smooth the surface after rough shaping — producing the characteristic faceted but smooth surface of quality hand-raised metalwork

**Soldering**: joining copper components using lower-melting-point metal alloys — essential for handles, spouts, and other attachments

**Chasing and repoussé**: decorative techniques — chasing pushing design into the metal from the front, repoussé pushing design from the back to create relief decoration — connecting to the goldsmith entry's discussion of similar decorative metalwork techniques at a precious-metal level

**Engraving**: cutting design into the metal surface — particularly significant for the memorial brass production mentioned above
### Guild Structure
- Coppersmiths (and braziers) organized into guilds in towns with sufficient demand — sometimes unified with other metalworking trades, sometimes forming distinct guilds
- **The Worshipful Company of Founders**: covered foundry/casting work in metals including copper alloys — with some overlap with coppersmith production in the casting realm
- **Braziers' guild**: in some cities, braziers (brass workers specifically) maintained distinct guild organization from both coppersmiths (pure copper work) and founders (casting rather than wrought work)
- Guild regulation around metal purity and alloy composition was genuine — controlling the proportion of copper, tin, and zinc in different products to maintain quality standards and prevent fraud through substituting cheaper metals in alloy compositions
### The Tinsmith Connection
Worth briefly noting a related trade: **tinsmiths** (or tinners, or whitesmiths in some usages) worked tin and tin-plated iron — producing lighter, cheaper versions of some of the same products coppersmiths made (cups, plates, measures) for a less wealthy market. The overlap and competition between copper/brass goods and tin goods was real, with tin products accessible to a broader economic range than the more expensive copper and brass.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A coppersmith's distillation equipment as a connection to the alchemist trade — a metalworker whose customers include both ordinary cooks needing cauldrons and alchemists commissioning specialized still designs, giving them knowledge of both the mundane and potentially arcane uses their products serve
- A memorial brass commission as a plot element — a specific engraved memorial (connecting to the graveyard entry) whose inscription or imagery contains more information than a casual observer would notice, requiring both knowledge of the medium and attention to the specific imagery to extract its significance
- The astrolabe as a precision object connecting coppersmith craft to astronomical knowledge — a specific instrument whose maker's identity might be traceable through their distinctive engraving style or construction details, connecting the astronomical world of the astrologer entry to the craft world of this entry
- Copper cooking vessels as a specific, recognizable wealth marker for households — distinguishing between the peasant household with only iron or ceramic cooking equipment and the prosperous merchant household with copper cauldrons and brass candlesticks, with the visual difference immediately communicating economic position
