### The Craft and Its Structure
A **moneyer** was a licensed, highly trusted craftsman authorized to actually **strike coinage** on behalf of a king, bishop, lord, or town holding the legal right to mint money — a trade sitting at the direct intersection of craft skill, state authority, and enormous potential for both legitimate wealth and serious crime. Unlike most trades covered so far, moneyers didn't operate in an open market selling their skill to whoever would pay — they worked under **direct royal or lordly license**, since the right to mint coinage (**the right of coinage**, a genuine and jealously guarded royal/noble prerogative) was one of the most tightly controlled economic powers in medieval society. Controlling the money supply meant controlling trust in the realm's entire economy, and rulers took the integrity of their coinage extremely seriously.

**How minting worked** — A moneyer (or a small workshop of moneyers and assistants under one) received **bullion** — raw silver or gold, sometimes supplied by the authority commissioning coins, sometimes brought in by merchants or individuals to be minted into official coin for a fee — and turned it into standardized coinage through a genuinely skilled hand process: melting and refining the metal to the correct **fineness** (purity — a critical, legally specified standard), casting it into blanks of the correct weight, and then **striking** each blank between two engraved dies (one for each face) using a hammer, by hand, one coin at a time. This is real, exacting craft work: **weight and fineness had to be extremely consistent**, since a coin's value depended entirely on people trusting that it actually contained the silver or gold content it claimed to. Producing dies themselves required a related but distinct specialist skill — **die engraving**, cutting a reversed, detailed image into hardened steel that would then stamp thousands of identical coin faces, a task requiring real artistic and metalworking precision.

**Trust, oversight, and the death penalty** — Because currency integrity was so foundational to the whole economy, moneyers operated under **unusually severe legal oversight and unusually severe punishment for fraud**. Debasing coinage (secretly reducing the precious metal content while passing coins off at full value), clipping (shaving small amounts of metal off the edges of existing coins and pocketing the shavings — a real, widespread medieval crime that eventually led to the practice of **milled/reeded coin edges** specifically to make clipping detectable), or counterfeiting dies were treated as extremely serious crimes — in England, a notorious real historical event (the "Assize of Moneyers" under Henry I, 1124-25) saw the king order **mutilation of numerous moneyers accused of producing debased coin**, a genuinely brutal, historically real crackdown reflecting just how seriously the crown treated currency fraud. Moneyers occupied an unusual position where their trade was **simultaneously prestigious (royally licensed, economically essential, often personally wealthy) and under constant suspicion**, since the exact skill that made them valuable — controlled access to precious metal and the literal machinery of monetary trust — also made them the person best positioned to defraud everyone, if they chose to.

**Regional and lordly minting rights** — Coinage rights weren't always exclusively royal — bishops, abbeys, and powerful lords sometimes held genuine minting rights of their own in various parts of medieval Europe, producing an actual, real patchwork of different coinages circulating simultaneously in some regions, each stamped with a different authority's mark and, in some documented cases, actually varying in weight/fineness standard, creating real practical friction and exchange-rate complexity for merchants moving between jurisdictions. This is excellent, ready-made worldbuilding texture: a fantasy setting doesn't need a single unified currency any more than real medieval Europe had one, and the tension between competing coinages (whose money is trusted where, and why) is a genuinely useful economic/political throughline.

**Social position** — Moneyers were typically **prosperous, urban, and closely tied to the authority they served** — not guild-organized craftsmen selling to an open market, but licensed agents of the state (or lordly/ecclesiastical equivalent) occupying a position of real trust and real financial opportunity, similar in spirit to a modern licensed banker more than to an ordinary artisan. Some medieval mints employed multiple moneyers working under a **master of the mint** who oversaw the whole operation, standards, and accounting, adding another layer of hierarchy and institutional structure to the trade.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Investigation, Persuasion (or Insight, for detecting fraud/counterfeits)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Smith's Tools, or a custom "Mint Tools" proficiency covering assaying/weighing
- **Feat:** Skilled or Alert
- **Equipment:** A set of coin dies (real, or the tools to check/weigh coinage), an assayer's scale, a licensed mint worker's seal or badge, 20 gp

This background is a strong, distinctive fit for an **Artificer** (precision metalwork, genuine proto-technical process) or a **Rogue** (the built-in "person best positioned to defraud the system, and possibly did" angle is extremely rich for a con-artist or forger-turned-adventurer backstory). It's also a good background for a character motivated by genuine institutional loyalty or its opposite — someone who watched the severe punishment for currency fraud up close and either became fiercely honest as a result, or fled the trade entirely after witnessing (or barely escaping) exactly that kind of crackdown.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Master of the Mint** — Oversees an entire minting operation, personally accountable to the crown or lord for every coin's integrity — genuinely powerful, genuinely paranoid about fraud within their own operation, a good patron or investigator-ally.
- **The Suspected Debaser** — A moneyer under investigation for producing underweight or impure coin — maybe guilty, maybe framed by a rival or a corrupt superior covering their own fraud, a solid "uncover the truth" mystery NPC.
- **The Reformed Clipper** — Once shaved coin edges for illicit profit, caught and punished (mutilation, in the harshest real historical tradition, or your setting's equivalent), now living with the consequences and possibly using intimate knowledge of the crime to help detect it in others.
- **The Die Engraver** — A specialist artisan producing the actual coin dies, a rarer and even more specialized skill than striking coins themselves — a good source for identifying counterfeit dies or coins by their engraving quality.
- **The Rival Mint's Agent** — Represents a competing lordly or ecclesiastical mint whose coinage circulates alongside (and in tension with) the local currency — an economic-political operator navigating real inter-jurisdictional currency friction.
### Game Hooks
- **The Debasement Investigation** — Coins from a specific mint are found to be underweight or impure, threatening real economic and legal consequences for whoever's responsible — a genuine "corporate fraud" mystery with medieval stakes (potentially mutilation or execution for whoever's found guilty).
- **The Counterfeit Ring** — Someone's producing convincing fake coinage using stolen or forged dies, and the party has to trace the operation back to its source before it undermines trust in the whole local currency.
- **Competing Currencies** — The party operates in a region where multiple coinages (royal, episcopal, lordly) circulate simultaneously, and navigating trade, tolls, or a specific transaction requires understanding (or exploiting) the friction between them.
- **The Framed Moneyer** — A moneyer is falsely accused of debasement by a rival or a corrupt master seeking to cover their own crime — the party has to prove innocence before a brutal punishment is carried out.
- **Bullion Heist** — A shipment of raw silver or gold bound for the mint is stolen en route — valuable, traceable (if the party can identify its origin/destination), and a good classic heist/recovery hook with real economic stakes for the whole region.
