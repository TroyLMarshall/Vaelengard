### Another Useful Anachronism to Unpack
Like "boardinghouse" and "caravan guild," "construction company" is a modern organizational concept without a precise medieval equivalent — but the actual _work_ of large-scale building absolutely happened, organized through structures such as guilds and master craftsmen, plus a few specific arrangements unique to major building projects. Here's out what actually ran a big medieval construction project.
### No Single Contracting Firm — Instead, a Coordinated Web of Trades
Rather than one company hiring all necessary workers under a single corporate umbrella (the modern model), medieval major construction — especially the great cathedrals, castles, and civic buildings — was organized through a **site-specific coordination of multiple independent trades**, each with their own internal guild structure, brought together for a specific project:
- **Master masons**: for stone construction, the master mason was often the closest equivalent to what we'd now call a project architect/lead contractor — they designed the structure (often without detailed blueprints in the modern sense, relying instead on geometric templates, scaled drawings, and accumulated practical knowledge), oversaw the stone-cutting and assembly, and coordinated with other trades
- **Master carpenters**: oversaw timber elements (roof structures, scaffolding, formwork) as covered in the carpenter entry
- **Master smiths**: provided ironwork — fittings, tools, hinges, and structural ironwork as needed
- **Glaziers**: for window glass and stained glass in churches/cathedrals
- **Sculptors and carvers**: for decorative stonework, as covered in the artist/sculptor entry

Each of these trades operated through their own guild apprentice/journeyman/master structure, but for a major project, they'd work alongside each other under loose overall coordination — usually by the master mason or, for the grandest projects, sometimes a clergyman-administrator overseeing the whole effort on behalf of the patron.
### The "Fabric" — Cathedral Building Organizations
This is genuinely the closest historical equivalent to an ongoing "construction company" specifically for major church building:
- **The fabrica/"fabric" of a cathedral**: many great cathedral projects established a dedicated ongoing administrative body — the _fabrica_ — specifically to manage long-term construction finances, material sourcing, and labor organization across what were often **generations-long building projects**
- **Dedicated funding streams**: fabric funds drew on tithes, donations, indulgences sold specifically to fund construction, and ongoing patron support — a genuinely sophisticated, long-term financial management operation given how many decades (sometimes a century or more) these projects spanned
- **Continuity across changing personnel**: since master masons, bishops, and patrons might die or move on during a multi-generational project, the fabric organization provided institutional continuity that any single person's career couldn't
### How Workers Were Recruited and Organized
- **Traveling skilled craftsmen**: master masons and other highly skilled specialists often **traveled between major projects** across regions or even countries, since the specific expertise needed for ambitious Gothic cathedral construction (complex vaulting, flying buttresses, structural innovation) wasn't universally available locally — a reputation for skill could draw a master mason recruitment offers from distant projects
- **Local labor**: less skilled labor (general site work, hauling, basic construction tasks) was typically drawn from the local area, providing significant local employment during major projects
- **Lodges**: stonemasons working on a major project often organized into a **lodge** — both a literal building near the construction site (providing shelter, workspace, and storage) and an organizational/social structure for the masons working there, regulating training, conduct, and trade secrets among the masons on-site. This lodge tradition is, incidentally, the genuine historical root that later **Freemasonry** symbolically drew on (heavily romanticized and reinterpreted in early modern and modern Masonic tradition, well beyond the original practical medieval lodge function).

### Patrons and Funding

- **The Church**: cathedrals and major church building were funded through tithes, donations, relic-related pilgrimage revenue, and sometimes specifically sold indulgences (a practice that became seriously controversial and was part of the grievances fueling the Reformation, though that's past the strictly medieval period)
- **Royal and noble patrons**: castles, palaces, and prestige civic buildings were funded directly by rulers or wealthy nobility, sometimes also drawing on conscripted or feudal labor obligations in addition to paid skilled craftsmen
- **Town governments**: civic buildings (guildhalls, town walls, bridges) were funded through town revenues, tolls, and sometimes specific levies on citizens or guilds with a stake in the project
- **Guild-funded chapels/additions**: as mentioned with churches, wealthy guilds sometimes funded specific additions or chapels within larger church projects, effectively acting as a sub-patron within the larger building effort

### Construction Logistics

- **Material sourcing**: stone quarrying (sometimes from substantial distances, requiring significant transport logistics via river or cart), timber sourcing (connecting to the carpentry and forest management trades), and metal — all required their own supply chain coordination
- **Scaffolding and lifting**: wooden scaffolding (carpenter-built) and various crane/lifting devices (often human or animal-powered treadwheel cranes) were essential for raising materials to height on tall structures
- **Site safety**: genuinely dangerous work — falls, material handling accidents, and structural failures during construction were real risks, without anything resembling modern safety standards or regulation
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A long-running "great work" — a temple, tower, or wall whose construction has spanned generations, with its own dedicated fabric-style organization, internal politics, and perhaps even a present-day crisis (funding dried up, the original master mason's plans were lost, a magical complication has arisen) that draws in PCs
- A traveling master mason character with genuine cross-regional reputation, recruited setting-wide for their unique expertise — perhaps the only one who knows how to incorporate some specific magical or structural technique your setting needs
- A mason's lodge with actual secret knowledge worth protecting — leaning into the real historical secrecy/trade-protection tradition that later fed Masonic mythology, but grounded in believable craft-guild logic (structural secrets, geometric techniques) rather than full conspiracy
- Indulgence-funded construction as a moral/political tension point if your setting has an equivalent religious institution — a temple's grand new construction funded by ethically questionable fundraising, creating a believable in-world controversy
