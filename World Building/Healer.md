### Pulling Together the Full Spectrum of Medical Care
We've covered most of the actual pieces of this picture already — physicians, apothecaries, barber-surgeons, astrologers, midwives — but "healer" as a category specifically invites us to focus on the _informal, folk, and non-university_ side of medieval medicine, which is where most people actually received care. Let's bring the whole landscape together with that emphasis.
### The Formal System — A Quick Recap
As covered in the doctor/dentist entry: **physicians** (university-trained, theoretical, expensive, rare) sat atop a formal hierarchy, with **apothecaries** compounding remedies and **barber-surgeons** doing hands-on cutting/bleeding work. This system served almost exclusively the wealthy and urban population. For the vast majority of medieval people — rural peasants, the urban poor, anyone without money or access — healing happened entirely outside this formal structure.
### Folk Healers — Where Most People Actually Got Care
**Wise women and cunning folk**: this is genuinely the backbone of everyday medieval healthcare
- Practiced herbal medicine, often with real, empirically-accumulated effectiveness passed down through generations of oral tradition and practical experimentation, even without any theoretical medical framework behind it
- Treated everyday ailments — wounds, fevers, digestive complaints, skin conditions — using locally available plants, often grown in household or village gardens specifically for medicinal use
- Frequently combined herbal knowledge with **charms, prayers, or ritual elements** — medieval folk healing rarely drew the sharp line modern thinking does between "practical remedy" and "spiritual/magical practice," since illness itself was often understood through a blended lens of natural cause, divine will, and sometimes malevolent supernatural influence
- Predominantly **women**, similar to the dairying and ale-brewing trades — herbal/folk medicine was one of the relatively few areas where women could exercise genuine, recognized (if informal) expertise and community authority

**Midwives**: as touched on in the doctor/dentist entry, childbirth was almost entirely managed by **midwives** rather than physicians
- Handled the entire birthing process for the overwhelming majority of births across all social classes (even wealthy households relied on midwives for normal deliveries, calling a physician only for serious complications)
- Possessed genuine, specialized practical knowledge — recognizing complications, basic interventions, and the social/emotional support role surrounding childbirth
- Sometimes held semi-official recognition in larger towns, occasionally even licensed or examined by Church or civic authorities specifically because midwives also played a role in **emergency baptism** of infants at risk of dying shortly after birth — a genuinely important religious responsibility given the theological stakes around infant baptism discussed in the graveyard entry

**Bonesetters**: specialized folk practitioners focused specifically on setting broken or dislocated bones — distinct from the barber-surgeon's more formal role, bonesetters often operated as a recognized local specialist using accumulated practical/anatomical knowledge passed through informal apprenticeship or family tradition rather than guild training

**Herbalists and root-gatherers**: some folk healers specialized particularly in gathering, preparing, and selling medicinal plants, occupying a position somewhat parallel to (and sometimes overlapping with) the apothecary trade, but operating outside formal guild structure and serving a broader, less wealthy clientele
### Religious Healing
This deserves real emphasis, since it represented a genuinely significant parallel "treatment" pathway, not a fringe practice:
- **Saints associated with specific ailments**: particular saints became traditionally associated with curing or protecting against specific conditions, and praying to or invoking the relevant saint was a completely standard medical response, not separate from "real" treatment in medieval thinking
- **Pilgrimage to healing shrines**: traveling to a shrine associated with healing miracles (often housing relics of a relevant saint) was a genuine, widely practiced treatment-seeking behavior, sometimes undertaken alongside or instead of other medical treatment
- **Holy wells**: numerous wells and springs across medieval Europe acquired reputations for healing properties, often Christianized versions of earlier pre-Christian sacred site traditions, visited specifically for their believed curative power
- **Touching relics or receiving blessing**: direct physical or ritual contact with sacred objects or blessed items was believed to offer genuine healing potential
### Monastic and Hospital Care
As covered in the doctor/dentist and beggar entries: **monasteries** often provided real care for the sick poor, sometimes maintaining dedicated infirmaries with monks/nuns possessing genuine herbal and basic medical knowledge (monastic physic gardens, mentioned with apothecaries, fed directly into this). Medieval "**hospitals**" more broadly functioned mostly as charitable shelter-and-basic-care institutions for the poor and traveling pilgrims, run by religious orders, rather than specialized medical treatment centers.
### The Blurry, Sometimes Dangerous Edge: Suspicion of Folk Healing
This is worth addressing directly, since it connects to themes from the broom-maker and gambler entries around folk practice and suspicion:
- **Most folk healing was tolerated and valued**, genuinely essential to community health given the limited reach of formal medicine
- **But the line between accepted folk healing and suspected witchcraft could be genuinely dangerous and inconsistent** — a wise woman's herbal knowledge and charm-work might be viewed as perfectly normal, valued community service for years, yet become the basis for witchcraft accusation if a treatment failed, if she made enemies, or amid broader periods of social anxiety and witch-hunting (which intensified significantly in the late medieval into early modern period specifically)
- **Worth being careful not to overstate this danger as constant or universal** — most folk healers throughout most of the medieval period practiced without persecution, and the most intense witch-hunting periods are more accurately characterized as late medieval/early modern phenomena rather than a constant, defining feature of the entire medieval period
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A village wise-woman/cunning-folk healer NPC whose herbal/charm-based practice has genuine minor magical effect in your setting — grounding a hedge-witch or low-magic healer character in real historical practice rather than needing to invent the role from scratch
- A respected midwife character with real standing and specialized knowledge — a strong, grounded basis for a competent female NPC or PC background, given how genuinely essential and skilled this role was
- The folk-healer/witchcraft-suspicion line as a worldbuilding tension point — a sympathetic, genuinely helpful healer NPC who could become a target for fear/scapegoating if your plot needs a believable, non-cartoonish "witch hunt" storyline
- A pilgrimage to a healing shrine as a quest framework — PCs escorting a sick patron, investigating whether a shrine's healing reputation is genuine miracle or something else (con artistry, or even something stranger), grounded in real medieval treatment-seeking behavior
