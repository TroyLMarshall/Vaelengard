### A Specialized Trade We Flagged in the Carpenter Entry
Back in the carpenter entry, we listed the wheelwright as a distinct specialization within the broader woodworking trade family — focused specifically on making wheels for carts and wagons, a genuinely demanding sub-craft requiring precise curved woodworking and metal tire-fitting. Let's give it the full dedicated treatment it deserves, since wheel-making was genuinely more complex and important than it might initially seem.
### Why Wheel-Making Required Specialization
A wheel isn't simply a circle of wood — it's a precision mechanical component that needs to:
- Bear significant static loads (the weight of the cart and its cargo)
- Withstand dynamic impact loads (hitting ruts, stones, and uneven terrain constantly)
- Rotate reliably for extended periods without failing
- Maintain structural integrity despite the wood's natural tendency to expand, contract, and warp with changing moisture and temperature

Achieving all of this with wood and iron, without modern materials science or precision machinery, required real accumulated craft knowledge that justified dedicated specialization separate from general carpentry.
### The Wheel's Components
Understanding what a medieval wheel actually consisted of is essential to understanding the craft:

**The hub/nave**: the central wooden cylinder through which the axle passed
- Required dense, strong wood resistant to splitting under the stress of the rotating axle — elm was particularly favored for hubs given its interlocking grain structure that resisted splitting remarkably well
- Required a central metal (iron) lining called the **box** to protect the hub's bore from wear as the axle rotated within it — connecting to the blacksmith entry's ironwork
- Required metal straps called **bonds** or **hoops** around the outside to prevent splitting under the lateral stress of spoke insertion

**The spokes**: radiating wooden members connecting hub to rim
- Required strong, straight-grained wood — oak typically favored for its strength-to-weight ratio
- Required precise shaping — tapering from a square-section mortise end (fitting into the hub) to a round-section tenon end (fitting into the rim), a specific shape requiring real skill to produce consistently
- Required precise angular alignment — spokes needed to be set at consistent angles both around the hub and with a slight forward lean (called **dishing**, covered below)

**The felloes/fellies**: the curved wooden segments forming the wheel's rim
- Each felloe spanning roughly two spokes, multiple felloes forming the complete circle
- Required careful selection of appropriately curved timber — ideally wood with a natural curve following the grain, rather than cutting a curve across the grain which would produce a weaker result
- Required precise shaping to fit tightly against neighboring felloes and accept the spoke tenons at exactly the right positions

**The iron tire**: a continuous iron hoop fitted around the outside of the assembled wooden wheel
- Provided wear resistance for the wheel's contact with road surfaces
- Critically, provided the binding tension that held the entire wooden assembly together — the tire fitted hot (allowing the thermally expanded iron to be placed over the wheel) and then quenched with water (causing it to contract and grip the wooden rim with enormous compressive force)
- This shrink-fitting of the iron tire was genuinely the wheelwright's most dramatic and technically demanding operation
### The Dishing — A Crucial Technical Feature
One of the less obvious but genuinely important technical aspects of wheel construction:

**What dishing is**: a properly made wagon wheel wasn't flat — it was slightly dished, like a shallow bowl, with the hub offset toward the outside of the vehicle relative to the rim

**Why it mattered**: a dished wheel was significantly stronger than a flat wheel under the specific loads of vehicle use
- When a loaded wagon leaned or when the wheel hit an obstacle, a flat wheel would receive stress tending to collapse it sideways — a dished wheel's geometry converted this lateral stress into compression in the spokes (wood's strong direction) rather than bending
- Achieving the correct degree of dishing required real geometric understanding and precise execution throughout the construction process
### Materials and Their Selection
**Elm for hubs**: as mentioned, elm's interlocking grain structure made it exceptionally resistant to splitting — a critical property for a component receiving the concentrated stress of spoke insertion and axle load

**Oak for spokes**: strong, stiff, and relatively light — the right combination for an element needing to transmit load between hub and rim without excess weight

**Ash for felloes**: ash's combination of strength and flexibility made it well-suited to the rim segments — able to absorb impact without shattering, an important property for components in constant contact with road surfaces

**Iron for hardware**: as covered in the blacksmith entry, the various iron components (tire, hub box, bonds) required smithing skill — making wheelwrighting a trade requiring close collaboration with or personal competence in basic ironwork, and explaining why wheelwrights often maintained a forge alongside their woodworking shop
### The Assembly Process
**Hub preparation**: boring the central hole precisely, fitting the iron box, and fitting the hub bonds

**Spoke insertion**: driving the precisely shaped spokes into the hub mortises at the correct angles — requiring both accuracy and force

**Felloe fitting**: fitting the curved rim segments onto the spoke tenons, with neighboring felloes' joints between spokes

**Tiring**: the most dramatic operation
- The iron tire was measured precisely against the assembled wheel
- The tire was then heated in a fire until thermally expanded enough to fit over the wheel
- Rapidly placed over the wooden wheel while still hot
- Immediately quenched with water — the sudden contraction gripping the wood with enormous force
- This required fast, coordinated team effort — the tire cooled quickly, and uneven quenching or misalignment could ruin the work
### Guild Structure and Distribution
- In towns with sufficient cart and wagon traffic to support the trade, wheelwrights organized into guilds following the standard pattern
- **Rural distribution**: unlike some specialized trades concentrated in major cities, wheelwrights needed to be relatively widely distributed — every agricultural district needed wheel repair capability, since a broken wheel on a harvest cart could be economically catastrophic
- **Overlap with other trades**: in smaller communities without enough business for a dedicated wheelwright, a skilled carpenter or blacksmith might handle wheel work as part of their broader trade rather than pure specialization
### Economic Significance
- **Essential agricultural infrastructure**: as covered in the farm entry, carts were essential to agricultural operations — harvest transport, market transport, and general farm logistics all depending on functional wheeled vehicles
- **Wheels as genuinely valuable objects**: a properly made wheel represented significant material and labor investment — worth repairing carefully rather than replacing, creating ongoing maintenance and repair work alongside new wheel production
- **Military logistics**: as covered in the militaries entry, military campaigns required wagon transport for supply and equipment — military wheelwrights serving army logistics trains as recognized specialist support roles
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A wheelwright's smithing forge alongside their woodworking shop as a specific, dual-trade workshop detail — a more technically equipped and materially diverse workplace than most purely woodworking operations, reflecting the trade's genuine overlap between craft disciplines
- Wheel failure as a specific, dramatic plot element — a broken wheel at a critical moment (a harvest race against incoming weather, a military supply train that must reach its destination, a noble's carriage on a dangerous mountain road) creating time-pressured urgency that requires either finding a wheelwright or improvising a solution
- The tire-fitting operation as a memorable scene — the hot iron tire, the rapid placement, the hissing quench, and the sudden dramatic contraction creating a vivid, specific craft moment that could serve as atmospheric background or active plot element
- A master wheelwright's geometric knowledge — the precise dishing calculations, the angular spoke alignment, the exact rim circumference measurement — as a specific skill set that translates unexpectedly to other precise geometric challenges, giving a wheelwright NPC or PC background an unexpected intellectual dimension
