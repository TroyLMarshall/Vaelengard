### Water Infrastructure's Most Universal Form
We touched on wells briefly in the town pump entry — noting that dug or bored wells accessing groundwater were the most universal water supply solution across medieval Europe, and promising a dedicated treatment here. Let's give wells their full dedicated coverage, since access to water was genuinely one of the most fundamental practical concerns of medieval daily life.
### Why Wells Mattered So Much
Everything in daily medieval life required water — cooking, brewing (connecting to the bartender and brewer entries), washing, animal care (connecting to the stable entry), and countless craft processes covered throughout this list (tanning, dyeing, ropemaking, pottery, and many others all requiring significant water access). Without reliable water supply, no settlement could function. Wells represented the most widely deployable solution to this universal need, accessible wherever groundwater existed within reasonable digging depth.
### Types of Wells
**Dug wells**: the most common type throughout the medieval period
- Hand-dug shafts descending to the water table, lined with stone, brick, or timber to prevent collapse
- Width sufficient for a person to work inside during construction and periodic maintenance — typically several feet across
- Depth limited by practical digging and lining capability, and by the need to manage water seepage during construction
- Stone or brick lining providing both structural stability and some filtering of soil contamination as water seeped through

**Bored wells**: narrower, deeper wells created using drilling/boring tools rather than hand digging
- Allowing access to deeper, potentially cleaner groundwater sources than hand-dug wells could reach
- Requiring more sophisticated technical capability than simple hand-digging

**Artesian wells**: in specific geological conditions, water under natural pressure in underground aquifers could flow to the surface without pumping when a well was drilled into the pressurized layer — a genuinely valued discovery in appropriate geology, providing self-flowing water supply without lifting effort

**Spring-fed wells**: in some cases, natural springs emerging at or near the surface were improved and controlled by well-like structures — the springhouse entry covering the specific use of spring water for cooling, while here the focus is on spring water as drinking supply
### Physical Construction
**Digging**: excavating down to the water table, a physically demanding, genuinely dangerous operation — cave-in risk during construction being a serious mortality hazard

**Lining**: as the shaft was dug, lining materials were installed to prevent collapse and reduce contamination
- **Stone**: mortared or dry-laid stone lining providing durable, long-lasting well structure — surviving medieval stone-lined wells are among the more commonly found archaeological features
- **Brick**: in regions with established brick production (connecting to the stoneworker entry's brief discussion of brick), fired brick provided similar structural function
- **Timber**: wooden lining (sometimes using a hollowed log or constructed timber framework) for shallower or less permanent wells — less durable than stone but requiring less specialized material and skill

**The wellhead**: the structure at ground level surrounding the well opening
- **Curb or coping**: a stone, brick, or timber ring at ground level preventing surface water, animal waste, and other contaminants from running directly into the well — a genuine, recognized contamination prevention measure
- **Cover**: a wooden cover or roof protecting the well from rain, debris, and deliberate contamination
- **Windlass or pulley**: mechanical assistance for raising the water bucket — a simple but genuinely useful mechanical advantage device, connecting to the broader discussion of medieval mechanical systems in the mills and construction entries

**The bucket and rope**: the actual water-retrieval mechanism
- Hemp rope (connecting to the ropemaker entry) or leather rope used to lower and raise the bucket
- Wooden or leather buckets suited to repeated immersion
- Rope wear and bucket maintenance being ongoing operational concerns
### Who Had Wells
**Private household wells**: where land and water table depth permitted, wealthy households maintained their own private wells — a genuine convenience advantage compared to dependence on shared facilities
- As covered in the private homes entry, wealthy urban merchants and noble households were most likely to have private well access
- Castle infrastructure (connecting to the guard and noble entries) always included private well access within the defensive perimeter — a genuine military necessity, since a castle besieged without internal water supply faced rapid surrender regardless of food reserves

**Shared community wells**: the most common arrangement for most of the population
- A single well serving multiple households in a village or urban neighborhood
- Common wells as genuinely communal infrastructure, often maintained through collective community effort or civic administration
- Sometimes specifically associated with a particular public space — the village green, the market square, or a public courtyard

**Monastic wells**: as covered in the monastery entry, monasteries maintained sophisticated internal water infrastructure — wells, piped water systems, and other water management forming part of the self-sufficient monastic complex

**Urban public wells**: in larger towns, specific public wells at recognized locations served as water supply points for the surrounding neighborhood
- Civic maintenance responsibility connecting to the town hall and government official entries' discussion of civic infrastructure management
### Water Quality Concerns
As touched on in the town pump and public baths entries, water quality was a genuine concern:

**Contamination sources**: as urban density increased and waste disposal infrastructure remained primitive (as covered in the outhouses entry), groundwater contamination became increasingly serious
- **Cesspit proximity**: as covered in the outhouses entry, cesspits dug too close to wells allowed sewage contamination to percolate through soil into groundwater — a connection medieval people recognized empirically without germ theory understanding
- **Surface runoff**: animal waste from the substantial livestock presence in even urban settings, rainwater carrying street contamination, and various craft-process waste (tanning, dyeing) all contributed to surface contamination that could enter wells

**Recognition without understanding**: medieval people recognized that certain wells produced illness-associated water without understanding the bacteriological mechanism
- **Well closure**: specific wells known to produce illness were sometimes closed by civic authorities — empirical public health intervention even without theoretical underpinning
- **Preference for spring water**: as covered in the town pump entry, spring water from elevated sources outside urban contamination zones was recognized as superior — the willingness to invest in piped spring water systems reflecting this quality awareness

**The ale/water safety comparison**: as covered in the bartender entry, ale was often safer than available water sources — the brewing process's heating killing pathogens, and the resulting mild alcohol content providing additional antimicrobial effect. This genuine quality consideration drove some of the widespread daily ale consumption that modern observers find remarkable.
### Sacred and Symbolic Dimensions
Wells carried significant symbolic and sometimes explicitly sacred meaning in medieval culture:

**Holy wells**: as touched on in the healers entry, numerous wells across medieval Europe (particularly strong tradition in Ireland, Wales, and Britain) acquired reputations for healing properties — often Christianized versions of pre-Christian sacred spring traditions, with specific saints associated with specific wells
- Pilgrimage to healing wells as a recognized religious practice, connecting to the broader pilgrimage tradition covered throughout this list
- Votive offerings left at well sites — a practice with ancient roots continuing through the medieval period

**Wishing wells**: the tradition of dropping coins or offerings into wells while making a wish represents a folk religious practice with deep roots — the well as a liminal point between the surface world and the underground, appropriate for communication with supernatural forces

**Well dedication to saints**: many medieval wells were formally dedicated to specific saints, with the saint's protective influence believed to ensure the water's quality and sometimes healing properties
### Social Dimensions of the Public Well
The public well as a community gathering point:

**Daily social interaction**: drawing water was a daily necessity — everyone in a community visited the well regularly, making it a natural site for information exchange, social contact, and community interaction
- Connecting to the broader discussion throughout this list of how specific locations (markets, taverns, churches) served as community hubs beyond their primary functional purpose

**Women's social space**: water-drawing was typically women's work in many medieval communities — making the public well a specifically female social space where women gathered, talked, and exchanged information in ways that paralleled (in a more everyday register) the male-dominated tavern social space covered in that entry

**Disputes over access**: in communities where a single well served many households, access timing, queue-jumping, and contamination responsibility could generate real disputes — the kind of small-scale but genuinely important community conflicts that manorial courts (covered in the government official and judge entries) sometimes had to adjudicate
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A holy well with genuine healing properties as a worldbuilding location — grounded in the real medieval tradition but with actual magical effect in your setting, perhaps requiring specific ritual approach, limited use, or specific conditions to activate its beneficial properties
- Well contamination as a mystery hook — illness spreading through a community connected to a specific contaminated well, with the investigation requiring understanding both the contamination source and the well's connection to surrounding groundwater and waste disposal infrastructure
- A castle well as a siege warfare element — the specific military significance of internal water supply making the well a genuine tactical objective, whether in defending (protecting the well from contamination attack) or attacking (threatening or actually contaminating the water supply)
- A public well's social function as a scene-setting tool — a daily gathering point where PCs can encounter a cross-section of a community, overhear conversations, and pick up local information in a way that feels natural and grounded rather than contrived
