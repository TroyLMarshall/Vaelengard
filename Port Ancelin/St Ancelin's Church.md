**St Ancelin's Church** stands near the center of town, between Celyn Street and the wool market — close enough to the trade that its bells mark the rhythm of the market days as much as the holy ones.
## **Origins & the Tithe**
The church takes its name not from a saint, but from the founder himself — an unusual honor that speaks to how thoroughly the de Varnay legacy shaped the town's identity. Its construction was funded almost entirely by the **wool tithe**, a portion of every fleece sold at La Foire set aside for the church, collected and recorded alongside the regular market weighing. Over generations, the steady flow of wool wealth let the parish build something far grander than a town of its size would typically support.
## **Architecture**
The result is a church that looks almost out of proportion to Port Ancelin itself — a soaring spire visible from well out at sea, said to double as an informal landmark for ships navigating into the harbour. The nave is lined with stained glass funded by individual wool families, each window a small act of civic competition: the de Varnays, naturally, claim the grandest window above the altar, but several wealthy flockmaster families have purchased windows of their own over the years, turning the church into an unofficial ledger of the town's mercantile hierarchy.
## **The Wool Connection, Literally**
A few details root the building unmistakably in its trade:
- The weathervane atop the spire is shaped like a ram rather than the usual cockerel.
- Carved into the stone above the main door is a relief of sheep being sheared — a strange but beloved detail, since most churches reserve their carved tympanum for biblical scenes.
- The church bell, cast from melted-down old weighing scales according to local legend, is known as **"the Fleece Bell."**
## **Role in Town Life**
Beyond worship, the church functions as something close to a record-keeper for the wool trade — births, deaths, and marriages are recorded here, but so too are major trade disputes and guild oaths, sworn on the altar steps when a matter is considered serious enough. The current parish priest, by long tradition, sits as a neutral witness during major wool guild proceedings, since he draws his living from the tithe but has no stake in any single merchant's fortune.
## **A Quiet Tension**
Not everyone loves the arrangement. Some clergy from outside Port Ancelin have murmured that a church funded by trade tithe, bearing a founder's name, and decorated with merchant heraldry blurs the line between devotion and commerce a little too comfortably. Locals shrug this off — to them, the wool tithe isn't a compromise of faith, it's simply how Port Ancelin has always done things.
