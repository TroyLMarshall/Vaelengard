### Far More Than Haircuts
This is one of the more surprising medieval trades for modern readers: the barber wasn't just a hair-and-beard specialist — they were a genuine **medical practitioner**, performing surgery, dentistry, and bloodletting alongside grooming services. The full historical term, **barber-surgeon**, captures this dual role, and it persisted as the standard arrangement for most of the medieval period and well beyond.
### Why Barbers Did Surgery
This connection traces back to the Church's discomfort with clergy performing bloody medical procedures. Early medieval medicine was substantially practiced by monks, but a series of Church councils (notably restricting clergy from surgery and bloodletting by the 12th-13th centuries) pushed this "manual," blood-involving work out of monastic hands. Barbers — already skilled with sharp blades and comfortable with the body — stepped into the gap, since university-trained physicians considered themselves above hands-on cutting work, preferring diagnosis and theory (often without ever touching the patient).

This created a three-tier medical hierarchy:
- **Physicians**: university-educated, diagnosed using humoral theory, high status, rarely did hands-on procedures
- **Barber-surgeons**: trade-trained, did the actual cutting, bleeding, and manual treatment
- **Apothecaries**: prepared and sold remedies (as covered earlier)
### What a Barber-Surgeon Actually Did
**Grooming services** (the part that survived into the modern "barber"):
- Haircutting and beard trimming/shaving
- Tooth-pulling — yes, dentistry fell under the barber's purview, with no anesthesia beyond perhaps alcohol or numbing herbs

**Bloodletting**: probably the single most common medical procedure of the era, based on humoral theory — believing many illnesses stemmed from an excess or imbalance of blood (one of the four humors). Methods included:
- **Venesection**: opening a vein, usually at the arm, to draw blood
- **Cupping**: heated cups applied to skin to draw blood to the surface, sometimes combined with small cuts
- **Leeches**: applying live leeches to draw blood from specific areas

**Minor surgery**:
- Setting broken bones
- Treating wounds, including battlefield injuries — barber-surgeons often accompanied armies
- Lancing boils and abscesses
- Amputations, in serious cases — again, without anesthesia beyond herbal preparations and whatever the patient could bear
- Cauterization (using heat to seal wounds or stop bleeding)

**Other procedures**:
- Enemas
- Basic wound dressing and care
- Some basic dental work beyond pulling teeth
### The Red and White Pole
The classic **barber pole** (red and white, sometimes with blue added later in some traditions) has a direct origin in this medical history: red represented blood, white represented the bandages used to wrap the arm during bloodletting, and the pole itself is said to represent the staff patients gripped during the procedure. It's a surviving visual relic of a trade most people now only associate with haircuts.
### Training and Guild Structure
Barber-surgeons trained through standard **apprenticeship**, typically under a master barber, learning both the grooming and surgical sides of the trade together — there wasn't a meaningful separation in training even though the skills seem worlds apart to modern eyes.

Guild structures varied by region and time:
- In England, the **Worshipful Company of Barbers** existed from the medieval period, and barbers and surgeons formed a combined guild — the **Company of Barber-Surgeons** — officially merged in 1540 (just past the medieval period, but the professional overlap long predates the formal merger).
- Some cities eventually developed separate, more elite surgeon guilds distinct from barbers as surgery slowly gained more professional respect, but this separation accelerated mostly in the early modern period rather than fully completing within medieval times.
### Tools of the Trade
- **Razors**: for shaving and minor cutting procedures
- **Lancets**: small blades specifically for bloodletting incisions
- **Bleeding bowls**: special bowls, sometimes with measurement markings, to catch and measure drawn blood
- **Cupping vessels**: glass or metal cups for the cupping technique
- **Forceps and dental tools**: for tooth extraction
- **Cautery irons**: heated for cauterization
- **Basic surgical knives and saws**: for more invasive procedures
### Status and Reputation
Barber-surgeons occupied a genuinely awkward social position: essential and widely used (far more accessible and affordable than a university physician for most people), but looked down upon by the physician class as manual laborers rather than learned men. This tension — practical, hands-on medical skill versus theoretical, university-sanctioned medical authority — created ongoing professional friction that, frankly, echoes in some forms even into modern medical hierarchies (surgeons vs. physicians retained some vestige of this status distinction for a surprisingly long time).

Despite the lower formal status, a skilled barber-surgeon could become a trusted, respected figure in a community or even a noble household, and military barber-surgeons traveling with armies gained real practical experience treating traumatic injuries that often exceeded what a physician would ever encounter directly.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A barber-surgeon who's secretly far more skilled than the local physicians, creating class tension when nobles are forced to rely on "lowly" hands-on skill during an emergency
- Bloodletting with a magical or alchemical twist — perhaps draining a corrupted or cursed humor, with the barber unknowingly performing actual medicine in a magical sense
- A barber-surgeon character as a battlefield medic NPC, gritty and practical compared to a more theoretical wizard-healer
- The barber pole's red/white symbolism repurposed for a guild that does something a bit stranger — bloodletting as part of a ritual economy, for instance
