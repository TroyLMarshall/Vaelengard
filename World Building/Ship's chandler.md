### The Craft and Its Structure
A **ship's chandler** supplied vessels with the enormous range of goods and equipment needed to actually put to sea — a trade that existed less as a single specialized craft and more as a **commercial hub function**, aggregating and reselling goods from many other trades specifically packaged for maritime need. The core "chandler" concept traces back to **candle-selling** (from the French "chandelle"), and general chandlers dealing in candles, soap, and household provisions existed as an ordinary land-based trade — but in port towns, the role naturally specialized toward **outfitting ships**, since a busy harbor created constant, concentrated demand for exactly the kind of bulk provisioning and equipment resupply a chandler's business model was built around.

**What a chandler actually supplied** — Rope and cordage (rigging wore out constantly and needed regular replacement — connecting directly to the rope-maker's trade), sailcloth and canvas, pitch and tar (for waterproofing hull seams and rigging — a genuinely essential, foul-smelling maritime material), candles and lanterns (ships needed reliable light for night navigation and below-decks work), barrels and casks (for water, ale, salted meat, and other stores — connecting to the cooper's trade), salted and preserved food (connecting directly to the salt-maker and salt-merchant trades covered earlier — a ship provisioning for a long voyage needed serious quantities of preserved food that wouldn't spoil at sea), navigational instruments and charts where available, and general hardware — nails, fittings, tools, replacement parts for anything likely to break or wear during a voyage. This made the chandler a genuine **nexus point pulling together output from many other trades** — rope-makers, coopers, salt-merchants, candle-makers, blacksmiths — repackaged and resold specifically for the maritime market, similar in economic function to how a stationer aggregated the book trade's various specialists for university customers.

**Location and business model** — Chandlers operated **directly at or very near the harbor**, since their entire business depended on immediate access to arriving and departing ships needing quick resupply — a ship in port for only a few days needed to provision fast, making proximity and reliable stock genuinely critical to a chandler's success. A chandler's shop or warehouse functioned as a real one-stop resupply point, saving a ship's captain or purser from having to separately deal with a dozen different specialist suppliers scattered across town while their vessel sat idle burning time and money in port.

**Credit and trust** — Provisioning a ship for a real voyage represented substantial expense, and ships often needed to resupply **before** their cargo had sold and generated the income to pay for it — meaning chandlers, like several merchant trades already covered, frequently extended **credit**, effectively financing a portion of a voyage's costs against expected returns. This gave established, trusted chandlers real financial relationships with ship-owners and captains, sometimes over years of repeat business, and correspondingly real risk if a voyage was lost at sea (taking the chandler's extended credit down with it) or if a captain simply defaulted after safely returning to a different port.

**Quality and reputation stakes** — A chandler's reputation for supplying **genuinely sound goods** — properly cured rope that wouldn't fail under strain, correctly sealed casks that wouldn't spoil their contents on a long voyage, honest measure rather than shorted quantities — mattered enormously, since faulty supplies discovered mid-voyage could genuinely endanger a ship and crew far from any chance to correct the problem. This created real incentive for honest dealing, but also real temptation and documented risk of **corner-cutting fraud** — supplying inferior rope, watered provisions, or short-weighted stores to desperate or unwary buyers, precisely the kind of adulteration/fraud pattern seen already in the pewterer, currier, and miller trades, but with genuinely higher, life-threatening stakes given how far from help a discovered fraud might be found.

**Social and economic position** — Chandlers occupied a comfortable, often prosperous middling-to-good position in a busy port town — commercially savvy, well-connected across multiple other trades, essential to the port's whole maritime economy functioning smoothly, without necessarily holding the guild-protected craft status of the specialist trades whose goods they resold. Successful chandlers in major ports could become genuinely wealthy and locally influential, given how thoroughly the whole shipping economy depended on their function.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Persuasion, Investigation (or Insight, for judging trade partners and creditworthiness)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Navigator's Tools, or Vehicles (Water)
- **Feat:** Skilled or Lucky
- **Equipment:** A merchant's ledger tracking credit extended to various captains, a sample case of maritime goods (rope, pitch, candles), a small warehouse key, fine but practical clothes, 20 gp

This background suits a **Bard or Rogue** with strong maritime and mercantile flavor — well-connected across a harbor town's whole trade network, comfortable extending and calling in favors/credit, plausible reason to know captains, sailors, and every other harbor-adjacent trade. It also works for an **Artificer** interested in ship's equipment and practical maritime technology, or a character whose family's chandlery went under from a lost voyage's bad debt, now adventuring to recover what was lost.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Trusted Chandler** — A well-established supplier known across the port for honest goods and fair credit terms, personally connected to most of the harbor's captains and merchants — a great networking/information-hub NPC.
- **The Overextended Creditor** — Has financed too many voyages on credit and is genuinely at risk of ruin if even one or two ships fail to return — desperate, possibly willing to cut corners or take real risks to recover.
- **The Corner-Cutter** — Quietly supplying inferior goods (rotten rope, short-weighted provisions) to save costs, creating real, deadly risk for whichever ship discovers the fraud too late — echoes the miller/pewterer adulteration pattern with mortal stakes.
- **The Chandler's Widow** — Kept the family business running after a husband's death or lost-at-sea disappearance, fighting for respect and continued trust from captains skeptical of dealing with a woman-run chandlery.
- **The Well-Connected Fixer** — Uses chandlery business as cover or foundation for a broader information/smuggling network, since their legitimate trade already requires knowing every ship's business, cargo, and schedule.
### Game Hooks
- **The Faulty Supply** — A ship (perhaps one the party's aboard or invested in) suffers a genuine crisis at sea traceable to bad chandlery goods — rotten rope failing, spoiled provisions — and the party has to trace the fraud back to its source, possibly before the same chandler outfits another doomed voyage.
- **The Called-In Debt** — A chandler who extended the party (or an ally) credit for supplies now needs that debt repaid urgently, for reasons that pull the party into the chandler's own financial troubles.
- **Provisioning Under Pressure** — The party needs to provision a ship fast, cheap, or discreetly (evading a blockade, a monopoly, hostile attention) and has to navigate the practical, textured logistics of real maritime resupply.
- **The Missing Captain's Debt** — A captain who owed a chandler significant credit has vanished or died at sea, and the party gets pulled into sorting out the tangled financial and legal aftermath — who's owed what, and by whom.
- **Smuggler's Cover** — The party discovers a chandlery is being used as cover for moving contraband alongside legitimate ship's stores — investigation, infiltration, or a moral question about whether to expose a otherwise well-liked local figure.
