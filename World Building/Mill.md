### Genuine Mechanical Sophistication and Real Economic/Social Significance
We've referenced mills throughout this list — the farm entry mentioned them as significant capital investment and connected them to the lord's banal rights system, the carpenter entry noted their sophisticated mechanical demands — so let's give mills their full dedicated treatment, since they represent some of medieval Europe's most significant applied mechanical technology and a genuinely important point of economic and social friction.
### Watermills — The Dominant Type
- **Genuine ancient technology, refined through the medieval period**: watermills existed since antiquity, but medieval Europe saw substantial expansion and refinement of mill technology and use
- **Mechanism**: a water wheel (turned by flowing or falling water) drove a system of gears connecting to **millstones** — large, heavy paired stones, the upper one rotating against the fixed lower stone, grinding grain caught between them into flour
- **Wheel types**: undershot wheels (water flowing beneath, pushing paddles) were simpler but less efficient than overshot wheels (water falling onto the wheel from above, using gravity more effectively) — site selection and available water flow determined which design suited a particular location
- **Construction demands**: building a functional, durable mill required real combined carpentry and engineering knowledge — the carpenter entry's mention of mills as requiring "sophisticated carpentry for both structure and mechanism" reflects genuine technical complexity beyond simple building construction
### Windmills — A Later Medieval Innovation
- **Genuinely significant medieval-period innovation**: unlike watermills' ancient origins, windmills are substantially a **medieval European development**, becoming increasingly widespread from roughly the 12th century onward
- **Post mills**: the earliest common European windmill design — the entire mill structure pivoted on a central post, allowing the whole building to be turned to face the prevailing wind direction, a clever if labor-intensive solution
- **Particularly significant in flatter, low-lying regions**: areas without strong, reliable water flow (the Netherlands and other flat coastal/lowland regions particularly) found windmills especially valuable, eventually becoming strongly associated with Dutch landscape and culture specifically, including their famous later use in land drainage/water management beyond just grinding grain
### What Mills Actually Ground
- **Grain into flour**: the primary function, processing wheat, rye, barley, and other grains covered extensively in the farm and baker entries — connecting directly to the entire bread-production chain that began this whole list
- **Other applications**: beyond grain, mill mechanism principles were sometimes adapted for other industrial purposes — fulling mills (mechanically pounding/thickening woven wool cloth, connecting to the dyer entry's broader textile production chain) and various other applications as mechanical milling technology found new uses through the medieval period
### The Lord's Monopoly — Banal Rights Revisited
As established in the baker entry's discussion of oven monopolies, mills operated under a very similar system:
- **Exclusive milling rights**: lords frequently held legal monopoly over milling within their manor, requiring tenants to bring their grain specifically to the lord's mill (rather than grinding it themselves with a simple hand quern, or using another mill) and pay a fee — typically a portion of the flour or grain itself
- **Genuine peasant resentment**: this represented a real, ongoing source of friction — peasants paying a toll simply to process their own grown grain, with the lord profiting from infrastructure peasants had little choice but to use
- **Hand mills/querns prohibited or restricted**: in places with strict enforcement, lords sometimes specifically banned or restricted tenants from using simple hand-querns (small hand-operated grinding stones) at home, specifically to force reliance on the lord's mill and its associated fees — a detail that vividly illustrates how seriously this monopoly right was protected and enforced
- **Significant capital investment justifying the monopoly (from the lord's perspective)**: building and maintaining a mill required real capital and ongoing upkeep, giving lords genuine economic justification (alongside the more purely extractive motivation) for wanting to recoup that investment through guaranteed, exclusive use
### The Miller
- **A position of genuine local economic significance**: the miller (often operating the mill on behalf of the lord, sometimes leasing the mill rights for an agreed payment) held real local importance, given that virtually the entire community depended on the mill to process their grain into usable flour
- **Mixed reputation**: millers occupied a genuinely ambivalent position in medieval folk culture and literature — necessary and economically significant, but also frequently suspected of **cheating customers** by skimming flour or taking more than the legitimate fee during processing, a suspicion vivid enough to appear repeatedly in period literature (Chaucer's Miller in _The Canterbury Tales_ reflects exactly this kind of cultural stereotype) — whether this reputation reflected genuine widespread dishonesty or simply the structural tension of operating an unavoidable monopoly service is a matter of historical interpretation, but the _cultural_ suspicion was real and persistent
- **Relative prosperity**: despite the mixed reputation, successful millers could achieve genuine local prosperity, given the mill's essential, continuous demand
### Mill Maintenance and Technical Demands
- **Millstone dressing**: millstones required periodic **dressing** — re-cutting the grinding grooves/pattern on the stone surface as it wore smooth from use, a specialized skill in its own right
- **Mechanism upkeep**: the wooden gearing and structural elements required ongoing carpentry maintenance, connecting to the carpenter entry's broader discussion
- **Water management**: watermills required management of mill ponds, leats (channels directing water to the wheel), and sluices controlling water flow — genuine hydraulic engineering knowledge, even without modern theoretical understanding behind it
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- The mill monopoly/banal rights tension as a grounded, low-stakes peasant-grievance plot hook — similar to the baker's oven-rights tension suggested earlier, perhaps escalating into a small local conflict PCs might mediate, exploit, or get drawn into
- A miller NPC playing against or into the classic dishonest-miller stereotype — either a refreshingly honest miller fighting against unfair local suspicion, or one whose cheating becomes a genuine, grounded mystery/investigation hook
- A windmill or watermill as a distinctive, recognizable settlement landmark — useful as a meeting point, defensible structure, or simply atmospheric detail grounding a rural settlement in genuine medieval technology
- A mill's mechanism repurposed or sabotaged for non-grain purposes — perhaps a mill secretly powers something else (a hidden workshop, a smuggling operation's pump/grinding needs), playing on the genuine real-world adaptability of milling mechanism technology
