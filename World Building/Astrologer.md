### A Respected, Even Essential, Profession
Unlike alchemy (which carried more mixed associations) or some other medieval trades, astrology occupied a genuinely prestigious and intellectually mainstream position for much of the medieval period. It was considered a legitimate branch of natural philosophy, taught at universities, and practiced by — or consulted by — popes, kings, and physicians alike. The tension between astrology and Christian doctrine existed, but it was managed rather than resolved through outright rejection (more on that below).
### What an Astrologer Actually Did
**Medical astrology** was probably the most common practical application. Physicians were expected to understand astrology as part of their formal training — the position of the moon and planets was believed to affect bodily humors, so:
- Timing bloodletting or surgery according to the moon's position in the zodiac (certain signs were considered dangerous for treating corresponding body parts — a system sometimes called "zodiac man")
- Diagnosing illness partly through casting a chart for when the patient fell ill or first consulted the physician
- Predicting the course of a disease, including crisis points, based on planetary movements

**Casting nativities (birth charts)**: determining a person's character, fortunes, and likely life events based on planetary positions at birth — done for nobility and royalty especially, since a future ruler's chart was considered a matter of political importance.

**Electional astrology**: choosing auspicious times for important actions — when to start a journey, sign a treaty, crown a king, lay a foundation stone, or get married. Major political and military decisions were sometimes scheduled around astrological timing.

**Judicial/interrogational astrology**: answering specific questions ("will this venture succeed?" "where is my lost property?") by casting a chart for the moment the question was asked.

**Weather and agricultural prediction**: forecasting weather patterns, harvests, and even broader events like plagues or famines based on planetary configurations — almanacs combining astrology with practical calendar information were widely produced.

**Political and astrological prognostication**: predicting the fates of kingdoms, the outcomes of wars, or the rise and fall of rulers — this could be politically sensitive, even dangerous, depending on what you predicted and for whom.
### Tools and Techniques
- **Astrolabes**: the signature instrument — a sophisticated device for measuring the position of celestial bodies and performing astronomical calculations, often beautifully engineered brass instruments
- **Ephemerides and astronomical tables**: pre-calculated tables of planetary positions (the _Alfonsine Tables_, commissioned by Alfonso X of Castile, were hugely influential from the 13th century onward)
- **Horoscope charts**: divided into the twelve houses, mapping planetary positions at a given moment
- **Books of astrological theory**: many translated or transmitted through Arabic sources, since Islamic astronomers had preserved and advanced on Greek astrological/astronomical knowledge during Europe's earlier medieval period
### Training and Status
Astrology was taught as part of the **quadrivium** (arithmetic, geometry, music, astronomy) at universities, with astrology often inseparable from astronomy as a discipline — the distinction we draw today between "real astronomy" and "astrology" wasn't nearly as sharp then. A physician's medical training included substantial astrological education, and many of the era's most sophisticated astrologers were also serious astronomers, mathematicians, or physicians.

Court astrologers held genuinely influential positions, advising rulers on timing for coronations, battles, and treaties. Some, like **Michael Scot** (advisor to Emperor Frederick II) or, later, figures associated with various royal courts, became well-known, sometimes acquiring a slightly sinister mystique alongside their genuine erudition.
### The Theological Tension
The Catholic Church had a complicated relationship with astrology:
- **Natural astrology** (general influences of planets on weather, bodies, agriculture) was largely accepted as compatible with a divinely ordered universe — God set the heavens in motion, and their influence on earthly matters was just part of natural causation.
- **Judicial astrology** (predicting specific human choices or events, especially anything implying fate overriding free will) was more contested, since it bordered on denying free will and divine providence.
- Various theologians, including **Thomas Aquinas**, tried to draw careful lines — planets could _incline_ human behavior without _determining_ it, preserving free will while still allowing astrology some legitimacy.
- Despite periodic condemnations and suspicion (astrology could shade into forbidden divination), it never received the kind of sustained, severe persecution that, say, witchcraft accusations did. Most working astrologers were not in serious danger, particularly if they served powerful patrons.
### Daily Practice
A working astrologer's day might involve:
- Consulting with patients alongside or in place of a physician
- Calculating charts using astronomical tables (laborious mathematical work before any timesaving tools existed)
- Writing or updating almanacs for the coming year
- Meeting with patrons or clients seeking guidance on timing decisions
- Studying and copying astrological/astronomical texts, often in Latin translations from Arabic source material
### Worldbuilding Angles
#### Historical Grounding (real medieval practices to reskin)
- **Astrology and astronomy were the same discipline** – No separation existed; the same scholar tracked planetary movements for both navigation/calendars and horoscopes. Your setting's astrologers could plausibly double as scholars, calendar-keepers, or advisors on "when to plant crops."
- **Medicine ran on the stars** – Physicians consulted astrological charts before bloodletting or surgery (certain zodiac signs governed certain body parts). An astrologer and a physician might work as a team, or be the same person—great basis for a "sky-doctor" NPC archetype.
- **Court astrologers** – Kings and nobles retained astrologers to pick auspicious dates for battles, weddings, coronations, and treaties. Real political decisions were delayed or accelerated based on planetary alignment—a strong lever for political intrigue in your setting.
- **University subject** – Astrology was taught alongside mathematics and medicine at universities; it was intellectually respectable, not fringe. This lets you frame astrologers as credentialed scholars rather than back-alley fortune tellers.
- **Church tension** – The church was uneasy about astrology (it implied fate could override free will and divine will), leading to periodic condemnation even while popes and bishops privately consulted astrologers themselves. Built-in hypocrisy/political hook.
- **Tools of the trade** – Astrolabes, armillary spheres, ephemeris tables (books of precalculated planetary positions), and hand-copied star charts—expensive, precise instruments that mark an astrologer as learned and well-funded.
#### Fantasy Twists to Layer On
- **Stars that are actually real** – In a fantasy cosmology, constellations might be literal beings, sleeping gods, or portals to other planes—astrology isn't superstition, it's applied cosmology, and a skilled astrologer is reading something _true_.
- **Planar alignment magic** – Certain rituals or magic items only function during specific celestial alignments (a once-a-century conjunction unlocks a vault, a comet's passage weakens the veil to the Shadowfell)—astrologers become essential timekeepers for high-stakes magic.
- **Prophetic bloodlines** – Some astrologers don't calculate, they _see_—a hereditary or fey-granted gift that manifests as visions tied to the stars, blurring astrologer and oracle/seer.
- **Star-touched curses/blessings** – Being born under a particular alignment might grant (or curse) a person with a trait—a whole nobility obsessed with "correcting" or hiding a child's ill-omened birth chart.
- **Rival cosmologies** – Different cultures/races read the sky differently (dwarves track deep-cave mineral cycles instead of stars, elves follow a moon-based system tied to the Feywild, an orc culture reads omens in meteor showers)—creates natural friction when their predictions conflict.
- **The astrologer-wizard overlap** – Divination wizards or diviner-type spellcasters might apprentice under astrologers first, treating star-charting as the "entry level" version of true divination magic.
#### Social & Political Hooks
- **The wrong prediction** – An astrologer who advised a lord to attack (or not attack) based on a chart, and was wrong, could be executed, exiled, or quietly discredited—a great backstory for a disgraced NPC.
- **Competing court astrologers** – Two astrologers serving rival factions publicly contradict each other's predictions, and the populace/nobility must choose who to believe—information warfare via star charts.
- **Astrology as calendar control** – Whoever sets the "correct" auspicious dates for festivals, harvests, and holy days holds subtle power over the rhythm of daily life—a guild or church monopoly on this could be a slow-burn power struggle.
- **Marriage and birth charts** – Noble marriages and heirs' birth times are recorded and consulted obsessively; a forged or altered birth chart could be the linchpin of an inheritance dispute or hidden identity plot.
- **Heresy accusations** – An astrologer who gets too accurate, too fast, or too politically inconvenient might be accused of witchcraft or demon-consultation by rivals—even if their methods are purely "scientific" by in-world standards.
#### Sensory/Flavor Details
- Towers or rooftop observatories cluttered with brass instruments, star charts pinned to every wall, ink-stained star tables in careful spiraling script.
- The astrologer's own eyes strained and slightly unfocused from years of squinting at faint lights and fine calculations.
- A constant background tension between what the stars "say" and what the patron wants to hear—astrologers often hedge predictions in deliberately vague language (a very real historical practice worth keeping).
- Personal ephemeris books passed down or stolen, containing years of painstaking calculations that would take a rival astrologer just as long to redo.
- Star-reading done with ritual solemnity—candles snuffed, silence enforced, sometimes incense or chant involved even in "secular" astrology, blurring the line with religious rite.
