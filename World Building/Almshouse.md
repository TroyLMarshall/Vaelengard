### The Institution and Its Structure
An **almshouse** was a charitable residence providing shelter, basic sustenance, and care for the poor, elderly, sick, or otherwise destitute members of a community — a genuinely widespread, real medieval institution (unlike the orphanage, this one squarely belongs to the period) reflecting the era's understanding of charity as a **religious obligation and a mark of civic and personal virtue**, not a modern welfare-state concept. Almshouses ranged enormously in scale and sophistication, from a wealthy individual's small endowed foundation housing a handful of residents to substantial civic or monastic institutions caring for dozens.

**Religious foundation and motivation** — The overwhelming majority of almshouses were founded and funded through **religious charitable giving** — a wealthy merchant, noble, guild, or churchman endowing an almshouse (often in their will, or during their lifetime as a mark of piety) as a genuine act of Christian charity, frequently with an explicit **spiritual transaction** built directly into the arrangement: residents of the almshouse were typically expected to **pray regularly for the soul of the founder** (and often named specific deceased family members too), reflecting the medieval belief that prayers offered by the poor and needy carried particular spiritual weight, and that supporting such prayer was a genuine investment in the founder's own soul's passage through purgatory. This is a wonderfully concrete, specific piece of medieval religious economics — charity wasn't understood as a one-way gift so much as a **spiritual exchange**, the poor residents' prayers functioning as real payment flowing back to their benefactor.

**Who ran them and who lived there** — Almshouses were typically overseen by a **warden or master** (sometimes a cleric, sometimes a lay administrator answerable to the founding institution — a guild, cathedral chapter, or the founder's own family/estate), managing admission, daily routine, and the endowment's finances. Residents were usually the **"deserving poor"** by the standards of the time — typically the elderly, infirm, or those who had fallen into poverty through misfortune rather than (in period judgment) personal failing, sometimes with specific preference given to former guild members (a guild's almshouse caring specifically for its own aged or incapacitated former members and their widows), former servants of a particular noble household, or residents of a specific parish or town, reflecting the almshouse's often quite localized and particular charitable identity rather than open, universal charity.

**Daily life and structure** — Residents typically lived in **small individual or shared rooms** within a purpose-built or converted building, received basic food and sometimes clothing from the foundation's endowment, and were often expected to follow a **structured daily routine** including regular attendance at prayer or chapel services — the almshouse functioning almost like a small, secular-adjacent quasi-religious community, with real rules and expectations attached to residency rather than simple, unconditional shelter. Some almshouses attached to or maintained a small **chapel** specifically for this purpose, echoing the same "charitable religious infrastructure with an attached devotional space" pattern seen in bridge chapels and beacon-keeper monasteries.

**Guild and civic almshouses** — Guilds frequently maintained almshouses specifically for their own aged, infirm, or widowed members and dependents, treating this as a genuine extension of guild fraternal obligation (echoing the same impulse behind guild-supported orphan care) — a guild member's lifetime of dues and membership carried the real, tangible benefit of a dignified old age within the guild's own charitable provision, rather than destitution. Towns and cities also maintained or supported civic almshouses as a mark of communal responsibility and civic pride, sometimes funded through a combination of individual bequests, guild contributions, and municipal support.

**Scale and famous examples** — Almshouse foundations ranged from tiny, single-benefactor establishments housing just a few residents to substantial institutions — England alone developed a rich tradition of almshouse foundations across the medieval and early-modern period, many surviving (in much-modified form) as genuine historical charitable institutions into the present day, a real testament to how seriously and durably this specific form of charitable endowment was taken.

**Social position and stigma** — Unlike some of the more marginalized trades covered in this list, almshouse residency carried **relatively little shame** by period standards — falling into poverty through age, infirmity, or misfortune (rather than perceived moral failing like idleness or vice) was broadly understood as a legitimate, sympathetic condition, and receiving structured charitable support in a respectable, religiously-sanctioned institution was a real, dignified outcome compared to begging or destitution. That said, real distinctions between the "deserving" and "undeserving" poor did shape who received this kind of institutional charity versus who was turned away or treated with real suspicion and hostility.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
Almshouses work best as a **PC's origin circumstance** (raised or sheltered there) rather than a craft-trade background, similar to the orphanage entry:
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Religion, Insight (a childhood or old age spent in close religious routine and observation of a small, structured community)
- **Feat:** Healer or Inspiring Leader
- **Equipment:** A small prayer book, simple clothes, a token or badge marking the founding guild/institution, 5 gp
- Works well layered onto an existing career background if the character spent time there before or after their working life (an aged former tradesperson now living out retirement in an almshouse, praying for their benefactor, is a lovely small detail for an older PC or NPC)

This background suits a **Cleric** particularly well (genuine, lived religious structure and routine, real understanding of charity's practical and spiritual dimensions) and offers a compelling, quieter option for a character motivated by **genuine gratitude toward, or resentment of, institutional charity** — someone who either deeply values the dignity that charitable structure gave them, or chafed against its conditions and rules their whole life.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Almshouse Warden** — Oversees residents' daily life and the endowment's finances, genuinely devoted to the founder's memory and the residents' welfare, or (in a darker version) quietly skimming from the endowment meant for the poor.
- **The Prayer-Bound Elder** — An aged resident who takes their obligation to pray for the founder's soul with real, sincere devotion, having outlived everyone who remembers the founder personally except through that daily ritual.
- **The Guild's Retired Master** — A former craftsman spending their final years in their guild's almshouse, a good source of deep trade-history knowledge and guild-politics gossip from decades of experience.
- **The Almshouse Founder, Still Living** — A wealthy patron who founded and endowed the almshouse during their own lifetime, personally invested in seeing "their" charity succeed, possibly using it for reputation-building as much as genuine piety.
- **The Turned-Away Petitioner** — Someone denied almshouse admission as "undeserving" by period judgment, despite genuine need — a sympathetic figure highlighting the real limits and judgments built into medieval charity.
### Game Hooks
- **The Embezzled Endowment** — An almshouse warden has been quietly diverting funds meant for residents' care, and the party investigates when conditions mysteriously worsen despite the endowment's supposed wealth.
- **The Founder's Unfulfilled Wish** — A benefactor's specific charitable instructions (who should be admitted, what prayers should be said, how funds should be used) are being ignored or twisted by current administrators, and someone (a descendant, a guild, a cleric) wants the party to investigate and correct it.
- **The Almshouse Under Threat** — A lord, guild dispute, or civic reorganization threatens to close or repurpose an almshouse, displacing its elderly and infirm residents — a sympathetic "protect the vulnerable" civic hook without an obvious clean villain.
- **The Grateful Prayer** — An old almshouse resident's decades of sincere prayer for their founder's soul becomes unexpectedly, genuinely significant in your setting (if it has literal spiritual mechanics) — a nice magical-realism hook exploring whether such devotion has real effect.
- **Inheriting the Charity** — A PC or NPC unexpectedly inherits responsibility for an almshouse foundation (as founder's heir or through unusual circumstance) and has to navigate the real administrative, financial, and moral responsibilities of running it well.
