### Primary Goals
**The Philosopher's Stone** — the holy grail of alchemy. Alchemists believed this substance could transmute base metals (like lead or copper) into gold or silver, and many also believed it could grant immortality or perfect health when made into an "elixir of life."

**Transmutation of metals** — even short of the ultimate Stone, alchemists experimented constantly with ways to purify or transform metals, guided by the belief that all matter was composed of varying balances of the four classical elements (earth, air, fire, water) and could theoretically be rebalanced into other substances.

**Medicine** — many alchemists, especially those following the tradition of Paracelsus (more 16th century, but building on medieval foundations), worked to create remedies, tinctures, and purified substances for healing.
### Daily Practice
- **Laboratory work**: distillation, calcination (heating substances until they broke down), sublimation, and dissolution were core techniques, often performed with furnaces, alembics (distillation vessels), and crucibles.
- **Record-keeping in code**: alchemists wrote in deliberately obscure, symbolic language — partly to protect secrets from rivals, partly because they saw their work as sacred knowledge not meant for the uninitiated.
- **Study of texts**: they pored over works attributed to figures like Hermes Trismegistus, Geber (Jabir ibn Hayyan), and others, treating alchemy as an inherited, ancient wisdom tradition.
- **Astrological timing**: many believed celestial alignments affected the success of experiments, so they'd plan procedures around planetary positions.
### Philosophical Dimension
Alchemy wasn't purely materialistic — many practitioners saw the transformation of metals as a mirror of spiritual purification. The alchemist's own soul, in this view, was meant to be refined alongside the substances in the crucible. This is why alchemical texts are full of religious and mystical imagery (dragons, kings and queens, marriages, deaths and rebirths) layered over what were, practically speaking, chemistry experiments.
### Social Position
Alchemists ranged widely — from court-sponsored scholars seeking patronage (some rulers funded alchemists hoping for gold to fund wars or debts) to wandering charlatans running scams, to genuine scholars who made real contributions to early chemistry, like discovering or refining acids, alcohol distillation, and various salts.
### Worldbuilding Angles
**The Failed Alchemist's Legacy**  
A previous court alchemist — perhaps generations ago — actually succeeded at something, but not what they were trying to do. They didn't find the Philosopher's Stone, but their experiments produced a stable magical compound that's been quietly embedded in your setting's infrastructure ever since. Perhaps a specific dye that never fades, a mortar used in a particular building's construction that makes the walls supernaturally durable, or a preserved food preparation that extends shelf life far beyond natural limits. Nobody currently knows where it came from. The alchemist's notes (written in the characteristic obscure symbolic language covered in that entry) survive somewhere, incomprehensible to most but potentially revealing to someone who knows how to read them.

**The Patron Problem**  
An alchemist NPC is in genuine trouble — their noble patron has grown impatient with decades of expense and no Philosopher's Stone, and is considering either executing them for fraud or selling them to a rival court. The alchemist has real knowledge (genuine distillation techniques, practical chemistry, some genuine magical discoveries) but can't produce the specific impossible thing they were hired for. PCs might be hired by the patron to audit the alchemist's work, by the alchemist to help fake sufficient progress to buy more time, by a rival court to acquire the alchemist's genuine practical knowledge, or by the alchemist's guild (if your setting has one, connecting to the magic guild entry) to protect a member from a patron's abuse.

**Alchemical Infrastructure**  
Rather than treating alchemy as purely individual quest, consider making it genuinely embedded in your setting's economy — certain trades depend on alchemical processes even without acknowledging them as such. The dyer entry noted that dyeing was real applied chemistry; perhaps in your setting, specific dyeing operations, metalworking processes, or preservation techniques are openly recognized as alchemically derived, creating a legitimate industrial alchemy sector distinct from the mystical Philosopher's Stone pursuit. An alchemists' guild in your setting might therefore have two genuinely distinct factions — practical industrial alchemists making real products, and philosophical alchemists pursuing transformation and immortality — with real professional tension between them about what alchemy is actually for.

**The Dangerous Discovery**  
An alchemist has accidentally discovered something genuinely significant — not the Philosopher's Stone, but something with real world-altering potential. Perhaps a substance that dissolves a specific metal, a compound that temporarily suppresses magical ability, or a preparation that reveals invisible things. They don't fully understand what they've found, don't know how to reproduce it consistently, and are terrified of what will happen if the wrong people learn about it. The discovery has attracted attention they didn't want, and they need help — either protection, help understanding what they've made, or help destroying the evidence before it causes catastrophe.

**Alchemical Pollution**  
A settlement near a major alchemical workshop is experiencing strange effects — not dramatic magical disaster, but subtle wrongness. Crops that grow but taste wrong, livestock behaving strangely, water from specific wells having unusual effects. The workshop's operators (perhaps genuinely trying to do legitimate work) have been disposing of experimental byproducts carelessly, and the accumulated chemical/magical contamination is affecting the local environment. This isn't a villain plot — it's an industrial accident with genuine victims and genuinely well-meaning (if careless) perpetrators, creating moral complexity about how to resolve it.

**The Immortality Seeker**  
An elderly, genuinely brilliant alchemist is running out of time in the most personal sense — approaching death after a lifetime pursuing the Philosopher's Stone's promise of immortality without achieving it. They're now genuinely desperate, cutting corners on ethical and practical precautions they previously maintained, pursuing increasingly dangerous experiments, and potentially willing to harm others to get what they need. This isn't cartoonish villainy — it's recognizable human fear of death expressed through the specific cultural framework of alchemy, creating a sympathetic but genuinely dangerous antagonist.
