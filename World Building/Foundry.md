### Distinct From the Blacksmith's Forge
We've covered blacksmiths extensively, but it's worth drawing out a real distinction: a **foundry** specifically refers to operations centered on **casting** — melting metal completely to a liquid state and pouring it into molds to take a specific shape — as opposed to the blacksmith's primary method of heating metal to a workable-but-solid state and shaping it through hammering. Both involve fire and metal, but they're genuinely different technical processes requiring different equipment, skills, and typically different metals.
### Why Casting Required Different Techniques
- **Iron's melting point** is quite high, and medieval furnace technology for most of the period struggled to reliably achieve full liquid-melt temperatures for iron at scale — this is part of why wrought iron (worked hot but not fully melted, as covered with blacksmiths) dominated for so long
- **Bronze and bell-metal** (copper-tin alloys) melt at considerably lower temperatures than iron, making them much more practical for casting with available medieval furnace technology — this is why so much medieval casting work centered on bronze rather than iron
- **Blast furnaces**, as mentioned briefly in the blacksmith entry, began spreading in parts of Europe especially from the 14th-15th centuries onward, finally enabling more practical iron casting (producing cast iron) — but this is a later medieval development, not something available throughout the whole period
### What Medieval Foundries Actually Cast
**Bells**: probably the single most significant and visible foundry product
- Church bells required substantial bronze casting expertise — getting the alloy ratio and casting process right directly affected the bell's tone and durability, making **bell-founders** genuinely respected specialists
- Casting a large bell was a significant undertaking, sometimes done on-site near the bell's eventual installation (particularly for very large bells, where transport of the finished, heavy object was more difficult than transporting raw materials and casting locally)
- Bell-founders developed real expertise in mold-making (often clay-based molds built up in layers around a core, then the mold broken away after casting — a "lost" mold technique, distinct from but related to lost-wax casting)

**Cannons and early gunpowder weapons**: as gunpowder weaponry developed and spread through the later medieval period (connecting to the gunpowder transmission mentioned in the "chemist" entry), cannon-founding became an increasingly significant and militarily critical specialization
- Early cannons were sometimes made through other methods (wrought iron hoops and staves, similar in principle to barrel construction) before cast bronze and eventually cast iron cannon became more dominant
- Cannon-founders required real precision, since casting flaws could cause catastrophic failure when the weapon was actually fired

**Bronze sculptural and decorative work**:
- Church fonts, decorative fittings, and some sculptural commissions (connecting back to the artist/sculptor entry) used bronze casting, particularly for prestigious commissions where bronze's durability and prestige justified the expense
- **Lost-wax casting**: a technique inherited from antiquity, involving creating a wax model of the desired object, encasing it in a mold material, then melting/burning out the wax (hence "lost") and pouring molten metal into the resulting cavity — used for finer, more detailed bronze work than simpler bell or cannon casting required

**Other cast items**:
- Cooking pots and cauldrons (bronze and later cast iron)
- Decorative fittings, candlesticks, and similar household or church items in wealthier contexts
- Coin-minting dies and related metalworking (though coin striking itself is a related but distinct process from casting)
### The Casting Process
1. **Pattern/model creation**: for lost-wax work, a wax model of the final object; for simpler items, sometimes a wooden or clay pattern
2. **Mold construction**: building a mold around the pattern, often from clay, sand, or other heat-resistant material, sometimes in multiple pieces for complex shapes
3. **Removing the pattern**: for lost-wax, melting/burning the wax out; for other methods, carefully removing the solid pattern while preserving the mold's cavity shape
4. **Melting the metal**: heating bronze, bell-metal, or (later) iron to full liquid state in a furnace — requiring real understanding of fuel, airflow (bellows), and temperature management
5. **Pouring**: carefully pouring the molten metal into the prepared mold, a moment requiring real skill and care given the genuine danger of handling molten metal
6. **Cooling and mold removal**: allowing the casting to cool sufficiently before breaking away or removing the mold
7. **Finishing**: filing, polishing, and any additional decorative work on the cooled casting
### Risk and Skill
- **Genuine physical danger**: working with molten metal carried real risk of severe burns or worse if something went wrong — splashing, mold failures, or furnace accidents were serious hazards
- **High stakes for failure**: a flawed bell casting or a cannon with internal casting defects represented enormous wasted material and labor investment, or in the cannon's case, a weapon that might kill its own crew when fired — this made experienced, reliable foundry masters genuinely valuable
### Status and Organization
- **Bell-founders and gun-founders** occupied a position of real specialized respect, given the technical demands and importance of their products (church bells mattered enormously to community religious life, while cannon casting became increasingly militarily vital toward the end of the medieval period)
- **Guild structure**: where towns supported enough foundry activity, founders organized into guilds following the standard pattern, sometimes overlapping with or closely associated with related metalworking guilds
- **Royal/state interest in cannon-founding**: given gunpowder weapons' growing military significance, rulers had direct strategic interest in cannon-founding capacity, sometimes leading to more direct state involvement, patronage, or control over this specific specialization compared to more purely civilian trades
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A bell-founder casting a bell with genuine magical properties — perhaps the bronze alloy incorporates a rare material, or the casting process is paired with ritual/enchantment, making the church bell itself a meaningful magic item rather than purely decorative lore
- An early cannon-founder character representing a disruptive new military technology in your setting — control of (or access to) this knowledge could be a significant strategic plot element, mirroring the real historical transition gunpowder weapons caused
- A lost-wax casting commission for an important statue or magical item as a grounded craft-process hook — PCs might need to source a rare metal, find a skilled founder, or protect the casting process from sabotage
- A catastrophic foundry accident (a bell or cannon casting gone wrong) as a dramatic, visceral scene-setting detail for a settlement's history or a current crisis
