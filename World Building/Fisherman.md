### A Trade With Real Religious-Calendar Importance
We've referenced fishing's economic significance repeatedly throughout this list — connecting to dairying and the fasting calendar, coopers and barrel-packed preserved fish, docks and the herring trade — so it's worth giving fishermen their own proper treatment. Given how often meat was off-limits under Church fasting rules, fish wasn't a minor dietary supplement; it was a major, structurally essential food category.
### Why Fish Mattered So Much
The Church's fasting calendar (touched on with bakers, chefs, and dairying) prohibited meat on a remarkable number of days throughout the year — every Friday, much of Lent, and numerous other fast days, adding up to roughly a third or more of the calendar year in many interpretations. Fish was the standard substitute, making consistent fish supply a genuine structural necessity for medieval food systems, not a niche preference.
### Types of Fishing
**Freshwater fishing**:
- **Rivers and streams**: a widespread, accessible source for inland communities, though access was often legally restricted — fishing rights on rivers were frequently controlled by the lord who owned the adjacent land, similar to hunting rights, meaning unauthorized fishing could constitute genuine poaching with real legal consequences
- **Fishponds**: many manors and especially monasteries maintained dedicated, deliberately stocked and managed fishponds — a controlled, reliable protein source independent of wild catch variability, particularly valuable given how heavily the fasting calendar demanded fish. Monastery fishponds in particular could be genuinely sophisticated aquaculture operations.
- **Eel traps and weirs**: as mentioned with basket weavers, woven traps captured eels and other fish in rivers, a significant and widely consumed catch

**Sea fishing**:

- **Coastal/inshore fishing**: smaller boats working near shore, a daily or short-trip operation for coastal communities
- **Deep-sea fishing**: longer voyages further from shore, particularly significant for certain catches like cod — by the later medieval period, European fishermen (particularly from regions like the Basque country and later English/other northern fleets) were ranging quite far for productive fishing grounds, including eventually reaching the rich waters off Newfoundland/North America even before Columbus's voyages became widely known, based on some historical evidence and ongoing scholarly debate about the extent of pre-Columbian Atlantic fishing range
- **Herring**: an enormously significant catch, particularly in the North Sea and Baltic — entire regional economies (and major trade through the Hanseatic network, as covered with docks/wharfs) were built substantially around herring fishing and the salt-preservation industry that made the catch transportable and storable
### Equipment and Methods
- **Boats**: ranging from small river craft to substantial seagoing fishing vessels, built by the shipwrights covered in the carpenter entry
- **Nets**: various types depending on target fish and water conditions — seine nets, gill nets, and other designs requiring real skill to make and maintain (sometimes a distinct net-making specialization existed in larger fishing communities)
- **Lines and hooks**: simpler individual-line fishing for various catches
- **Traps and weirs**: fixed structures, particularly for eels and certain river fish, as mentioned
- **Spears and gaffs**: for certain catches, particularly larger fish
### Preservation — Essential Given No Refrigeration
Fresh fish spoiled fast, so preservation was as economically important as the catch itself:
- **Salting**: packing fish in salt, often into barrels (connecting directly to the cooper trade) for storage and transport — this is specifically how herring and cod became major long-distance trade goods rather than only locally consumable
- **Smoking**: another preservation method, producing distinctively flavored preserved fish
- **Drying**: particularly associated with cod ("stockfish" — air-dried without salt, a significant Norwegian/North Atlantic trade good that could keep for years and traveled well)
### Social and Economic Organization
- **Fishing communities**: coastal villages and towns often built their entire economic and social identity around fishing, with boats frequently family-owned (as mentioned in the boat rentals entry) and fishing knowledge passed through generations
- **Less guild-structured than urban crafts**: fishing, especially small-scale coastal/river fishing, operated more through family and community structures than the formal apprentice-journeyman-master guild system seen in urban crafts — though larger fishing ports with significant trade volume might support more formal organization
- **Monastic fishing operations**: monasteries with significant fishpond or coastal access sometimes ran substantial, well-organized fishing operations as part of their broader self-sufficiency and commercial activity
- **Women's role**: while the actual fishing (especially sea fishing) was predominantly male work given the physical demands and danger, women in fishing communities often handled significant related labor — net-mending, fish processing/preservation, and selling the catch at market
### Risk and Hardship
- **Genuine danger**: sea fishing especially carried real mortality risk from weather, accidents, and the simple unpredictability of small wooden boats on open water without modern weather forecasting or rescue capability
- **Economic precariousness**: catch variability meant real income instability — a poor fishing season could mean genuine hardship for a community heavily dependent on the trade
- **Competition and disputes**: fishing rights, net placement, and access to good fishing grounds could generate real local disputes, particularly where freshwater fishing rights intersected with lordly land ownership
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A fishing village's livelihood threatened by something supernatural — a sea monster disrupting the catch, a cursed stretch of water, or a magical blight on the local fishpond — giving low-level PCs a grounded, community-stakes adventure hook
- Monastery fishponds as a specific, textured location detail — a peaceful, well-managed religious site with surprisingly sophisticated aquaculture, useful as a quiet waypoint or a place where a specific magical fish/creature might be hidden
- Disputed fishing/river rights (lord vs. peasant community) as a small-scale legal/social conflict PCs might mediate, echoing the real historical tension between lordly land control and common subsistence access
- The herring trade's massive economic importance as a worldbuilding detail for a coastal city or region's wealth and political significance — control of rich fishing grounds or the preservation/trade infrastructure around them as a believable source of regional power
