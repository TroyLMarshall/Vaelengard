### The Role and Its Structure
A **town crier** was the official public announcer of a town or city — the living, walking mechanism by which official news, proclamations, laws, market information, and civic announcements actually reached an overwhelmingly **illiterate population** in an era with no printing, no mass communication, and no reliable way to post written notices that most people could read anyway. This is the essential fact to hold onto: the crier wasn't decorative or ceremonial in origin — he was **genuinely necessary communications infrastructure**, filling a real informational gap that nothing else in medieval society could fill at scale.

**Who employed criers and why** — Town councils, guilds, and sometimes the crown itself employed criers to announce: new laws and ordinances, royal proclamations, market opening times and price regulations, tax and toll changes, public health notices (quarantine orders during plague outbreaks are a real and significant use case), announcements of executions or public punishments, lost-and-found notices, and outright commercial advertising for merchants willing to pay the crier a fee to announce their goods. A crier typically worked a set circuit through a town's main streets and market squares, stopping at recognized points — often literal **market crosses**, standing stone or wooden crosses erected specifically as public gathering/announcement points — ringing a **handbell** to draw attention, then delivering the message in a loud, clear, formulaic voice.

**"Oyez, oyez, oyez"** — The traditional opening cry (from Anglo-Norman French, meaning roughly "hear ye") is genuinely period-accurate and instantly recognizable — a real linguistic fossil surviving from the era when Anglo-Norman French remained the language of law and official proclamation in England long after Old English/Middle English dominated everyday speech, meaning the crier's formal opening literally preserved a linguistic layer of conquest and administration in a phrase ordinary people heard regularly without necessarily understanding its origin.

**Legal protection and status** — Like the herald, a town crier delivering official proclamations carried a degree of **protected, quasi-official status** — harming or seriously interfering with a crier in the act of delivering official news was treated as an offense against the town's or crown's authority itself, not just against the individual, since the message being disrupted was the sovereign's or council's business. This gave an otherwise fairly ordinary tradesperson a real, if narrow, layer of institutional protection while actively performing their duty.

**Skill required** — The job demanded a genuinely **loud, clear, carrying voice** (essential before amplification existed — a crier needed to be heard over market noise, crowds, and open-air acoustics), good **memory or reading ability** (delivering announcements accurately, sometimes reading directly from an official written proclamation handed down from the council, which required the crier to be literate or at least skilled at accurate memorization/rehearsal), and a degree of **showmanship** — an engaging, authoritative public delivery that people would actually stop and listen to rather than ignore. This made criers something like a cross between a civil servant and a public performer, and a genuinely competent crier could develop a real local reputation and following purely for how well they held a crowd's attention.

**Social position** — Modest but respectable — a paid civic post (sometimes salaried by the town, sometimes paid per proclamation or by commercial clients paying for advertisement), carrying real, if narrow, institutional authority while performing duties, but without independent power of the kind a beadle or constable held. Unlike the beadle's association with petty enforcement (and its slightly comic-officious reputation), the crier's role was more purely informational and performative — closer to a trusted public voice than a minor enforcer, giving the two related-sounding roles genuinely distinct flavors worth keeping separate in your setting.

**The crier as information hub** — Precisely because criers constantly moved through town delivering and often _hearing_ news, gossip, and reactions from the crowds who gathered to listen, an experienced crier accumulated real, wide-ranging knowledge of a town's current affairs, rumors, and public mood — arguably even more so than a settled shopkeeper, since the crier's entire job was standing in the busiest public spaces and being the focal point of whatever the town was currently talking about.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Performance, Persuasion (or Investigation, for the "hears everything" angle)
- **Tool Proficiency:** A musical instrument (the handbell, framed as an instrument) or proficiency in one additional language (Anglo-Norman French-equivalent, reflecting formal proclamation language)
- **Feat:** Actor or Skilled
- **Equipment:** A crier's handbell, a scroll case for official proclamations, a formal tabard or badge marking civic authority, 10 gp

This background is an excellent, almost perfect fit for a **Bard** — the performative public speaking, the loud carrying voice, the accumulated town gossip, and even the legally protected status while "performing" all map directly onto core Bard fantasy. It also suits a **Rogue** nicely (a crier who's learned exactly how to read a crowd, deliver a convincing performance, and quietly profit from being the most trusted information conduit in town) or a **Cleric/Paladin** delivering religious proclamations and public devotional announcements.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Beloved Town Voice** — A long-serving crier known and liked by the whole town, whose announcements people genuinely trust and gather to hear — a great, warm recurring NPC and reliable information source for the party.
- **The Commercially Compromised Crier** — Takes payment from merchants to announce (and subtly favor) their goods over rivals — a minor, believable corruption angle with real economic stakes for competing local businesses.
- **The Crier Who Knows the Mood** — Uniquely positioned to sense a town's rising unrest, fear, or anger simply by how crowds react to each day's proclamations — a good early-warning NPC for a brewing riot, uprising, or panic.
- **The Silenced Crier** — Someone has intimidated, bribed, or otherwise pressured a crier into NOT announcing something official — a genuine breach of civic function, and a mystery hook about who benefits from the silence.
- **The Rival Criers** — Two criers (perhaps competing for the same paid civic post, or one official and one an unauthorized rival) locked in a dispute over who has the right to deliver news in a district — echoing the guild-jurisdiction disputes from earlier trades, but framed around information and public voice.
### Game Hooks
- **The Altered Proclamation** — A crier unknowingly delivers a proclamation that's been tampered with or forged before reaching them — spreading false information, a fake tax, a fraudulent decree — and the party has to trace it back to whoever altered the official message.
- **Breaking the News** — The party needs a piece of information spread quickly and credibly through a town (a warning, a call to arms, an exoneration) and enlists a crier — a good practical, mechanically flavorful way to handle "spreading word" as an actual scene rather than a summary.
- **The Silenced Announcement** — Someone is actively preventing a specific proclamation from being delivered (bribing, threatening, or removing the crier) — the party has to figure out what message was meant to go out, and why someone wanted it stopped.
- **Public Panic** — A crier's announcement (accurate or not) about plague, invasion, or disaster triggers real public panic, and the party has to manage or calm the resulting chaos — a good crowd-scene set piece.
- **The Crier's Testimony** — A crier, positioned constantly in public and privy to gathered crowd reactions and gossip, turns out to be the key witness to something the party is investigating — a "track down and properly question the overlooked witness" hook, similar to the beadle's missed-detail hook but centered on public performance rather than quiet observation.
