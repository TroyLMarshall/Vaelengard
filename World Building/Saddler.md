### The Craft and Its Structure
A **saddler** made and repaired saddles, harnesses, bridles, and the broader category of leather horse-tack — a trade that sat at a genuinely important intersection of medieval life: transportation, agriculture, trade logistics, and warfare all ran on horses, and horses all ran on properly fitted, well-made leatherwork. Like the farrier, the saddler was one of those unglamorous-but-indispensable trades every town, manor, and army needed functioning at all times.

**Guild structure and specialization** — In larger towns, saddlery organized into formal guilds (London's Worshipful Company of Saddlers is a real, well-documented example, with roots reaching back to the 1100s-1200s), and the trade itself split into related specializations that sometimes operated as distinct guilds competing over turf: **saddlers** proper (the saddle tree — often wood or leather-covered wood — and the finished saddle), **loriners** (metal fittings — bits, spurs, stirrups, buckles), and **girdlers** (belts and girdles, overlapping with saddle girths). This created real, sometimes contentious jurisdictional lines — who's allowed to make which exact component was a genuine source of guild disputes, much like the tailor/seamstress tension.

**The work itself** — A saddler built the saddle tree (the rigid frame, historically wood, sometimes reinforced with iron or horn/bone), covered and padded it with leather, and fitted it precisely to both horse and rider — a poorly fitted saddle could injure a horse's back or unseat a rider dangerously, especially in the high-stakes context of a mounted knight in combat. Beyond saddles, saddlers made **bridles, reins, harnesses for draft/cart horses, stirrup leathers, and saddlebags** — essentially the full leather infrastructure of anything a horse pulled, carried, or was ridden with. Fine saddlery for nobility involved decorative tooling, dyed leather, and sometimes precious metal fittings (crossing back into loriner/goldsmith territory) — a knight's saddle could be a genuine status display, much like a sword.

**Military importance** — War saddles were a specialized, high-stakes product: a knight's combat saddle needed a high cantle and pommel to keep the rider seated through a lance impact, different from a riding or pack saddle entirely. Saddlers supplying armies or noble households had steady, well-paying, and strategically important work — much like farriers and fletchers, they were essential camp trades that armies couldn't function without, and a besieging or campaigning army's saddlers were kept busy with constant repair, not just initial construction.

**Materials and trade connections** — Saddlers depended on tanners and leatherworkers for their raw hide, and on loriners/smiths for metal fittings, making them a naturally networked trade sitting in the middle of a small supply chain — useful if you want an NPC with connections across several other trades in your setting.

**Status** — Middling but respectable: not as prestigious as a swordsmith or armorer, but skilled, guild-protected in most cities, and enjoying steady demand from every social class that used horses (which, functionally, was most of it in some capacity — even a peasant with one plow horse needed harness repair).
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Animal Handling, Persuasion (or Investigation, for the "reads fit and function" angle)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Leatherworker's Tools
- **Feat:** Skilled or Tavern Brawler
- **Equipment:** Leatherworking tools, a partially finished bridle or saddlebag, a few lengths of good leather, riding clothes, 10 gp

This background is a natural companion to the Farrier background from earlier — together they cover "everything a mount needs" — and fits **Rangers, Cavaliers, Mounted Fighters, or Paladins** especially well. It's also a good fit for a character who traveled with an army as camp-trade support before taking up arms themselves, having seen war from the supply lines rather than the front.
### NPC Hooks
- **The War-Saddle Specialist** — Makes and repairs combat saddles for knights and cavalry, deeply knowledgeable about what keeps a rider seated through violence — a great NPC for a party needing mounted-combat gear or advice before a battle.
- **The Guild Jurisdiction Rival** — A saddler locked in an ongoing dispute with the local loriners' or girdlers' guild over who's allowed to make which fittings — petty on its surface, genuinely disruptive to local trade underneath.
- **The Nobleman's Saddler** — Attached to a noble household, making increasingly elaborate ceremonial and status-display saddlery — caught between artistic ambition and a patron's demanding, shifting taste.
- **The Traveling Repairer** — Rides a circuit between smaller villages too small to support a full-time saddler, similar to the traveling farrier — a good recurring NPC who shows up between adventures with news from elsewhere.
- **The Ill-Fitted Saddle** — A saddler whose work injured a valuable warhorse or a rider through bad fit — maybe genuinely at fault, maybe sabotaged by a rival, maybe unaware the horse's condition changed — a character wrestling with blame, reputation, or a frame-up.
### Game Hooks
- **The Cavalry Bottleneck** — A campaign or tournament is stalled because there aren't enough working saddles/harnesses in time — the party needs to source materials, protect a saddler under deadline pressure, or track down stolen tack.
- **The Sabotaged Saddle** — A noble or knight is injured (or nearly killed) when their saddle fails in a tournament or battle — accident, faulty work, or deliberate sabotage? A classic "investigate the craftsman" mystery hook.
- **Guild War Over Fittings** — The saddlers' and loriners' guilds escalate their jurisdictional dispute into real economic sabotage or violence, and the party gets pulled in as mediators, muscle, or investigators.
- **The Warhorse's True Fit** — The party needs a mount properly outfitted for an unusual purpose (an exotic or monstrous mount, a magically altered horse, a griffon or other creature) and the local saddler is out of their depth — a fun small commission-quest, echoing the whitesmith's "unusual material" hook.
- **Tracing the Tack** — Stolen or abandoned horse gear found at a crime scene or battlefield can be traced back to a specific saddler's distinctive work (stitching style, maker's stamp, a custom fitting) — a solid "follow the craftsmanship" investigation thread.
