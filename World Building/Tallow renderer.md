### The Craft and Its Structure
A **tallow renderer** (sometimes just called a "chandler" where the trades overlapped, though chandlers more specifically made candles from the rendered product) took animal fat — trimmings, suet, and other fatty byproducts from butchered livestock — and rendered it into **tallow**: purified, stable animal fat used overwhelmingly for candles and soap, plus a range of secondary uses (lubricating cart axles and leather, waterproofing, and even some crude medicinal salve applications). This is a trade defined almost entirely by **necessity and unpleasantness** — genuinely essential to daily medieval life (candles and soap being basic, universal household needs), but among the most foul-smelling, low-status physical labor a town could contain.

**The rendering process** — Raw animal fat was cut up and slowly heated/boiled in large vats, melting it down and separating the pure fat from connective tissue, blood, and other impurities, which were skimmed and strained off, leaving a purified fat that would set into a firm, usable substance once cooled. This process produced an intense, genuinely nauseating stench — rendering rotting or lower-quality fat sources smelled considerably worse than fresh trimmings, and the smoke and vapor from the boiling vats carried that smell for a significant distance. This single fact — the overwhelming, community-noticeable stink of the trade — shapes almost everything else about how renderers were treated and where they were allowed to work.

**Civic regulation and exile to the margins** — Because of the smell (and associated public health concerns, reasonably or not, about miasma and contagion in an era that believed bad air itself could spread disease), town governments across medieval Europe frequently **regulated tallow rendering to the physical edges of town**, downwind, near rivers (used both for water access and to carry waste/runoff away), often clustered together with other "noxious" trades like tanning and butchery into a designated district — genuinely, a real and common piece of medieval urban planning, sometimes formalized in town ordinances specifying exactly where such trades could legally operate. This gives you an actual, planned "bad part of town" for your setting that isn't about crime or poverty exactly, but about **zoning around smell and nuisance** — a different and interesting flavor of urban marginalization than the usual "slum" trope.

**Social status — low, but not dishonorable in the executioner's sense** — Tallow rendering sat toward the bottom of craft prestige, grouped in the popular and sometimes official imagination with other unpleasant trades (tanning, butchery, dyeing with certain foul-smelling processes) as necessary-but-disreputable work. Unlike the executioner, this wasn't typically a formally "dishonorable," hereditarily stigmatized trade with legal disabilities attached — it was simply low-status, unpleasant, physically demanding labor that respectable society preferred to keep at arm's length (and crosswind) rather than a legally marked caste. Practically, this meant renderers were often drawn from the poorer working classes, sometimes overlapping directly with butchers (who had ready access to the raw fat trimmings as a byproduct of their own trade) rather than existing as a fully separate specialist guild in every town.

**Guild connections and overlaps** — In cities large enough to formally organize the trade, tallow rendering sometimes fell under or alongside the **chandlers' guild** (candle-makers), since rendered tallow's primary and most valuable end use was candle-making — tallow candles being the cheap, smoky, smelly, but affordable everyday lighting for ordinary households, in real contrast to the clean-burning, expensive **beeswax candles** used by the wealthy and the Church (a genuinely useful, class-coded lighting distinction you can use for flavor — a scene's social status can be signaled just by which kind of candle is burning). Soap-making similarly connected to the trade, since soap production also relied on rendered fat combined with lye.

**Economic necessity beneath the disgust** — For all the low status, the trade was genuinely indispensable: every household needed light after dark and needed soap for washing, meaning tallow renderers, like the miller or the net maker, occupied that particular medieval-worldbuilding sweet spot of **essential-but-unglamorous** — nobody thought highly of them, but nobody could do without what they produced either.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Nature, Survival (or Investigation, for a trade requiring judgment of fat quality/spoilage)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Alchemist's Supplies or Cook's Utensils
- **Feat:** Tough or Skilled
- **Equipment:** Rendering tools (large ladle, straining cloth, boiling vat access), a stock of tallow blocks or candles, working clothes that never quite lose the smell, 5 gp

This background works well for a **Barbarian or Fighter** (hard physical labor, social marginalization breeding real toughness and a chip on the shoulder), and offers a genuinely underused flavor for a **Rogue or Cleric** motivated by resentment of, or hard-won empathy from, being looked down on for essential but "unclean" work — a strong thematic parallel to the executioner background but without the hereditary legal stigma, making it a gentler entry point into "marginalized trade" backstory territory.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Downwind District Elder** — A long-time renderer who's essentially become the informal leader of the town's designated "noxious trades" district — tanners, butchers, renderers all know and trust them, a good contact for anyone needing help or information from that marginalized corner of town.
- **The Butcher-Renderer** — Runs both trades together out of practical necessity (ready access to fat trimmings), pragmatic and unsentimental, a good grounded working-class NPC.
- **The Chandler's Supplier** — A renderer whose product exclusively feeds a specific candle-maker's shop, their fortunes tied together — a nice small "supply chain" NPC pairing, similar to the currier/saddler relationship.
- **The Ambitious Renderer** — Trying to expand beyond low-status tallow work into soap-making or even attempting the leap into more "respectable" chandlery — a character arc about trying to escape a trade's stigma through hard work and ambition.
- **The Miasma Believer Official** — A town health official convinced (per genuine period belief) that the rendering district's smell is actively spreading illness, pushing for harsher restriction or relocation — a source of real, if misguided, civic conflict.
### Game Hooks
- **The District Relocation Fight** — A town council wants to push the noxious-trades district even further out (or shut it down) over health/smell complaints from wealthier neighbors, threatening the livelihoods of everyone working there — a "defend the marginalized underdogs" civic hook.
- **Something Rendered Wrong** — A batch of tallow (or the fat supply feeding it) is contaminated or comes from a suspicious source — diseased livestock, something worse — and the party has to trace the problem back before tainted candles or soap spread illness through town.
- **The Miasma Panic** — A real illness outbreak gets wrongly blamed on the rendering district's smell rather than its actual cause, and the party has to protect the falsely blamed community while finding the real source of the sickness.
- **Fat, Fuel, and Fraud** — A renderer or supplier is caught cutting corners (using spoiled or diseased animal fat, or short-weighting customers) — a small-scale "corruption in an unglamorous trade" mystery, echoing the pewterer/currier adulteration hooks.
- **The Unlikely Ally** — The party needs help, shelter, or information nobody "respectable" in town will provide, and it's the shunned rendering district — and its dismissed, overlooked residents — who actually come through for them.
