### A Modest But Genuinely Important Craft
Bone carving doesn't get the spotlight that, say, goldsmithing or armoring does, but it was a real and useful medieval trade — partly because bone was a cheap, abundant byproduct of everyday life (slaughtering animals for food left plenty of bone and horn behind), and partly because it served as an accessible substitute for more expensive materials like ivory when crafting small decorative or functional objects.
### Materials
- **Animal bone**: primarily cattle, sheep, and pig bones — leftover material from butchery, making this trade naturally connected to and dependent on the meat trade
- **Antler**: deer antler was prized for being naturally dense and strong, useful for tool handles and similar functional items
- **Horn**: from cattle and sheep, often worked alongside bone by the same craftsmen, though horn's properties (it can be softened with heat and shaped, almost like an early plastic) made it suited to somewhat different techniques and products
- **Ivory**: true ivory (walrus or elephant, the latter imported via long trade routes) was a luxury material, and bone carving in some ways served as the working-class cousin to ivory carving — similar techniques, far cheaper material, the difference visible mainly in fineness of grain and prestige rather than fundamental method
### What Bone Carvers Made
**Tools and practical objects**:
- **Combs**: extremely common bone-carved item, used by virtually everyone regardless of class
- **Knife handles and tool handles**: bone and antler provided a durable, grippable material for hafting blades and tools
- **Needles and pins**: sewing needles, hairpins
- **Buttons and toggles**: fastenings for clothing
- **Dice and game pieces**: bone was a favored material for dice, chess pieces, and other gaming implements
- **Spindle whorls**: weights used in hand-spinning thread/yarn

**Decorative and devotional objects**:
- **Small religious items**: pilgrim badges, small devotional plaques, rosary beads
- **Decorative inlay**: bone pieces inlaid into wood furniture, boxes, or musical instruments for visual contrast (a technique with strong continuity into Islamic and later European decorative arts as well)
- **Caskets and boxes**: small bone-paneled boxes, sometimes carved with scenes, used for storing valuables or as gifts

**Musical and specialized items**:
- **Whistles and simple wind instrument parts**
- **Skates**: this is a genuinely fun, lesser-known detail — bone skates (animal leg bones, often horse or cattle, shaped and strapped to the foot) were used for sliding across ice in northern Europe, a practical winter transport/recreation solution predating metal ice skates
### The Craft Itself
- **Sawing and shaping**: bone needed to be sawn into workable pieces, then shaped using knives, files, and rasps
- **Boiling/preparation**: bone was sometimes boiled to remove remaining fat, tissue, and marrow before working, both for practical cleanliness and to make the material easier to carve
- **Carving and incising**: fine detail work using small knives and gouges, similar in principle to woodcarving but requiring adaptation to bone's harder, more brittle nature
- **Polishing**: finished pieces were polished to bring out a smooth, sometimes attractively translucent or ivory-like finish
- **Horn-specific technique**: horn could be softened in hot water or over heat, then pressed, flattened, or molded — used for things like lantern panes (thin, semi-translucent horn sheets were a real medieval substitute for glass in lanterns, since it let light through while being far cheaper and more durable than glass) and drinking vessels
### Social and Economic Position
- Bone and horn working was generally a **lower-prestige craft** compared to metalworking or fine ivory carving, fitting into the broader category of practical, everyday-goods crafts rather than luxury trades
- Often **closely connected to or overlapping with other trades**: butchers (the source of raw material), turners (who worked various materials on a lathe, including bone), and sometimes combined with horn-working and even leatherworking in a single craftsman's repertoire
- In larger cities, bone/horn workers might form their own modest guild or be grouped with related trades under a broader craft guild umbrella
- **Accessible entry point**: like basket weaving, this trade required relatively modest capital investment compared to metalworking, making it accessible to craftsmen without the resources for a forge or expensive tools
### A Trade With Deep Archaeological Visibility
Worth noting as an aside: because bone survives well archaeologically (unlike wood, leather, or textiles, which usually rot away), medieval bone-carved objects — combs, dice, pins, skates — are disproportionately well-represented in what we actually know about medieval material culture compared to more perishable everyday items. A lot of what historians know about ordinary medieval daily life comes through exactly this kind of humble, overlooked craft.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A bone carver who works with **monster bone/horn/antler** rather than ordinary livestock remains — a natural tie-in to your setting's bestiary, turning slain monster parts into useful or magical crafted goods (a nice mechanical/narrative hook if your game has any crafting-from-loot elements)
- **Necromantic associations**: bone carving sits adjacent to some obviously dark fantasy territory — a carver whose seemingly mundane trade has uncomfortable overlaps with necromancy, or who's suspected of such even if innocent
- **Horn lantern-making** as a small, specific detail that adds texture to a low-magic or low-tech settlement — characters might specifically need horn lanterns rather than glass in a poorer or more rustic location
- A carver of **gaming pieces and dice** as a recurring minor NPC tied to a gambling den or tavern subplot, given how central dice and game pieces were to this trade
- Bone/ivory inlay work as a status marker — a wealthy NPC's furniture or weapon hilt bearing fine bone inlay as a subtle, flavorful detail signaling their means without needing to over-describe wealth directly
