### Distinguishing From the Mason Entry
We covered masons thoroughly already — their guild structure, the lodge system, Gothic architectural techniques, freemason/rough mason distinctions, and the master mason's architect-like coordinating role. "Stoneworker" as a broader category lets us expand beyond pure construction masonry to cover the full range of people working stone in medieval Europe, including several related specializations the mason entry didn't fully address.
### The Full Range of Stone Work
**Quarrymen**: the entry point of the stone supply chain, extracting raw stone from the earth — a genuinely distinct, physically demanding trade separate from the skilled masonry work done with the extracted stone
- **Quarry techniques**: splitting stone along natural fault lines using wedges, hammers, and chisels — requiring real knowledge of specific stone's grain and fracture behavior
- **Channeling and undercutting**: cutting channels around blocks to free them from the surrounding rock
- **Lifting and transport**: moving quarried stone to the surface and onto transport — genuinely challenging given stone's weight, requiring both mechanical assistance (levers, simple cranes) and substantial human labor
- **Geographic concentration**: quarrying happened where useful stone existed, not necessarily near construction sites — creating the transport logistics challenge covered in the construction company and freight company entries, since heavy stone moved most economically by water when possible

**Rough masons/layers**: as briefly distinguished in the mason entry
- Laying rougher stone for walls, foundations, and basic structural work
- Less prestigious than freemason carving work but essential for the bulk of actual construction volume

**Freemasons**: as covered in the mason entry
- Working fine-grained freestone capable of detailed carving in any direction
- The most skilled and prestigious end of stone construction work

**Stone carvers/sculptors**: as covered in the artist/sculptor entry
- Decorative and figurative carving — the elaborate sculptural programs of Gothic cathedrals, tomb effigies (covered in the graveyard entry), and architectural ornament
- Sometimes distinct from purely structural masons, representing a more artistic specialization

**Flint knappers**: working specifically with flint — a distinctly different material from the dimensional stone used in construction
- Flint's particular properties (extremely hard, fractures predictably along conchoidal lines producing sharp edges) made it useful for specific applications even in the medieval period
- **Flint in construction**: particularly significant in regions like East Anglia in England where flint was abundantly available — knapped flint facing on building exteriors, sometimes in elaborate decorative patterns (**flushwork**), representing a regional building tradition quite distinct from the ashlar stonework of major cathedral construction
- **Fire-starting**: flint and steel fire-starting (connecting to the candlemaker entry's discussion of pre-match lighting) required specific flint preparation

**Lime burners**: producing the lime mortar binding stone construction together
- **The lime-burning process**: heating limestone in a kiln to extremely high temperatures drove off carbon dioxide, converting calcium carbonate to calcium oxide (quicklime) — then slaking the quicklime with water produced hydrated lime for mortar production
- **Essential to construction**: without lime mortar, stone construction couldn't achieve the structural integrity of the great buildings covered throughout this list — making lime burning a critical, if unglamorous, support trade
- **Significant fuel consumption**: connecting to the charcoal maker and logger entries, lime burning was extremely fuel-intensive, requiring substantial wood or charcoal supply

**Pavier/paviors**: specializing specifically in paving — laying stone paving for floors, streets, and courtyard surfaces
- **Urban street paving**: as touched on in the private homes and outhouses entries, urban streets varied considerably in surface quality, and stone paving represented a significant civic infrastructure investment where it existed
- **Interior flooring**: the decorated floor tiles mentioned in the potter entry represent one end of this spectrum, while simple stone-flag paving represents the more basic, utilitarian end

**Alabaster workers**: a specific luxury stone specialization
- **Alabaster**: a fine-grained, translucent stone (calcium sulfate rather than calcium carbonate) particularly associated with English medieval production
- **Devotional sculptures and tombs**: English alabaster workshops produced significant quantities of devotional religious panels, tomb effigies, and altarpiece elements — sometimes for export across Europe, representing a genuine English medieval artistic export industry connecting to the broader trade networks covered throughout this list
### Stone Types and Their Applications
- **Limestone**: the most widely used building stone across much of England and France — variable quality, from rough rubble to the fine oolitic limestone of Bath or Caen in Normandy
- **Sandstone**: common in various regions, softer and easier to carve but generally less durable than limestone
- **Marble**: prestige stone, relatively rare in northern European contexts but significant in Mediterranean/Italian building tradition
- **Granite**: extremely hard and durable, difficult to work finely but important where available (particularly in southwestern Britain)
- **Flint**: as discussed above, a regional specialty material
- **Alabaster**: as discussed above, for fine carved work
### Guild Structure
- Stoneworkers organized along the lines covered in the mason entry — the lodge system for major construction projects, with more standard guild organization for settled urban stoneworkers
- **Quarrymen's organization**: somewhat distinct from construction masons — quarry workers sometimes organized separately given their different working conditions and employer relationships (often working for quarry owners rather than construction contractors)
- **Regional stone traditions**: as with pottery and glassmaking, regional variation in available stone types produced distinct local building traditions and associated craft knowledge
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A quarry as a specific worldbuilding location — not just background infrastructure but a working site with its own population, hierarchy, and potential for adventure (a quarry cutting into something unexpected, forced labor conditions creating tension, or a specific stone type with unusual properties being extracted)
- Flushwork flint decoration as a regional architectural marker — a specific area of your setting distinguished by its characteristic building style using locally abundant flint, giving that region an instantly recognizable visual identity
- Alabaster workshops as a luxury export industry — a region whose fine stone carving is sought across your setting, giving it economic significance and potentially making its craftsmen targets for patronage competition, kidnapping, or industrial espionage
- Lime burning's enormous fuel consumption as a worldbuilding detail connecting construction ambition to forest management — a major building project creating real pressure on surrounding woodland, with ecological and economic consequences for the region
