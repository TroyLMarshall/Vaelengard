### Who the Armorer Was
Armorers were highly skilled metalworkers, occupying a respected rung among craft guilds — more specialized (and often better paid) than a general blacksmith, since their work required not just metallurgy but an understanding of anatomy, mobility, and the practical mechanics of combat. By the late medieval period, especially in major armor-producing centers, "armorer" had splintered into several distinct specializations rather than being one generalist trade.
### Specializations Within the Trade
- **Plate armorers**: shaped and fitted solid plate armor — the most technically demanding work, requiring precise measurements of the individual wearer for proper mobility and protection.
- **Mail makers**: produced chainmail, an earlier and longer-running technology, by drawing wire, cutting it into rings, and riveting thousands of individual links by hand — an almost unbelievably labor-intensive process.
- **Helm makers**: specialized in helmets, which needed careful shaping for both protection and visibility/breathing.
- **Locksmiths and finishers**: handled hinges, buckles, and the articulated joints that let plate armor move with the body.
- **Etchers and decorators**: added engraving, gilding, or etched designs to armor for wealthy clients who wanted both protection and display.

Major centers like **Milan**, **Augsburg**, **Nuremberg**, and later **Innsbruck** and **Greenwich** (England, under royal workshops) became famous brand names of sorts — a "Milanese harness" (a full suit of armor) carried prestige much like a luxury good today.
### The Armory (Workshop)
A working armory combined several distinct stations:
- **The forge**: hearths for heating metal, often multiple to allow several pieces to be worked simultaneously
- **Anvils and hammers** of varying sizes for different stages — rough shaping versus fine detail work
- **Stakes and mandrels**: specialized shaped tools for hammering plate into curved forms over a form, rather than flat
- **Water troughs**: for quenching/tempering steel
- **Polishing and grinding equipment**: armor needed extensive finishing work after shaping — polishing wheels and abrasives to remove tool marks and create a smooth, often mirror-bright surface
- **Pattern templates and measuring tools**: for custom-fitted plate armor, accurate body measurements were essential
### The Process of Making Plate Armor
1. **Smelting/sourcing iron**: starting material was bloomery iron, later increasingly refined through repeated heating and hammering to remove impurities
2. **Shaping**: heating sheets of metal and hammering them over stakes and forms into curved plates matching body contours (breastplate, pauldrons for shoulders, cuisses for thighs, etc.)
3. **Hardening and tempering**: heat-treating the steel to balance hardness (resistance to penetration) against brittleness
4. **Fitting and articulation**: attaching plates with rivets, leather straps, and sliding rivets that allowed plates to move relative to each other without gaps
5. **Finishing**: polishing, sometimes blueing or browning for rust resistance, decorative etching for high-end pieces
6. **Assembly**: a complete "harness" (full suit) involved dozens of interlocking pieces that needed to work together as a system
### Apprenticeship and Guild Structure
Like other crafts, armorers trained through **apprenticeship**, often 7+ years, before becoming a journeyman and eventually (if skilled and fortunate enough) a master with their own workshop. Armorer guilds controlled quality standards, training, and often had close relationships with city governments since military equipment was a matter of civic and state interest, not just private commerce.
### Clientele and Economics
- **Knights and nobility** commissioned custom-fitted full plate harnesses — extraordinarily expensive items, sometimes worth as much as a small estate, that doubled as status symbols
- **Common soldiers and town militias** bought cheaper, mass-produced or older-style equipment — simple breastplates, brigandines (cloth/leather with riveted metal plates inside), or just mail
- **Tournament armor** became its own specialized sub-market — heavier, more reinforced gear specifically for jousting that would have been impractical on an actual battlefield
- **Civic armories**: cities and towns often maintained communal stockpiles of weapons and armor for militia use, maintained by city-employed armorers
### Tools and Materials Beyond Metal
- **Leather**: for straps, padding, and the arming doublet worn underneath armor
- **Textiles**: padded garments worn under armor (the "gambeson" or "aketon") to cushion and distribute impact
- **Brass and bronze**: for decorative fittings and sometimes buckles
- **Wax and oil**: for rust prevention during storage
### Worldbuilding Angles
#### Historical Grounding (real medieval practices to reskin)
- **Specialization within the trade** – Real armorers split into sub-trades: platers (plate armor), mailers (chainmail), helmet-makers, and even specific joint specialists. A single suit of plate might pass through several craftsmen's hands—great basis for a guild with internal hierarchy and rivalry.
- **City reputations** – Certain real cities (Milan, Augsburg, Nuremberg) were renowned armor-making centers, each with a distinct house style. Your setting could have "the steel city" or "the mountain forges" as a recognizable brand, the way people today know Damascus steel by name.
- **Bespoke fitting** – High-end armor was custom-fitted to the wearer, sometimes requiring the noble to travel to the armorer or vice versa. This makes armorers intimate with the powerful—they know a lord's exact measurements, weak points, even old wounds.
- **Guild secrecy** – Techniques like tempering and metal-folding were closely guarded trade secrets, passed master to apprentice. Espionage between rival armor houses is very true to history.
- **The apprentice pipeline** – Years of grinding, polishing, and rivet-work before ever touching the forge for real work—useful for aging up NPC armorers or explaining why a "master" might be quite old.
#### Fantasy Twists to Layer On
- **Monster-hide/bone armor** – Beyond steel, armorers might work dragon scale, chimera hide, or troll bone—each material with different magical properties (fire resistance, self-mending, etc.), and each requiring different tools/rituals to work.
- **Runesmith crossover** – Some armorers etch protective runes or sigils into the metal as part of the forging process, blurring the line between blacksmith and low-level enchanter.
- **Living metal** – A rare, unsettling material (adamantine-adjacent, or something stranger) that has to be "convinced" or "sung" into shape rather than simply hammered—armorers who work it are treated as eccentric or slightly mad.
- **Ancestral/spirit-bound armor** – Dwarven or ancestor-worshipping cultures might forge armor meant to house a spirit or ancestor's blessing, requiring funerary rites as part of the crafting process.
- **Fey-touched enchantment** – An armorer who trades with the Feywild might produce armor with quirky drawbacks (can't remove it during a full moon, sings when you lie) alongside its magical benefits.
- **War-forge economies** – During a war, armories might be magically accelerated—golems or bound elementals doing the hammering—raising ethical and political questions about labor and magic.
#### Social & Political Hooks
- **Armorers as war profiteers** – A master armorer's favor can shift the outcome of a conflict; they may be courted, bribed, or threatened by competing factions.
- **Sumptuary laws** – Just as commoners were once barred from wearing certain furs or colors, your setting could restrict who's allowed to wear plate, mithral, or magic armor—making armorers gatekeepers of a status symbol as much as protection.
- **Guild vs. crown tension** – A king might try to nationalize or tax the armorer's guild during wartime, causing friction with a traditionally independent trade.
- **The cursed commission** – A noble orders armor for an enemy (a wedding gift, a "peace offering") that's secretly flawed or cursed—armorers become pawns or conspirators in political assassination plots.
- **Retired soldier armorers** – Many historical armorers had combat experience themselves, which shaped their designs. An armorer NPC who's also a veteran adds weight to their expertise and could have old grudges or trauma tied to specific battle types.
#### Sensory/Flavor Details
- Forges ring with distinct rhythms—apprentices can identify which master is working by the hammer pattern alone.
- Finished pieces bear a maker's mark (a stamped sigil), which becomes a signature players can recognize or track.
- Fitting rooms with wax models or leather mannequins shaped to specific nobles' bodies, lining the walls like eerie portraits.
- The smell of hot metal, oil, and scorched leather; the armorer's hands permanently blackened and calloused.
- A "trophy wall" of notably strange commissions—an antler-crowned helm, a suit made for someone with six fingers, armor sized for a giant's child.
