### What a Guild Actually Was
A craft guild was an **association of practitioners of a specific trade**, organized within a town or city, that regulated nearly every aspect of that trade's practice: who could legally work in it, how they were trained, what quality standards applied, what prices could be charged, and how disputes within the trade were resolved. Guilds existed alongside (and are sometimes confused with) **merchant guilds**, which organized traders/merchants rather than craft producers — related but distinct institutions, with merchant guilds often holding broader town governance influence in many cities.
### Core Functions
**Controlling entry into the trade**: this is arguably the guild's most fundamental power — you generally couldn't legally practice a guilded trade in a town without going through the guild's recognized training path (apprentice → journeyman → master) and being formally admitted as a guild member. This created genuine economic protection for existing members by limiting competition.

**Setting and enforcing quality standards**: guilds inspected members' work, and substandard or fraudulent goods (recall the bread weight violations, adulterated ale, or shoddy ironwork mentioned throughout this list) could result in real penalties — fines, confiscation of goods, or in serious/repeated cases, expulsion from the guild and loss of the right to practice the trade at all.

**Regulating prices and wages**: guilds often set standardized pricing for goods/services and wages for journeymen, preventing both undercutting between members and (in theory) protecting customers from price gouging — though this regulatory function obviously also served to protect guild members' collective economic interests.

**Training and credentialing**: the apprentice-journeyman-master progression we've mentioned repeatedly was guild-administered:
- **Apprentices**: typically bound to a master for a fixed term (commonly 7 years), living in the master's household, learning the trade while providing labor, usually starting in early adolescence
- **Journeymen**: those who'd completed apprenticeship and could work for wages under a master, but weren't yet independent — the term comes from French _journée_ (day), reflecting day-wage labor status, not literal travel, though journeymen sometimes did travel between masters or cities to gain experience
- **Masters**: those who'd demonstrated sufficient skill (sometimes via a formal "masterpiece" submission demonstrating top-level craft ability) and had the capital to establish their own independent workshop, taking on apprentices and journeymen themselves

**Mutual aid and welfare**: guilds often functioned as a support network for members — providing assistance to members who fell ill, supporting widows and orphans of deceased members, and sometimes maintaining shared funds for these charitable purposes, similar to what we touched on with the beggar entry's mention of guild charity as one limited form of medieval social safety net.

**Religious and social life**: as covered with churches, guilds frequently maintained dedicated chapels or altars, participated in religious processions and feast day celebrations, performed guild-assigned scenes in civic mystery plays (mentioned with butchers), and provided a genuine social/communal identity beyond pure economic function — being a guild member meant belonging to a recognized community with shared identity, obligations, and mutual support.

**Self-governance**: guilds typically had their own internal leadership (wardens, masters, or similarly titled officers), met regularly to conduct business and resolve internal disputes, and maintained their own regulations (often called ordinances) — operating with real autonomy, though always within the broader authority of town government and, ultimately, royal or noble overlordship.
### Guild Relationships With Town Government
This varied significantly by region and period, but generally:
- **Guilds often held real political influence** in town governance — in many cities, guild membership or guild leadership directly correlated with eligibility for civic office, and town councils might be explicitly structured around guild representation
- **Tension between guilds and town authorities** was common — towns wanted tax revenue and orderly commerce, guilds wanted protected privilege and autonomy, and these interests didn't always align cleanly
- **Inter-guild rivalry and disputes**: as touched on with the cordwainer/cobbler divide, guilds sometimes fought over jurisdictional boundaries — who had the legal right to perform which specific tasks — leading to genuine, sometimes lengthy legal and political disputes between competing trades
### The Guildhall
Larger, wealthier guilds often constructed or maintained a dedicated **guildhall** — a building serving as the guild's administrative headquarters, meeting space, and sometimes ceremonial/feasting hall, reflecting and reinforcing the guild's civic standing and collective wealth. Surviving guildhalls in various European cities still stand as visible markers of how significant these institutions were.
### Limits and Exclusions
- **Gender**: most craft guilds were predominantly or exclusively male institutions, though there were real exceptions and complications — widows of guild masters sometimes could continue operating a workshop, and certain trades (like the alewife/brewster trade) had genuinely significant female participation, sometimes with their own guild structures in specific cities
- **Religious and ethnic restrictions**: guild membership in many places was restricted to Christians, creating the kind of exclusion that pushed groups like Jewish communities toward economic niches (moneylending, as covered with bankers) that existed outside guild-controlled trades entirely
- **Geographic and legal status restrictions**: full guild membership and the rights that came with it were often tied to specific town citizenship status, meaning outsiders, recent migrants, or those of unfree/serf legal status faced real additional barriers to entry even if they had the relevant skill
### Decline and Tension Over Time
Toward the later medieval period and beyond, guild structures faced growing strain:
- **Journeymen's associations**: as the path from journeyman to independent master became harder to achieve in some saturated, wealth-concentrated trades (since masters increasingly limited how many new masters could be admitted, protecting their own market position), journeymen sometimes formed their own separate organizations to advocate for their interests against established masters — an early echo of labor-versus-ownership tension
- **Economic shifts**: as trade scale and capital requirements grew in various industries, the traditional small-workshop guild model began facing competition from larger-scale, less guild-bound production arrangements in some sectors, a process that accelerated significantly in the early modern period
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks, especially useful since guilds are such a natural fit for D&D faction-building:
- **A guild as a genuine political faction** in your setting's city — with real influence over civic decisions, internal leadership struggles, and the capacity to support or obstruct PC goals depending on how the party navigates guild relationships
- **Inter-guild rivalry** (cordwainer/cobbler style) as a recurring local intrigue thread — PCs might get pulled into mediating, exploiting, or accidentally triggering a jurisdictional dispute between two trades
- **A magic-using guild** structured exactly like a craft guild — apprentice/journeyman/master wizard progression, guild-enforced quality and safety standards (very useful in-world justification for why random untrained magic-use might be restricted or dangerous), and a guildhall serving as a recognizable, navigable home base
- **Excluded groups finding their own guild-adjacent structures** — perhaps a setting's marginalized group (by race, origin, or magical nature) has formed its own informal mutual-aid network mirroring guild functions, without formal recognition or protection, mirroring the real historical Jewish community economic patterns discussed with banking
