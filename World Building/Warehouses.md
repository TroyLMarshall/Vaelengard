### Storage Infrastructure We've Referenced Throughout This List
Warehouses have appeared repeatedly as background infrastructure — the docks/wharfs entry mentioned them as essential port facilities, the trading post entry discussed the fondaco's warehousing function, the freight company entry noted goods storage as part of the transport chain, and the treasury entry covered secure document and valuables storage. Let's give dedicated storage infrastructure its own full treatment, since it was genuinely significant to medieval commerce.
### What Warehouses Actually Were
Medieval warehouse equivalents weren't standardized, purpose-built commercial facilities in quite the modern sense — storage infrastructure existed along a spectrum from simple cellar space to sophisticated, purpose-built commercial facilities, with the specific form depending on what was being stored, where, and for whom.
### Types of Medieval Storage
**Cellars and undercrofts**:
- The most universal and accessible storage form — underground or semi-underground spaces beneath buildings providing naturally cool, relatively humidity-stable storage
- **Merchant undercrofts**: urban merchants often used the ground-floor undercroft beneath their townhouse as commercial storage space — sometimes genuinely substantial stone-vaulted spaces surviving in various medieval towns today as some of the best-preserved medieval secular architecture
- Temperature stability made them particularly valuable for wine, ale, and food storage connecting to the tavern, brewer, and chef entries

**Purpose-built warehouse structures**:
- In major trading centers with sufficient commercial volume, dedicated warehouse buildings emerged — particularly significant in port cities where goods needed storage between ship unloading and onward distribution
- **Timber-framed warehouses**: as covered in the carpenter entry, timber-frame construction dominated — large-span structures with substantial floor loadings to handle heavy goods storage
- **Stone warehouses**: for particularly valuable or fire-sensitive storage, stone construction provided better security and fire resistance connecting to the fire risk themes throughout this list

**The Fondaco as warehouse**:
- As covered in the trading post entry, the fondaco combined warehousing with accommodation and commercial facilities — providing foreign merchant communities with integrated storage and living space
- Venice's fondaci specifically controlled access to warehouse space as a mechanism of commercial regulation — requiring foreign merchants to store goods in state-controlled facilities where they could be monitored and taxed

**Granaries**:
- Specialized food grain storage required specific design considerations — dry conditions, ventilation to prevent mold, and protection from vermin (connecting to the cat-keeping mentioned in the animal trainer entry)
- **Civic granaries**: some towns maintained public granaries as food security infrastructure — strategically storing grain to smooth out harvest variation and provide emergency food supplies during shortage, a form of civic welfare infrastructure connecting to the government official entry's discussion of civic administration

**Salt warehouses**:
- Salt's enormous importance for food preservation (covered throughout this list) and the significant trade in salt (connecting to the smuggler entry's discussion of salt monopolies and taxes) required specific salt storage infrastructure
- Salt's chemical properties (highly corrosive to metal, hygroscopic — absorbing moisture from the air) required specific construction considerations
### Who Owned and Operated Storage
**Merchant self-storage**: most prosperous merchants maintained their own storage space as part of their commercial premises — the undercroft beneath their townhouse providing basic storage for their own goods

**Commercial storage rental**: in major trading centers, renting storage space to others was a recognized commercial activity — someone with substantial undercroft or warehouse space could generate income by renting it to merchants needing temporary storage

**Guild storage**: as covered in the guild office entry, guilds sometimes maintained collective storage facilities for members — grain merchants' guilds storing collective grain reserves, textile guilds storing cloth and raw materials

**Inn and tavern storage**: as covered in those entries, establishments serving travelers maintained storage for guests' goods — a commercial service connecting accommodation with secure goods keeping

**Port authority storage**: in well-organized ports, some form of official storage facility under civic or merchant guild control provided bonded storage for goods awaiting customs clearance or onward transport
### What Was Stored and Special Requirements
**Textiles**: as the medieval economy's most important traded commodity (connecting to the dyer, weaver, and merchant guilds entries), cloth and wool required dry storage protecting against moisture, insects, and rodents

**Wine and ale**: liquid storage requiring appropriate temperature and humidity conditions — the undercroft's cool conditions particularly suited to wine storage connecting to the winemaker entry coming shortly

**Grain**: as discussed above, specialized conditions preventing mold and vermin

**Metals and metalwork**: as covered in the blacksmith, goldsmith, and armorer entries, finished metalwork and raw metal required storage — relatively straightforward compared to organic materials but requiring security given high value

**Spices and luxury goods**: high-value imported goods requiring both physical security and appropriate storage conditions — the apothecary entry's discussion of albarello storage jars reflecting the specific containers used for valuable dried goods

**Weapons and military equipment**: as covered in the militaries and armorer entries, military equipment required organized storage between campaigns — the armory function connecting directly to warehouse infrastructure
### Security Concerns
Valuable goods concentrated in one location created obvious security challenges:

**Physical security**: strong locks (connecting to the locksmith entry), substantial doors and walls, and sometimes guards (connecting to the guard entry) protecting valuable stored goods

**Fire risk**: as covered repeatedly throughout this list, fire was the constant threat to wooden stored goods — the docks/wharfs entry specifically mentioned fire as a significant warehouse concern, since textiles, grain, and other stored goods were often highly flammable

**Theft and fraud**: as covered in the thieves' guild entry, concentrations of valuable goods attracted criminal attention — sophisticated theft operations specifically targeting warehouse storage existing in major trading centers

**Flood risk**: waterfront warehouses (the most commercially located position) also faced obvious flood risk from high tides, storm surges, and river flooding
### Customs and Bonded Storage
In sophisticated trading contexts, the intersection of warehouse storage with customs regulation created specific controlled storage arrangements:

**Bonded storage**: goods on which import duties hadn't yet been paid could be stored in approved facilities under customs authority's oversight — allowing merchants to store goods before deciding whether to import them for local sale (paying duty) or re-export them (no duty payable), with the storage facility providing the physical control mechanism ensuring duty wasn't evaded

**Customs oversight**: as covered in the docks/wharfs entry, customs officials' ability to inspect stored goods was essential to revenue collection — warehouse design sometimes reflected this requirement, with accessible inspection points built into storage arrangements
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A warehouse district as an urban adventure location — a specific area of a port city characterized by large storage buildings, commercial activity, and the specific population (dock workers, carmen, warehouse keepers, merchants' agents) that warehouse commerce generates, distinct in character from the residential or market districts covered elsewhere in this list
- Hidden compartments and concealed storage within legitimate warehouse space as a smuggling infrastructure detail — connecting to the smuggler entry, a legitimate warehouse keeper whose facility includes specific concealed spaces for goods avoiding customs inspection
- A warehouse fire as a dramatic, high-stakes event — the concentrated value of stored goods, the fire's rapid spread through flammable stored material, and the immediate impact on multiple merchants whose goods are stored there creating genuine crisis with multiple affected parties and competing interests
- Bonded storage's legal implications as a plot element — goods stored under customs oversight having specific legal status that affects who can access them, what can be done with them, and what legal consequences follow from their theft or damage
