### A Craft We've Referenced Repeatedly — Now Given Full Treatment
We've touched on woodcarving throughout this list — the artist/sculptor entry covered decorative stone and wood carving for churches, the carpenter entry mentioned joiners doing carved furniture, the furniture maker entry referenced carved decorative elements like linenfold paneling, the church entry described elaborate carved choir stalls and rood screens — so let's bring together the woodcarver as a distinct specialist, since ornamental woodcarving was genuinely different from either structural carpentry or basic joinery.
### Woodcarving vs. Carpentry vs. Joinery — The Distinctions
As covered in the carpenter and furniture maker entries, medieval woodworking split into several distinct specializations:
- **Carpenters**: structural timber framing — the load-bearing skeleton of buildings
- **Joiners**: precise fitted furniture and interior woodwork — doors, paneling, chests
- **Turners**: lathe-worked rounded objects — bowls, furniture legs, tool handles
- **Woodcarvers**: ornamental, decorative, and figurative carving — the artistic end of woodworking

A woodcarver's primary product wasn't structural or purely functional but **visual and decorative** — figures, patterns, scenes, and ornamental detail that made surfaces beautiful or symbolically meaningful rather than simply useful.
### What Woodcarvers Actually Made
**Religious sculpture and devotional objects**:
- **Altarpieces**: often elaborate, multi-paneled carved wood structures behind church altars — some of the most ambitious woodcarving commissions, combining architectural structure with figurative carving in complex assemblages
- **Devotional figures**: carved representations of Christ, the Virgin Mary, and saints for church interiors and private devotional use — ranging from simple, locally-produced figures to sophisticated workshop products
- **Crucifixes**: carved representations of Christ on the cross, required in virtually every church
- **Reliquary caskets**: decorated wooden containers for holy relics, sometimes with extraordinary carved detail

**Church furniture and fittings**:
- **Choir stalls**: as mentioned in the church and furniture maker entries, carved wooden seating for clergy in cathedral and monastic choirs — often with misericords (small carved ledge seats allowing clergy to rest while technically standing) featuring remarkably inventive, sometimes whimsical carved imagery
- **Rood screens**: as covered in the church entry, elaborately carved wooden partitions separating nave from chancel — often the most ambitious single woodcarving commission in a given church
- **Pulpits**: carved wooden preaching platforms
- **Font covers**: often elaborate carved wood canopies protecting baptismal fonts
- **Bench ends**: carved decorative terminations to church pew rows — another location for the inventive, sometimes surprising imagery that characterizes medieval ecclesiastical woodcarving

**Secular decorative work**:
- **Roof bosses**: carved decorative elements at the intersection of roof vault ribs — sometimes high enough that fine detail wasn't practically visible from below, yet still executed with real craft care
- **Heraldic carving**: coats of arms, heraldic animals, and similar status-display imagery for noble patrons
- **Furniture decoration**: carved ornament on prestigious furniture pieces — the linenfold paneling mentioned in the furniture maker entry being a specific, recognizable decorative style
- **Ship's figureheads and decorative carvings**: as touched on in the carpenter/shipwright entry, ships carried decorative carved elements

**Misericords — A Specific Category Worth Highlighting**:
- The small ledge seats carved on the underside of hinged choir stall seats, allowing clergy to rest during long services while appearing to stand
- Because misericords were hidden when seats were up, carvers had unusual creative freedom — producing some of the most inventive and sometimes frankly irreverent imagery in all medieval art
- Secular, comic, and sometimes bawdy subjects appear alongside religious imagery — tournaments, everyday life scenes, fantastic creatures, and comic vignettes giving genuine insight into medieval popular imagination
### Techniques and Tools
**Chisels and gouges**: the primary woodcarving tools — chisels for straight cuts and flat surfaces, gouges (curved-section chisels) for hollowed surfaces and rounded forms
- Various sizes for different scales of work — from broad blocking-out chisels to fine detail tools
- Quality tool steel essential — carving tools needed to hold a sharp edge while being hard enough to cut even dense, dry wood cleanly

**Mallets**: driving chisels and gouges for heavier cuts — wooden mallets rather than metal hammers to avoid damaging tool handles

**V-tools and parting tools**: for cutting crisp lines and separating adjacent carved elements

**Knives**: for fine detail work where chisel and gouge access was difficult

**Rasps and files**: for surface refining between tool cuts

**The approach — roughing to finishing**:
1. **Blocking out**: establishing the major forms with large tools, removing bulk material rapidly
2. **Modeling**: defining specific forms more precisely — figure anatomy, drapery folds, architectural elements
3. **Detailing**: adding fine surface detail — facial features, hair, textile texture, decorative patterns
4. **Finishing**: smoothing tool marks where smooth surfaces were desired, or deliberately leaving tooled texture where this suited the work
### Wood Selection
Different wood species suited different carving applications:

**Oak**: the dominant English carving wood — hard, durable, with consistent grain — used for most ecclesiastical woodwork and exterior applications where weather or wear resistance mattered

- Sometimes carved green (unseasoned) when large timber needed to be worked quickly, then allowed to dry — risking checking (surface cracking) but sometimes unavoidable for large commissions

**Limewood (linden)**: in German and Central European traditions, limewood was preferred for fine figurative carving — its tight, consistent grain and relatively soft texture allowing exceptionally fine detail work without the grain "fighting" the carver, producing the extraordinary technical achievement visible in carved altarpieces by artists like Tilman Riemenschneider

**Walnut**: used in some continental traditions for fine furniture carving

**Box and fruitwoods**: for smaller, exceptionally detailed work — very fine grain allowing intricate small-scale carving
### Polychromy — The Painted Original
An important corrective to modern expectations: medieval carved wood figures and altarpieces were **almost universally painted** in vivid colors, not left as bare wood
- The "natural wood" aesthetic we now associate with medieval carving is largely the result of paint loss over centuries
- Original polychrome finish included flesh tones for figures, gold leaf for halos and decorative elements, vivid colors for drapery — producing a visually rich, colorful effect quite different from modern perceptions of "medieval wood carving"
- Painting was sometimes done by the carver themselves, sometimes by a separate specialist painter — the two crafts sometimes distinct, sometimes combined in one workshop
### Guild Structure and Workshop Organization
- Woodcarvers organized into guilds in towns with sufficient demand — sometimes within broader carpentry/joinery guilds, sometimes as distinct carving guilds where workshop concentration warranted separation
- **Workshop practice**: significant commissions (large altarpieces, cathedral choir stalls) typically required workshop organization with a master designing and executing key elements while journeymen and apprentices produced more routine elements — a collaborative production model similar to the construction company entry's discussion of cathedral building organization
- **Pattern books and templates**: design patterns for standard decorative elements circulated through workshops, allowing consistent reproduction of recognized motifs — connecting to the scribe entry's discussion of pattern transmission through manuscript culture
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A carved wooden figure with unusual properties — perhaps a devotional figure that moves slightly when unobserved, an altarpiece whose carved scenes change to depict current events, or a heraldic carving that warns of danger — grounding magical objects in genuine craft tradition rather than generic "magic items"
- A woodcarver's misericord commission as a worldbuilding detail revealing character — what a specific carver chose to depict on hidden misericords (where creative freedom was greatest) telling PCs something about that person's actual values, humor, or secret knowledge
- Polychromy as a mystery tool — a carved figure whose faded paint conceals something (a map, a message, a specific marking) that was painted onto the surface and is now barely visible under centuries of grime, requiring careful investigation to reveal
- A master woodcarver's workshop as a location combining genuine craft environment with social complexity — apprentices working on routine elements, the master executing fine detail, a wealthy patron's representative checking on commission progress, and the specific commission's subject matter potentially plot-relevant
