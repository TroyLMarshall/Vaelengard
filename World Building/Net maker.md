### The Craft and Its Structure
Net making was a widespread, humble, but genuinely essential trade wherever communities depended on fishing, fowling, or hunting for food — which in medieval Europe meant nearly everywhere with a coastline, a river, a lake, or open fields worth snaring birds over. Unlike the guild-regulated urban crafts, net making was often a **rural, semi-domestic trade** — closely tied to fishing communities, where net-making skill might belong to the fishing household itself (made and repaired by fishermen, their wives, or dedicated net-menders) rather than existing as a fully separate specialist profession in every town. In larger fishing ports, though, dedicated **net makers ("netters")** and **ropers/rope-makers** (a closely related trade, since both worked cordage) did operate as recognized tradespeople, sometimes with their own minor guild organization.

**Materials and technique** — Nets were made from **hemp, flax, or nettle fiber**, twisted into cord and then knotted by hand into mesh, one knot at a time, using a **netting needle** (a small shuttle-like tool wound with cord) and a **mesh gauge/spacer** (a flat gauge determining knot spacing, which set the net's mesh size for its intended catch). This is slow, repetitive, meditative handwork — a skilled net maker could produce consistent, even mesh at real speed, but a full net for serious fishing (especially large seine or trawl-type nets) represented substantial hours of labor. Nets also needed **preservation treatment** — tarring, or steeping in bark tannin solutions ("barking" the net) — to slow rot from constant wet use, since untreated plant-fiber cordage degraded quickly in water.

**Beyond fishing** — Net making covered a wider range of uses than just fish: **fowling nets** for catching birds (a significant food source, especially waterfowl), **snares and traps**, **cargo nets** for shipping and dockwork, and **hair nets/cauls** worn as a fashion accessory by medieval women (a much finer, decorative version of the same knotting skill, often in silk rather than hemp — a nice split between "rough utility trade" and "fine domestic craft" using the same core technique, similar in spirit to the whitesmith's black/white divide).

**Economic and social position** — Because it required no expensive tools or forge, no guild membership in most places, and could be done seasonally or as a supplement to fishing income, net making sat toward the **bottom of craft prestige** — reliable, necessary, low-capital, low-status work. It was, however, genuinely difficult to do _well_: a poorly made or poorly maintained net meant a failed catch, a torn net at sea, or worse, and fishing communities depended entirely on this unglamorous skill being done right. Coastal and riverine settlements without a dedicated net maker relied on informal cooperative net-repair — communal mending sessions after a haul, a very visual, communal piece of daily life you can use for scene-setting.

**Guild/legal notes** — In some fishing ports, net mesh size was **regulated by local ordinance** to prevent overfishing of young/small fish — an early, practical conservation measure enforced by town or guild authorities, with fines for using illegally fine mesh. This is a nice, concrete piece of medieval bureaucracy in the same vein as the aleconner testing ale quality.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Survival, Nature (or Sleight of Hand, for the fine handwork)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Weaver's Tools, or a custom "Net Maker's Tools" (netting needle + mesh gauge)
- **Feat:** Skilled or Alert (net-menders develop a sharp eye for flaws and patterns)
- **Equipment:** Netting needle and mesh gauge, a partially finished net, 50 feet of hempen rope, worn working clothes, 5 gp

This background pairs naturally with a **Ranger or Druid (coastal/river upbringing, practical survival skill, comfort with wild environments), a Rogue (repurposing net-making tools and knot-skill toward traps, restraints, or improvised weapons), or a Fighter/Barbarian from a hardworking fishing community**. Mechanically, this is a great flavor justification for carrying and being proficient with a **net weapon**, and thematically it's a strong "humble origins, practical hands-on skill, community-minded" background — someone who grew up mending nets by lamplight after a haul.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Net Ordinance Inspector** — A minor town official who checks mesh sizes at the docks to enforce conservation law, disliked by fishers trying to cut corners in a lean season — a small, textured bit of local bureaucracy the party might run afoul of or use for information.
- **The Barking Widow** — Runs the communal tarring/tanning vats where nets are preserved, and by extension knows every boat's condition, every crew's habits, and who's been out fishing (or doing something else) at odd hours.
- **The Fowler-Netter** — Specializes in bird-catching nets and snares rather than fishing nets, deeply knowledgeable about local wildlife patterns, migratory routes, and terrain — a natural NPC guide, tracker, or information source for a wilderness-adjacent hook.
- **The Caul-Maker** — Makes fine silk hairnets for wealthy women in town, using the exact same core skill as the rough dockside net menders but occupying a completely different social world — a nice "same craft, two classes" NPC pairing with a fishing-village net maker.
- **The Net Maker Who Doesn't Fish** — An outsider or outcast who took up net making because it required no guild membership and few tools, now quietly excellent at it despite (or because of) not belonging to the fishing community they serve — a "skilled but never quite accepted" NPC.
### Game Hooks
- **The Illegal Mesh** — A net maker is caught producing illegally fine-mesh nets for a fisher trying to scrape by in a bad season — a small, morally gray "desperation vs. law" hook the party might be asked to investigate or quietly overlook.
- **Something in the Net** — A haul comes up with something far stranger than fish (a body, a magic item, a piece of a wreck, a creature) and the net maker/fisher who found it wants it gone, understood, or protected before word spreads.
- **The Torn Net Sabotage** — A fishing community's nets are being deliberately cut or ruined overnight, threatening the town's food supply — investigation hook blending mundane rivalry with the possibility of something worse (a rival village, a smuggler covering tracks, a creature in the water).
- **The Snare Line** — A skilled fowler-netter's traplines in a nearby wood keep coming up empty or destroyed, and they suspect poaching, a rival, or something unnatural moving through the area — a good low-level wilderness investigation/combat hook.
- **Net and Rope, Bound Tight** — The party needs specialty cordage or netting for an unusual job (climbing a cursed structure, trapping a creature alive, rigging a trap of their own) and the local net maker is the only one with both the skill and the raw materials to make exactly what's needed in time.
