### Terminology: Cordwainers vs. Cobblers
This distinction actually mattered a lot in medieval guild structure, more than it does in casual modern usage:
- **Cordwainers**: made _new_ shoes and boots from new leather — the term derives from "cordovan" leather, originally fine leather from Córdoba, Spain, reflecting the trade's roots in working with quality new material
- **Cobblers**: repaired old, worn shoes — and crucially, in many medieval towns, **guild rules legally restricted cobblers from using new leather or making new shoes**, reserving that more profitable work exclusively for cordwainers. This wasn't just custom — it was actively enforced guild protectionism, with real legal disputes when a cobbler overstepped into cordwainer territory.

So a "boot maker" in the sense of someone crafting new footwear from scratch would specifically be a cordwainer, while a cobbler kept old boots functional for those who couldn't afford new ones.
### What They Made
- **Boots**: various heights and styles, from simple ankle boots to taller riding boots for those who could afford and needed them (horsemen, soldiers, wealthier travelers)
- **Shoes**: the more common everyday footwear — simple, low-cut shoes for most of the population
- **Pattens**: wooden-soled overshoes worn over regular shoes to protect them from mud and wet ground — a practical solution to medieval streets that were, frankly, often filthy
- **Specialized footwear**: poulaines (the famously long, pointed-toe shoes fashionable among the wealthy in the later medieval period, sometimes so exaggeratedly long they required internal stiffening or were even regulated by sumptuary laws limiting toe length by social class)
- **Slippers and indoor footwear** for wealthier households
### Materials and Process
- **Leather**: primarily cattle hide, though finer goods might use goat or other leathers — leather quality and tanning method significantly affected both cost and durability
- **Tanning connection**: cordwainers depended on **tanners**, who processed raw animal hides into usable leather through a genuinely unpleasant process (involving substances like lime and even dung or urine in some tanning methods) — tanneries were typically located at town edges or downwind due to the smell, and the tanner-cordwainer relationship was an essential supply chain link
- **Lasts**: wooden foot-shaped forms used to shape the shoe during construction — a cordwainer needed a range of lasts in different sizes, and custom work for wealthy clients might involve a last shaped to an individual's specific foot
- **Construction technique**: medieval shoes were predominantly made using the **turnshoe method** — the shoe was sewn together inside-out (easier to stitch the seams this way), then turned right-side-out once assembly was complete, before the sole was attached. This differs from later welted construction techniques and produced a softer, more flexible shoe than what you might picture in a sturdier modern boot.
- **Wooden pegs and nails**: used in some constructions, particularly for sole attachment, alongside stitching
- **Awls, needles, and waxed thread**: the cordwainer's hand tools for the actual sewing work
### Guild Organization
- Cordwainers typically formed well-established **guilds** in towns with sufficient demand, following the standard apprentice-journeyman-master progression covered in earlier trades
- Guild regulation covered not just the cobbler/cordwainer divide, but also quality standards, pricing, and sometimes restrictions on who could sell footwear within town bounds (protecting local guild members from outside competition)
- **Patron saint**: Saint Crispin (and sometimes Crispinian) became associated with shoemakers, giving the trade a recognized religious/guild identity reflected in feast day celebrations and guild patronage
### Social Status and Footwear as Class Marker
Footwear, like much medieval clothing, functioned as a visible class signal:
- **The poor** often went barefoot, especially in warmer months or among children, or wore the simplest, cheapest constructed shoes
- **The poulaine's exaggerated length** became such a pronounced status marker among the wealthy that several regions enacted **sumptuary laws** specifically regulating how long a shoe's point could be based on social rank — a vivid example of how thoroughly medieval society tried to encode hierarchy into visible dress
- **Sturdy boots** for riding, working, or military use represented a different but equally meaningful practical investment — good boots were genuinely valuable, durable possessions worth repairing repeatedly (feeding the cobbler trade) rather than replacing
### A Trade Tied to Daily Discomfort
Worth noting for flavor: medieval shoes, especially turnshoe construction without much in the way of arch support or hard soles, likely provided far less comfort and protection than modern footwear — archaeological evidence (shoes survive reasonably well, similar to the bone artifacts mentioned earlier) shows significant wear patterns and even evidence of related foot problems in skeletal remains from this era. It wasn't a trivial craft; durable, well-fitted footwear had genuine quality-of-life impact.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A cordwainer who makes boots with subtle enchantments — silent steps, surefooting on ice, or resistance to fatigue — turning a mundane trade into a quietly valuable specialist for adventuring parties
- The cordwainer/cobbler guild divide as a small but textured local conflict — perhaps a talented cobbler chafing against guild rules preventing them from doing the "real" work they're capable of, a nice low-stakes story about ambition and restrictive institutions
- Sumptuary laws around footwear (the poulaine length restrictions) as a worldbuilding detail revealing your setting's class anxieties — maybe a noble house obsessed with shoe-point length as a status battlefield, played for some social comedy
- A skilled boot maker as a trusted, recurring NPC who PCs return to for gear maintenance between adventures — practical, grounding, and a nice contrast to flashier magic shops
