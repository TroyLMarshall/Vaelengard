### A Trade Sharply Divided by Class and Legal Right
We've touched on hunting repeatedly throughout this list — falconry and hunting dogs in the animal trainer entry, the ranger/forester D&D class mapping, forest law mentioned with charcoal makers and government officials — so let's bring the full picture together, since hunting in medieval Europe was less a single unified "profession" and more a sharply class-divided activity with real legal teeth behind who could do it and how.
### Hunting as Elite Privilege — Forest Law
This is the essential starting point: across much of medieval Europe, **hunting rights were heavily restricted by law**, particularly for large game, and particularly within designated **royal forests** or noble hunting preserves.
- **Royal forests**: in England specifically, vast tracts of land (not necessarily all actually wooded — "forest" was a legal designation, not purely a description of terrain) were set aside under special **forest law**, distinct from common law, specifically to preserve hunting rights (particularly deer and boar) for the king
- **Forest law's harsh reputation**: forest law was historically notorious for its severity — restricting or criminalizing activities ordinary people would otherwise consider normal subsistence use of land (gathering wood, grazing animals, and especially hunting), with real, sometimes brutal punishment for poaching
- **Noble hunting preserves**: similar restricted hunting grounds existed at the level of individual noble estates, with gamekeepers and foresters (covered below) enforcing the lord's exclusive or controlled hunting rights
- **Class-based game restrictions**: similar to the falconry bird hierarchy covered in the animal trainer entry, the _right_ to hunt specific game was often explicitly tied to social rank — deer and boar hunting in particular carried serious status association, while smaller game might be more broadly accessible
### Hunting as Noble Sport and Social Activity
For nobility, hunting wasn't simply about acquiring food — it served real social, martial-training, and status functions, as touched on in the gentlemen/ladies entry:
- **The hunt as martial training**: hunting large, genuinely dangerous game (particularly boar) provided real practice in horsemanship, weapon use, and physical courage relevant to actual warfare
- **Elaborate hunting culture**: specific terminology, ritual, and etiquette surrounded noble hunting (different horn calls for different situations, specific roles for different hunt participants, formal rules about how a kill was processed and divided) — genuinely sophisticated codified practice rather than simple pursuit
- **Hunting with hounds and falcons**: as covered in the animal trainer entry, this required real specialized staff (kennel masters/berners, falconers) supporting the noble hunter, making a "hunt" a substantial coordinated household activity rather than one person heading into the woods alone
- **Social and diplomatic function**: hunting parties provided informal settings for political negotiation, alliance-building, and demonstrating hospitality to guests, similar in social function to the feasting covered in the chef and gentlemen/ladies entries
### Professional and Subsistence Hunters
**Foresters/gamekeepers**: employed specifically to manage and protect a lord's hunting grounds — preventing poaching, managing game populations, and supporting the lord's own hunting activities, representing a genuine specialized employment position rather than free, independent hunting for personal gain

**Professional huntsmen**: wealthy households employed dedicated huntsmen as skilled staff, similar to falconers and kennel masters — managing the practical logistics of organizing and conducting hunts

**Poachers**: given how restrictive forest law and noble hunting preserves could be, **illegal hunting for subsistence or profit** was a genuine, persistent feature of medieval rural life — ordinary people hunting on technically restricted land to supplement inadequate diets, sometimes organized into more deliberate poaching operations, and sometimes romanticized in later folklore (Robin Hood being the most famous cultural touchstone, even though the historical reality behind such legends is considerably murkier than the folklore)

**Subsistence hunting on permitted land**: not all hunting was restricted — smaller game, particularly on common land or land without specific forest law restriction, provided genuine supplementary food for ordinary rural households, especially trapping rabbits, birds, and similar accessible game
### Methods and Equipment
- **Bows**: as covered extensively in the bowyer and fletcher entries, archery served hunting alongside its military application
- **Spears**: particularly associated with boar hunting specifically, given the genuine danger of a charging boar requiring a weapon that could be braced and used defensively as well as offensively
- **Nets and traps**: for smaller game, snares and traps provided a less dramatic but practical hunting method, particularly accessible to those without the equipment or rights for more formal hunting
- **Hounds and falcons**: as covered in the animal trainer entry, trained animals were central to elite hunting practice specifically
### What Was Hunted
- **Deer**: the premier elite game animal across much of medieval Europe, heavily protected under forest law
- **Boar**: genuinely dangerous, prestigious game requiring real courage and skill, valued both for the hunt's difficulty and the meat
- **Wolves**: hunted both as genuine pest/threat management (livestock predation was a real practical concern) and sometimes as a hunting challenge in its own right
- **Smaller game**: rabbits, hares, various birds — more broadly accessible across social classes, less encumbered by the same strict elite hunting-right restrictions as deer/boar
- **Wildfowl**: hunted using various methods including falconry (as covered) and netting
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- Forest law's genuine harshness as a worldbuilding tool for exploring class tension and injustice in your setting — a sympathetic peasant poacher caught hunting to feed their family, facing brutal punishment under a lord's hunting-preservation law, offering real moral complexity for PCs to navigate
- A Robin-Hood-style outlaw poacher band as a faction your PCs might ally with, oppose, or simply encounter — grounded in genuine historical legal tension even if the specific folklore is romanticized
- A noble hunting party as a social set-piece scene — letting PCs navigate elite hunting culture's etiquette and politics while something goes wrong (a genuine monster encountered instead of the expected boar, sabotage, or a political assassination attempt disguised as a hunting accident)
- A gamekeeper/forester NPC enforcing a lord's hunting rights as a recurring local authority figure — not necessarily villainous, but representing a genuine, historically-grounded source of local tension between PCs (or sympathetic local poachers) and lordly authority
