### Another Modern Organizational Concept, Real Underlying Practice
Like "construction company" and "caravan guild," there wasn't a single business entity called a "freight company" — but moving goods overland and by water was obviously essential to medieval trade, and it was organized through several real, overlapping structures: carters, the merchant guilds we've already covered, and some genuinely sophisticated long-distance shipping arrangements. Let's pull together what actually moved freight in medieval Europe.
### Overland Freight — Carters and Carriers
- **Carters/carriers**: individuals or small operations owning carts, wagons, and draft animals (horses or oxen) who transported goods for hire — this is the closest direct equivalent to a small modern trucking operation, just at vastly smaller scale and speed
- **Common carriers**: some carriers operated on **fixed, regular routes** between specific towns, building a recognized, reliable service reputation — merchants and others could count on a known carrier's regular schedule for moving smaller consignments of goods, similar in concept to a modern scheduled freight service, just operating at a fraction of the speed and volume
- **Pack trains**: for routes unsuitable for wheeled carts (poor roads, mountain passes), goods moved via packhorses or mules carrying panniers, as mentioned in the caravan guild discussion
- **Legal liability**: carriers bore some recognized legal responsibility for goods entrusted to them, and disputes over lost, damaged, or stolen goods in transit generated real legal precedent and customary practice around a carrier's duty of care — an early form of what we'd now think of as freight/shipping liability law
### River Transport — Often Preferred Over Roads
As mentioned with the carpenter and docks entries, **water transport was frequently faster, cheaper, and capable of moving much greater volume** than overland cartage, given medieval road quality:
- **River barges and boats**: moved bulk goods (grain, timber, stone, wine) along navigable rivers, taking advantage of current and, where developed, towing by animals or men along riverside paths
- **Boatmen and bargemen**: operated this transport, sometimes organized into their own guild-like structures in major river trade centers
- **River tolls**: similar to road tolls and ferry rights, river transport was subject to tolls collected at various points by lords or towns controlling stretches of river — a genuine ongoing cost and occasional source of dispute for river-based freight
### Sea Freight — The Largest Scale Operations
- **Merchant ships**: the largest-volume freight movement happened by sea, particularly for major trade goods — wool, wine, grain, and the long-distance luxury goods (spices, dyes) discussed throughout this list
- **Chartering**: as mentioned in the boat rentals entry, merchants without their own vessels chartered cargo space or entire ships for specific voyages — this is genuinely the closest medieval equivalent to "hiring a freight company" in a recognizable commercial sense
- **Ship's masters and crews**: operated under complex customary and increasingly codified maritime law governing cargo responsibility, profit-sharing arrangements, and what happened if a voyage went wrong (storm damage, piracy, jettisoned cargo to save a ship in distress)
- **General average**: a genuinely sophisticated piece of medieval maritime law worth knowing — if cargo had to be deliberately jettisoned to save a ship in an emergency, the resulting loss was shared proportionally among all cargo owners on the voyage, not borne solely by whoever's goods got thrown overboard. This represents real, fairly advanced risk-sharing legal thinking.
- **Marine insurance**: by the later medieval period, especially in Italian maritime trading cities, early forms of marine insurance developed — merchants paying a premium to have a voyage's risk underwritten by others, connecting back to the sophisticated banking and financial instruments covered in the banker entry
### The Hanseatic League — Organized Freight at Scale
Worth circling back to this since it's genuinely the closest thing to a coordinated, multi-city "freight network" in medieval Europe: the **Hanseatic League** (covered in the [[caravan guild]] entry) didn't just facilitate trade relationships — it organized substantial, regular freight movement of bulk goods (grain, timber, furs, fish, cloth) across an extensive network of ports and inland trading posts, with shared standards, legal protections, and infrastructure investment supporting this movement.
### Specialized Freight Considerations
- **Perishables**: fish (especially salted/preserved, as covered with fishermen) and other perishable goods required faster transport or proper preservation before/during transit
- **Valuable/fragile goods**: fine cloth, spices, and luxury items needed more careful handling and security than bulk grain or timber, sometimes commanding higher transport fees reflecting both the care required and the value at risk
- **Bulk vs. value-density**: heavy, low-value bulk goods (grain, timber, stone) generally needed water transport to be economically viable over distance, while high-value, low-bulk goods (spices, fine textiles, precious metals) could justify the higher cost of overland transport even across longer distances
### Risk and Security
- **Banditry on roads**: as covered with the caravan guild discussion, overland freight faced real robbery risk, motivating group travel and sometimes hired guards
- **Piracy at sea**: similarly significant for maritime freight, particularly in certain waters or during periods of conflict
- **Weather and accident**: both overland and water transport faced genuine, costly risk from weather, accidents, and infrastructure failure (bridges, roads, and ships were all less robust than modern equivalents)
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A regular carter/freight route between two settlements as a recurring logistics element in your campaign — PCs might escort a shipment, investigate why goods keep disappearing en route, or notice something's wrong when an expected delivery doesn't arrive
- General average and early marine insurance as a worldbuilding detail for a sophisticated trading city — letting you show genuine commercial/legal sophistication without needing modern terminology, useful if a PC needs to navigate a cargo-loss dispute or insurance claim in-world
- A Hanseatic-League-style trade confederation (as suggested in the caravan guild entry) running organized freight operations as a major faction — offering PCs escort work, investigation into smuggling or sabotage, or a path into larger political/economic intrigue
- Magical or monster-related freight complications — a route plagued by something supernatural rather than ordinary bandits, a cargo that requires special magical handling/containment, or a "freight company" that specializes in transporting dangerous magical goods other carriers won't touch
