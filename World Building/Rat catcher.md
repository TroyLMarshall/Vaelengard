### The Craft and Its Structure
A **rat catcher** (sometimes called a "ratter" or, in later terminology, a "rat officer") made a living killing or capturing rats, mice, and other vermin infesting homes, granaries, warehouses, and ships — a trade defined almost entirely by **practical necessity, low formal status, and a strange, informal expertise** that sat well outside any guild structure. Unlike most trades covered in this list, rat catching had **no guild, no apprenticeship system, and no formal credentialing** for most of the medieval period — it was itinerant, freelance, often part-time work, taken up by the poor, the itinerant, or anyone willing to do genuinely unpleasant, sometimes dangerous hands-on pest work for a fee paid by whoever needed vermin cleared out.

**Why it mattered** — Rats and mice caused real, significant economic damage in a subsistence-level agrarian society: destroying stored grain (the difference between a family or town surviving winter comfortably or facing real hardship), gnawing through structural material, contaminating food stores, and — though medieval people didn't understand the actual mechanism — genuinely serving as **disease vectors**, most catastrophically in the transmission chain behind the Black Death (rats and their fleas carrying plague bacteria, though the precise causal understanding is modern; medieval people connected rats with sickness and misfortune in a general, correct-instinct-but-wrong-mechanism way, associating vermin infestation with a place being unclean, cursed, or unlucky rather than germ-theory disease transmission). This gave rat catching a real, if informally recognized, public-health function centuries before anyone understood why it mattered in scientific terms.

**Tools and methods** — Rat catchers worked with **traps** (various wood-and-cord or pit-trap designs), **ferrets** (a genuinely widespread and effective medieval pest-control technique — trained ferrets sent into burrows and wall-gaps to flush or kill rats, a method requiring real practical animal-handling skill and a trained ferret or two as valuable working animals), **dogs** bred or trained specifically for ratting (small, quick terrier-type dogs excelling at this task, a real precursor to later formally-bred ratting terrier breeds), and **poison** (various toxic substances, applied with real risk to the user and to anyone else who might encounter poisoned bait or a poisoned rat carcass — a genuinely dangerous, imprecise tool in an era without any real toxicological understanding or safety practice). A skilled rat catcher combined practical animal handling, some rough knowledge of rat behavior and nesting patterns, and a tolerance for genuinely unpleasant, sometimes dangerous physical work (rat bites carried real infection risk, and handling large numbers of dead or dying vermin was inherently unsanitary work by any standard).

**Social position — low, itinerant, but not stigmatized like the executioner** — Rat catching sat at the bottom of the informal trade hierarchy: no guild protection, no formal training pipeline, often taken up by those with few other options — the poor, transient laborers, or people supplementing income from another trade. Unlike the executioner's hereditary, legally-marked dishonor, rat catching wasn't formally disreputable so much as simply **beneath notice** — low-paid, unglamorous, and largely invisible work that respectable society needed done but didn't think much about who did it or how. Rat catchers moved between towns and estates as itinerant freelancers, hired for a specific job (clear this granary, this ship's hold, this manor house) rather than holding a settled civic post.

**A useful, informal expertise** — Precisely because the work required understanding vermin behavior, nesting patterns, and practical extermination methods that ordinary townsfolk didn't bother learning, an experienced rat catcher accumulated genuine, if entirely informal and non-institutional, **practical expertise** — knowing which buildings were prone to infestation, how vermin moved through a town's structures and sewers/drains, and where rats (and therefore, unknowingly, disease risk) tended to concentrate. This gives a rat catcher character a plausible, grounded reason to know a town's hidden physical infrastructure — cellars, drains, wall-gaps, crawlspaces — better than almost anyone else, including people with far higher formal status.

**Ships and granaries — specialized niches** — Rat catchers found particularly steady, valued work aboard **ships** (where rat infestation threatened stored food and cargo on long voyages, and where a ship's ratting cat or trained ratter was a genuinely valued, practical necessity) and at **granaries and warehouses** (where large stored-grain quantities were an obvious and constant vermin target) — both contexts where a rat catcher's skill translated into real, direct economic protection worth paying for.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Stealth, Survival (or Animal Handling, for the ferret/dog-handling angle)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Poisoner's Kit, or proficiency handling a specific trained animal (ferret/terrier) as a feature
- **Feat:** Alert or Skilled
- **Equipment:** Traps and snares, a trained ferret or small ratting dog, a small vial of pest poison, worn traveling clothes, 5 gp

This background is an excellent, distinctive fit for a **Rogue** — genuine, grounded expertise in a town's hidden physical spaces (drains, cellars, wall-gaps, crawlspaces), comfort with stealth and small dangerous animals, and a plausible reason to move freely and unnoticed through places others avoid. It also suits a **Ranger** (animal handling, practical survival skill, itinerant lifestyle) or an **Artificer** leaning into trap-making. The genuinely low, itinerant, "beneath notice" social position makes this one of the strongest backgrounds for a character who's used to being underestimated or overlooked — a real mechanical and roleplay advantage in disguise.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Itinerant Ratter** — Moves between towns and estates taking vermin-clearing jobs, knows an unusual amount about the hidden physical layout of every place they've worked — a good recurring low-key information source or guide to a town's underbelly (literally).
- **The Ship's Ratter** — Works the docks and ships specifically, deeply familiar with maritime superstition and the real, practical stakes of keeping a vessel's stores vermin-free on long voyages.
- **The Granary Specialist** — Contracted regularly by a lord or town to protect stored grain, quietly essential to the community's actual food security in a way nobody formally credits them for.
- **The Poison-Handler Gone Wrong** — A rat catcher whose poison use has caused a real accident — a child, a pet, or livestock poisoned by careless or desperate bait placement — carrying guilt or facing consequences for a genuinely dangerous, imprecise tool of the trade.
- **The Vermin Whisperer** — An eccentric rat catcher with an unusually deep, almost unsettling understanding of vermin behavior — playable straight as folk expertise, or, if your setting supports it, hinting at something genuinely uncanny (a minor fey or primal connection to small creatures).
### Game Hooks
- **The Infestation That Isn't Rats** — The party is hired for what looks like an ordinary rat-catching job, only to discover the real infestation is something far more dangerous (giant rats, a swarm, a monster nesting in the walls, a wererat, or something worse) — a classic low-level dungeon-crawl-in-miniature hook.
- **Following the Rat Catcher's Map** — The party needs to move through a town unseen (escaping pursuit, infiltrating a building) and the local rat catcher's intimate knowledge of drains, cellars, and wall-gaps becomes the key resource.
- **The Poisoned Well** — A rat catcher's poison, used carelessly or maliciously, contaminates something it shouldn't have (a well, a food store), and the party has to trace the source and deal with the consequences.
- **Plague Omens** — Rat catchers begin reporting unusual vermin behavior or unusually high infestation right before a wave of illness — playing into real (if scientifically half-understood by the setting's people) folk association between rats and disease, giving the party an early warning sign worth investigating.
- **The Vermin Uprising** — Something is driving rats and vermin into unusually aggressive, coordinated, or unnatural behavior across a town — a mundane ecological explanation, or a genuinely magical one (a curse, a summoner, a rat king), with the local rat catcher as the first and most knowledgeable witness to the change.
