### Falconry — The Aristocratic Art
This was the most prestigious form of animal training, tightly bound up with social status.
- **Training process**: a falconer would "man" a wild-caught hawk or falcon — getting it accustomed to human presence by carrying it on the gloved fist for hours, feeding it by hand, and gradually reducing its fear. Birds were trained to a lure, then flown on a leash (creance) before being trusted off-lead.
- **Equipment**: hoods (to keep the bird calm and unstimulated), jesses (leather straps on the legs), bells (to track the bird in flight or brush), and gauntlets.
- **Social hierarchy**: strict rules even governed which bird matched which rank — a peregrine falcon for an earl, a goshawk for a yeoman, a kestrel for a servant, according to texts like the _Boke of Saint Albans_. Owning the wrong bird for your station could be a social transgression.
- **Falconers' status**: a noble household's falconer (sometimes called an "ostringer" or "austringer" for those who trained hawks rather than falcons) held a respected position, often living close to the birds and dedicating most of their life to the craft.
### Hunting Dogs
- **Breeding and training kennels** were maintained by nobility, with different breeds developed for different game: greyhounds and similar coursing hounds for deer and hare, scent hounds for tracking, and mastiff-type dogs for boar (a genuinely dangerous task requiring fearless, heavily-built dogs).
- Trainers (often called **berners** or **veutrers**) taught dogs to follow horn signals, work in packs, and distinguish prey scents.
- Hunting was both sport and practical food-gathering for elites, so a good kennel master was a valuable household figure.
### Horse Trainers
- **Destriers** (war horses) needed specialized training to tolerate the chaos of battle — noise, weapons, crowding — and to respond to leg and weight cues since a knight's hands were occupied with weapon and shield.
- **Palfreys** were trained for smooth, comfortable riding gaits for travel.
- Horse trainers (sometimes overlapping with farriers or stable masters) used repetitive groundwork, lunging, and increasingly elaborate maneuvers — some of this tradition fed into what later became dressage.
### Traveling Entertainers and Menagerie Keepers
- **Bear-wards** trained bears for "bear-baiting" (a brutal blood sport) or sometimes for dancing displays at fairs.
- **Performing animal acts**: trained monkeys, dogs, and sometimes goats appeared at markets and fairs doing tricks for coin.
- **Exotic animal keepers**: wealthy courts sometimes kept menageries (lions, leopards) as status symbols — the Tower of London famously housed one — and these required keepers who, with very limited zoological knowledge, tried to keep dangerous animals alive and occasionally tame enough for display.
### Working Animals
- **Oxen and plow horses** were trained for steady field labor.
- **Carrier pigeons**, while less developed than later eras, had some early use for messages.
- **Cats** weren't formally "trained" but were kept and somewhat encouraged for pest control around granaries and ships.
### Worldbuilding Angles
#### Historical Grounding (real medieval trades to reskin)
- **Falconers/Austringers** – Highly ranked, often tied to nobility; different birns for different social classes (kings got gyrfalcons, commoners got kestrels). Great basis for a strict social hierarchy of "who's allowed to train what."
- **Houndsmen/Kennelmasters** – Bred and trained hunting packs for lords; often lived apart from the manor in kennels, treated as half-servant half-specialist.
- **Horse trainers/Ostlers** – Warhorses, palfreys, destriers—training was tied to knighthood and cavalry, high prestige.
- **Bear wards & menagerie keepers** – Traveling entertainers or keepers of a noble's exotic animal collection (lions, bears)—rare, dangerous, and mysterious to common folk.
- **Beekeepers** – Overlooked but deeply symbolic (bees = order, monarchy, industry); could tie into fey or druidic themes.
#### Fantasy Twists to Layer On
- **Monster tamers, not just animal trainers** – A guild that specializes in taming displacer beast kittens or hippogriffs, seen as either heroic or heretical depending on the region.
- **Bloodline bonding** – Some trainers can only work with certain creatures because of an inherited magical rapport (like a soft ranger's Beast Master flavor), making animal handling partly hereditary and partly trade-taught.
- **Familiar breeders** – A shadowy market supplying witches, warlocks, and wizards with pre-bonded familiars—raises questions about consent and animal sapience.
- **Church-sanctioned handlers** – Only clergy-blessed trainers are legally allowed to work with "unnatural" beasts (owlbears, griffons), giving the church a monopoly and a reason for black-market trainers to exist.
- **Fey-taught trainers** – A rare few learn their craft from fey creatures in exchange for debts or favors, and their animals behave just a little too intelligently.
#### Social & Political Hooks
- Animal trainers as a **guild with monopolistic control** over war-beast supply to armies (dire wolves, war boars)—political leverage for the guild.
- A **noble house's status symbol** is its menagerie; a trainer who can produce something no one else has (a griffon, a baby wyvern) becomes hugely valuable and hugely targeted.
- **Poacher-trainers**, outlaws who capture and tame illegal creatures (unicorns, protected fey-beasts) for black market sale.
- Village-level distrust: a trainer who's _too_ good with animals gets whispered about as a witch or hexer.
#### Sensory/Flavor Details
- Trainers carry **specific tools/charms**—a bell-laden glove, a whistle carved from monster bone, a satchel of enchanted treats.
- Regional beast specialties: mountain clans train giant lynxes, coastal folk train giant crabs or trained cormorants for fishing, plains riders train warhorses or even young pegasi.
- Old **taming rites**—a trainer isn't considered a master until they've bonded a creature that could kill them.
