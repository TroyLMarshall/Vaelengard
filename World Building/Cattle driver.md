### The Craft and Its Structure
A **drover** moved livestock — primarily cattle, but also sheep, geese, and pigs depending on region and season — over long distances on foot, from pasture regions and breeding areas to the markets, fairs, and cities where they'd actually be sold and slaughtered. Before refrigeration or any reliable way to transport meat itself over distance, the only practical solution was **moving the animal alive to wherever it would ultimately be consumed**, and droving was the skilled, physically demanding trade built entirely around making that journey work.

**Drove roads** — Because large herds moving over long distances needed reliable grazing, water, and rest along the way, established **drove roads** developed across medieval Europe as genuine, recognized trade infrastructure — wide tracks (sometimes deliberately kept broader than ordinary roads specifically to accommodate large herds and allow some roadside grazing as they passed), with recognized stopping points, y water sources, and eventually inns or stopping-places specifically catering to drovers and their animals. Wales and Scotland droving cattle into English markets is a particularly well-documented real historical pattern — Welsh and Scottish cattle, raised in upland pasture regions, were driven hundreds of miles to English fairs and cities, a journey that could take weeks and represented a serious logistical undertaking, not a casual walk.

**The actual work** — Droving demanded real, specific skill: **managing large herds of unpredictable animals** over difficult, changing terrain, keeping them together, calm, and moving at a sustainable pace (driving too hard lost weight and value off the animals before sale — a drover's whole income depended on delivering healthy, reasonably well-conditioned stock, not just live ones), **navigating unfamiliar country** using knowledge of established routes, safe river crossings, and reliable grazing/watering stops, **negotiating tolls and grazing rights** with landowners along the route (drovers regularly needed to pay for the right to graze or pass through private or common land, a real, constant point of negotiation and occasional dispute), and **protecting the herd** from theft, predators, and the general risks of extended travel through territory that wasn't the drover's own. Drovers often traveled with trained **dogs** (herding and driving breeds, working animals bred and trained specifically for managing livestock on the move) as essential working partners, not pets.

**Shoeing cattle** — A genuinely distinctive, memorable detail: cattle intended for long droves were sometimes fitted with **iron shoes** (cues, split into two per hoof rather than a single horseshoe, since cattle hooves are cloven) to protect their feet over the long overland journey — a real historical practice connecting droving directly back to the farrier's trade, and a nice bit of texture (imagine a blacksmith or farrier specifically known for shoeing cattle for the big drive season).

**Economic role and trust** — Drovers frequently carried **significant sums of money** on the return journey — proceeds from selling an entire herd at market, sometimes representing an entire village or region's accumulated livestock wealth for the year — making the trade genuinely economically significant beyond just the animals themselves, and drovers correspondingly needed to be **trusted** by the farmers, landowners, or communities who entrusted them with herds worth a serious fortune. Some drovers effectively functioned as informal **rural bankers or money-carriers** as an extension of this trust, particularly in regions (Wales is again the best-documented real example) where droving networks became closely tied to early rural credit and banking development — droving profits and routes helping seed some of the earliest country banks in exactly the regions most associated with long-distance cattle droving.

**Social position** — Physically hard, itinerant, weather-exposed work — drovers spent weeks at a time away from home, sleeping rough or in basic wayside accommodations, walking enormous distances at an animal's pace. Yet the trade commanded real respect and trust precisely because of the money and livestock at stake — a good drover wasn't a low-status vagrant but a trusted, skilled professional handling other people's serious wealth, occupying a position somewhat like a caravan master or trusted courier in other trade contexts. Droving was often a **seasonal occupation**, tied to specific market fairs and the agricultural calendar, meaning many drovers combined it with farming or other work during the rest of the year rather than droving as a year-round livelihood.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Animal Handling, Survival
- **Tool Proficiency:** Vehicles (Land), or proficiency in Herbalism Kit (basic livestock care)
- **Feat:** Tough or Alert
- **Equipment:** A drover's staff/crook, a trained herding dog, a small purse (sometimes representing a whole community's entrusted funds), sturdy weatherworn traveling clothes, 10 gp

This background is a strong, natural fit for a **Ranger** (animal handling, long-distance overland travel expertise, genuine outdoor survival skill) or a **Barbarian/Fighter** from a hard, physically demanding rural background. It's also a compelling pick for a **Rogue or Bard** leaning into the "trusted money-carrier" angle — someone who's handled serious sums on behalf of others and either built a reputation for absolute reliability, or is running from having once broken that trust.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Veteran Route-Walker** — Decades of experience driving the same long route, knows every safe crossing, grazing stop, and toll-keeper personally — a great practical guide NPC for any overland travel through their territory.
- **The Entrusted Money-Carrier** — Carries an entire village's accumulated livestock-sale proceeds on the return journey, genuinely trusted by an entire community, and correspondingly a real target for robbery.
- **The Toll Dispute Veteran** — A drover locked in ongoing tension with a landowner or lord over grazing rights and toll fees along an established route — small-scale but real economic friction.
- **The Rural Money-Lender** — A former or current drover who's parlayed droving profits and trust into informal rural banking/lending — genuinely influential in a quiet, unglamorous way.
- **The Herd-Shoer** — A farrier specifically known for shoeing cattle ahead of the big seasonal drive — a good small connective NPC tying the drover and farrier trades together.
### Game Hooks
- **The Escort Job** — The party is hired to guard a drover and their herd (and the eventual sale proceeds) along a dangerous stretch of drove road — bandits, difficult terrain, and weather all threaten a valuable, slow-moving target.
- **The Missing Drover** — A trusted drover never returned from a routine drive with a community's entrusted herd and money — did they abscond, get robbed, or meet something worse on the road? A investigation/rescue hook with real communal stakes.
- **Toll Road Tension** — A dispute over grazing rights or toll fees between drovers and a landowner escalates toward real violence or blockade, threatening an entire season's livestock trade — the party gets pulled in as mediators or muscle for one side.
- **The Herd Gone Wild** — Something (a predator, a monster, a storm, sabotage) spooks and scatters a herd mid-drive, and the party has to help recover the animals before they're lost, stolen piecemeal, or ruin the drover financially.
- **Following the Route** — The party needs to travel cross-country discreetly or efficiently, and an established drove road (with its known stops, water, and grazing) becomes the practical, textured path they follow — a good way to make overland travel feel like a real, storied route rather than a blank map line.
