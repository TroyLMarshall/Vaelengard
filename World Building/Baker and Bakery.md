### A Trade Bound Up With Survival and Politics
Bread wasn't just a staple food in medieval Europe — for most people, it _was_ the meal, often making up 70-80% of daily caloric intake. That made bakers economically and politically essential in a way that's hard to overstate, and it's a big part of why their trade was so heavily regulated.
### Types of Bakers
- **White bread bakers** (sometimes called _boulangers blancs_ or similar regionally): made the finer, more expensive wheat bread for wealthier customers and nobility
- **Brown/coarse bread bakers**: made cheaper bread from rye, barley, oats, or mixed grains (sometimes called "maslin") for the common population
- **Pastry/oublie bakers**: a more specialized trade making wafers, pastries, and sweeter goods for wealthier clients
- **Communal/manorial bakers**: in rural areas, many peasants didn't have their own ovens (or weren't legally allowed to use them — see below) and instead brought dough to a communal or lord-owned oven
### The Bakery Itself
- **The oven** was the central, expensive piece of infrastructure — typically a large brick or stone structure, often domed, heated by burning wood inside the oven chamber itself, then raking out the embers before loading in the bread to bake in the retained heat.
- **Proofing areas**: space for dough to rise, often near the oven for warmth
- **Mixing troughs and kneading surfaces**
- **Storage for flour and grain** — flour quality and freshness mattered enormously, and adulteration (more below) was a constant temptation and concern
- Many bakeries operated on the ground floor of the baker's own house, with living quarters above — a common pattern across medieval crafts
### The Lord's Monopoly on Ovens
This is a detail that surprises people: in many manorial systems, **the lord of the manor held exclusive rights to the oven**, and peasants were legally required to bake their bread there (and pay a fee, often in kind — a portion of the loaves or flour) rather than building their own. This was one of several "banal rights" (from _ban_, the lord's right to compel) alongside similar monopolies on mills and wine presses. It was a genuine source of peasant resentment, since it meant paying a toll just to eat.
### Guild Structure and Regulation
Bakers' guilds existed in most substantial towns, but baking was unusual in how heavily **city and royal authorities regulated it directly**, beyond normal guild self-governance:
- **The Assize of Bread and Ale** (England, dating back to the 13th century) set legally mandated prices and weights for bread based on current grain prices — essentially a medieval price-control law tied to a sliding scale.
- **Weight and quality inspections** were common and serious. Selling underweight or adulterated bread (stretched with chalk, ground bone, or other fillers to save on flour) could result in real punishment.
- A famous, possibly apocryphal but widely repeated explanation for the **"baker's dozen"** (13 instead of 12) is that bakers added an extra loaf or roll as insurance against accusations of shortweighting — better to give away a little extra than risk the fines or punishments for coming in under the legal weight.
- Punishments for cheating could include fines, pillory, or in some places literally being dragged through town tied to a hurdle with the offending loaf hung around the neck — public shaming was a major tool of trade enforcement.
### The Bread Itself
- **Bread types correlated strongly with class**: the finest white "manchet" bread for nobility, coarser "cheat" or "maslin" bread for the middling sort, and dense, dark, often partly-inedible-by-modern-standards bread (sometimes including ground peas, beans, or even bark in famine years) for the poor.
- **Trenchers**: thick, stale slices of bread used as edible plates at meals, later given to the poor or animals after soaking up food juices — a practical use of bread beyond direct eating.
- **Fasting and religious calendar**: bread (along with fish) was a key food during religious fasting periods, since the Church restricted meat consumption on many days throughout the year.
### Apprenticeship and Daily Life
Like other crafts, baking ran on a guild apprenticeship system, but with some distinctive features:
- **Brutally early hours**: bread needed to be ready for morning sale, meaning bakers and apprentices typically started work in the very early hours of the morning, well before dawn — a pattern that's honestly persisted into the modern bakery trade.
- **Fire risk**: bakeries were a significant fire hazard in towns built largely of wood, leading to regulations about oven placement, sometimes pushing bakeries to town edges or requiring stone construction for the oven itself.
- **Physical demands**: kneading large quantities of dough by hand and managing a wood-fired oven were genuinely strenuous, sustained physical labor.
### Worldbuilding Angle
For your setting, a few fantasy-flavored hooks this trade lends itself to:
- A baker's guild with magical ovens or enchanted yeast that never runs out (and the political fight over who controls that secret)
- A corrupt lord's banal oven-right becoming a flashpoint for rebellion
- A baker secretly working with an alchemist to "improve" bread in ways that are either miraculous or sinister
- Bread as a trust/poison vector — given how central it was to every meal, a baker with ill intent (or under threat/bribery) is a classic and grounded source of court intrigue
