### Another Term Requiring Careful Historical Mapping
"Indentured servant" as a specific legal category is actually most precisely associated with a **later period** — particularly the 17th-18th century colonial labor system where individuals contracted to work for a fixed term in exchange for passage to the Americas or other colonies. The precise term and that specific institutional form don't belong to the medieval period.

However, the underlying concept — **bound labor obligations of various kinds**, ranging from outright serfdom to contractual service arrangements — absolutely existed in medieval Europe, and we've actually touched on most of the relevant ground already. Let's map what actually existed and draw the distinctions carefully.
### What Medieval "Bound Service" Actually Looked Like
**Serfdom/villeinage**: the most significant form of bound labor, covered extensively in the farm and peasant entries
- Villeins were legally bound to their lord's land, owing labor service and various fees as covered in those entries
- This wasn't a voluntary contract but an inherited legal status — you were born a villein, not contracted into it
- Genuinely the closest medieval equivalent to systematic bound labor, though the legal mechanism differed significantly from the later indentured contract system

**Apprenticeship**: covered repeatedly throughout this list across virtually every guild trade
- A formal contractual arrangement binding an apprentice to a master for a fixed term (typically seven years)
- The apprentice owed labor and obedience; the master owed training, housing, and basic maintenance
- An **indenture** (a specific type of contract document, with two copies cut from a single sheet along a jagged/indented line — hence "indenture" — so both parties held matching halves that could be verified against each other) was literally the document formalizing this arrangement
- This is actually the direct etymological origin of "indenture" as a labor contract term — medieval guild apprenticeship used precisely this document type, making guild apprenticeship genuinely the medieval ancestor of what later became "indentured service" in its colonial form

**Household service contracts**: as covered in the hired servers entry, some household service arrangements involved formal contractual terms — length of service, wages, and obligations specified in advance, creating a genuine (if less severe than serfdom) form of contractual bound labor

**Debt bondage**: as touched on in the jails and banker entries, debt could create effective labor obligation — a debtor unable to pay might work off the debt through labor for the creditor, a genuinely common informal arrangement even without a specific legal "indenture" framework formalizing it
### The Indenture Document Itself
Worth developing further since it's genuinely interesting and directly relevant:
- **Physical document**: as mentioned above, an indenture was literally a document cut in two along an irregular (indented/toothed) line, with each party keeping one half
- **Verification mechanism**: the matching irregular cut meant the two halves could be verified against each other — a clever, forgery-resistant authentication method without requiring sophisticated technology, connecting to the broader medieval concern with document authentication covered in the land office and scribe entries
- **Used beyond apprenticeship**: indenture documents formalized various contractual arrangements beyond just apprenticeship — land agreements, military contracts (soldiers sometimes served under indenture to a lord or captain), and other formal obligations might use this same document form
### Military Indenture — A Specific Important Application
Worth highlighting since it's less commonly known:
- **Retaining soldiers**: as touched on in the militaries and mercenary entries, later medieval military organization increasingly relied on **retaining** soldiers through formal contracts — a lord paying a captain to provide a specified number of soldiers for a specified period, formalized through an indenture document
- **Indentured retinues**: particularly significant in England during the later medieval period, this system of formally contracted military service represented a significant development in how armies were organized, moving away from pure feudal obligation toward something closer to professional contracted service
- **Livery and maintenance**: the broader system connecting military retaining to household livery and patronage — lords maintaining networks of contracted followers who wore their livery and could be called on for military service, creating real political and military power bases through these contracted relationships
### Social and Legal Position
- **Varying degrees of agency**: the range from villein serfdom (no meaningful choice) through formal apprenticeship indenture (genuine contractual agreement, even if entered under economic pressure) to military retaining contract (relatively free negotiation between parties) represents a genuine spectrum of how much agency the bound party actually had
- **Legal enforceability**: formal indenture contracts were legally binding and enforceable through the court systems covered in the judge and lawyer entries — a runaway apprentice or a lord who failed to provide promised training could face real legal consequences
- **The seal**: as covered repeatedly in this list (beekeeping, goldsmith, land office entries), formal contracts required proper sealing to carry legal weight — an indenture without proper seals from both parties was less legally secure
### The Villein's Path to Freedom
Connecting to the farm and peasant entries' discussion of serfdom's gradual decline:
- **Manumission**: a lord could formally free a serf through a manumission document — sometimes purchased by the serf themselves (with money accumulated through their limited economic activities), sometimes granted as reward for service
- **Flight to towns**: the legal principle that a villein who successfully lived in a chartered town for a year and a day without being reclaimed by their lord gained freedom — "town air makes you free" in the famous medieval saying — represented a genuine, if risky, path out of serfdom
- **Post-Black-Death loosening**: as covered in the laborer and peasant entries, labor scarcity after the plague accelerated the practical decline of strict serfdom even before it was fully abolished legally in various regions
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- The indenture document's physical split-and-match authentication mechanism as a plot device — perhaps one half of a crucial indenture (proving land ownership, a military obligation, or a significant contract) is missing or has been forged, with the split-document verification system being the key to proving authenticity
- Military indenture retinues as a worldbuilding detail for your setting's power structure — great lords' political power measured partly in how many contracted soldiers they can call on through formal retaining arrangements, creating a visible, legally-grounded military patronage network
- A serf character's "year and a day in the city" quest as a genuinely grounded freedom-seeking narrative — perhaps a PC or NPC is actively being sought by their lord before the year expires, creating real time pressure and pursuit stakes
- Apprenticeship indenture violation as a legal complication — a PC who ran away from their apprenticeship before completing the term technically still owes that service, potentially creating an unexpected legal vulnerability when they encounter their former master's guild in a new city
