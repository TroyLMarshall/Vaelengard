### The Historical Trade (Victorian Britain)
A **crossing sweeper** was typically a child, elderly person, or someone otherwise unable to secure steadier work, who staked out a specific **street crossing** — a spot where pedestrians needed to cross from one side to the other — and used a broom to sweep a clear path through the mud, horse manure, and general filth of the unpaved or poorly-paved road immediately ahead of anyone willing to pay a small tip for the service. This was genuinely useful, low-barrier-to-entry work: a Victorian street, thick with horse traffic, was a real mess to cross in fine clothes or good shoes, and a sweeper's quick work with a broom bought a clean path for a coin. Sweepers were among the most visible faces of urban poverty in Victorian literature (Dickens's Jo in _Bleak House_ is the standout literary example) — occupying a genuinely precarious economic position with **no guild, no formal recognition, and total dependence on the charity/generosity of passersby**, often working a specific "claimed" corner as their unofficial territory, sometimes defending that spot from other sweepers trying to move in. It required no capital, no training, and no permission — just a broom and the willingness to stand outside in all weather hoping for pennies, making it one of the purest examples of subsistence-level informal urban labor in the period.
### Adapting It to Medieval Fantasy
The core appeal transfers cleanly if you loosen a couple of period-specific assumptions:
- **Swap "street mud from horse traffic" for medieval street conditions generally** — unpaved or poorly-paved streets, open gutters/kennels running down the street center carrying waste and runoff, and genuinely filthy conditions were very real in medieval towns (arguably worse before later civic sanitation efforts), so the underlying problem — streets are foul, and clearing a path is a real service — holds up perfectly well without needing 19th-century traffic specifically.
- **Drop the "broom-sweeping specifically for tips at a fixed crossing" as a named, recognized trade**, since that specific packaging is Victorian, and replace it with the broader, genuinely medieval reality: **poor children and adults did take on informal odd-job labor for coins** — running errands, clearing paths, holding horses, carrying messages, guiding travelers around town — without a single fixed occupational title, but functionally filling the same social niche.
- **Keep everything else** — the child-or-desperate-adult worker, the claimed territory, the total dependence on passersby's generosity, the total absence of guild protection or formal status, the "invisible fixture of city life that everyone sees and almost no one really notices" quality. This is the actually valuable, transferable part.

Framed this way, your setting's "crossing sweeper" equivalent is a **street urchin or destitute odd-jobber who clears a path, holds a spot, or performs some small unskilled service at a fixed corner for tips** — genuinely medieval-plausible, evocative, and doing the same narrative work the Victorian original does.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Stealth, Persuasion (or Survival, reflecting genuine hand-to-mouth street life)
- **Tool Proficiency:** None specific, or Thieves' Tools (a very natural crossover — someone who's spent years watching a street corner has learned to watch people, pockets, and patterns)
- **Feat:** Lucky or Alert
- **Equipment:** A worn broom or simple tool, a few coppers, ragged but weather-practical clothes, an intimate knowledge of one specific street/district, 2 gp

This background is an excellent fit for a **Rogue** — the "claimed corner," constant close observation of passersby, and total invisibility to respectable society all map directly onto classic Rogue fantasy without needing to frame the character as a thief at all (though it works fine for that too). It's also a strong, sympathetic origin for **any class built around a hard-knocks, streetwise childhood** — a Fighter, Barbarian, or Bard who grew up with nothing, noticed by no one, and learned to survive on tips, scraps, and sharp observation.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Corner Fixture** — A particular child or old beggar who's held the same spot for years, known (if not really _seen_) by everyone who regularly passes that way — a good "hiding in plain sight" information source, since they've silently watched the same faces go by for a long time.
- **The Territorial Dispute** — Two sweepers/odd-jobbers fighting over the same lucrative corner or gate — small-scale, sympathetic, a good low-stakes intro conflict for a party passing through a poor district.
- **The Silent Witness** — Was standing at their corner during a crime, disappearance, or suspicious meeting, and saw everything — but nobody official thinks to ask a street urchin, so the party has to think to look.
- **The Runner** — Rather than sweeping, specializes in running quick errands and messages across town for coin — genuinely useful for a party needing something delivered discreetly and fast without going through official channels.
- **The Almost-Invisible Guide** — Knows every alley, gate, and shortcut in their district intimately from years standing on that one corner watching the town move — an excellent low-cost local guide NPC.
### Game Hooks
- **The Overlooked Witness** — The party's investigation stalls until they think to ask the corner-sweeper/urchin nobody else considered a real witness — a satisfying "the answer was in plain sight, held by someone beneath everyone's notice" reveal.
- **Turf War Escalation** — A dispute between rival street kids/odd-jobbers over a valuable corner escalates into something the party has to mediate or gets pulled into, possibly revealing an adult exploiting or controlling the children's "territory" for their own ends.
- **The Message Chain** — The party needs to get information across town discreetly and uses a network of runners/sweepers — fun for showing off a city's informal economy as a working system the party can tap into.
- **Recruiting the Overlooked** — The party takes on a street kid as a genuine ally, contact, or ward — a good found-family/mentorship subplot with real emotional stakes given the character's precarious existence.
- **Someone's Using Them** — A local crime figure, corrupt official, or worse is exploiting the town's street children/odd-jobbers for information, smuggling, or worse — the party uncovers and has to decide how to intervene.
