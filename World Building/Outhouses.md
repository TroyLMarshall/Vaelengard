### A Topic That Reveals A Lot About Medieval Daily Life
Sanitation is one of those areas where medieval reality diverges most sharply from both romantic fantasy idealization and simple "it was all awful" dismissal — the actual picture is more nuanced, more varied by wealth and location, and in some ways more thoughtfully managed than people assume, even while being genuinely far below modern standards by any measure.
### Terminology First
"Outhouse" as a specific term is somewhat anachronistic for medieval Europe — the period had its own vocabulary:
- **Privy**: the most common general term for a private toilet facility
- **Garderobe**: specifically the toilet built into a castle or wealthy building's wall, typically a small projecting room or alcove with a hole leading to a shaft dropping outside the wall or into a pit/moat
- **Jakes**: a common informal term for a toilet facility
- **House of easement/house of office**: more formal euphemistic terms appearing in various period sources
### Castle and Wealthy Household Sanitation
- **Garderobes**: as mentioned, built directly into the castle or manor house wall — a small stone room projecting outward, with a seat over a hole dropping waste either down the exterior wall into a pit, moat, or simply the ground below, or occasionally into a shaft connecting to a cesspit
- **Moat disposal**: the romantic image of castle moats as clean, decorative water features doesn't survive contact with the reality that they frequently received significant waste, among other things — moat water was not something you'd want to fall into
- **Multiple garderobes**: larger castles might have several garderobes at different points, serving different parts of the building, rather than one centralized facility
- **Clothing storage connection**: "garderobe" also referred to a wardrobe/clothing storage room, and the two uses weren't entirely unrelated — the ammonia from waste was believed to deter moths and vermin from clothing stored nearby, suggesting these spaces sometimes genuinely served dual purposes
### Urban Sanitation
- **Cesspits**: the dominant urban sanitation infrastructure — pits dug beneath or near privies, collecting waste, requiring periodic emptying
- **Gong farmers/nightsoil men**: a specific, genuinely unpleasant trade — those employed to empty cesspits, typically working at night (giving them the alternative name "nightmen") to minimize disruption and perhaps social visibility. This was among the most unpleasant and lowest-status work imaginable, yet genuinely essential to urban sanitation functioning at all.
- **River disposal**: as touched on in the butcher and dyer entries, rivers received significant waste in medieval towns — both directly (privies built over rivers or draining into them) and indirectly through street drainage, creating real downstream water quality problems
- **Street conditions**: urban streets received more waste than modern sensibilities would tolerate — chamber pots emptied from windows (the "gardez l'eau" warning supposedly called before doing so), animal waste from the livestock present even in urban settings, and general refuse created conditions requiring some management even if imperfect
- **Rakers and scavengers**: some towns employed workers specifically to clean streets, collect refuse, and generally manage the waste accumulation problem — an early form of municipal sanitation service, connecting to the government official entry's discussion of civic administration functions
### Rural Sanitation
- **Simple pit privies**: for rural households, a basic pit with some form of covering structure — genuinely the simplest, most accessible sanitation solution, requiring no infrastructure beyond digging and basic construction
- **Agricultural reuse**: human waste's value as fertilizer (connecting to the farm entry's discussion of maintaining soil fertility without modern fertilizers) meant rural waste disposal sometimes fed back into agricultural use, particularly for garden rather than main crop fertilization
- **Less concentrated problem**: rural population dispersal meant waste management was genuinely less acute than the urban density problem — the same amount of waste spread across a much larger area with more natural absorption capacity created less public health crisis than equivalent waste concentrated in a walled town
### Public/Communal Facilities
- **Public privies**: some larger medieval towns maintained public toilet facilities — London had documented public privies over the Thames and elsewhere, sometimes multi-seat communal structures (the concept of individual privacy in toilet use being considerably less developed than modern Western expectations)
- **Monastery facilities**: as touched on in the monastery entry, monastic communities maintained their own sanitation infrastructure, often relatively sophisticated given monasteries' general organizational capability — the **reredorter** (literally "behind the dormitory") was a specific monastic building housing communal toilet facilities, often built over a water channel for flushing
### Public Health Impact
- **Disease connection**: medieval authorities didn't understand germ theory but did recognize empirical connections between filthy conditions and illness — periodic civic clean-up orders, regulations about waste disposal, and location restrictions on the worst-smelling trades (tanneries, dye-works, slaughterhouses) all reflect practical, experience-based awareness that some connection existed between sanitation and health, even without the theoretical understanding to properly explain it
- **The Black Death context**: while the Black Death's primary transmission mechanism (flea-borne, connecting to rat populations) wasn't directly a sanitation issue in the simple "dirty streets cause plague" sense, the general urban sanitation conditions of medieval towns certainly didn't help disease resistance or overall population health
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- Garderobe shafts as a classic, if unpleasant, infiltration route for PCs entering a castle — a genuinely historically-used method, occasionally documented in real medieval military history, where attackers used garderobe shafts to gain access to fortifications
- A gong farmer NPC with surprisingly useful knowledge — someone who works in the literal underground of a city, knows where every cesspit connects, what gets thrown away by whom, and has access to spaces most people never think about, making them a natural information source for urban investigations
- Urban sanitation management as a civic governance detail — a city that invests in proper rakers, public privies, and cesspit regulation feels subtly more sophisticated and livable than one that doesn't, giving you a wordless way to signal different levels of civic organization between settlements in your setting
- A cesspit discovery as an investigation hook — medieval cesspits are, in real archaeology, genuinely valuable sources of historical evidence (well-preserved organic material), and in your setting, something valuable or incriminating disposed of down a privy shaft might need recovering
