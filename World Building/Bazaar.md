### A Quick Terminology Note
"Bazaar" is actually a Persian/Middle Eastern term, and while it's sometimes used loosely (especially in fantasy and pop culture) to describe any bustling marketplace, in a specifically medieval _European_ context, you'd more accurately be looking at **markets** and **fairs** as the core institutions. I'll cover both, since they served overlapping but distinct functions — and if your setting wants to use "bazaar" as flavor language for a market with a more exotic or cosmopolitan feel (perhaps in a port city or crossroads trade hub), that's a very reasonable worldbuilding choice even if it's not the historically precise European term.
### Weekly/Local Markets
The backbone of everyday medieval commerce:
- **Market charters**: towns needed legal permission (often a royal or noble charter) to hold a market, which was a valuable economic right — towns competed for and jealously guarded market rights, since a market drew trade, tolls, and prestige
- **Market days**: typically held on fixed days each week (varying by town to avoid direct competition with neighbors) in a central square or designated marketplace
- **Market crosses**: many English towns had a literal stone cross marking the official market site, sometimes serving as a symbolic guarantee of fair dealing under the Church's watchful eye
- **Stalls and pitches**: temporary stalls, often family-run, selling local produce, meat, fish, bread, ale, cloth, and basic crafted goods
- **Tolls and fees**: market-goers and especially outside traders paid tolls to the town or lord controlling the market right, a meaningful revenue source
### Great Trade Fairs
Bigger, less frequent, and far more economically significant for long-distance and luxury trade:
- **The Champagne Fairs** (France) were probably the most famous medieval example — a cycle of fairs rotating through several towns in the Champagne region, drawing merchants from across Europe (Italian bankers, Flemish cloth merchants, and others) for weeks at a time, becoming a genuine hub of international trade and finance in the 12th-13th centuries
- **Other major fairs**: Stourbridge Fair (England), various fairs tied to specific saints' feast days across the continent
- **Duration**: unlike weekly markets, fairs often ran for days or weeks, justifying merchants traveling long distances to attend
- **Specialized goods**: fairs were where you'd find luxury and long-distance trade goods — fine cloth, spices, dyes, exotic imports — alongside the banking and currency exchange services covered earlier, since merchants needed financial services to facilitate large transactions
### What You'd Actually Find There
- **Food**: fresh produce, meat, fish, bread, cheese, ale and wine
- **Cloth and textiles**: raw wool, finished cloth, dyed fabrics — cloth was one of medieval Europe's most important trade commodities
- **Crafted goods**: pottery, tools, leather goods, basic metalwork
- **Livestock**: separate livestock markets often existed alongside general markets
- **Luxury and imported goods** (especially at fairs): spices, silk, exotic dyes, fine metalwork — goods that had traveled significant distances, sometimes via Mediterranean trade connecting to Byzantine and Islamic trade networks
- **Services**: moneychangers, scribes for those needing letters written, traveling entertainers, and itinerant craftspeople offering repairs
### Regulation and Order
Markets weren't a free-for-all — they were carefully overseen:
- **Market officials/clerks of the market**: checked weights, measures, and quality (echoing the ale-conner and bread assize regulations covered earlier)
- **Guild oversight**: local guilds often had rules about who could sell what, protecting local craftsmen from outside competition undercutting them
- **Peace of the market**: many markets operated under special legal protections — a kind of temporary truce or heightened legal protection for the duration of the market, since you needed strangers and even rival town merchants to feel safe enough to trade
- **Standard weights and measures**: enforced (imperfectly) to prevent cheating, though regional variation in measures was still common and a source of ongoing friction
### The Social Dimension
Markets and fairs were genuinely central to medieval social life, not just commerce:
- **News and gossip** spread through market gatherings, similar to taverns but with a wider, more diverse cross-section of people
- **Public punishments and proclamations**: officials often used market day, when crowds gathered, as the moment to read out new laws, punish criminals in stocks or pillory, or post official notices — maximizing the audience
- **Social mixing**: markets brought together people across social classes and sometimes across significant geographic and cultural distances (especially at the big international fairs), in a way that was relatively unusual for the era
### A More Cosmopolitan Variant: Port and Crossroads Markets
If the setting has trade hubs analogous to real medieval cities like **Venice**, **Constantinople** (more Byzantine than "medieval Europe" proper, but hugely influential on European trade), or other crossroads cities, you'd see markets with a notably more international, "bazaar-like" character — multiple languages, a wider range of exotic goods, religious and ethnic diversity among merchants, and specialized merchant quarters (Venice's various _fondaco_ — trading houses for specific foreign merchant communities — are a good real-world reference point if you want that cosmopolitan market flavor).
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A market under a "peace bond" enforced by actual magic rather than just social convention — anyone drawing a weapon or committing violence within the market bounds suffers a magical consequence
- A traveling fair that only appears periodically (tied to a season, a moon phase, or a magical schedule) drawing merchants from multiple planes or distant lands — useful as a recurring, slightly mysterious set piece
- A market clerk/official NPC who's secretly enforcing fairness through subtle magic or divination, catching cheats no mundane inspection could
- A port city market with a fantasy "fondaco" system — distinct merchant quarters for different races/cultures/factions, each with their own customs, creating natural roleplay and intrigue opportunities
