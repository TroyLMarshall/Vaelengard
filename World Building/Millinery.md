### A Genuine Anachronism Worth Addressing Directly
"Millinery" specifically — as a term for women's hat-making as a distinct, recognized trade — is actually a notably **later** development than the medieval period. The word itself derives from "Milaner" (someone selling fine goods from Milan, originally including a broad range of fashionable accessories, not just hats), and millinery's crystallization into a specifically female-associated hat-making trade is much more an 18th-19th century phenomenon. So let's address what medieval Europe actually had for headwear production, since the underlying need (and a real trade serving it) absolutely existed, just organized differently than the term "millinery" implies.
### Headwear Was Genuinely Universal — Far More Than Today
This is worth establishing first: medieval people of virtually every social class wore some form of head covering as a matter of course, far more consistently than modern Western practice. Practical reasons (warmth, sun protection, cleanliness given infrequent hair-washing) combined with genuine social and religious expectation — married women in particular were often expected, sometimes by specific custom or even local regulation, to keep their hair covered in public.
### Who Actually Made Headwear
Rather than one dedicated "milliner" trade, headwear production was distributed across several trades we've already covered:

**Cappers/hatters**: a recognized trade specifically focused on hat and cap-making did exist — London had a recognized **Worshipful Company of Cappers** historically (sometimes called feltmakers, reflecting the dominant material), reflecting genuine guild organization around headwear production specifically

**Felt-making**: a significant proportion of medieval hats and caps were made from **felted wool** — a process of matting wool fibers together using moisture, heat, and pressure (rather than weaving) to create a dense, durable fabric, then shaped into the desired hat form. This connects to the broader wool/textile economy covered in the dyer entry, since felted wool hats might also be dyed using the same techniques and materials discussed there.

**Tailors and seamstresses**: for cloth headwear — coifs (close-fitting caps, particularly associated with covering women's hair), hoods, and various soft cloth head coverings — general cloth-working skill (tailoring) could produce these without necessarily requiring a separate specialized hat-maker

**Furriers**: as covered in that entry, fur-trimmed or fur hats for wealthy/cold-weather wear connected directly to the furrier's trade

**Straw-workers**: simple straw hats, particularly for practical agricultural/outdoor work, connected to basic plaiting/weaving skill similar in principle to the basket weaver's trade
### Types of Medieval Headwear
- **Coifs**: simple, close-fitting linen caps worn by both men and women, often as an under-layer beneath other headwear or alone for more modest, practical covering
- **Hoods**: attached to cloaks or worn separately, genuinely universal across social classes for practical weather protection
- **Wimples and veils**: specifically associated with women's hair-covering, particularly prominent for married women and those in religious life (nuns' specific head-covering traditions connect to this broader practice)
- **Hennin**: the tall, pointed conical headdress associated with later medieval aristocratic fashion (particularly 15th century) — a genuinely elaborate, status-driven fashion item far removed from practical coifs and hoods
- **Caps and hats with brims**: various practical and fashionable styles, increasingly elaborate for wealthy wearers as the medieval period progressed
- **Crowns and coronets**: for nobility and royalty, connecting to the goldsmith entry's discussion of royal regalia — genuinely a category beyond ordinary hat-making, requiring precious metal craft skill
### Status and Fashion Signaling
Connecting to themes throughout this list (shoe length, fur type, dye color): headwear style, material, and elaborateness served as another visible **status marker**, sometimes subject to the same kind of sumptuary regulation discussed with footwear and fur — certain elaborate headwear styles, particularly among aristocratic women in the later medieval period, became genuinely extravagant fashion statements (the hennin being a prime example), drawing both admiration and moral criticism from those who saw such fashion excess as vain or improper.
### Worldbuilding Angle
Given there's no single clean "millinery" institution to import, you have real freedom here:
- **A felt-capper's guild** as a grounded, specific small trade guild for your setting — useful if you want a minor but textured craft guild beyond the "big" trades (smiths, bakers) already well-represented in fantasy settings
- **Headwear as an immediate, visible status/role signal** — connecting to the broader sumptuary law theme throughout this list, a character's specific head-covering (a wimple, an elaborate hennin, a simple coif) could instantly communicate marital status, social class, or religious role to period-savvy NPCs (and to players, if you lean into the detail), without needing exposition
- **A milliner-equivalent character as a deliberate, acknowledged anachronism**: if your setting wants a recognizable "hat shop" the way modern audiences would picture millinery, it's entirely reasonable to use the term loosely for flavor even though it's not strictly period-accurate terminology — many D&D tables wouldn't blink at this kind of soft anachronism, and it's a perfectly fine creative choice as long as you're aware you're doing it
- **A coif or veil as a meaningful plot object** — perhaps a specific, recognizable head-covering belongs to a missing or disguised character, becoming a visual identifier or clue in an investigation
