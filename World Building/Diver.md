### A Rare, Specialized, and Genuinely Dangerous Trade
Diving in medieval Europe was nowhere near as common or organized as the land-based trades we've covered, but it absolutely existed as a recognized — if niche and hazardous — skill, performed without any of the equipment modern divers take for granted. No diving suits, no air supply beyond held breath, no real way to manage the genuine physiological risks involved. This was raw human breath-holding ability applied to a small number of specific practical needs.
### What Medieval Divers Actually Did
**Ship and harbor maintenance**:
- Inspecting and repairing ship hulls below the waterline, particularly checking for damage or attaching/detaching equipment that needed underwater access
- Recovering items dropped overboard — tools, cargo, or equipment lost during loading/unloading or storms
- Clearing obstructions from harbors, river channels, or around docks/piers

**Salvage work**: this was probably the most economically significant application
- **Recovering cargo from wrecked ships**: shipwrecks were a real and recurring hazard given medieval navigation limitations, and valuable cargo (especially metal goods, which sink and don't degrade like organic cargo) represented a real salvage incentive
- **Recovering anchors and ship equipment**: lost anchors, chains, and gear represented genuine material value worth diving to retrieve

**Pearl diving and specific resource gathering**: in regions where pearl mussels or other underwater-harvested resources existed, divers gathered these directly — though this was geographically limited compared to, say, Mediterranean or other warmer-water pearl diving traditions elsewhere in the world

**Military applications**:
- Some recorded historical instances suggest divers were used for underwater sabotage or obstacle-clearing in siege or naval contexts — disabling enemy ship moorings, clearing underwater defensive obstacles, or similar specialized military-adjacent tasks
- This wasn't a standing "military diver" role so much as occasionally calling on someone with diving skill for a specific tactical need

**Construction-adjacent work**: assisting with underwater elements of bridge or dock construction/repair, where foundational work needed to happen below the water surface
### Techniques and Limitations
- **Free diving only**: medieval divers had no breathing apparatus — all work was limited to however long a skilled diver could hold their breath, meaning tasks needed to be quick, well-prepared in advance, or broken into many repeated short dives
- **Weighted descent aids**: some divers may have used weights to descend more quickly, conserving breath-holding time for actual work rather than the descent itself
- **Diving bells**: this is worth mentioning as a documented (if rare and not strictly "medieval" in its more developed forms) technology — a large inverted container lowered into water, trapping air inside that a diver could access for extended underwater time without surfacing. Aristotle described a primitive version in antiquity, and various accounts suggest continued knowledge/experimentation with the concept through the medieval period, though widespread practical use and significant refinement of diving bells is more strongly associated with the early modern period onward. If your setting wants a "high-tech" medieval diving solution, a simple diving bell is a reasonably grounded choice, even if real widespread medieval use is more speculative/limited than confirmed.
- **Real physiological risk**: without modern understanding of decompression, barotrauma, or related risks, medieval divers faced genuine, poorly understood physical dangers beyond the obvious immediate risk of drowning — though for the shallow, short-duration work most medieval diving involved, the more severe deep-diving physiological risks (like decompression sickness) were less likely to be major factors compared to simple breath-holding limits and cold-water exposure
### Who Did This Work
- **Not a widespread guild-organized trade**: given how niche and specialized diving was, it's unlikely to have supported the kind of formal guild structure we've seen repeatedly throughout this list — more likely practiced by specific skilled individuals (often associated with maritime communities, fishing villages, or port towns) called upon as needed rather than maintaining it as a primary, full-time profession
- **Overlap with fishing and maritime trades**: people already comfortable with water and swimming through fishing or sailing backgrounds were the natural pool from which occasional diving work would be drawn
- **Some regional traditions**: certain coastal or island communities with strong maritime traditions likely developed more concentrated diving skill and reputation than inland regions would ever need or develop
### Worldbuilding Angle
Diving is a genuinely fun, underused trade for fantasy worldbuilding precisely because it's so rare and specialized historically:
- **A diver NPC with a reputation for shipwreck salvage** — a natural contact for PCs needing underwater recovery work, treasure hunting, or investigating a sunken vessel, grounded in a real historical niche rather than needing magical justification
- **A diving bell as a notable, slightly exotic piece of equipment** in a port city — not universal technology, but a specific, valuable tool that a wealthy merchant, salvage operation, or even a magical academy might possess, creating a recognizable "high-tech for this setting" item without breaking historical plausibility too badly
- **Diving as a dangerous, high-risk/high-reward profession** befitting a hardy NPC or even a PC background — someone who's made a living (and earned scars or stories) from the genuine physical danger of breath-held underwater work, fitting naturally with a sailor, fisherman, or dockworker background
- **A magical or monstrous complication on top of mundane diving danger** — sunken ruins, an underwater monster lair, or a magically cursed wreck giving an otherwise mundane salvage job real adventure stakes, letting the historically-grounded diver trade serve as the entry point into a more fantastical underwater scenario
