### The Economic Lifeline of Trading Cities
Docks and wharfs were genuinely critical infrastructure for any medieval settlement engaged in significant trade — given how much more efficient water transport was compared to overland routes (a theme we touched on with the caravan guild discussion), coastal ports and river towns built their entire economic identity around the facilities that let ships load, unload, and dock safely.
### Physical Infrastructure
**Wharfs and quays**: the actual structures along the waterfront where ships moored and goods were transferred — typically built from timber (piling driven into the riverbed or seabed, with planking on top) or, in wealthier or more permanent installations, stone construction. Stone quays represented significant capital investment and were generally found in major, well-established port cities rather than smaller river towns.

**Cranes**: larger ports developed mechanical lifting devices for moving heavy cargo — often human or treadwheel-powered (someone or several people walking inside a large wheel to generate lifting force through simple mechanical advantage), used for loading and unloading bulky or heavy goods that couldn't practically be carried by hand.

**Warehouses**: storage buildings near the waterfront, holding goods awaiting onward transport, sale, or further processing — these needed to be reasonably secure given the value of goods often stored there, and warehouse capacity was a genuine measure of a port's trading significance.

**Customs houses**: buildings where officials assessed and collected tolls, duties, or taxes on incoming and outgoing goods — port towns generated significant revenue this way, and customs collection was a serious administrative function, not an informal afterthought.

**Tidal considerations**: ports affected by tides needed infrastructure accounting for significant water level changes — some harbors used tidal basins or were specifically sited where natural geography minimized this complication, while others simply worked around the practical reality of ships grounding or floating free depending on the tide.
### Who Worked the Docks
- **Dockworkers/porters**: handled the physical labor of loading and unloading cargo — genuinely demanding manual work, organized sometimes informally and sometimes through their own guild-like structures in major ports, given how essential and labor-intensive this work was
- **Watermen**: as covered in the "boat rentals" discussion, operated smaller boats ferrying passengers and goods between larger ships (which often couldn't dock directly at shallow wharfs) and the shore
- **Customs officials**: assessed goods and collected duties, wielding genuine authority over what could legally enter or leave
- **Harbor masters**: oversaw the practical operation of the port — managing mooring space, resolving disputes over dock access, and maintaining basic order in what could be a chaotic, congested space during busy trading periods
- **Ship's chandlers**: suppliers providing ships with necessary provisions, rope, tar, and other maritime goods needed for departing voyages (a different specific usage of "chandler" from the candle-making trade, worth noting since the term applied to general ship's-supplies dealers too)
### Major Medieval Ports
Real historical examples worth knowing if you want grounding for your setting:
- **London**: on the Thames, became one of medieval England's dominant ports, with significant wharf development and the watermen trade discussed earlier
- **Venice**: an extraordinary case of a city literally built around maritime trade, with the Arsenal (state-run shipyard) and extensive port infrastructure supporting its Mediterranean trading empire
- **Bruges**: a major Hanseatic League trading hub (connecting back to the merchant confederation discussion), significant until its harbor began silting up later in the medieval period, which actually contributed to its relative economic decline as trade shifted to alternative ports
- **Genoa**: another major Italian maritime trading power with extensive port infrastructure supporting Mediterranean and beyond trade
- **Hanseatic ports**: Lübeck, Hamburg, and other Baltic/North Sea cities developed substantial port infrastructure supporting the Hanseatic League's trade network
### Goods Flowing Through Medieval Ports
Connecting to several trades already covered:
- **Wool and cloth**: especially significant for English and Flemish trade
- **Wine**: substantial trade particularly from regions like Bordeaux
- **Spices and luxury goods**: arriving via longer Mediterranean trade connections to Eastern/Islamic trade networks
- **Fish**: especially salted/preserved fish (herring particularly significant in North Sea/Baltic trade)
- **Timber, furs, and raw materials**: especially from Baltic/Scandinavian trade via the Hanseatic network
- **Grain**: bulk food trade, sometimes critically important for cities unable to feed themselves purely from local agricultural production
### Risks and Hazards
- **Piracy**: a genuine ongoing threat to shipping, requiring escort arrangements or armed merchant vessels in higher-risk waters
- **Storm damage**: ships and even dock infrastructure itself could be damaged or destroyed by severe weather
- **Fire**: warehouses full of goods (some flammable) packed densely near a working waterfront represented real fire risk, similar to the urban fire concerns we've touched on repeatedly
- **Theft and corruption**: valuable goods sitting in warehouses or being transferred at busy, crowded docks created real opportunity for theft, smuggling, or corrupt customs officials taking bribes to look the other way
### Legal and Economic Status
- **Port/harbor rights**: similar to market rights and ferry rights covered earlier, the right to operate a port or collect associated tolls was a valuable, often contested privilege granted by royal or noble authority
- **Staple port status**: certain ports held exclusive legal rights for trade in specific goods (connecting to the staple town concept mentioned with the caravan guild discussion), channeling trade through specific controlled points
- **Civic investment and pride**: a thriving port represented major civic wealth and status, and town governments often invested significantly in harbor improvements, recognizing direct economic self-interest in maintaining and expanding this infrastructure
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A bustling port city as a natural adventure hub — diverse NPCs, multiple competing factions (merchant guilds, customs officials, smugglers, the watermen we discussed), and built-in plot hooks around trade, smuggling, or piracy
- A magically-maintained harbor (perhaps protected from storms by a standing enchantment, or with magically assisted cargo-lifting in place of treadwheel cranes) as a way to subtly signal a wealthy, magic-integrated society without over-explaining it
- Corrupt customs officials as a classic, grounded intrigue hook — PCs might need to smuggle something past inspection, investigate official corruption, or navigate a bribery-soaked bureaucracy to get a legitimate shipment through
- A port's decline (mirroring Bruges' real historical silting problem) as a worldbuilding detail explaining a once-great city's reduced current circumstances — useful for a "faded glory" location with old wealth, resentment, and opportunity for those willing to invest in revival
