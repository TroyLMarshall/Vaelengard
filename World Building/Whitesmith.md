### The Craft and Its Structure
A **whitesmith** worked metal _cold_ rather than at the forge — or, in a second common usage, specialized in "white metals" like tin, pewter, and sometimes silver, as opposed to the "black" ironwork of a blacksmith. Both meanings coexisted and overlapped depending on region and period, so you have flexibility to pick whichever suits your setting (or blend them, which many real whitesmiths effectively did).

**Sense one: the finisher.** In this meaning, the whitesmith took roughly-forged ironwork from a blacksmith and did the fine work — filing, polishing, scraping, and burnishing it to a smooth, bright ("white") finish rather than the dark, scaled surface straight off the forge. This made them essential for **precision and decorative metalwork**: locks, hinges, cutlery, candlesticks, buckles, small tools, and fittings that needed to look refined or fit with tight tolerances. Where a blacksmith worked in raw power and heat, a whitesmith worked in patience and fine control — closer to what we'd think of as a locksmith, finisher, or precision toolmaker today.

**Sense two: the tin/pewter worker.** Here the whitesmith cast and worked soft, light-colored metals — tin, pewter, sometimes lead — into household goods: plates, cups, spoons, candlesticks, buttons, badges. This was much less physically demanding than blacksmithing (lower melting points, no heavy forge-hammering) and produced the everyday tableware of the middling and lower classes, since pewter was the affordable stand-in for silver.

**Guild status:** Whitesmiths generally sat lower in prestige than blacksmiths or armorers — the "black" trades were associated with strength, war-material, and dramatic transformation of raw ore, while "white" work was seen as more finicky and less heroic, despite requiring genuine and different skill. In cities large enough to support separate guilds, pewterers frequently organized into their own guild distinct from smiths (London's Worshipful Company of Pewterers is a real historical example), regulating alloy purity (pewter quality was graded, and cheap/adulterated pewter was a real consumer protection issue) and marking finished goods with a maker's touchmark, much like silver hallmarking.

**Locksmithing** is a great D&D-relevant offshoot of the finisher sense — whitesmiths who specialized in locks, keys, and small mechanisms occupied a niche that required real precision and a bit of a reputation for cleverness (and, inevitably, suspicion — someone who can make a lock can often defeat one).

**Daily texture worth stealing:**
- Whitesmiths often worked in **cooler, quieter shops** compared to a blacksmith's forge — no roaring fire, more filing/scraping/polishing by hand or foot-powered lathe.
- Pewter goods were **recyclable** — old, worn plates and cups were melted down and recast, so a whitesmith's shop was also a kind of exchange/pawn point for household metal goods.
- Because whitesmiths handled fine mechanisms and precision fittings, they were natural people to consult (or hire) for anything requiring **small, exact metal parts** — a category that maps neatly onto tinkering, trap components, or early mechanical devices in a fantasy setting.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Sleight of Hand, Investigation
- **Tool Proficiency:** Tinker's Tools (fits the precision/mechanism angle better than Smith's Tools) or Smith's Tools if you want the finisher/pewterer flavor instead
- **Feat:** Alert or Skilled
- **Equipment:** A set of fine files and polishing tools, a lockpick set (period-appropriate framing: "key-fitting tools"), a few pewter goods or a half-finished lock mechanism, traveling clothes, 10 gp

This background is a great reskin option for a **Rogue who isn't a criminal by trade** — someone whose legitimate, guild-respectable precision-metalwork background happens to have taught them exactly the skills a lockpick needs. It also fits Artificers extremely well if you're using that class, as the "maker of small precise mechanisms" flavor lines up almost perfectly.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Lock-Maker Who Can't Resist** — A whitesmith locksmith with an itch to test their own work by breaking into places, purely out of professional curiosity — not a thief exactly, but a walking ethical gray area the party might catch red-handed, or hire.
- **The Adulterator** — A pewterer caught cutting their alloy with cheap lead to save money, endangering customers (lead poisoning was a real period risk) — guild inspectors, a sick noble family, or a poisoned meal could all trace back to this NPC.
- **The Finisher in a Blacksmith's Shadow** — A whitesmith who does the delicate final work on a famous blacksmith's weapons but gets none of the credit or coin — a quiet resentment plot, or a hook where the "real" secret to a legendary blade's balance was this NPC all along.
- **The Touchmark Forger** — Someone stamping counterfeit maker's touchmarks on cheap pewter to sell it as guild-certified quality — small-scale, very local fraud-mystery hook, lower stakes than a swordsmith equivalent but great for a low-level urban adventure.
- **The Retired Locksmith Consultant** — An older whitesmith who no longer works but is the person everyone quietly visits when they need a lock identified, defeated, or built — a recurring "quest-giver via expertise" NPC.
### Game Hooks
- **The Melted Evidence** — A murder weapon or incriminating object was pewter and has since been melted down and recast into something innocuous by a whitesmith (willingly or not) — the PCs have to figure out what it became and track it down.
- **The Master Lock** — A vault, door, or magical container is sealed by a whitesmith-crafted lock famous for being nearly unbeatable — the PCs need to find the maker (alive or dead), steal their notes, or find someone who apprenticed under them.
- **Bad Batch** — A run of adulterated pewter tableware is making people sick across a town, and no one's connected the cases yet — a slow-burn mystery/investigation hook with a satisfying, mundane-but-dangerous villain (greed, not malice).
- **The Touchmark Trail** — Following counterfeit or stolen touchmark stamps from victim to forger to buyer, a heist/mystery hybrid that's easy to scale from a one-shot to a longer urban arc.
- **A Mechanism Beyond Their Skill** — A whitesmith is hired to build or repair something clearly beyond mundane technology (a piece of a golem, an artificer's device, part of a magical trap) and doesn't fully understand what they're working on — the party gets pulled in either to help them or to stop whoever hired them.
