### A Genuinely Sophisticated Mechanical Trade
We've referenced locksmiths briefly throughout this list — mentioned alongside blacksmith specializations, connected to the armorer's articulation/hinge work, touched on with chest construction in the furniture maker entry — so let's give this trade its dedicated treatment, since lock-making represented some of the most mechanically sophisticated metalwork available in the medieval period.
### Why Locksmithing Required Real Specialized Skill
Unlike the more universally-needed blacksmith trade covered earlier, locksmithing demanded a different kind of precision — not the raw strength and heat-shaping skill of general ironwork, but **fine mechanical design and precise fitting**, since a lock needed to function reliably through repeated use while resisting unauthorized opening. This made locksmithing a genuine specialization within the broader metalworking trade family, sometimes practiced by dedicated locksmiths and sometimes by particularly skilled blacksmiths taking on this specific work alongside their general trade.
### What Needed Locking
Connecting to themes throughout this list:
- **Chests**: as covered in the furniture maker entry, chests served as the primary household storage and security furniture, and a well-made lock was essential to their actual protective function — a chest holding valuables, documents, or money needed genuine security, not just a decorative latch
- **Doors**: house, workshop, and especially warehouse doors (connecting to the docks/wharfs entry's discussion of goods storage) required reliable locking mechanisms to protect contents
- **Gates**: castle and town gates, as covered in the guard entry, needed substantial locking mechanisms appropriate to their defensive significance
- **Strongboxes and coffers**: smaller, portable secure containers for valuables, coin, or important documents — particularly relevant for merchants (connecting to the freight company and banker entries) needing to transport or store money and goods securely
- **Cabinets and cupboards**: as mentioned in the furniture maker entry, storage furniture for valuable items required locking mechanisms
### Types of Locks
- **Padlocks**: detachable locks, often used with chests, gates, or other applications where a separate, removable locking mechanism suited the need
- **Built-in door/chest locks**: integrated mechanisms requiring more complex fitting work, built directly into the door or chest construction
- **Warded locks**: the dominant lock mechanism type for most of the medieval period — using fixed obstructions ("wards") inside the lock that only a correctly shaped key could navigate around to engage the locking mechanism. This is genuinely different from (and generally less secure than) the pin-tumbler mechanisms that became standard much later, but represented real mechanical sophistication for the available technology and metalworking precision of the era.
### The Craft Itself
- **Precise metal fitting**: unlike general blacksmith work that could tolerate some imprecision in non-critical dimensions, lock mechanisms required components that fit together with genuine precision to function reliably
- **Filing and fine shaping**: extensive hand-filing work to shape the internal mechanism components (wards, bolts, springs) to the necessary precision
- **Key-cutting**: producing the corresponding key required matching precision, since the key's shape needed to correctly navigate the lock's internal wards
- **Springs**: locks requiring spring-loaded mechanisms (for the bolt or other moving parts) needed properly tempered metal springs, connecting to the heat-treatment/tempering knowledge covered in the blacksmith entry
### Decorative and Status Dimensions
- **Elaborate decorative locks**: similar to several luxury crafts covered throughout this list, wealthy clients commissioned locks with genuine decorative elaboration — engraved, sometimes gilded or otherwise embellished locking mechanisms and keys, turning a purely functional security item into a status display object as well
- **Lock quality as wealth indicator**: similar to the housing quality signal discussed in the private homes entry, the sophistication and quality of a household's locks (and the furniture/doors they protected) provided another subtle wealth/status indicator
### Guild Structure
- Locksmiths sometimes constituted their own distinct guild in larger towns with sufficient specialized demand, or were grouped together with general smiths/metalworkers in smaller towns lacking enough business to support fully separate organization
- This connects to the broader pattern seen throughout this list where genuinely specialized trades (locksmiths, bowyers vs. fletchers, wax vs. tallow chandlers) sometimes achieved independent guild status in major cities while remaining folded into broader trade categories in smaller settlements
### Locks, Security, and Trust
Worth noting the broader social function: locks represented a genuine, tangible expression of medieval society's approach to property security in an era without other security technology (no alarms, no surveillance) — connecting to themes throughout this list about trust, reputation, and the relatively limited formal security apparatus available compared to modern expectations (recall the guard entry's discussion of how limited actual policing capability was). A good lock was a meaningful, practical investment in protecting one's property in a world where formal law enforcement and recovery of stolen goods couldn't be relied upon nearly as heavily as in modern contexts.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A master locksmith whose mechanisms are specifically sought after for protecting valuable or magical items — a natural, grounded "I need someone to make this chest/door secure" commission for a PC or NPC protecting something important
- Warded lock mechanisms as a genuinely satisfying basis for an actual lockpicking minigame or puzzle in your game — since real warded locks have an understandable, describable mechanical logic (navigating around fixed obstructions) that can translate into a tangible in-fiction challenge rather than an abstract dice roll
- A locksmith secretly moonlighting in less legitimate work — connecting to the rogue archetype discussed in the D&D classes entry, a locksmith with genuine legitimate trade standing who also has less savory skills/connections on the side
- An elaborately decorated lock or key as a meaningful plot object — perhaps a specific old, ornate lock (and its lost or hidden key) protects something significant to your plot, giving a mundane craft object real narrative weight
