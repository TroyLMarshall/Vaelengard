### A Trade We've Already Touched on Repeatedly — Now Given Full Treatment
We've referenced goldsmiths throughout this list — paired with the gem cutter, mentioned alongside furriers in luxury garment commissions, connected to banking and coinage — so let's bring the full picture together, since this was genuinely one of the most prestigious and well-regulated craft trades in medieval Europe.
### Goldsmith — The Primary Term
Worth establishing upfront: "jeweler" as a distinct specialized term is somewhat less precise historically than **goldsmith**, which was the dominant guild category covering work in precious metals broadly — gold and silver smithing, with gem-setting (often in collaboration with the lapidary/gem cutter covered earlier) as part of the broader craft rather than always a fully separate trade.
### What Goldsmiths Made
**Jewelry**: rings, brooches, necklaces, and other personal adornment for wealthy clientele, often incorporating gems prepared by lapidaries (as covered in that entry) — connecting the two luxury crafts in frequent direct collaboration

**Religious and ecclesiastical objects**: this was genuinely significant given the Church's wealth and the symbolic importance of precious materials in sacred contexts
- **Chalices and patens**: vessels used in the Mass, requiring precious metal given their sacred function
- **Reliquaries**: elaborate containers for housing holy relics, often extraordinarily ornate given the relic's sacred importance — some of the most impressive surviving medieval goldsmith work comes from these commissions
- **Crosses and crucifixes**: for both church and personal devotional use
- **Bishops' rings and croziers**: marking ecclesiastical office and status

**Royal and noble regalia**: crowns, scepters, and other symbols of authority required the highest level of goldsmith skill, often incorporating the finest available gems

**Coinage**: goldsmiths' technical expertise with precious metals connected directly to **minting** — striking coins required similar metalworking skill, and goldsmiths sometimes had direct involvement in mint operations or coin-related work given the overlapping technical knowledge

**Plate**: wealthy households displayed status through silver and gold "plate" — dishes, cups, and serving vessels valued both for use and as a visible, literally weighable store of wealth (precious metal plate could be melted down and used as currency/value in genuine financial emergency, making it a form of portable, displayable savings as much as pure decoration)

**Seals and signet rings**: connecting to the gem cutter entry's discussion of engraved seals — goldsmiths crafted the metal setting and mounting for these important authentication tools
### Techniques
- **Casting**: using lost-wax casting (as covered in the foundry entry) for more complex three-dimensional forms
- **Hammering and raising**: shaping sheet metal into vessels and forms through careful, repeated hammering
- **Engraving**: cutting decorative or inscriptional detail into finished metal surfaces
- **Filigree**: intricate decorative work using fine wires of gold or silver, twisted and soldered into delicate ornamental patterns
- **Enameling**: applying colored glass-like material (enamel) fused onto metal through heat, creating vivid decorative color effects — genuinely technically demanding work requiring real understanding of both metalworking and the glass-adjacent chemistry of enamel materials (connecting to the glassblower entry's discussion of glass chemistry)
- **Gem-setting**: mounting prepared gemstones securely and attractively into metal settings, requiring collaboration with or absorption of lapidary skill
### Guild Structure and Regulation — Particularly Strict
Given how much value passed through a goldsmith's hands, regulation was genuinely more intensive than many other trades:
- **Hallmarking**: many regions developed systems for officially testing and marking precious metal purity — London's goldsmiths' guild (the Worshipful Company of Goldsmiths) developed a particularly well-documented hallmarking system, stamping verified items with marks confirming metal purity, partly to protect customers from fraud and partly to protect the guild's collective reputation
- **Assaying**: the technical process of testing metal purity, a specialized skill in its own right, often performed by designated guild officials specifically empowered to inspect and certify goldsmith work
- **Severe penalties for fraud**: given how easily precious metal work could be debased or misrepresented (mixing in cheaper metal, for instance), guild and civic regulation treated goldsmith fraud with particular severity compared to many other trade violations
- **High capital requirements**: working with gold and silver required genuine capital investment in raw material, making goldsmithing naturally more restricted to those with either inherited wealth, patron backing, or substantial accumulated capital compared to lower-investment trades covered elsewhere in this list
### Goldsmiths and Early Banking
This connects directly back to the banker entry: goldsmiths' natural position — handling, storing, and verifying precious metal — meant they sometimes evolved informal banking-adjacent functions
- **Safekeeping**: customers might deposit gold or valuable items with a trusted goldsmith for secure storage, given the goldsmith's existing secure premises and handling expertise
- **Goldsmith's notes**: in some contexts (more fully developed in the early modern period than strictly medieval, but with roots extending backward), receipts for deposited gold began functioning somewhat like early paper currency or negotiable notes, foreshadowing more developed banking instruments
- This overlap reflects the same logical connection between precious-metal expertise and financial services seen with the Italian banking houses covered in the banker entry
### Status and Clientele
- **Among the most prestigious craft trades**: goldsmithing ranked alongside or above most other guild crafts in social status, given the wealth involved, the skill required, and the prestigious nature of the clientele (royalty, high nobility, the Church)
- **Urban concentration**: goldsmiths concentrated heavily in major cities with sufficient wealthy clientele to support the trade — London, Paris, major Italian cities, and similar significant urban centers
- **Real political/economic influence**: wealthy, successful goldsmiths could achieve genuine civic prominence and political influence, similar to other prosperous merchant/luxury-trade guild members covered throughout this list
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A goldsmith's hallmarking/assaying system as a worldbuilding tool for magic item authentication — perhaps your setting has an equivalent guild-verified marking system for confirming a magic item's properties or removing curse-risk uncertainty, grounded in genuine historical precedent for trusted third-party verification
- A goldsmith secretly functioning as an informal banker (echoing the real historical evolution) for a city's wealthy or noble clientele — a natural, grounded way to introduce a financially significant NPC without requiring a full separate banking institution
- An elaborate reliquary or crown commission as a "great work" plot element (echoing the construction company and glassblower entries' similar framing) — PCs might need to protect, recover, or authenticate such an object, with its construction history and craftsmanship mattering to the plot
- Goldsmith fraud (debased metal, false hallmarks) as a grounded mystery/investigation hook — uncovering a counterfeiting or fraud scheme within a respected, trusted trade, playing against the genuine historical strictness of real goldsmith guild regulation
