**La Foire** is the commercial heart of Port Ancelin — a sprawling open market square that sits between Warehouse Row and the harbour, close enough to the quay that wool can move from ship to stall to buyer in a single morning.
## **Layout & Rhythm**
The market runs on a weekly cycle, busiest on what locals call **Fleece Day**, when wagons come down from the inland pastures loaded with raw wool, and factors from Warehouse Row haul out their graded bales for inspection. Stalls are arranged roughly by trade: raw fleece nearest the road in from the hills, graded and baled wool nearer the warehouses, and a smaller ring of secondary trade — dyes, rope, sailcloth, tools — filling the gaps between.
## **The Weighing**
At the center stands the **Tron**, the great public scale where wool is officially weighed and taxed before sale — a holdover from the "staple" system, where exported wool had to pass through a licensed, inspected market. The Tron-keeper's word on weight is final, and bribing him is considered one of the few crimes the town genuinely prosecutes, since the entire town's credibility with foreign buyers depends on honest measure.
## **The Foreign Presence**
Flemish and Hanseatic merchants keep semi-permanent stalls or lease space from local factors during the trading season, and it's not unusual to hear three or four languages shouted across the square at once. Italian wool brokers tend to deal less in the open market and more through private arrangements brokered at the Fleece & Anchor — which is part of why the tavern's quiet back tables matter as much as La Foire's open stalls.
## **Disputes**
Wool grading is the market's perpetual argument — a farmer's fleece judged "second quality" instead of "first" can mean the difference between a good year and a ruinous one, and the **graders** (local men appointed by the wool guild) walk a constant line between honesty and the social pressure of judging their neighbors' livelihoods.
