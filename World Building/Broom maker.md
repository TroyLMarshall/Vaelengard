### A Humble, Rural-Adjacent Trade
Broom-making sits in similar territory to basket weaving — a modest, low-prestige craft requiring minimal capital investment, often practiced as a cottage industry or by itinerant craftsmen rather than organized into the elaborate guild structures of more prestigious trades. It's another one of those "everyone needs this, but it's not glamorous" professions that quietly underpinned daily medieval domestic life.
### The Besom — The Classic Medieval Broom
The traditional medieval broom is what's specifically called a **besom**: a bundle of twigs or brush bound around a wooden handle — visually, this is almost exactly the "witch's broom" image that's persisted in folklore and popular imagination, and that connection isn't coincidental (more on that below).
### Materials
- **Birch twigs**: probably the most classic and common besom material — birch's thin, flexible twigs bundled tightly made an effective sweeping surface
- **Heather**: used in regions where it grew abundantly, particularly in moorland areas
- **Broom plant (Cytisus scoparius)**: worth noting this shrub is literally named for its historical use in broom-making — its long, flexible, brushy stems were directly suited to the purpose
- **Other brush/twiggy material**: depending on local availability — willow, heath, or various scrubby undergrowth
- **Wooden handles**: a straight branch or cut wooden shaft, often hazel or similar straight-growing wood, sometimes simply debarked and used with minimal additional shaping
### Construction
The besom-making process was straightforward in concept, though it required practiced technique to produce a durable, well-balanced result:
1. **Gathering material**: cutting twigs, heather, or broom-plant stems, typically harvested at appropriate times of year for flexibility and durability
2. **Bundling**: gathering a substantial bundle of the brush material around one end of the handle
3. **Binding**: securing the bundle tightly using cord, willow withies (connecting back to basket-weaving material and technique), or sometimes wire in later periods — this needed to be tight and durable, since the binding point took continuous stress during use
4. **Trimming**: evening out the bristle ends for a functional sweeping surface
### Who Made and Used Besoms
- **Cottage/household production**: many rural households likely made their own simple besoms as needed, using readily available local material, rather than relying exclusively on a dedicated specialist — similar to basic basketry, this was a skill many people possessed at a basic level
- **Itinerant broom-sellers**: traveling craftsmen who made and sold besoms door-to-door or at markets, especially in areas without a settled dedicated broom-maker
- **Rural cottage industry**: in some regions, broom-making became a recognized small-scale trade, particularly where suitable raw material (heather moorland, broom-plant scrubland, birch woodland) was locally abundant, supporting people who specialized in production for sale rather than household self-sufficiency
- **Limited urban guild presence**: given the simplicity and low capital requirements, broom-making rarely warranted the kind of dedicated guild structure seen in higher-prestige trades — it's the kind of trade more likely handled informally or folded into broader categories
### Uses Beyond Simple Sweeping
- **Household cleaning**: the obvious primary function — sweeping floors (often dirt, rush-covered, or simple stone, given how rare fine flooring was for most of the population)
- **Threshing and agricultural use**: besom-style implements sometimes served agricultural functions like brushing grain
- **Ritual and symbolic sweeping**: some folk customs involved ceremonial sweeping (clearing out the old year, sweeping a threshold for luck or protection) — broom-related folklore around thresholds, doorways, and boundaries is widespread across European folk tradition
### The Witch Connection
The iconic image of a **witch flying on a broomstick** has genuine, if debated, roots reaching back toward the medieval and early modern periods. Several threads likely fed into this imagery:
- **Brooms as domestic, woman-associated objects**: like the alewife connection mentioned earlier, household tools strongly associated with women's domestic labor sometimes became symbolically loaded in later witch-persecution imagery and accusations
- **Folk ointments and "flying" claims**: some witch-trial confessions (often extracted under torture or duress, and not reliable evidence of actual practice) described applying hallucinogenic ointments to a staff or broom handle, possibly connected to genuine folk knowledge of psychoactive plant preparations, then straddling it — this is genuinely murky, debated historical territory, and most modern scholars treat these confession details with real skepticism given the coercive conditions under which they were obtained
- **The "broom" association solidifying later**: the fully iconic flying-witch-on-broomstick image as we'd recognize it today crystallized more in the **early modern period** (especially through witch-trial-era woodcuts and illustrations, 15th-17th centuries) rather than being a clearly defined medieval belief — so if your setting wants strict historical grounding, this image is more "late medieval into early modern" than purely medieval, though the broom's domestic/folk-magic associations likely have earlier roots
### Worldbuilding Angle
Given the witch connection is probably exactly why broom-makers are interesting to you:
- A broom maker whose besoms are sought after by a coven or individual witch/hedge-mage NPC for actual ritual or transportation use — letting you play with the folklore directly rather than just referencing it
- The broom-maker as an unwitting supplier to local "witch" suspicion — perhaps villagers side-eye the broom-maker's customer list, creating a small social-tension hook in a witch-trial-adjacent plotline
- A magically-treated besom as a minor, flavorful magic item — self-sweeping, or genuinely capable of minor levitation/flight as a nod to the folklore, sold by a broom-maker who either knows exactly what they're doing or stumbled into the enchantment by accident
- Broom-plant or heather harvesting tied to specific seasonal village festivals or folk customs, giving a rural settlement some lived-in texture beyond just "there's a broom maker here"
