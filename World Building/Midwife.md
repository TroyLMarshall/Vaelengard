### The Craft and Its Structure
Midwifery was almost entirely a **women's trade**, practiced and passed down within a female knowledge network largely separate from the male-dominated, university-trained medical establishment described in the surgeon entry. This separation matters enormously for how the profession functioned socially: midwives operated largely **outside formal guild or university structures** for most of the medieval period, learning through informal apprenticeship — often literally mother to daughter, or experienced midwife to younger woman in the community — rather than through any institutional credentialing system. This gave midwives real, hands-on practical authority within their specific domain while leaving them with little formal social status or legal protection compared to guild-organized male trades.

**What the work involved** — Attending births (the overwhelming core of the role), providing prenatal guidance and reassurance drawn from generations of accumulated practical experience, managing labor and delivery, handling complications as best contemporary knowledge allowed, cutting and tying the umbilical cord, and often performing **emergency baptism** of infants in danger of dying before a priest could arrive — a genuinely significant religious responsibility granted to midwives specifically because infant mortality was high and the era's theology placed enormous weight on baptism for a soul's fate. This baptismal authority is a fascinating and important detail: it meant the Church, despite general unease about women holding any kind of authority, formally recognized and depended on midwives for a task with real spiritual stakes, creating an unusual pocket of sanctioned female religious authority within an otherwise male-controlled institution.

**Knowledge base** — Midwives worked from **accumulated practical/oral tradition** — herbal remedies for pain and complications, physical techniques for managing difficult presentations, and hard-won experiential knowledge of what actually helped versus what didn't — rather than from the classical texts and humoral theory physicians relied on. This practical, empirical, trial-tested knowledge base was often genuinely more effective for actual childbirth outcomes than contemporary "learned" medicine, even though it carried far less institutional prestige. Some later medieval midwives, especially in wealthier or more urban contexts, did become literate in vernacular medical texts that began circulating, blending oral tradition with written knowledge.

**Real danger and real stakes** — Childbirth was genuinely one of the leading causes of death for women in the medieval period, and infant mortality was high regardless of the birth attendant's skill, given the era's medical limitations (no understanding of infection, no safe surgical intervention for complications like breech or obstructed labor beyond very limited manual techniques). A midwife's skill and judgment carried real weight in outcomes that were nonetheless frequently beyond anyone's control — this made the role emotionally weighty and gave experienced, trusted midwives significant standing within their communities, since families depended on them at the most dangerous moment in a woman's ordinary life.

**Social position and civic role** — In some towns and cities, midwives took on a **quasi-official civic function**: testifying in legitimacy disputes (confirming whether a child was born alive, full-term, or legitimate for inheritance purposes), examining women in cases involving claimed pregnancy (including, in some documented legal contexts, examining women who claimed pregnancy to delay execution), and generally serving as recognized authorities on matters of the female body that male officials had no direct access to or competence in. Some cities eventually moved toward licensing or examining midwives formally (a trend that accelerates into the early modern period), but for most of the medieval era the trade remained informally regulated, resting on **community reputation and trust** built over years of practice.

**The witchcraft association — historical caution** — Popular modern narratives often draw a strong direct line between midwives and witchcraft persecution; the real historical picture is more complicated and contested among historians, with midwife-witchcraft connections varying enormously by region, period, and specific case rather than being a uniform, constant danger throughout the "medieval" era proper (the most intense witch-hunt periods are largely early modern, not medieval). It's true that women with specialized herbal/bodily knowledge and community influence could become targets of suspicion, accusation, or scapegoating, especially after a bad outcome — a stillbirth, a maternal death, a difficult delivery — but this should be handled as a real, uneven social risk layered onto a generally respected and depended-upon trade, not a certainty or the trade's defining feature. Playing midwives as broadly trusted, valued community figures who _could_ become scapegoats under specific bad circumstances is more accurate than assuming constant persecution.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Medicine, Insight
- **Tool Proficiency:** Herbalism Kit
- **Feat:** Healer or Inspiring Leader
- **Equipment:** A birthing/herbalism kit, a small vial of blessed water (for emergency baptism, if fitting your setting's religion), a ledger of names — every birth attended and outcome recorded, common clothes, 8 gp

This background is a strong, grounded fit for **Clerics (Life or Peace domain, the emergency-baptism angle gives real built-in religious authority), Druids (herbal/oral-tradition knowledge base), or Bards (deep community trust and standing, a natural "everyone in town knows and confides in her" NPC-turned-PC angle)**. It's also a compelling background for a character motivated by **injustice or loss** — a midwife blamed unfairly for a bad outcome she couldn't have prevented, now carrying that weight (or that grievance) into adventuring life.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Trusted Community Midwife** — Decades of practice, respected and depended upon by an entire village or district, a natural source of deep local knowledge, gossip, and genuine authority that cuts across class lines (she's attended births among nobles and peasants alike).
- **The Blamed Midwife** — Facing suspicion, ostracism, or formal accusation after a difficult birth went badly despite her best effort — a sympathetic figure caught in a genuinely tragic, morally uncomplicated injustice.
- **The Civic Examiner** — Called upon by town officials for legitimacy disputes or legal examinations — a midwife with real, if unofficial, standing within local law and governance, and a good source of information about noble or wealthy family secrets.
- **The Herbal Innovator** — Known for unusually effective remedies and techniques, quietly building genuine informal medical expertise that a university physician would never credit her with — a good rival/ally dynamic with a Surgeon or Physician NPC from the earlier entry.
- **The Traveling Midwife** — Moves between villages too small to support their own, similar to the traveling farrier and saddler — a recurring NPC with wide-reaching local knowledge and relationships across a whole region.
### Game Hooks
- **The Wrongful Accusation** — A midwife is blamed for a tragic birth outcome and faces real social or legal danger — the party can investigate, defend, or advocate for her, a grounded and emotionally resonant "protect the innocent from scapegoating" hook.
- **The Legitimacy Dispute** — An inheritance or succession case hinges on a midwife's testimony about a birth years ago — political intrigue with the midwife as a key, quietly powerful witness both sides want to influence.
- **The Difficult Delivery** — A noble or otherwise important birth is going badly, and the party needs to secure the region's best midwife, protect her on the journey, or help her past whatever obstacle (weather, distance, a rival, a monster) stands between her and the patient — a tense, ticking-clock hook.
- **Knowledge Worth Stealing** — A midwife's genuinely effective herbal remedy or technique draws unwanted attention from a physician, alchemist, or apothecary wanting to claim or steal it for their own credit and profit.
- **The Emergency Baptism** — A midwife's sanctioned religious authority to baptize a dying infant becomes unexpectedly significant — a plot involving inheritance, legitimacy, or even (if your setting supports it) something genuinely spiritual/magical tied to that moment of sanctioned ritual authority.
