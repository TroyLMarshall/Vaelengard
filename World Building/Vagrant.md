### A Category Defined by What It Lacked
"Vagrant" in medieval Europe wasn't simply a description of someone who traveled — it was a specific, legally and socially loaded category describing someone who moved **without legitimate purpose, fixed residence, or recognized social attachment**. Understanding vagrancy requires understanding how thoroughly medieval society organized itself around fixed, recognized social positions — and how threatening someone who fell outside all of these categories seemed to established order.
### The Fundamental Medieval Social Expectation — Fixedness
Medieval social theory organized everyone into recognized categories with specific places, obligations, and relationships:
- **The three estates**: those who pray (clergy), those who fight (nobility), and those who work (everyone else) — a framework that assumed everyone belonged somewhere
- **The manorial system**: as covered in the farm and peasant entries, peasants were specifically bound to specific land and lord — geographical fixedness was literally built into the legal structure of rural life
- **Guild membership**: as covered in the craft guilds entry, urban craft workers operated within recognized institutional structures connecting them to specific trades, communities, and obligations
- **Household membership**: everyone was ideally part of some household — a family unit, a master's workshop household, a monastic community, a noble retinue — providing recognized social attachment

A **vagrant** was precisely someone who lacked all of these anchors — no fixed residence, no recognized lord or employer, no guild membership, no household attachment, moving between places without legitimate purpose. This wasn't simply poverty (the deserving poor covered in the beggar entry had recognized, socially accepted positions) but a specific kind of social disconnection that medieval authorities found genuinely threatening.
### Why Vagrancy Was Feared
The anxiety around vagrancy wasn't purely irrational — it reflected real concerns:

**Loss of social control mechanisms**: medieval law enforcement and social regulation operated primarily through community knowledge and community pressure, as covered in the constable and frankpledge entries. A stranger without local connections was outside these control mechanisms — unknown, without local reputation at stake, and therefore potentially dangerous in ways established community members weren't.

**Labor discipline concerns**: particularly after the Black Death (as covered in the laborer and peasant entries), labor shortages gave workers leverage, and authorities were specifically concerned about mobile workers who moved seeking better wages rather than staying put and accepting whatever local employers offered. Vagrancy laws were explicitly connected to labor discipline — vagrant as "able-bodied person not working where they should."

**Criminal association**: the same mobility that made vagrants socially threatening also made them natural associates with the criminal networks covered in the thieves' guild entry — not because all vagrants were criminals but because the same social disconnection that produced vagrancy also provided useful cover for criminal activity, and vagrant populations genuinely did include criminals alongside the simply unfortunate.

**Religious anxiety**: connecting to the moral framework covered throughout this list, vagrancy was associated with idleness (a sin), begging (potentially fraudulent), and association with the disreputable trades (gambling, prostitution) covered in earlier entries — the vagrant as morally as well as socially suspect.
### Who Actually Became Vagrants
The vagrant population wasn't monolithic — it included diverse people who'd ended up outside normal social structures for various reasons:

**Displaced peasants**: those who'd left their home manor (legally or illegally, as covered in the indentured servants entry's discussion of serf flight) without establishing themselves elsewhere — between their old fixed position and any new one

**Demobilized soldiers**: particularly significant after major military campaigns ended, soldiers who'd been fighting and living on military wages suddenly without employment, without skills applicable to civilian guild trades, and without established community connections — a recognized, specific vagrancy problem that intensified at the end of major conflicts like the Hundred Years' War

**Failed craftsmen and journeymen**: as covered in the craft guilds entry, journeymen who couldn't achieve master status sometimes became permanently mobile workers without settled position — legitimately skilled but without institutional anchor, sometimes gradually sliding toward more genuinely vagrant status

**Escaped serfs**: as covered in the indentured servants entry, serfs who'd fled their manors were technically fugitives whose legal status created real difficulties establishing themselves legitimately elsewhere, pushing them toward vagrancy

**Unemployed clergy**: clergy without positions (benefices) who weren't attached to any parish, monastery, or household — a recognized problem the Church itself acknowledged, since unattached clergy without income were both economically desperate and potentially theologically dangerous

**Plague survivors**: the Black Death's mass mortality (covered repeatedly throughout this list) disrupted family and community structures dramatically — orphaned children, widowed people without family support, those whose communities had been so devastated that normal social structures had collapsed — producing genuine involuntary vagrancy as a side effect of demographic catastrophe

**Pilgrims and students**: a more legitimate category of mobile person whose movement had recognized purpose — but who shaded into vagrancy when resources ran out and the recognized purpose became less clearly sustainable
### Legal Treatment — Increasingly Harsh
As the medieval period progressed, particularly in the later period, vagrancy legislation became significantly harsher:

**The English Statute of Labourers (1351)**: as covered in the laborer entry, explicitly targeted mobile workers — requiring able-bodied men to work for whoever requested their labor at pre-plague wage rates, essentially criminalizing the labor mobility that workers were using to leverage better wages

**Vagrancy Acts**: various English legislation through the 14th-15th centuries specifically targeted vagrants — requiring them to return to their home parishes, permitting their punishment (whipping, branding, or worse in later periods), and placing responsibility for the local poor on specific parishes rather than allowing mobile begging

**The distinction between "deserving" and "undeserving" poor**: as covered in the beggar entry, this distinction became increasingly legally codified — the disabled, elderly, and genuinely unable to work having some recognized claim to charitable support, while able-bodied vagrants were increasingly treated as criminal rather than simply unfortunate

**Bridewells and houses of correction**: toward the very end of the medieval and into the early modern period, specific institutions developed specifically to house and set to work vagrants — an early form of workhouse reflecting both genuine welfare concern and punitive labor discipline motivation
### The Lived Experience of Vagrancy
What actually being a vagrant looked like in medieval Europe:

**Constant movement**: by definition, vagrants lacked settled residence — sleeping in barns, under hedges, in abandoned buildings, or wherever shelter could be found, connecting to the huts entry's discussion of minimal improvised shelter

**Marginal economic survival**: begging (connecting to that entry), occasional casual labor (connecting to the laborer entry), petty theft, and whatever combination of opportunistic economic activity presented itself

**Mutual support networks**: vagrant populations developed their own informal mutual support — information about where food and shelter could be found, which routes were safer, and which communities were hospitable or dangerous, passed through networks of those sharing similar circumstances

**The thieves' guild overlap**: as covered in that entry, the vagrant population and criminal underworld overlapped significantly — not because vagrancy caused criminality but because both existed in the same marginal social space
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A vagrant PC background as genuinely rich character territory — someone who's fallen outside all the normal social structures covered throughout this list, whose knowledge is broad but whose social position is precarious, who's developed the specific skills of survival on the margins (reading people quickly, finding shelter, moving unnoticed) that adventuring life rewards
- The demobilized soldier vagrant as a specific, sympathetic NPC type — someone with genuine military competence (connecting to the militaries and mercenary entries) whose post-war circumstances have left them without legitimate employment or community, making them potentially recruitable, potentially dangerous, and certainly interesting
- A vagrant network's information as a specific resource — people who move constantly, sleep in unexpected places, and are generally invisible to settled society see things that settled people miss, making the vagrant community a genuine intelligence resource for PCs willing to engage with rather than dismiss them
- The parish responsibility system for managing local poor as a plot element — a specific vagrant whose presence in a town creates genuine administrative and moral pressure on local authorities, with competing interests (the priest's pastoral concern, the constable's order-maintenance concern, the guild's labor-discipline concern) producing complex local politics around one person's circumstances
