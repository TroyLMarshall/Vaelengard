### The Key Distinction From Craft Guilds
As established in the craft guilds entry: where craft guilds organized **producers** of a specific good (bakers, smiths, dyers), merchant guilds organized **traders** — people whose business was buying, selling, and moving goods rather than making them. A wealthy cloth merchant guild member might never touch a loom himself, instead controlling the buying of raw wool, financing weavers and dyers, and selling finished cloth at profit, sometimes across considerable distances.
### Why Merchant Guilds Often Outranked Craft Guilds Politically
This is worth drawing out explicitly, since it wasn't always obvious from the entries that mentioned it in passing: merchant guilds frequently held **greater civic political power** than craft guilds, even though craft guilds were often more numerous. This reflects simple economic reality — merchants typically commanded more capital, controlled supply chains craftsmen depended on (a weaver might be effectively beholden to the merchant who supplied his wool and bought his finished cloth), and their wealth translated directly into political influence in town governance. In many towns, the **merchant guild essentially _was_ the town government**, or close to it — aldermen, mayors, and council seats disproportionately filled by merchant guild members.
### The Staple System
Briefly mentioned in the caravan guild entry, worth expanding: certain merchant guilds secured **staple rights** — exclusive legal control over trade in a specific commodity through a specific town. England's wool trade is the clearest example: the **Company of the Staple** held a government-sanctioned monopoly directing wool exports through specific designated staple towns (Calais was the most famous staple port for English wool for a significant stretch), letting the crown more easily tax and control this hugely important trade while giving staple merchants enormous, legally protected commercial advantage.
### Merchant Guild Self-Protection Functions
- **Price and quality standards for trade goods**: similar to craft guild quality regulation, merchant guilds often set standards for how goods should be graded, packaged, and represented, protecting collective reputation in distant markets where buyers couldn't personally verify a specific merchant's reliability
- **Collective bargaining power**: a unified merchant guild could negotiate trade privileges, tax exemptions, or protected status with foreign rulers or rival cities far more effectively than scattered individual merchants — exactly the function the Hanseatic League performed at massive scale
- **Restricting outside competition**: like craft guilds, merchant guilds often restricted full trading rights within a town to guild members, protecting members from being undercut by outside merchants — visiting traders might face tolls, restricted selling locations/times, or other limitations non-members didn't.
### Worldbuilding Angle
Since we've covered the Hanseatic League and general structure thoroughly already, the genuinely fresh hook here is the **merchant-guild-as-de-facto-government** angle: a city in your setting where the wealthy merchant guild's leadership effectively _is_ the ruling council creates a different power dynamic than the noble-court politics covered in the gentlemen/ladies entry — commerce-driven rather than birthright-driven, with succession, alliance, and rivalry playing out through trade deals and capital rather than marriage and inheritance.
