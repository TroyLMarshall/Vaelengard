### A Trade We've Referenced Repeatedly, Now Given Full Dedicated Treatment
Thatching has appeared throughout this list as background detail — the private homes entry mentioned thatched roofs as the dominant roofing material for ordinary medieval housing, the huts entry referenced thatching in simple shelter construction, the fire risk discussions in the baker and candlemaker entries noted thatch as a major urban fire hazard — so let's give the actual craft and its practitioners their proper dedicated treatment.
### Why Thatching Was So Dominant
Before understanding the craft, worth establishing why thatch was so universally used for ordinary medieval housing across much of Europe:
- **Readily available locally**: thatching materials (straw, reed, heather) were agricultural byproducts or naturally growing materials available in most regions without the transport costs of alternative roofing materials
- **Cheap**: compared to stone slates, clay tiles, or wooden shingles, thatch represented minimal material cost — primarily labor rather than expensive raw materials
- **Effective insulation**: a well-laid thick thatch provides genuine thermal insulation significantly better than thin tile or slate alternatives — genuinely warm in winter, relatively cool in summer
- **Workable with simple tools**: unlike masonry or tile work requiring specialized equipment and materials, thatching required primarily physical skill, a few simple tools, and the raw material itself
- **Self-draining**: properly angled and laid thatch sheds rain effectively, with the dense outer layer shedding water before it penetrates to the underlying structure
### Thatching Materials
Different materials produced different quality and durability results:

**Long straw**: threshed wheat or rye straw, the most widely available thatching material across much of England and continental Europe
- **Availability**: directly connected to the grain harvest covered in the farm entry — threshing grain for food produced straw as a byproduct, making it abundantly available in agricultural communities
- **Durability**: typically lasting 15-25 years before requiring replacement or significant repair — shorter-lived than water reed but widely accessible

**Water reed** (Norfolk reed particularly):
- **Superior durability**: water reed thatching can last 50-60 years in good conditions — significantly longer-lived than straw thatching
- **Specialized sourcing**: requires wetland areas where suitable reed grows — connecting to the regional variation theme throughout this list, with reed-thatching traditions particularly associated with wetland regions like the Norfolk Broads in England, the Netherlands, and similar wetland areas
- **More prestigious**: the superior durability made water reed the higher-quality, more expensive option

**Heather**: particularly used in upland and moorland regions where straw and reed were less available, connecting to the broom-making entry's discussion of heather as a regional material

**Sedge**: sometimes used for ridge capping even on reed or straw-thatched roofs, as it bends more readily around the roof's peak
### The Thatching Process
**Preparation**:
- **Sorting and cleaning material**: raw straw or reed needed sorting to remove damaged or short material unsuitable for thatching — consistent length and quality produced better, more uniform finished work
- **Dampening**: material often dampened slightly before use, making it more flexible and easier to work

**Base preparation**:
- **Battens or spars**: wooden battens fixed horizontally across the roof structure provided attachment points — connecting to the carpenter entry's roof construction discussion
- **Existing thatch management**: when re-thatching an existing building, the thatcher needed to assess whether the old thatch could simply have new material laid over it (common practice, with layers accumulating over generations) or required full stripping

**Laying the thatch**:
- **Working bottom to top**: thatching proceeded from the eaves upward, each course overlapping the one below — similar in principle to roof tile laying, ensuring water always ran onto rather than under the edge of each course
- **Fixing**: bundles (called **yealms** for straw, **bunches** for reed) were fixed to the underlying battens using hazel spars — split hazel rods bent into a staple-like shape and driven through the thatch into the batten — or tied with cord
- **Dressing**: a leggett (a flat board with a serrated face) was used to drive the material upward into a tight, compact surface — a distinctive rapping action visible in period depictions of thatching
- **Achieving consistent thickness**: a proper thatch needed sufficient depth (typically a foot or more) to shed water effectively without penetrating to the underlying structure

**Ridge work**:
- **The most skilled element**: the ridge — where two roof slopes met at the peak — required particular skill to finish weather-tightly and neatly, since this was both the most structurally vulnerable point and the most visually prominent
- **Decorative ridge patterns**: skilled thatchers developed recognizable decorative ridge patterns — straw twisted into ornamental shapes along the ridge peak — which became a genuine craft signature, with regional traditions producing distinctive local styles visible to anyone familiar with the tradition
### Tools
- **Leggett/legget**: the paddle-like dressing tool described above
- **Side rake**: for trimming the thatch surface to a neat, even appearance after laying
- **Shears**: for trimming eaves and edges cleanly
- **Needle and cord**: for some fixing methods
- **Simple ladder and scaffolding**: for access — thatchers working at height on simple wooden scaffolding
### Who Thatched
- **Itinerant specialist thatchers**: many thatchers traveled between jobs, moving from village to village as work required — similar to the peddler and itinerant craftsman pattern seen elsewhere in this list, since no single village generated enough continuous thatching demand to support a full-time resident specialist
- **Local farmer/craftsman overlap**: in smaller communities, someone with basic thatching knowledge might handle straightforward repairs without waiting for a specialist
- **Seasonal work patterns**: thatching was strongly seasonal — dry summer and autumn weather being preferable for both the work itself and the material condition, with winter weather making rooftop work difficult and wet thatch harder to work
### Guild Structure and Status
- Thatching's itinerant, rural nature meant it was less guild-organized than urban craft trades
- In larger towns with sufficient demand, thatchers might be absorbed into broader building trades guilds alongside other roofing and construction workers
- **Social position**: modest but recognized — skilled thatchers with good reputations for durable, neat work could find steady employment across a wide area, while poorer quality work was immediately and literally exposed to the weather
### Fire Risk — The Thatch's Fundamental Weakness
As touched on throughout this list, thatched roofs were a genuine, serious fire hazard:
- **Highly flammable**: dry thatch caught fire easily from sparks, particularly in summer dry conditions
- **Rapid fire spread**: once alight, thatch burned quickly and spread fire readily to adjacent buildings in densely packed medieval towns
- **Urban regulation response**: many medieval towns eventually banned or restricted thatch within city walls, requiring stone, slate, or tile roofing specifically to reduce urban fire risk — driving the gradual replacement of thatch in urban contexts even while it remained dominant in rural areas
- **Military significance**: thatch made settlements extremely vulnerable to deliberate fire attack — an enemy force could devastate a village with relatively little effort by simply setting fire to roofs, connecting to the militaries entry's discussion of medieval warfare's impact on civilian populations
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- **Arson and fire risk as a genuine plot mechanic**: a settlement's thatched roofs making deliberate fire attack a real, devastating threat that changes the stakes of conflict — not just "fight the bandits" but "stop them before they torch the village," with the vulnerable thatch making this urgency believable
- **A traveling thatcher NPC** with wide geographic knowledge — someone who moves between villages, knows the landscape and people across a wide area, and accumulates information about local conditions, strange occurrences, and recent events as a natural byproduct of their itinerant work, similar to the peddler's information-network role
- **Decorative ridge patterns as regional identity markers** — a worldbuilding detail where specific thatching styles identify a building's region of origin or a thatcher's training tradition, useful for establishing regional variation and potentially for identifying where someone comes from based on the roof over their head
- **Thatch layer archaeology**: the practice of laying new thatch over old (sometimes accumulating many layers over generations) as a worldbuilding detail — something hidden in a building's deep thatch layers (old correspondence, a concealed object, even old evidence of past events) discoverable during re-thatching work
