### The Economic Foundation Underlying Everything Else
This is worth pausing on, since virtually every trade we've covered — bakers, butchers, brewers, dyers, even the wealthy nobility paying for armor and feasts — ultimately depended on agricultural surplus produced by farms. The overwhelming majority of medieval Europe's population (commonly estimated around 85-90%) worked the land in some capacity, making the farm the actual economic bedrock beneath all the specialized urban trades.
### The Manor System
Most medieval farmland operated within the **manorial system** — a structure of landholding and obligation that organized rural life:
- **The lord's demesne**: land directly held and farmed for the lord's own benefit, worked through peasant labor obligations rather than the lord's own hands
- **Peasant holdings**: land allocated to peasant families for their own use and subsistence, in exchange for labor service, rent, or a combination of both
- **The manor house**: the lord's residence, the administrative center of the estate
- **Common/waste land**: shared land used for grazing livestock, gathering firewood, and other communal purposes — genuinely important to peasant subsistence beyond their individual holdings, and a frequent source of later historical conflict as enclosure movements (mostly post-medieval) gradually restricted these traditional shared rights
### Types of Peasant Status
This significantly shaped a farmer's actual freedom and obligations:
- **Serfs/villeins**: legally bound to the land and lord, owing labor service (working the lord's demesne land a set number of days per week) and various other obligations, generally unable to leave the manor without permission — not slaves exactly, since they had recognized rights to their own holdings and couldn't simply be sold separately from the land, but genuinely unfree in meaningful ways
- **Free peasants/free tenants**: held land with more independence, typically owing rent (in cash or kind) rather than labor service, and with more freedom of movement
- **Cottagers/smallholders**: held smaller plots, often needing to supplement their farming income with wage labor for wealthier neighbors or the lord
- **Landless laborers**: worked for wages on others' land, occupying the most economically precarious position in rural society
### The Open Field System
Much of medieval European arable farming (particularly well-documented in England, though variations existed across the continent) operated through the **open field system**:
- **Strips, not enclosed fields**: rather than the hedged, enclosed fields familiar from later periods, farmland was divided into long, narrow strips scattered across two or three large open fields, with individual peasants holding scattered strips rather than one consolidated plot
- **Crop rotation**: fields were rotated between different crops and **fallow** (left unplanted to recover fertility) on a multi-year cycle — commonly a three-field system (one field for autumn-sown crops like wheat or rye, one for spring-sown crops like oats, barley, or legumes, and one left fallow) became increasingly common, an improvement over the earlier two-field system in terms of land productivity
- **Communal decision-making**: because everyone's strips lay within shared large fields, decisions about what to plant, when to harvest, and field management were necessarily made collectively at the village level, through manorial courts or established custom, rather than individual farmers having full independent control over their own land
### Crops and Livestock
**Grains** — the dietary backbone:
- **Wheat**: higher-value, often associated with bread for wealthier consumption (recall the "manchet" white bread distinction from the baker entry)
- **Rye and barley**: hardier, often making up the bulk grain for ordinary bread and also significant for ale brewing
- **Oats**: animal feed and human consumption both, particularly important in regions/climates less suited to wheat

**Legumes**: peas and beans, valuable both for direct food and for their soil-enriching nitrogen-fixing properties (understood empirically through accumulated practice, without the underlying scientific explanation)

**Livestock**:
- **Cattle**: draft power (oxen, primarily, rather than horses for much of the medieval period in plowing), dairy (as covered), and eventually meat
- **Sheep**: enormously economically significant, particularly for wool feeding the textile trade discussed with dyers — England's wool trade was a massive driver of the medieval economy specifically
- **Pigs**: efficient, could forage on marginal land/forest mast (acorns, beechnuts), providing meat without competing directly with humans for grain
- **Horses**: increasingly used for plowing and transport as the medieval period progressed, supplementing or replacing oxen in various regions, partly tied to improvements in horse collar technology that let horses pull more effectively without choking
### Tools and Technology
- **The plow**: heavy moldboard plows, often requiring a full team (sometimes 6-8 oxen for heavier clay soils) capable of actually turning the soil rather than just scratching it — a significant technological advance over lighter Mediterranean-style plows, particularly important for opening up heavier, wetter northern European soils to cultivation
- **Harrows**: for breaking up soil after plowing
- **Sickles and scythes**: for harvesting grain by hand — genuinely demanding seasonal labor requiring the whole community's coordinated effort during harvest time
- **Watermills and windmills**: for grinding grain into flour, representing significant capital investment (often lord-owned, tied to the same banal rights system discussed with bakers' ovens) and genuine mechanical sophistication
### The Agricultural Calendar
Farm life followed an inescapable seasonal rhythm:
- **Spring**: plowing and planting spring crops, lambing and calving
- **Summer**: tending crops, haymaking (critical for winter animal feed)
- **Harvest** (late summer/autumn): the most labor-intensive, time-critical period of the year — getting grain harvested before weather damage was an existential community priority, often requiring even non-farming community members to help
- **Autumn**: plowing and planting autumn-sown crops, slaughtering livestock that couldn't be fed through winter (connecting to the butcher entry and the seasonal logic behind salt preservation)
- **Winter**: lower-intensity but still essential work — equipment maintenance, indoor crafts, livestock care, and surviving on stored/preserved food
### Vulnerability and Risk
- **Weather dependency**: a bad harvest meant genuine famine risk, not just inconvenience — medieval agriculture had limited buffer against poor weather, pest outbreaks, or disease
- **The Black Death's impact**: the mid-14th century plague devastated rural populations specifically, leading to significant long-term shifts in the manorial system as labor scarcity gave surviving peasants genuinely improved bargaining power, accelerating the gradual decline of strict serfdom in many regions
- **Tax and obligation burden**: between lordly dues, Church tithes, and royal taxation, peasant households operated with real, often heavy extraction from their agricultural output, leaving relatively thin margins for the producers themselves
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A farming village's manorial obligations as a believable, grounded backdrop for a low-level adventure — a corrupt steward extracting excessive labor dues, a magical blight threatening the harvest, or peasant unrest brewing over genuine, historically-grounded grievances
- The open-field, communal-decision-making system as a worldbuilding detail explaining why a village acts collectively rather than as scattered independent farmsteads — useful for grounding "the whole village" as a coherent social unit PCs might need to negotiate with or help
- A magically blighted or blessed harvest as a classic, well-grounded quest hook — the stakes of crop failure were genuinely existential in this period, giving real weight to a seemingly mundane "help the harvest" plot
- The serf/free peasant distinction as a worldbuilding tool for exploring your setting's social mobility and justice themes — a PC's background as an escaped or freed serf carries real, historically-grounded narrative weight
