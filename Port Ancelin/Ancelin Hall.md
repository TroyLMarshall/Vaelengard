**Ancelin Hall** crowns the high ground at the northern edge of town, overlooking the harbour, the market, and the rooftops of Port Ancelin spread out below — a position chosen as much for visibility and authority as for defense.
## **Origins**
Built by Ancelin de Varnay himself once his original harborside home (later the site of the Fleece & Anchor) became too modest for a man whose fortune was growing with every shipload of wool that left the quay. The Hall was meant to announce permanence — that the de Varnays weren't passing through, but intended to root themselves in Port Ancelin for generations.
## **Architecture**
Less a fortress than a fortified manor — thick stone walls and a modest gatehouse speak to genuine Norman caution in unsettled times, but the Hall was never built to withstand a real siege. Its real grandeur is in scale and position: long stone halls, a great chamber for receiving guests and conducting town business, and windows deliberately placed to frame views of the harbour, a constant reminder of where the family's wealth comes from.
## **The de Varnay Line**
For generations, the Hall has been the seat of the de Varnay family, who hold a position somewhere between landed nobility and merchant dynasty — a blending the Hall's architecture reflects, with a knight's defensive instincts layered under a wool baron's taste for comfort and display. The family's coat of arms, the same silver ship and wool bales found on the town's crest, is carved above the Hall's main entrance.
## **Role in Town Governance**
Though Port Ancelin has its own wool guild, market officials, and church to manage daily affairs, the de Varnay family retains real influence — collecting certain harbour dues, holding a customary seat in major guild disputes, and serving as a final court of appeal when town institutions can't resolve a matter themselves. It's a soft power rather than absolute rule: the family needs the town's prosperity as much as the town tolerates their authority, and a de Varnay who governed too heavy-handedly would quickly find wool merchants quietly taking their business elsewhere.
## **A Living Connection**
Celyn Street, running from the Hall down toward the harbour, is named for the family and was the original processional route between the Hall and the original de Varnay home — meaning every wool cart, every merchant, and every traveler walking that street is, knowingly or not, retracing the family's own journey from modest founder to established power.
## **A Quiet Tension**
As with the church, some in town mutter that the de Varnays' continued influence — collecting dues, sitting in judgment, claiming the grandest church window — sits uneasily with the town's pride in guild self-governance and earned mercantile success rather than inherited title. So far, this tension has stayed at the level of grumbling rather than open conflict, in part because the current generation of de Varnays has been careful not to overreach.
