### The Craft and Its Structure
Salt was one of the most strategically vital commodities in medieval Europe — essential for basic survival (a genuine dietary necessity), and absolutely critical for **preserving meat and fish** in an age with no refrigeration, meaning entire populations' winter food security depended on adequate salt supply. This gave salt production, unlike most crafts covered so far, a **direct national/regional strategic importance** comparable to wool or grain — control over salt sources meant control over a population's ability to survive the lean season, and rulers taxed, licensed, and fought over salt production and trade accordingly.

**Two production methods, two very different trades:**

**Solar/brine evaporation (salterns)** — In coastal regions with suitable climate (the Atlantic coast of France, parts of Iberia, the Mediterranean generally), seawater was channeled into shallow **salt pans** and allowed to evaporate under sun and wind over the course of a season, leaving behind crystallized salt that workers then raked and harvested by hand. This was seasonal, weather-dependent, labor-intensive work, often organized around whole coastal communities whose economic life revolved around the salt-harvest calendar the way inland communities revolved around the grain harvest.

**Brine boiling (wich-houses)** — In inland regions with natural brine springs (England's Droitwich and Nantwich — the "wich" suffix in English place names literally marks historic salt-production sites — plus significant Continental sites like Lüneburg in Germany, and Salzburg, whose very name means "salt fortress"), workers pumped or channeled naturally salty groundwater into large lead or iron pans and **boiled it continuously** over wood or (later, controversially, as forests depleted) coal fires to evaporate the water and leave crystallized salt behind. This was brutally hot, smoky, exhausting labor — tending massive boiling pans for hours in intense heat — and consumed enormous quantities of **fuel wood**, making salt-boiling towns significant drivers of local deforestation and a real, documented source of medieval environmental strain and fuel-supply conflict.

**Salt as a state monopoly** — Because salt was universally needed and difficult to produce anywhere and everywhere, it became one of the medieval and early-modern world's favorite targets for **taxation and state monopoly**. France's infamous **gabelle** (a mandatory salt tax requiring households to purchase a fixed minimum quantity of state-monopoly salt at fixed, often punishingly high prices) is the standout historical example — though it reached its most notorious, rigid form slightly past the strict medieval period, the underlying pattern of rulers treating salt production and sale as a lucrative, tightly controlled revenue source is genuinely medieval and widespread across many regions and periods. This created real, sustained popular resentment — a tax on a literal survival necessity, impossible to avoid or substitute away from, hit poorer households hardest and generated exactly the kind of grinding, unglamorous grievance that fueled real unrest and rebellion in multiple documented cases.

**Smuggling** — Given the huge gap between actual production cost and state-taxed sale price, **salt smuggling** was a widespread, genuinely significant medieval and early-modern criminal economy — smugglers ("faux-sauniers" in the French tradition) moved illicit untaxed salt across jurisdictional and tax-zone borders, facing serious punishment if caught, but supported by real, sympathetic popular demand from communities squeezed by official salt pricing. This is fantastic, ready-made adventure material: an entire underground economy built around evading a hated tax on a basic necessity, with real moral ambiguity (breaking the law vs. surviving an unjust system) built in.

**Guild and labor structure** — Salt production organized differently depending on method and region: coastal saltern work was often organized around **community or family-worked pans** with rights to specific plots passed down generationally, while inland wich-house boiling operations, requiring more capital (lead pans, fuel supply, brine-rights) sometimes organized more like the paper mill model — capital-intensive operations owned by wealthier proprietors, worked by hired laborers doing genuinely brutal, low-status physical labor for wages. Salt merchants and rights-holders (owning a productive brine spring, or holding a monopoly license) could become genuinely wealthy, while the actual boilers and pan-workers occupied a harder, more physically punishing, lower-status position within the trade.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Nature, Survival (or Persuasion, for a smuggler-leaning version)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Alchemist's Supplies (fits the boiling/evaporation/purity-judging process) or Cook's Utensils
- **Feat:** Tough or Skilled
- **Equipment:** A set of salt-working tools (rakes, boiling pan fragments, or evaporation gear), a small stock of salt (genuinely valuable trade good), a smuggler's hidden compartment or false-bottomed cart (optional, if leaning into that angle), 10 gp

This background suits a **Fighter or Barbarian** (physically brutal labor background, comfortable with hard, dangerous work) particularly well, and offers an excellent, distinct **smuggler-flavored Rogue** variant — someone who grew up in the illicit salt trade specifically, skilled at evading tax officials and moving contraband, with sympathetic (not villainous) motivations baked directly into the concept. It also works for a **Druid or Ranger** tied to coastal saltern life and its seasonal, weather-dependent rhythms.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Wich-House Boiler** — Works brutal, smoke-choked hours tending brine pans, physically hardened and weary, a good grounded working-class NPC whose life illustrates the real cost behind an everyday commodity.
- **The Gabelle Collector** — A tax official enforcing mandatory salt purchase quotas, genuinely disliked, caught between unpopular duty and the crown's demand for revenue — a good minor antagonist without being a cartoonish villain.
- **The Faux-Saunier** — A salt smuggler, sympathetic and resourceful, running contraband salt to communities squeezed by official pricing — a natural ally or recruiter for a party sympathetic to the cause, or a moral dilemma if the party's hired to stop them.
- **The Saltern Family** — A coastal family holding generational rights to a specific salt pan, deeply tied to the tide and season, resistant to a lord or state trying to seize or monopolize their traditional rights.
- **The Fuel-Rights Disputant** — A wich-house owner locked in conflict with local woodland rights-holders or a rival operation over increasingly scarce fuel wood — an environmental-economic conflict hook, genuinely medieval in flavor.
### Game Hooks
- **The Smuggling Run** — The party is hired (or chooses) to move contraband salt past tax checkpoints for a community that can't afford official prices — a smuggling/stealth/social-evasion adventure with real, sympathetic stakes on both sides of the law.
- **The Salt Tax Revolt** — Local anger over crushing salt taxation boils toward real unrest or rebellion, and the party gets pulled into a genuinely grounded "unjust law vs. desperate populace" civic crisis, without an easy clean villain.
- **The Poisoned Batch** — A wich-house's salt supply is somehow contaminated or sabotaged, threatening a whole region's ability to preserve food before winter — a ticking-clock investigation with real survival stakes.
- **Fuel and Fire** — Escalating conflict between a salt-boiling operation and a community/woodland it depends on for fuel, forcing the party to broker, investigate, or intervene in a resource conflict with real medieval texture.
- **The Monopoly Seizure** — A lord or crown moves to seize a traditionally-held saltern or brine-spring right from a family or community that's worked it for generations, and the party is asked to help resist, negotiate, or document the community's traditional claim before it's lost.
