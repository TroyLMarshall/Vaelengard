### The Craft and Its Structure
Ink making was rarely a fully independent, guild-organized trade the way bookbinding or brewing was — for most of the medieval period it existed as a **specialized skill practiced by or adjacent to the people who used it**: monks in the scriptorium, professional scribes, illuminators, and stationers in university towns. Recipes were often closely guarded knowledge passed down within a monastery or workshop rather than sold as a finished commercial product on an open market, though by the later medieval period, as secular book production expanded, dedicated ink-sellers and specialist preparers did emerge in larger cities, especially those tied to university and stationer networks.

**Iron gall ink** was the dominant writing ink of the medieval period, and it's worth describing in some detail because the process itself is genuinely evocative material. It was made by combining **oak galls** (the round growths oak trees form around wasp larvae — collected, dried, crushed, and steeped, essentially a byproduct of an insect's parasitism on a tree) with **iron sulfate** (called "copperas" or green vitriol) and a binding agent, usually **gum arabic** (tree resin, imported from North Africa/the Middle East, which kept the ink workable and gave it body). The reaction between the tannic acid in the crushed galls and the iron sulfate produced the ink's dark color, which actually **deepened and set into the parchment over time** as it oxidized — freshly written iron gall ink looked pale grey-brown and only darkened to true black-brown over subsequent days, a detail scribes had to work around. This ink was prized for permanence (genuinely long-lasting, still legible on medieval manuscripts today) but had a real downside: it's mildly acidic and, over centuries, can actually eat through the parchment or paper it's written on — a slow-motion self-destruction built into the material itself.

**Other inks and materials** — **Carbon/lampblack ink** (soot mixed with gum and water, the older and simpler tradition inherited from antiquity) remained in use, especially for less permanent or lower-stakes writing, since it didn't have iron gall's corrosive long-term bite, though it smudged more easily and didn't bond to parchment as durably. **Colored inks and pigments** for illumination were a related but distinct specialty — illuminators needed reds, blues, greens, and gold, sourced from minerals (like lapis lazuli, imported at extraordinary expense for the deepest blues), plant dyes, and occasionally genuinely exotic materials, making pigment preparation its own skilled sub-craft often practiced by the illuminator personally rather than outsourced.

**Who made it** — Because recipes were workshop/monastery knowledge and ingredients (galls, vitriol, gum arabic) required some sourcing effort but not extraordinary expense, ink was frequently **made in-house** by the scriptorium or scribe who'd use it, rather than purchased ready-made — meaning an "ink maker" in the fullest sense might be a specific monk assigned the task within a monastery, or a stationer's employee preparing batches for sale to scribes and students in a university town. As secular book trade grew, dedicated small-scale commercial ink sellers did appear, particularly clustered around university quarters where demand was concentrated and constant.

**Economic and practical texture** — Ink quality mattered enormously and was noticeably variable — bad ink could be too thin (skipping and blotting), too thick (clogging the pen), too pale, or apt to flake off the page over time, and a scribe's finished work depended entirely on starting with good material. This gives you a craft that's simultaneously **humble and utterly foundational** — nobody remembers the ink maker's name, but every single surviving medieval manuscript exists because someone got the gall-to-vitriol-to-gum ratio right.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Arcana or Nature (ingredient sourcing/preparation), Investigation
- **Tool Proficiency:** Alchemist's Supplies (a very natural fit — ink making is genuinely proto-chemistry) or Calligrapher's Supplies
- **Feat:** Skilled or Alchemist (if using a feat that grants minor alchemical utility)
- **Equipment:** A set of ink-making materials (dried oak galls, vitriol, gum arabic), several prepared ink vials, a recipe book/notes, scholar's or monastic-adjacent clothing, 8 gp

This background has an unusually strong crossover with **Wizards** (ink is quite literally the medium spellbooks are written in — a wizard with an ink-maker background has a wonderfully grounded, practical reason for detailed knowledge of magical writing materials) and **Artificers** (proto-chemist framing fits naturally). It's also a good, understated background for a **Cleric or Druid** tied to a monastery's scriptorium, or a **Rogue** who's learned to make convincing forged ink that ages/fades to match old documents.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Scriptorium's Ink Brother** — A monk whose entire assigned role for decades has been preparing ink for the monastery's scribes — a quiet, exacting figure who knows the recipe's every variable and treats it as close to sacred duty.
- **The University Ink Seller** — Runs a small shop or stall near a university, supplying students and scribes, and by extension hears everything being copied, studied, and debated in town.
- **The Pigment Importer** — Deals in expensive illumination materials (lapis for blue, imported gums, rare minerals) — wealthy, well-traveled contacts, and a target for theft given how valuable a small pouch of true ultramarine pigment actually was.
- **The Forger's Supplier** — Quietly sells (or is coerced into selling) specially aged or historically-accurate ink recipes to someone forging old documents, wills, or manuscripts — may not know or may willfully not ask what it's being used for.
- **The Ink That Won't Fade** — An ink maker obsessed with perfecting a formula, whose experiments occasionally produce something stranger than expected — unusually vivid color, unnatural permanence, or (if you want a magical hook) ink that seems to resist being altered or erased even by magic.
### Game Hooks
- **The Corroding Archive** — A monastery or library's oldest and most important texts are being slowly destroyed from within — not sabotage, but the acidic bite of old iron gall ink eating through parchment over centuries — a race to copy/preserve texts before they're lost, with an ink maker/binder team as key NPCs.
- **The Forged Document** — A will, treaty, or charter is suspected of being forged, and the party needs an ink expert to help determine its age and authenticity — a nice, grounded "expert testimony" investigation beat.
- **The Stolen Pigment** — A shipment of rare, extremely expensive illumination pigment (imported lapis, etc.) is stolen en route to a monastery or workshop — a heist/recovery hook with real, tangible stakes for a specific, visible commission.
- **The Ink That Remembers** — A magical or unusual ink (rediscovered old recipe, tampered-with formula, cursed batch) causes texts written with it to behave strangely — words shifting, refusing to be read by the "wrong" person, or slowly revealing something the writer didn't consciously intend.
- **Tracing the Recipe** — A distinctive, rare ink found on a document (a threat letter, a forged deed, a smuggler's ledger) can be traced back through its unusual ingredient sourcing to a specific maker or workshop — same "trace the craftsmanship" structure used for the saddler and glover hooks, filtered through chemistry instead of stitching.
