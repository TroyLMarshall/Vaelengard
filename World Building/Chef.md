### A Trade With Sharp Class Divides
Unlike many crafts where the same basic trade serves rich and poor alike (just with quality differences), cooking in medieval Europe split into genuinely distinct worlds depending on who you cooked for — a noble household's kitchen and a peasant family's hearth operated on completely different scales of skill, equipment, and ingredients.
### Where Cooks Worked
**Great households and royal courts**: this is where "chef" in any elevated, specialized sense really applied
- Large noble and royal kitchens employed substantial staff with real internal hierarchy
- **The master cook** oversaw the entire kitchen operation, planned menus for feasts, and held a position of genuine status and trust within the household — sometimes a notably well-paid, respected role, since poisoning fears and the importance of impressive feasts for political display made a trustworthy, skilled master cook valuable
- Beneath the master cook: various specialized roles — **roasters** (managing open-fire roasting, a demanding and skilled job given the lack of precise temperature control), **saucers** (preparing sauces, a genuinely refined skill area), **pastry cooks**, **larderers** (managing food storage and preservation), and numerous kitchen boys/scullions doing menial prep and cleaning work

**Monasteries**: maintained their own kitchens, sometimes quite sophisticated given monastic self-sufficiency traditions, though subject to the religious calendar's fasting restrictions (covered earlier) which shaped what could be prepared and when

**Urban cookshops**: city dwellers without their own adequate kitchen facilities (common in dense urban housing) could buy prepared food from **cookshops** — an early form of takeout/prepared food vendor, selling roasted meats, pies, and other ready-to-eat dishes. This was genuinely significant in cities like London, where many residents lacked space or fuel for extensive home cooking.

**Peasant and common households**: cooking here was overwhelmingly the unpaid domestic responsibility of women within the family, using simple equipment (a single hearth, basic pots) and limited ingredients — not a specialized trade in the way court cookery was, but absolutely essential labor.
### Court and Noble Feast Cookery
This is where medieval cooking reached its most elaborate and skilled expression:
- **Spices as luxury and status display**: imported spices (pepper, cinnamon, cloves, nutmeg, ginger, saffron) were extraordinarily expensive, having traveled long trade routes, and lavish spicing was a deliberate display of wealth — medieval elite cuisine tended toward heavily spiced, sweet-and-savory combinations that taste quite different from modern Western culinary expectations
- **Elaborate presentation**: feast cookery emphasized visual spectacle as much as flavor — **subtleties** (elaborate sculpted sugar or pastry centerpieces, sometimes depicting castles, animals, or heraldic scenes) and dramatically presented dishes (a roasted peacock redressed in its own feathers for presentation, for instance) served a real social/political display function beyond nutrition
- **Sauces**: medieval sauces were often thickened with bread, ground almonds, or egg, and flavored with the expensive spice combinations mentioned above, plus verjuice (sour unripe grape juice) or vinegar for acidity
- **Multiple courses**: elaborate feasts involved numerous courses and dishes, reflecting the host's wealth and generosity — a key aspect of medieval hospitality politics, where lavish feeding of guests and dependents reinforced social bonds and obligations
- **Cookbooks**: surviving texts like _The Forme of Cury_ (England, associated with Richard II's court) and various continental equivalents preserve genuine recipes, showing real recorded culinary knowledge rather than pure oral tradition by the later medieval period
### Equipment and Cooking Methods
- **Open hearth cooking**: the dominant method — large fireplaces with hanging pots (using adjustable hooks/cranes), spits for roasting (sometimes turned by a dedicated spit-turning boy or, in grander households, mechanical devices), and various positions near the fire offering different heat intensities
- **Cauldrons and pots**: typically iron or sometimes bronze/copper, used for boiling, stewing, and pottage-making
- **Bread ovens**: as covered with bakers, sometimes integrated into larger kitchen complexes for substantial households baking their own bread rather than relying on a town baker
- **Mortars and pestles**: for grinding spices and preparing thickened sauce bases
- **Spits and roasting equipment**: for the showcase roasted meats central to noble feasting
### Common Food and Cooking
For the vast majority of the population, daily cooking was far simpler:
- **Pottage**: the staple dish for most people — a thick soup/stew of grains, vegetables (whatever was available seasonally — onions, leeks, cabbage, beans), and occasionally small amounts of meat or fat for flavor, cooked slowly over the hearth fire, often kept going for extended periods with ingredients added as available
- **Bread**: as covered extensively with bakers, the other dietary cornerstone
- **Limited meat**: meat was a luxury for much of the population most of the year, supplemented by what could be raised (a pig was a common peasant household investment, since pigs could forage and didn't compete directly with humans for grain)
- **Dairy**: cheese and butter where livestock allowed
- **Seasonal and preserved food**: salting, smoking, and pickling were essential preservation techniques given the lack of refrigeration, especially crucial for surviving winter months
### Status of the Cooking Trade
- A master cook in a great household held genuine status and could be a trusted, even politically relevant figure (poisoning concerns alone made trustworthiness paramount)
- Court/noble cooking didn't typically organize into guild structures the way urban craft trades did, since most skilled cooks were household employees rather than independent tradesmen selling to the public
- **Cookshop operators** in cities, by contrast, did sometimes organize into more recognizable trade/guild structures, given they operated as independent businesses selling directly to customers
- Ordinary domestic cooking (overwhelmingly women's unpaid labor) carried no formal trade status at all, despite being universally necessary — a clear illustration of how "trade" and "skilled labor" weren't always treated equivalently when performed within the unpaid domestic sphere versus the paid public economy
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A master cook at a noble or royal court who's secretly an alchemist or has genuine magical skill — feast dishes that grant temporary buffs, or a cook who can detect poison through unusual sensory means, tying directly into court intrigue plotlines
- Elaborate feast "subtleties" reimagined with illusion magic — sculpted centerpieces that move, speak, or transform, turning a real historical practice into a natural magic showcase for a wealthy NPC patron
- A cookshop as a recurring urban location for PCs — affordable, flavorful, and a good place to overhear gossip or meet contacts, distinct from the tavern's drinking-focused social function
- Spice trade routes and their control as a worldbuilding/economic detail — a kingdom's wealth or a merchant house's power tied specifically to controlling access to magically-significant or simply rare spices, echoing the real historical importance of the spice trade
