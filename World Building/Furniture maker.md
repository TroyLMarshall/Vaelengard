### A Specialization Within Carpentry, Now Given Its Full Due
We flagged the **joiner** briefly in the [[carpenter]] entry as the specialization focused on finer woodworking — furniture, doors, paneling — distinct from the rougher structural framing work of house carpentry. Let's give furniture-making the dedicated treatment it deserves, since the craft, the social context around furniture ownership, and the actual pieces produced are genuinely interesting in their own right.
### Joiner vs. Carpenter — Why the Distinction Mattered
- **Carpenters**: handled structural, load-bearing timber work — house frames, roof structures, larger and rougher construction
- **Joiners**: specialized in precise joinery techniques for finer work — furniture, paneling, doors, and decorative woodwork requiring tighter tolerances and more refined finishing than structural framing demanded
- This distinction was genuine enough in larger towns to support **separate guilds** — joiners' guilds existed distinctly from carpenters' guilds in cities with sufficient specialized demand, reflecting real differences in required skill, tools, and clientele
### Furniture Was Genuinely Scarce by Modern Standards
This is worth establishing clearly: medieval households, especially below the wealthy elite, owned **far less furniture** than we'd consider normal today. Most peasant households had minimal furnishing — perhaps a table, a few stools or benches, a chest, and basic sleeping arrangements (which might be simple platforms, straw-filled mattresses, or even just bedding on the floor for poorer households) rather than the elaborate bedsteads we associate with "medieval" aesthetics from popular media. Furniture ownership scaled dramatically with wealth.
### What Joiners Made
**Chests**: probably the single most important and universal furniture piece across all social classes
- Served as storage (clothing, valuables, documents) **and** seating **and** sometimes as a travel container, given how often medieval households and nobility actually moved between residences
- Ranged from simple plank construction for ordinary households to elaborately carved, sometimes painted or iron-bound chests for wealthy clients
- **Marriage chests/dowry chests**: in some traditions, a specially made, often decorated chest specifically associated with a woman's dowry goods — a recognized, meaningful furniture category beyond pure utility

**Tables**:
- Often simple trestle构造 (trestle tables — a flat board top resting on separate trestle supports) that could be **disassembled when not in use**, reflecting how multi-purpose medieval rooms often were (a great hall might need to clear for dancing, sleeping, or other uses beyond dining)
- More substantial, permanently joined tables appeared in wealthier or more specialized contexts

**Benches and stools**: the dominant seating for most of the population — chairs specifically were a genuine luxury and status item for much of the medieval period (the phrase "chairman" reflecting a literal chair as a position of authority/honor has roots in exactly this scarcity)

**Chairs**:
- Reserved largely for the lord, host, or most honored person present — sitting in "the chair" (as opposed to a bench or stool) carried real status significance
- Wealthy or ecclesiastical chairs could be elaborately carved and decorated, serving as much as a symbol of authority as a functional seat

**Beds/bedsteads**:
- Wealthy households had constructed wooden bed frames, sometimes substantial and elaborately carved, with curtains/hangings for warmth and privacy
- Poorer households slept on simpler arrangements as mentioned above

**Cupboards and aumbries**: storage furniture, particularly for valuable or ceremonially significant items (church aumbries stored sacred vessels, for instance)

**Church furniture**: choir stalls, pews (less universal early on, becoming more common later in the medieval period), screens, and other ecclesiastical woodwork represented some of the most skilled, elaborate joinery work, given the Church's wealth and the prestige attached to such commissions
### Construction Techniques
- **Joinery over nails**: as with structural carpentry, joiners relied on precise wood-to-wood joints (mortise-and-tenon, dovetails, and similar techniques) rather than heavy reliance on nails, since well-fitted joints provided greater strength and durability, and metal fasteners remained relatively costly
- **Plank construction**: simpler furniture (basic chests, benches) might use straightforward plank-and-peg construction, less refined than true fine joinery but adequate for everyday use
- **Carving and decoration**: wealthier commissions featured carved decorative elements — linenfold paneling (a carved pattern resembling folded cloth, genuinely distinctive to late medieval/early modern furniture aesthetics) became a recognizable decorative style
- **Turned elements**: chair and table legs sometimes used turned (lathe-worked) elements, connecting to the turner specialization mentioned in the bone carver entry
### Materials
- **Oak**: the dominant furniture wood across much of medieval Europe, valued for strength and durability — genuinely heavy, solid furniture pieces, very different in character from lighter woods favored in later furniture traditions
- **Other woods**: depending on region and specific application, other timbers served various furniture needs, though oak's dominance in higher-quality work was significant
- **Iron fittings**: hinges, locks, and decorative iron-work (connecting to the blacksmith/locksmith trades) finished and secured many furniture pieces, particularly chests needing secure locking mechanisms for valuable contents
### Guild Structure and Status
- Joiners organized into guilds following the standard pattern in towns with sufficient specialized demand
- Master joiners capable of fine ecclesiastical or noble commission work held real status and could command good compensation, similar to other highly skilled craft specializations
- The trade's status sat comfortably within the broader respected craft hierarchy — skilled, necessary, but not carrying quite the same universal-necessity status as blacksmithing or baking, given that fine furniture specifically was more of a luxury/wealth-correlated demand than a universal need
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A master joiner crafting an elaborately carved chest or chair specifically commissioned for a noble house, with hidden compartments, a magical lock, or symbolic carved imagery relevant to your plot — furniture as a natural vessel for hidden objects or messages
- The chest's dual function as both storage and seating/travel container as a worldbuilding detail useful for adventuring logistics — PCs' own travel chest, or a notable NPC's distinctively decorated dowry chest as a recognizable plot object
- The real scarcity and status-significance of chairs specifically as a small but evocative detail — an NPC offering a PC "the chair" as a genuine, period-appropriate honor, or a power dynamic visible simply through who sits where in a scene
- A joiner's guild commission for a temple or guildhall's elaborate woodwork (choir stalls, decorative paneling) as a parallel to the construction company entry's "great work" concept, but scaled to interior craftsmanship rather than full building construction
