### A Trade We Flagged in the Coppersmith Entry
Back in the coppersmith entry, we specifically noted tinsmiths as worth dedicated treatment — briefly describing them as workers in tin and tin-plated iron producing lighter, cheaper versions of some of the same products coppersmiths made, accessible to a broader economic range than copper and brass. Let's give this trade its full dedicated treatment now.
### Tin and Its Properties — The Material Basis
Understanding tinsmithing requires understanding what tin actually offered as a working material:

**Pure tin**: a relatively soft, silvery-white metal with a low melting point — these properties making it easy to work but limiting its structural applications. Pure tin's most immediately notable property is its **bright, attractive silver appearance** — making it visually appealing for tableware and decorative applications where appearance mattered alongside function.

**Tin-plated iron (tinplate)**: the more practically significant material for much medieval tinsmith work — thin iron sheet with a coating of tin applied to its surface
- The tin coating provided **corrosion resistance** that uncoated iron lacked — iron rusting readily when exposed to food, moisture, and air, while the tin coating protected the iron substrate
- The iron substrate provided **structural strength** that pure tin couldn't match
- The combination produced a material that was durable, relatively lightweight, rust-resistant, and visually attractive — a genuinely useful combination for a wide range of applications

**Pewter's relationship to tin**: as covered in the pewterer entry, pewter was a tin-based alloy — the pewterer and tinsmith trades overlapping at their edges, with the distinction lying in technique (the pewterer primarily casting, the tinsmith primarily working sheet metal) and specific product range
### The Tinsmith's Distinctive Techniques
Unlike the coppersmith's raising technique or the blacksmith's forge work, tinsmithing worked with **sheet metal** using relatively distinctive methods:

**Cutting**: sheet tin or tinplate cut to pattern using shears — similar in concept to the tailor's cloth-cutting but applied to thin metal sheet rather than fabric

**Forming and shaping**: bending, folding, and shaping cut metal sheet
- **Stakes and forms**: working sheet metal over shaped metal stakes or wooden forms to achieve curved or angled shapes
- **Seaming**: folding edges together and crimping to create joints — the characteristic **folded seam** of tinsmith work, joining sheet metal edges without soldering where a mechanical joint sufficed
- **Raising**: limited raising technique for deeper curved forms, though less central to tinsmithing than to coppersmith work given tin's different properties

**Soldering**: joining tin components using lower-melting-point solder (itself tin-based, or tin-lead alloy) — essential for watertight joins and for attaching handles, spouts, and other components
- Tinsmithing's relatively low working temperatures (both for the tin/tinplate itself and for soldering) meant tinsmith work didn't require a large forge — a relatively modest workshop setup compared to heavier metalworking trades

**Planishing and finishing**: smoothing hammered surfaces for appearance
### What Tinsmiths Made
**Household kitchenware and tableware**:
- **Plates and dishes**: tin-plated iron plates providing an affordable alternative to the pewter plates covered in that entry, extending reasonable tableware to households that couldn't afford pewter
- **Cups and mugs**: drinking vessels in tin
- **Cooking utensils**: ladles, strainers, graters, and various kitchen implements where tin's rust-resistance made it preferable to iron
- **Candle holders and lanterns**: connecting to the candlemaker entry — tin lanterns (with pierced tin sides allowing light through while protecting the flame) were a significant and widespread product, the pierced-tin lantern being a recognizable form of modest everyday lighting equipment

**Storage containers**:
- **Boxes and canisters**: tin's rust-resistance making it useful for storing food, medicines (connecting to the apothecary entry), and other materials that would corrode unprotected iron
- **Flour and grain containers**: protecting stored food from moisture and vermin

**Agricultural and trade measures**:
- As covered in the marketplace entry, standardized measures were essential for honest trade — tin measures (cups, pots, and similar standard vessels) served this function at the modest end of the market, alongside the more expensive copper measures covered in the coppersmith entry

**Decorative items**:
- **Mirrors**: highly polished tin provided a reflective surface — an accessible alternative to the more expensive glass mirrors discussed in the glassblower entry, giving ordinary households access to some reflective surface without the expense of silvered glass
- **Decorative boxes and containers**: pierced tin work (creating patterns by piercing holes in tin sheet) allowed decorative effect alongside functional container production

**Church and religious items**:
- More modest tin versions of liturgical objects for smaller, less wealthy churches that couldn't afford the copper, brass, or silver versions covered in the coppersmith and goldsmith entries
### Itinerant Tinkers — The Mobile End of the Trade
This is perhaps the most significant aspect of tinsmithing's social character:

**The tinker**: the itinerant tinsmith traveling between settlements to repair and sometimes make tin goods was a genuinely recognized, widespread figure — the **tinker** (from "tinkering," the sound of small metalwork) becoming a recognized social type quite distinct from the settled urban tinsmith

**Repair work**: tin and tinplate goods could be repaired when damaged — holes soldered, joints re-sealed, and worn items restored to function — making repair work a significant part of the traveling tinker's trade alongside new production

**Rural service**: connecting to the peddler entry's discussion of itinerant traders serving rural communities without easy market access, the traveling tinker provided essential metalwork repair services to villages and farms between market towns — fixing cooking pots, replacing lantern parts, and making small items on the spot

**Social position**: like peddlers and traveling entertainers covered in those entries, tinkers occupied a somewhat socially marginal position — useful and genuinely needed, but without fixed residence and therefore subject to the same suspicion directed at the vagrant population covered in that entry. "Tinker" became a somewhat pejorative term in some contexts, though this was a social prejudice against itinerant workers generally rather than specific to the trade's actual character.
### Guild Structure
- **Settled urban tinsmiths**: in towns with sufficient demand, organized into guilds following standard patterns — though the trade's relatively modest product range and lower material value compared to copper or silver work meant tinsmith guilds were generally less prestigious than goldsmith or even coppersmith guilds
- **Itinerant tinkers**: by definition outside guild structure — operating beyond guild towns' jurisdictional reach, without fixed premises, and serving markets too dispersed for guild organization to function
- **Overlap with pewterers**: as covered in the pewterer entry, the boundary between tinsmith and pewterer work was sometimes genuinely blurry — both working tin-based materials, with the distinction lying primarily in technique (casting versus sheet work) and specific product range
### Tin Supply — A Strategic Material
Connecting to the broader raw material supply discussions throughout this list:

**Cornwall as tin source**: England's southwest (Cornwall and Devon particularly) was the dominant European tin-producing region throughout much of the medieval period — **Cornish tin** being a significant trade commodity exported across Europe and forming an important part of the regional economy

- The **Stannaries**: specific legal and administrative institutions governing Cornish tin mining and trade — tin miners in Cornwall having specific legal rights and privileges under stannary law, reflecting tin's economic significance
- Connecting to the stoneworker entry's discussion of quarrying, tin mining involved genuine extraction industry with its own labor force and technical requirements

**Long-distance tin trade**: since tin deposits weren't universally distributed, the tin trade connected producing regions (Cornwall, parts of central Europe) to consuming centers — a component of the broader medieval commodity trade discussed throughout this list
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A traveling tinker as a specific, useful NPC type — someone who moves between settlements, knows the roads and communities across a wide area, carries genuinely useful repair skills, and occupies a socially marginal position that gives them access to information and communities that more "respectable" figures might not encounter. Connecting to the peddler entry's similar information-network NPC suggestion, a tinker combines practical craft utility with the geographic range and social invisibility of the itinerant
- Pierced tin lanterns as a specific, atmospheric lighting detail — the characteristic pattern of light and shadow produced by a pierced tin lantern creating a distinctive visual environment in a low-light scene, more evocative than a generic "torch" or "candle"
- A strategic tin supply as a worldbuilding economic detail — a region's tin mining operation being a significant enough economic resource to attract political competition, military protection, or organized criminal interest, echoing the real historical importance of Cornish tin and the specific legal institutions it generated
- Tin mirrors as a social detail — a household's reflective surface being a polished tin sheet rather than glass immediately communicating modest but not destitute circumstances, the visual difference between tin's slightly warm, imperfect reflection and glass mirror's crisper image itself a tangible marker of economic position
