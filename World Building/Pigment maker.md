### The Craft and Its Structure
A **pigment maker** produced the colored powders and compounds that illuminators, painters, dyers, and manuscript workshops needed to create color — a trade that ranged from simple, cheap, locally-sourced earth pigments to some of the **most expensive materials in the entire medieval economy**, rivaling gold and gemstones in cost per ounce for the rarest colors. Unlike most crafts covered so far, pigment making sat at a genuine crossroads of **chemistry, mining/mineral sourcing, long-distance luxury trade, and fine art** — connecting a monastery's illuminator directly to trade networks stretching as far as Afghanistan.

**Sourcing and preparation** — Pigments came from three broad categories: **minerals** (ground and processed rock or ore — ochre and umber for yellows/browns, malachite for green, cinnabar/vermilion for red, and critically, **lapis lazuli** for the finest, most saturated blue), **organic/plant and animal sources** (woad and later indigo for blue dyes and some pigments, madder root for red, kermes and later cochineal insects for a brilliant crimson, saffron for yellow — genuinely worth its weight in gold in some periods), and **manufactured/chemical compounds** (white lead, produced by a real and genuinely toxic process of exposing lead to vinegar fumes; verdigris, made by corroding copper with acid; various compounds requiring real proto-chemical skill to produce safely and consistently, or not so safely, since lead and mercury-based pigments were used with essentially no understanding of their toxicity). Preparing a raw material into usable pigment required **grinding** it to an extremely fine, consistent powder (a laborious, skilled task — coarse pigment produced grainy, poor color when mixed with a binder), and then combining it with a **binder** (egg-based tempera, gum arabic, or later oils) to actually make it usable for painting or illumination.

**Ultramarine and the lapis trade** — This is the single best worldbuilding detail in the whole trade. True **ultramarine blue** was made by grinding imported **lapis lazuli**, a semi-precious stone mined at the time almost exclusively in a single region of what's now **Afghanistan**, then shipped an enormous distance across multiple trade networks (hence the name "ultramarine" — literally "beyond the sea") to reach European workshops. The processing itself was genuinely laborious and skilled — crude grinding produced a dull, grayish color, while the finest, most vivid ultramarine required a sophisticated purification process to extract just the blue mineral (lazurite) from the surrounding rock. The result was almost unbelievably expensive — genuinely comparable to gold by weight in some periods — meaning **ultramarine blue in a medieval painting or illuminated manuscript was a direct, visible statement of wealth and importance**: patrons specifically commissioned and paid extra for ultramarine in devotional paintings, often reserved specifically for the Virgin Mary's robes as the most precious color available for the most venerated figure. A cheaper substitute, "German blue" or azurite (a more common copper-based mineral), gave a similar but less vivid, less prestigious blue for patrons who couldn't afford the real thing.

**Who made pigments** — Similar to ink, pigment preparation was often done **in-house** by the artist or workshop that would use it — an illuminator or painter, or an apprentice under them, might personally grind and prepare their own colors as an integral, non-outsourced part of the craft, especially for common/cheap pigments. But the rarest, most technically demanding, or most exotic materials (true ultramarine especially) often ran through **dedicated specialist pigment merchants and preparers**, particularly in major art/manuscript centers, who dealt in importing, processing, and selling prepared pigments to workshops that didn't have the specialized skill or access to source materials themselves — much like the currier standing between tanner and saddler, or the ink seller standing between raw material and scribe.

**Toxicity and real danger** — A genuinely dark, underexplored angle: several common medieval pigments were **seriously toxic** — white lead causes lead poisoning with prolonged exposure, vermilion/cinnabar contains mercury, orpiment (a yellow pigment) is arsenic-based — and pigment workers, handling and grinding these materials constantly with no protective equipment and no understanding of the danger, likely suffered real, unrecognized occupational health consequences from their trade, a grim historical reality with real dramatic potential.

**Guild and economic position** — Where formally organized, pigment preparation and sale often fell under or alongside apothecary guilds (since the skills — grinding, compounding, understanding material properties — genuinely overlapped with medicine-making) or existed informally within the illuminator/painter trade itself. Dealing in the rarest pigments (true ultramarine especially) could make a specialist merchant genuinely wealthy, given the extraordinary markup on materials sourced from thousands of miles away.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Arcana or Nature, Investigation
- **Tool Proficiency:** Alchemist's Supplies, or Painter's Supplies if your setting has that tool category
- **Feat:** Skilled or Alchemist
- **Equipment:** A set of grinding tools (mortar and pestle, fine sieves), small pouches of prepared pigments including one precious color, a merchant's sample case, 15 gp

This background is an outstanding fit for a **Wizard or Artificer** (genuine proto-chemistry, exotic material sourcing, real technical precision — and pigment-as-spell-component flavor writes itself), and works beautifully for a **Rogue** specializing in acquiring or smuggling rare, exotic, extremely valuable small goods (a pouch of true ultramarine is a fortune in a small, easily concealed package). It also suits a **Cleric or Bard** tied to illumination/manuscript work, or a character whose family trade poisoned them slowly without their understanding why, if you want to lean into the toxicity angle for a personal, tragic backstory beat.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Ultramarine Merchant** — Deals exclusively in the rarest, most expensive pigment in the known world, wealthy, well-traveled, deeply connected to distant trade networks most people in town have never heard of — a great "window to a wider world" NPC.
- **The Poisoned Grinder** — A pigment worker slowly, unknowingly dying from years of handling lead- or mercury-based pigments, uncertain why they're ill, a genuinely tragic figure who doesn't know their own trade is killing them.
- **The Illuminator's Secret Recipe** — A workshop master with a personally perfected pigment-preparation technique (better purification, a cheaper convincing substitute, a genuinely new color) fiercely guards it from rivals.
- **The Substitute Peddler** — Sells cheaper substitute pigments (azurite instead of true ultramarine, for instance) as the genuine article to patrons who can't tell the difference — a fraud hook echoing the pewterer/currier adulteration pattern, but in the art world.
- **The Exotic Trader** — A merchant personally involved in the actual lapis-lazuli supply chain, having traveled remarkably far to source it — a rich contact for adventure hooks involving distant lands, dangerous trade routes, or exotic material components.
### Game Hooks
- **The Stolen Ultramarine** — A commissioned patron's extraordinarily expensive pigment shipment (or finished painting made with it) is stolen — genuine high-value heist stakes over something that isn't gold or gems but is worth just as much.
- **The Poisoning Mystery** — Multiple pigment workers or an illuminator's whole workshop are falling ill, and the party has to figure out it's the materials themselves (lead, mercury, arsenic) rather than a curse, plague, or poison plot — a "the real villain is occupational toxicity" mystery with a grounded, tragic reveal.
- **The Substitute Fraud** — A patron discovers (or suspects) they paid for true ultramarine and got a cheaper substitute — a fraud investigation with real reputational and financial stakes for a workshop or merchant.
- **Sourcing the Impossible Color** — A commission requires a pigment from a source the party has to travel to secure — tying a "gather rare material" quest directly to a specific, exotic trade good, similar to the smith's masterpiece-quest hook but for art rather than weaponry.
- **The Precious Blue Robe** — A painting or illuminated manuscript made with true ultramarine becomes a target, a diplomatic gift, or evidence in a dispute purely because of how much wealth is visibly, undeniably represented in that one color.
