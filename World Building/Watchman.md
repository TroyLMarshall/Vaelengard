### A Role We've Touched on Repeatedly — Now Given Full Treatment
We've referenced the night watch throughout this list — the constable entry covered the frankpledge system and civic watch obligations, the guard entry distinguished castle garrison guards from town watchmen, the town hall entry mentioned civic bell-ringing functions, and the tavern entry noted watch-imposed curfews. Let's bring the watchman specifically together as his own dedicated entry, since this was one of the most universally present (if often reluctant) civic roles in medieval urban life.
### The Fundamental Problem the Watch Solved
Medieval towns faced two persistent, serious threats that required ongoing nighttime vigilance:

**Fire**: as covered repeatedly throughout this list (baker, candlemaker, thatcher, warehouse entries particularly), fire was the constant, existential threat to medieval wooden towns — a single uncontrolled fire could devastate an entire district in hours. Early detection and alarm-raising was genuinely the difference between a manageable fire and catastrophic destruction.

**Disorder and crime**: as covered in the constable, guard, thieves' guild, and vagrant entries, nighttime was when criminal activity concentrated — darkness providing cover, reduced visibility limiting identification, and the absence of normal daytime community observation creating opportunity. A visible watch presence served both detection and deterrence functions.
### Who Actually Watched
As covered in the constable entry, the medieval watch operated on several different models depending on location and period:

**Rotated civic obligation**: in many towns, particularly earlier in the period, watch duty was simply a **civic obligation rotated among eligible householders** — similar to jury duty in modern terms, a responsibility that came with being a settled, established community member rather than a paid professional position
- This produced **reluctant, often incompetent watchmen** whose primary concern was completing their obligation without incident rather than actively seeking out crime or disorder
- Various period sources complain about watchmen sleeping on duty, abandoning their posts early, or simply not appearing at all — the inevitable consequence of using unwilling conscripts for a demanding nighttime role

**Paid civic watchmen**: as towns grew and the limitations of purely rotated obligation became clear, many larger towns developed paid watch positions — still relatively modest pay, but providing somewhat more consistent coverage than pure obligation-rotation
- Connecting to the government official entry's discussion of civic administration developing more professional capacity through the later medieval period

**Ward system**: many towns divided into **wards** (administrative districts) with watch obligations organized at ward level — connecting local responsibility to specific geographic areas and creating somewhat more manageable oversight
### Equipment and Appearance
A watchman's typical equipment was genuinely modest:

**Lantern**: essential for nighttime patrol — both providing personal visibility and enabling the watchman to examine dark spaces. The horn lantern covered in the bone carver entry (thin sheets of horn as semi-transparent windows) would have been common, with wealthier civic watches potentially affording glass-paned lanterns.

**Staff or bill**: a simple pole weapon serving both as walking aid on dark, uneven streets and as potential defensive/offensive tool if confrontation occurred — not the sophisticated armament of a garrison soldier but something more than nothing

**Horn or rattle**: for raising alarm — a watchman discovering a fire or serious crime needed to alert the rest of the community rapidly, and noise-making instruments served this function. The **watchman's rattle** (a hinged clapper producing a loud rattling sound when swung) became particularly associated with this role.

**Warm clothing**: patrolling outdoors through the night in all weather conditions required appropriate clothing — an overlooked but genuine practical consideration for someone expected to remain alert through cold, wet medieval nights
### What Watchmen Actually Did
**Patrol routes**: moving through assigned streets on regular patterns — the predictability of patrol routes being both a feature (potential criminals knew they'd be checked) and a vulnerability (predictable routes easier to work around)

**Calling the hours**: as covered in the town pump and church entries, medieval timekeeping depended on public announcement — watchmen calling out the time through the night both provided a public timekeeping function and served as an audible demonstration that the watch was present and awake

**Gate management**: as covered in the guard entry, town gates were locked at night — the watch including responsibility for managing who was admitted after gate-closing, checking credentials, and maintaining this basic access control

**Fire watching**: specific attention to signs of fire — smoke, unusual light, or the smell of burning — with rapid alarm-raising being the most immediately valuable service a watchman could provide

**Dealing with disorder**: managing drunkenness, fighting, and disturbance — the tavern entry's discussion of curfew regulations providing the watchman with authority to clear people from public spaces after specified hours, reducing nighttime disorder

**Challenging strangers**: stopping and questioning those moving through the town at unusual hours — the presumption of suspicious intent for nighttime movement being the basis for this challenge function
### The Watchman's Legal Authority
Considerably more limited than modern police:

**Detention not punishment**: watchmen could detain suspected wrongdoers until morning when they could be brought before the appropriate court — they couldn't themselves punish, fine, or sentence  

**Hue and cry authority**: could raise the legal obligation for others to assist in pursuit  
**Curfew enforcement**: authority to clear public spaces after curfew, backed by civic ordinance
### Corruption and Ineffectiveness
Period sources document consistent problems:

**Sleeping on duty**: perhaps the most universal complaint — night watchmen found sleeping rather than patrolling, sometimes in dedicated shelters called **watch houses** or **watch boxes** that were theoretically patrol bases but often became sleeping spots

**Bribery**: as with other enforcement functions covered throughout this list, watchmen could be paid to ignore specific activities — the smuggler entry's discussion of corrupt officials applying equally to the watch, who might accept payment to overlook a particular tavern staying open past curfew or a particular door through which contraband moved

**Incompetence during actual emergencies**: the rotated-obligation model's fundamental problem — when something genuinely serious happened, the person on watch might have no relevant training, experience, or inclination to handle it effectively
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A watchman's rattle as a specific sound cue in your setting — the sudden sound of a rattle in the night immediately communicating alarm, fire, or discovered crime to everyone within earshot, a distinctive audio signal with immediate community meaning
- A corrupt watch captain whose protection racket shapes the criminal landscape of a city district — not just individual corrupt watchmen but organized corruption where specific operations pay for immunity, creating a specific economic relationship between organized crime and official watch
- A reformed watchman as a PC background — someone who took their civic watch obligation seriously when others didn't, developed genuine investigative instincts from nights of patrol, and eventually moved toward more active pursuit of justice than the watch role officially provided
- The watch's genuine limitations as a realistic game mechanic — in your setting, "calling the watch" produces a response measured in minutes of searching rather than immediate appearance, the watchmen who arrive are reluctant civilians rather than trained fighters, and what they can actually do is limited to detaining and reporting rather than directly resolving serious situations
