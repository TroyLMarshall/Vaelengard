### A Foundational, Universally Needed Trade
Carpentry was every bit as essential as blacksmithing to medieval construction and daily life, given that wood was the primary building material for most structures, furniture, tools, and countless everyday objects across medieval Europe. Stone was reserved largely for churches, castles, and the wealthiest buildings — the vast majority of medieval construction, even in towns, was timber-framed.
### Types of Carpentry Specialization
As with several trades already covered, "carpenter" wasn't always one undifferentiated job — larger towns saw real specialization:
- **House carpenters/framers**: built the timber frameworks for houses and buildings — the structural skeleton that defined medieval timber-frame construction
- **Joiners**: a more refined specialization focused on finer woodworking — furniture, doors, paneling, screens — using more precise joining techniques than rough structural framing required
- **Wheelwrights**: specialized specifically in making wheels for carts and wagons, a genuinely demanding sub-craft requiring precise curved woodworking and metal tire-fitting
- **Shipwrights**: built ships and boats, a major specialization in coastal and river trade centers, requiring distinct skills for hull construction, curved timber shaping, and waterproofing
- **Cartwrights**: built the cart/wagon bodies themselves (sometimes overlapping with or distinct from wheelwrights)
- **Turners**: used a lathe to produce rounded wooden objects — bowls, cups, furniture legs, spindles
### Timber-Frame Construction
This is the dominant medieval building method worth understanding in some detail:
- **The frame as structural skeleton**: heavy wooden beams (posts, beams, braces) formed the actual load-bearing structure of a building, typically using oak for its strength and durability
- **Joinery over nails**: medieval carpenters relied heavily on **mortise-and-tenon joints**, pegged together with wooden dowels rather than metal nails (which were valuable and used more sparingly) — this required precise cutting since the joints needed to fit tightly for structural integrity
- **Wattle-and-daub infill**: the spaces between the timber frame were filled with the woven wattle panels (connecting back to basket-weaving skills) covered in daub — this is why timber-framed buildings show that distinctive pattern of exposed dark wood beams with lighter plastered panels in between, still visible in surviving medieval buildings today
- **Pre-fabrication and raising**: larger frames were often assembled and test-fitted on the ground first (sometimes with numbered joints marked for reassembly), then raised into position — a genuinely sophisticated process requiring coordinated teamwork, especially for larger structures
- **Roof construction**: complex roof framing (trusses, rafters) required real geometric and structural knowledge, especially for grander buildings like halls or churches needing wide unsupported spans
### Tools of the Trade
- **Axes and adzes**: for initial rough shaping of timber, especially squaring round logs into usable beams
- **Saws**: various types for cutting timber to length and ripping along the grain
- **Augers and braces**: for drilling holes, especially for the wooden pegs used in joinery
- **Chisels and mallets**: for cutting mortise joints and other precise shaping work
- **Planes**: for smoothing surfaces, particularly important for joiners' finer work
- **Squares, plumb bobs, and lines**: for ensuring accurate angles and verticals — basic but essential measurement tools
- **Compasses/dividers**: for marking out curves and precise measurements
### What Carpenters Built
**Structural work**:
- House frames, barns, and agricultural buildings
- Castle and fortification timber elements (hoardings, scaffolding, siege equipment)
- Church roof structures (some of the most technically demanding carpentry of the era, given the scale)
- Bridges (wooden bridges, or timber elements supporting stone ones)
- Mills (windmills and watermills required sophisticated carpentry for both structure and mechanism)

**Furniture and fittings**:
- Chests, tables, benches, beds
- Doors, screens, paneling
- Church furnishings: choir stalls, pews, rood screens (often elaborately carved)

**Transport**:
- Cart and wagon bodies and wheels
- Ship and boat hulls

**Tools and implements**:
- Wooden handles for other trades' tools
- Agricultural implements (plow frames, for instance, even when the working edge was iron)
- Barrels and casks (though this specific specialization usually fell to a distinct trade — the **cooper** — given how demanding proper barrel-making was)
### Guild Structure and Training
- Carpenters organized into guilds in towns large enough to support them, with the standard **apprentice-journeyman-master** progression
- A master's qualifying piece sometimes involved demonstrating complex joinery or framing skill
- **Master carpenters** overseeing significant construction projects (a cathedral roof, a castle's timber elements) held real professional authority and were sometimes treated similarly to what we'd now think of as architects, since formal "architect" as a distinct trained profession wasn't yet fully separated from master craftsman building expertise
- Large prestigious projects (major cathedrals, royal castles) might draw master carpenters with reputations significant enough to be recruited from other regions or even countries
### Social Status
- Carpentry carried solid, respected status among craft trades — essential, visibly skilled, and often well-compensated for the most accomplished practitioners, especially those capable of complex structural or decorative work
- The trade's universal necessity (everyone needs buildings, furniture, and wooden tools) gave carpenters consistent demand across both rural and urban contexts, similar to blacksmiths in that regard
- Joseph (in Christian tradition, as Jesus's earthly father) being identified as a carpenter gave the trade a degree of religious and cultural dignity beyond its practical value — carpentry guilds sometimes drew on this association for patronage and identity
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A master carpenter overseeing a temple or tower's construction, dealing with the genuine structural/engineering puzzle of an unusually tall or magically-influenced building (perhaps needing to account for ongoing magical stress, levitation effects, or non-standard materials)
- Shipwrights building vessels meant for unusual purposes — sky-ships, planar vessels, or craft meant to withstand magical sea hazards — letting the trade's real technical grounding extend naturally into fantastical territory
- A carpenters' guild dispute over a major civic project (a new guildhall, bridge, or town hall) as a believable, grounded source of local political intrigue, mirroring real historical guild rivalries over prestigious commissions
- A traveling master carpenter NPC with a reputation significant enough that multiple settlements compete for their services — useful as a recurring or one-off quest-giver figure
