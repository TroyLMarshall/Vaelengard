### The Craft and Its Structure
A **herald** began as a **battlefield and tournament official** — someone tasked with identifying combatants (crucial when knights were fully encased in armor and otherwise unrecognizable), announcing combatants and their lineages before a tournament or battle, managing the formal rules and conduct of chivalric contests, and carrying messages between opposing commanders under a recognized, protected status. Over the medieval period, this practical battlefield/tournament function grew into something much broader: heralds became **the era's official experts on heraldry, genealogy, ceremony, and precedence** — the specialists who knew, recorded, and adjudicated who bore which coat of arms, who ranked above whom, and how every formal occasion (coronations, funerals, tournaments, state visits) was supposed to be properly conducted.

**Identifying combatants** — This is the origin point worth understanding well: **coats of arms** developed specifically because armor made knights visually indistinguishable, and a distinctive, standardized heraldic device on shield and surcoat let a herald (and everyone else) identify exactly who was who at a glance, even fully armored, at range, in the chaos of a tournament or battlefield. Heralds became the recognized experts in **reading and cataloguing** these devices — a real, useful, specialized visual literacy, since correctly identifying dozens or hundreds of distinct armorial devices, and knowing which family/individual each belonged to, took real trained expertise.

**Growing responsibilities** — As the role professionalized, heralds took on: **recording and regulating coats of arms** (preventing duplication, adjudicating disputes when two families claimed similar devices, granting new arms to newly ennobled or honored individuals), **genealogy and lineage tracking** (essential for inheritance disputes, marriage alliances, and establishing noble legitimacy — precisely the kind of detailed, high-stakes record-keeping nobility depended on), **ceremonial officiating** (announcing at tournaments, managing court ceremony, proclaiming royal decrees, officiating at state funerals and coronations with exact, prescribed protocol), and **diplomatic/messenger functions** (heralds carried messages between warring or negotiating parties, protected by a widely recognized status of **inviolability** — attacking a herald engaged in their duties was a serious breach of the era's rules of war and chivalric conduct, giving heralds real safe passage even into hostile territory).

**The Herald's College** — By the later medieval period, heralds in major kingdoms organized into formal, chartered institutions — **England's College of Arms** (formally chartered in the 1480s, though heralds functioned informally as a body well before that) is the standout real-world example, structured with a clear internal hierarchy: **Kings of Arms** at the top (the most senior heralds, each typically overseeing a specific territorial jurisdiction), **Heralds** in the middle tier, and **Pursuivants** (junior, apprentice-tier officers still training in the full expertise) at the bottom. This hierarchy is genuinely useful, ready-made structure for your setting — a formal college with real internal rank, jurisdiction, and specialized titles gives you an institution players can interact with at multiple levels, from a junior pursuivant just learning the trade to a Garter King of Arms (a real historical title) with genuine authority over the whole system.

**Knowledge base and prestige** — A skilled herald needed to memorize an enormous, detailed body of information: hundreds of coats of arms and their bearers, complex genealogical relationships across noble houses, precise rules of precedence (who outranks whom in exactly which ceremonial circumstance — genuinely intricate and jealously guarded knowledge, since getting someone's precedence wrong at a formal occasion was a real social insult), and the exact, prescribed forms for every kind of ceremony. This made heralds **genuinely learned specialists**, respected for detailed expert knowledge the way a lawyer or physician might be, occupying a real, distinct professional status quite different from craft trades — closer to a civil servant or court official than an artisan.

**Social position** — Heralds held a genuinely unusual dual status: not nobly born themselves in most cases, yet operating constantly among the highest nobility, entrusted with knowledge (lineage, precedence, legitimacy) that directly affected inheritance, marriage, and political standing at the top of society. Their protected, quasi-diplomatic status in wartime, combined with their intimate procedural knowledge of court and ceremony, gave heralds real influence and access far beyond what their formal social rank alone would suggest — a herald could move between a battlefield, a royal court, and a tournament ground with a level of trusted access very few other professions enjoyed.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** History, Persuasion (or Insight, for reading precedence and court dynamics)
- **Languages:** One additional language
- **Tool Proficiency:** Calligrapher's Supplies (for recording arms and genealogies), or proficiency with a musical instrument (heraldic trumpets/announcements)
- **Feat:** Skilled or Actor
- **Equipment:** A roll of arms (a record of coats of arms and lineages), a herald's tabard displaying their authority, formal ceremonial garb, a letter of appointment from a King of Arms, 15 gp

This background is an excellent fit for a **Bard** (public speaking, ceremony, genealogical/lore knowledge as a real mechanical and narrative asset) or **Paladin** (chivalric procedure, formal honor-bound conduct, a natural connection to knightly institutions). It also suits a **Rogue** with an interesting twist — someone who's learned to forge or falsify coats of arms and lineage claims, using genuine heraldic expertise for exactly the kind of fraud the College exists to prevent, or a **Wizard/Cleric** drawn to the sheer depth of formal, precise, memorized knowledge the trade demands.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Pursuivant Eager to Prove Themselves** — Junior in rank, hungry to advance, sent on unglamorous verification errands (confirming a disputed coat of arms, tracking down an obscure lineage record) that could easily become an adventure hook.
- **The King of Arms** — Senior, formidable, genuinely powerful within their specific domain of expertise — an NPC whose favor or displeasure can affect a noble PC's standing, legitimacy claims, or right to bear arms at all.
- **The Battlefield Herald** — Carries messages between warring commanders under protected status, genuinely brave in an unusual way (walking into danger unarmed, trusting in a status that isn't always respected) — a good recurring wartime NPC.
- **The Forger of Arms** — Someone illegitimately using or granting coats of arms without true authority — a fraud the College takes very seriously, and a good "expose the impostor" investigation hook.
- **The Genealogist for Hire** — A herald quietly willing to "discover" favorable lineage connections for wealthy clients willing to pay — technically within their expertise, ethically dubious, a good morally gray NPC.
### Game Hooks
- **The Disputed Arms** — Two families or individuals both claim the same or dangerously similar coat of arms, and the party gets pulled into the investigation/resolution — political intrigue with a heraldic mystery at its core.
- **The Herald's Safe Passage** — The party needs to cross hostile or dangerous territory and does so under a herald's protected status — testing whether that protection is actually respected by whoever they encounter, with real tension if it isn't.
- **The Forged Lineage** — Someone has fabricated a genealogical claim to inherit land, title, or legitimacy, and the party (perhaps working with or against a herald) has to untangle the true lineage before the false claim succeeds.
- **The Precedence Incident** — A major ceremonial event (coronation, wedding, funeral) is thrown into crisis by a precedence dispute — who walks first, who's seated where — trivial-sounding but genuinely explosive in consequence, and the party has to help navigate or resolve it before it becomes a real diplomatic incident.
- **The Slain Herald** — A herald is killed while under protected status during wartime negotiations — a serious violation of the era's rules of conduct, and the party is drawn into the fallout: outrage, retaliation, or an investigation into who broke such a widely respected taboo and why.
