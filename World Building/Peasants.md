### The Foundation Everything Else Rested On
We've referenced peasants constantly throughout this list — the farm entry covered their agricultural labor and legal status, the private homes entry described their housing, the laborer entry covered the landless among them, the beggar entry touched on those who fell through the bottom — so this is a good synthesis entry, pulling the full picture of peasant life together rather than just the economic/agricultural angle.
### Who Actually Counted as a "Peasant"
"Peasant" is a broad, somewhat imprecise term covering a genuinely wide range of people united mainly by working the land and existing below the gentry/noble social threshold — but the internal variation within this category was real and significant:
- **Villeins/serfs**: legally unfree, bound to the land and lord as covered in the farm entry — owing labor service, unable to leave without permission, subject to various fees and obligations marking their unfree status
- **Free peasants**: legally free, typically owing rent rather than labor service, with more personal mobility and legal rights than villeins — a genuinely meaningful distinction even if both groups worked similar land and lived similar lives materially
- **Yeomen**: prosperous free peasants with enough land to be economically secure, sometimes approaching gentry status from below — the upper edge of the peasant category, blurring into the gentlemen/ladies entry's discussion of the social hierarchy's lower reaches
- **Cottagers and smallholders**: those with minimal land, supplementing subsistence farming with wage labor as covered in the laborer entry
- **Landless laborers**: the most precarious rural group, entirely dependent on wages and without even a small plot's subsistence buffer
### Daily Life — The Genuine Texture
**Work rhythms**: as covered in the farm entry, peasant life followed the agricultural calendar's inescapable seasonal rhythm — plowing, planting, tending, harvesting, slaughtering, then the relative quiet of winter. This wasn't a nine-to-five pattern but a genuinely variable cycle of intense seasonal labor peaks (harvest especially) and slower periods.

**The household economy**: peasant households weren't purely agricultural — they combined farming with various supplementary activities:
- Spinning and weaving for household cloth needs (connecting to the dyer and textile entries)
- Basic food preservation (salting, smoking, pickling — connecting to the fishermen and butcher entries)
- Kitchen gardens for vegetables and herbs (connecting to the herbalist entry)
- Keeping a few animals — typically a pig (as noted in the farm entry, pigs could forage independently and provided meat efficiently), perhaps chickens, and where possible a cow or goat for dairy

**Housing**: as covered in the private homes entry — simple one or two-room structures, earthen floors, thatched roofs, central open hearth, minimal furniture, sometimes sharing space with livestock in colder regions

**Food**: connecting to the chef and baker entries — pottage (thick grain/vegetable stew) as the daily staple, coarse bread, dairy and eggs where available, occasional meat (more common at autumn slaughter time), fish on fast days. Diet was monotonous, nutritionally adequate in good years, genuinely precarious in bad ones.

**Clothing**: simple, functional, homespun where possible — wool and linen rather than the fine dyed cloth covered in the dyer entry, which was beyond ordinary peasant means. Connecting to the furrier and millinery entries, peasants wore practical rather than fashionable clothing, with social/moral expectations around modesty and appropriate dress still applying even at this level.
### The Burden of Obligations
This is genuinely important for understanding peasant life's constraints — peasants weren't simply farming for themselves:
- **Labor service**: villeins owed working days on the lord's demesne, sometimes several days per week, in addition to working their own holdings
- **Rent**: free peasants paid cash or kind rent for their land
- **Church tithes**: as covered in the church entry, a tenth of production owed to the Church
- **Various fees and fines**: manorial courts (as covered in the government official and judge entries) extracted fees for a remarkable range of activities — marriage (a villein needing permission to marry, sometimes paying a fine called **merchet**), inheritance (a **heriot** payment, often the best animal, due when a villein died and their holding passed to heirs), milling fees (as covered in the mills entry), and various other obligations
- **Tallage**: additional arbitrary taxes a lord could levy on villeins specifically, reflecting their unfree status — free peasants had more protection against arbitrary extraction
### Social Life and Community
Peasant life wasn't purely drudgery — genuine social and communal texture existed within the constraints:
- **Village community**: the open field system covered in the farm entry required genuine communal decision-making, creating real village-level solidarity and collective identity beyond simple individual household survival
- **Ale houses and taverns**: as covered in the bartender entry, these were genuine community social hubs
- **Church and religious calendar**: as covered in the church and churchyard entries, religious festivals, saints' days, and the liturgical calendar provided genuine communal celebration and social occasion throughout the year — some of the year's only real "holidays" from agricultural labor
- **Dancing and entertainment**: as covered in the dance hall and entertainer entries, peasants participated in communal dancing and entertainment at festivals, not purely noble court activities
- **Gossip, storytelling, and oral culture**: without literacy (the vast majority of peasants were illiterate), cultural transmission happened through oral tradition — stories, songs, and community memory
### Legal Status and Rights
- **Manorial courts**: as covered in the government official and judge entries, peasants navigated a real (if limited) legal system at the manorial level — bringing disputes to the manorial court, having some recognized rights even as villeins, and sometimes successfully challenging unjust treatment within this system
- **Frankpledge system**: as covered in the constable entry, mutual responsibility structures tied peasant communities together in collective legal obligation
- **Post-Black-Death shift**: as covered in the laborer entry, the plague's demographic impact genuinely shifted peasant bargaining power — a real historical moment when the normally fixed constraints loosened somewhat, driving the Statute of Labourers, Peasants' Revolt, and gradual decline of strict serfdom in various regions
### Peasant Resistance and Agency
Worth emphasizing since it's easy to picture peasants as purely passive victims of the system:
- **Everyday resistance**: foot-dragging on labor service, poaching (as covered in the hunters entry), using hand-querns despite mill monopolies (as covered in the mills entry), and similar small-scale resistance to extractive obligations was constant and genuine
- **Legal challenges**: peasants did sometimes successfully challenge unfair obligations through manorial courts, demonstrating real engagement with available legal mechanisms
- **Collective action**: the Peasants' Revolt of 1381 (mentioned in the laborer entry) wasn't unique — various peasant uprisings and collective actions occurred across medieval Europe, reflecting genuine, organized resistance rather than purely passive suffering
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A peasant community's collective resistance to an unjust lord as a grounded, morally complex adventure hook — not cartoonish villainy but genuine systemic extraction, with real stakes for the community and real legal/social complexity around what "help" actually looks like
- A prosperous yeoman NPC straddling the peasant/gentry boundary — someone with genuine material comfort and community standing but without formal social recognition, creating interesting tension around respect, aspiration, and class anxiety
- The post-plague labor shortage as a campaign-world condition — if your setting has recently experienced a major catastrophic event (plague, war, magical disaster) that's dramatically reduced population, the resulting peasant leverage and noble/employer backlash could drive real, grounded social tension throughout your campaign
- A peasant PC background drawing on the genuine texture covered here — not "generic poor person" but someone with specific knowledge of agricultural cycles, communal village politics, manorial legal obligations, and the particular skills of a self-sufficient household economy
