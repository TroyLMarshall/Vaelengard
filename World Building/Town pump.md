### Water Infrastructure Without Modern Plumbing
Access to clean water was a genuine, daily practical challenge throughout medieval life — and the various solutions medieval communities developed for water supply ranged from simple wells (coming shortly in this list) to more sophisticated infrastructure including pumps, piped water systems, and public fountains. Let's examine what actually existed and how it functioned.
### First — The Terminology Problem
"Town pump" as a specific term is somewhat more associated with the early modern and 18th-19th century periods than the strictly medieval — the iconic village pump around which community life supposedly centered is more a Georgian/Victorian image than a purely medieval one. Medieval water supply infrastructure existed and was genuinely important, but took somewhat different forms than this later image suggests. Let's cover the full range of what actually provided public water access in medieval settlements.
### Wells — The Most Universal Solution
As we'll cover in the dedicated wells entry shortly, dug or bored wells accessing groundwater were the most universal water supply solution across medieval Europe — ranging from private household wells to shared community wells serving multiple households or an entire settlement. The well entry will cover this in detail, so here let's focus on the more infrastructure-intensive elements beyond simple wells.
### Mechanical Pumps — What Actually Existed
Genuine mechanical pumping technology did exist in the medieval period, though it was less universally deployed than the "town pump" image might suggest:

**Simple suction pumps**: using atmospheric pressure to lift water — a cylinder with a piston, drawing water up through the suction created by the piston's movement, were known and used in the medieval period, though their practical lift limit (roughly 25-30 feet, determined by atmospheric pressure physics) constrained their application

**Chain pumps**: an endless chain carrying buckets or discs through a pipe, lifting water continuously — used in mining contexts (connecting to the stoneworker entry's discussion of quarrying) and sometimes for water supply

**Treadwheel and animal-powered pumps**: as covered in the construction company entry's discussion of treadwheel cranes, human or animal-powered mechanical systems could drive pumping mechanisms for deeper water sources or higher-volume supply needs

**The windmill pump connection**: as covered in the mills entry, windmill technology could be applied to pumping rather than grinding — particularly significant in the Netherlands where wind-powered water management (pumping water from low-lying land) became genuinely culturally and economically central
### Piped Water Systems — More Common Than Often Assumed
This genuinely surprises people: medieval towns, particularly larger and more prosperous ones, sometimes had surprisingly sophisticated piped water systems:

**Lead pipe systems**: lead pipes carrying water from springs or elevated sources into towns, connecting to public fountains or specific institutional users — monasteries (as covered in the monastery entry) particularly developed sophisticated internal water management systems, and some systems extended into the broader urban area

**Conduit systems**: covered channels or pipes carrying water from a source outside town into public distribution points within — several major medieval English cities (London, Bristol, and others) developed conduit systems carrying spring water into the city through lead or ceramic pipes, distributing it at public conduit houses

**Conduit houses**: small structures housing the public distribution point of a piped water system — essentially a covered fountain or cistern where citizens could draw water, functioning as a genuine public water supply infrastructure point

**Monastic water engineering**: as mentioned in the monastery entry, monasteries developed sophisticated internal water systems — stone-built channels, pipe systems, and distribution infrastructure serving different parts of the complex (kitchen, infirmary, laundry) — sometimes more advanced than contemporary urban water infrastructure
### Public Fountains — The Continental Tradition
Particularly in Mediterranean and southern European traditions, **public fountains** served a function similar to the public well or conduit house:
- Often architecturally elaborate in wealthier Italian, French, and Spanish towns — the fountain as civic infrastructure combined with civic display, connecting to the town hall entry's discussion of civic architectural ambition
- Fed by spring water through piped systems, providing a reliable, gravity-fed supply at public gathering points
- Serving as community gathering points beyond pure water access — washing, socializing, and information exchange happening at the fountain
### Water Quality Concerns
As touched on in the public baths and outhouses entries, medieval water quality was a genuine concern:
- **Groundwater contamination**: as urban density increased, cesspits (covered in the outhouses entry), animal waste, and industrial waste from trades like tanning and dyeing (covered in those entries) contaminated groundwater, making urban wells of increasingly questionable quality
- **Spring water preference**: spring water, particularly from elevated sources outside urban contamination zones, was generally recognized as superior — explaining why piped spring water systems were worth the investment despite their cost
- **River water**: rivers provided water for many purposes but were also acknowledged waste receptacles (as discussed with butchers, tanners, and dyers) — river water was used but often with recognized quality concerns
### Water Carriers — The Human Solution
Where infrastructure didn't exist or was insufficient:
- **Water carriers**: as mentioned in the laborer entry, carrying water from wells, rivers, or conduit houses to individual urban households was a recognized paid occupation
- **A daily urban necessity**: in dense urban areas without household water access, buying water from a carrier was a regular household expense, similar in economic logic to buying firewood or other delivered necessities
### Guild and Civic Management
Water supply infrastructure required ongoing maintenance and management:
- **Civic responsibility**: conduit systems and public fountains were typically managed as civic infrastructure, with town councils (connecting to the town hall entry) responsible for maintenance funding and management
- **Pipe layers and plumbers**: maintaining lead pipe systems required specialist skills — the word "plumber" derives directly from the Latin **plumbum** (lead), reflecting the trade's historical association with lead pipe work
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A town's conduit system as infrastructure whose disruption has immediate, community-wide consequences — sabotage of the water supply creating genuine crisis and making its restoration an urgent quest with clear stakes
- Water quality as a mystery hook — illness spreading through a community that happens to draw from a specific contaminated source, with the investigation requiring understanding the water supply infrastructure to identify the contamination point
- A public fountain as a community gathering point with specific social significance — where news is exchanged, where a specific community gathers, or where a specific event (a drowning, a miraculous healing, a political confrontation) has historical significance in your setting
- Monastic water engineering as evidence of sophisticated knowledge — a monastery whose internal water system is more advanced than the surrounding town's, suggesting either exceptional engineering tradition or perhaps magical assistance that might be relevant to a quest
