### A Genuinely Developing Profession, Connecting to Several Earlier Entries
We've touched on legal practice throughout this list — the judge entry covered courts and judicial authority, the government official entry mentioned legal/administrative training, the land office entry discussed charters and deeds — so let's bring together the people who actually practiced law as a profession, since this developed real, if uneven, professional structure across the medieval period.
### Why "Lawyer" Looked Different by Region and Period
Legal practice developed along genuinely different lines in different parts of medieval Europe, largely tracking the divide between **English common law** and **continental Roman/canon law** traditions touched on in the judge entry:
### University Legal Training
- **Bologna**: as mentioned in the judge entry, this Italian university became the preeminent center for studying **Roman law** (rediscovered and intensively studied from the 11th-12th centuries onward) and developed enormous influence across continental Europe's legal scholarship
- **Canon law**: Church law represented its own substantial, sophisticated legal tradition, studied alongside or instead of Roman civil law, particularly relevant given the Church courts' significant jurisdiction (marriage, wills, clergy discipline) covered in the government official and judge entries
- **Legal doctorates**: university law faculties granted genuine advanced degrees, producing a recognizable class of formally trained legal scholars and practitioners, particularly significant in continental Europe where Roman/canon law training carried real prestige and practical application
### England's Distinct Path — The Inns of Court
English legal training developed along a notably different track than the continental university model:
- **Practical apprenticeship over university study**: rather than primarily training through university law faculties, English common law practitioners increasingly trained through practical apprenticeship and association with the **Inns of Court** — professional/educational institutions in London where aspiring lawyers learned through observing actual court proceedings, debate, and mentorship from established practitioners
- **The Inns of Court's origins**: these institutions (Lincoln's Inn, Gray's Inn, and others, with their core development happening particularly in the later medieval period) functioned as something between a professional guild, a residential college, and a practical training ground — genuinely distinctive compared to the continental university-centered model
- **Serjeants-at-law and barristers**: English legal practice developed its own internal hierarchy and terminology, with senior, especially qualified practitioners (serjeants-at-law) holding particular status and exclusive rights to practice before certain higher courts
### What Legal Practitioners Actually Did
**Advocates/pleaders**: representing parties in court proceedings, arguing their case according to the relevant law and procedure — requiring genuine knowledge of complex, sometimes locally variable legal custom and procedure

**Document drafters**: preparing the legal documents covered in the land office entry — charters, deeds, wills, contracts — requiring precise knowledge of proper legal form and language to ensure documents held up to scrutiny

**Legal advisors**: providing counsel to clients (individuals, guilds, the Church, nobility) on legal questions, obligations, and strategy — similar in function to modern legal consultation, even without the same formal professional structure

**Notaries**: particularly significant in continental legal tradition (drawing on Roman law precedent), notaries held official authority to draft and authenticate legal documents, witness transactions, and provide a recognized, trusted record-keeping function — a role with real continuity from Roman administrative tradition through the medieval period and beyond
### Who Became Lawyers
- **Often clergy or clergy-adjacent, especially earlier in the period**: given the literacy requirements and overlap with canon law training, clergy frequently occupied legal roles, similar to the pattern seen with the Chancellor position and other administrative roles covered in the government official entry
- **Increasingly lay professionals as the period progressed**: as legal training became more formalized (university faculties, the Inns of Court) and as the practical, increasingly secular business of royal/civic administration grew, a genuine lay professional legal class developed, separate from clerical career paths
- **Social mobility potential**: legal training and practice represented one of the relatively more accessible paths toward social advancement for talented individuals without noble birth — a skilled lawyer could achieve genuine wealth, royal/noble patronage, and even ennoblement through successful legal/administrative service, similar to the social mobility potential touched on in the gentlemen/ladies entry
### Legal Practice and the Church Courts
As covered in the government official and judge entries, the **overlapping jurisdiction** between secular and ecclesiastical courts meant legal practitioners often needed to navigate genuinely complex procedural questions about which court held authority over a given matter — creating real specialized expertise value for those who understood both systems and their interaction.
### Status and Reputation
- **Mixed reputation**: similar to several trades covered throughout this list (the gambler, the tooth-drawer with their showmanship/charlatanry overlap), lawyers attracted some of the same skepticism and satirical treatment in period literature that the profession has arguably retained ever since — accusations of using complex legal language to confuse rather than clarify, prolonging disputes for fees, or serving wealthy clients' interests over genuine justice appear in medieval satirical and moralizing literature
- **Genuine necessity despite the skepticism**: as legal systems grew more complex (particularly with the development of common law precedent and increasingly sophisticated Roman/canon law scholarship), the practical need for trained legal expertise became increasingly undeniable, regardless of the profession's mixed popular reputation
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A university-trained legal scholar NPC navigating the kind of overlapping jurisdiction puzzle discussed in the judge entry — a natural "we need a specialist" resource for PCs facing a legal problem too complex for straightforward combat or social resolution
- An Inns-of-Court-style practical legal training institution as a worldbuilding location — useful if you want a recognizable "where lawyers come from" institution distinct from a standard university, particularly fitting for an England-inspired region of your setting
- A lawyer character whose social mobility through legal skill (rather than noble birth) creates interesting tension with more traditionally-born nobility — a grounded basis for a commoner-born PC or NPC achieving real influence through professional competence rather than birthright
- The period's genuine satirical skepticism toward lawyers as a worldbuilding/roleplay texture detail — an NPC lawyer who's either refreshingly honest (subverting the stereotype) or thoroughly mercenary (playing into it), giving you a recognizable archetype to work with or against
