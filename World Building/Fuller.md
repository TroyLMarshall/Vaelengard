### A Trade Central to Cloth Production We've Referenced Repeatedly
We've mentioned fulling throughout this list — the weaver entry described it as an essential stage in the cloth production chain, the mills entry specifically noted fulling mills as a significant application of water-powered mill technology, and the dyer entry placed it within the broader textile production sequence. Let's give the fuller and the fulling process their full dedicated treatment, since this was a genuinely significant medieval trade whose technological development had real economic consequences.
### What Fulling Actually Was
Fulling (also called **walking** or **tucking** in regional English variants, giving us several English surnames) was the process of cleaning and thickening freshly woven wool cloth through a combination of **moisture, heat, and mechanical agitation**:

**What it achieved**:
- **Cleaning**: removing the lanolin, oils, and dirt accumulated in the wool fiber during spinning and weaving
- **Shrinking**: controlled shrinkage of the cloth, consolidating the weave structure into a denser, more compact fabric
- **Felting the surface**: causing the microscopic scales on wool fibers to interlock and mat together, producing a smoother, more uniform surface that obscured the individual thread structure of the weave
- **Thickening**: the combined shrinkage and felting produced cloth significantly thicker, denser, and more durable than the freshly woven fabric

The transformation was genuinely dramatic — loosely woven cloth entering the fulling process emerged considerably smaller, denser, and with a completely different surface character. A skilled fuller controlled this process to achieve specific desired properties rather than simply fulling until the cloth was ruined by over-processing.
### The Process — From Foot to Mill
**Foot fulling/walking**: the original, ancient technique — fullers literally treading the cloth with their feet in a trough containing water and a fulling agent (typically **fuller's earth**, a naturally occurring clay mineral with excellent cleansing properties, or sometimes stale urine as a source of ammonia for cleaning)
- Physically exhausting work — walking on wet, heavy cloth in a trough for extended periods
- Required skill in controlling pressure, duration, and the cloth's movement to achieve even fulling without damaging the fabric
- The surname "Walker" in English derives directly from this occupational tradition

**The fulling mill — a transformative technology**: as covered in the mills entry, water-powered fulling mills using hammers driven by a water wheel to pound the cloth represented a genuine technological revolution in cloth finishing
- **Mechanical advantage**: mill hammers could apply more consistent, controlled force over longer periods than human feet, with less labor cost
- **Spreading adoption**: fulling mills spread significantly from the 12th-13th centuries onward across England and continental Europe — the mills entry noted this as a significant application of water-powered mill technology beyond grain grinding
- **Geographic concentration effect**: because fulling mills required water power, cloth finishing became concentrated at mill sites with appropriate water supply, creating geographic specialization — mill towns developing around fulling operations, drawing cloth production activities to their vicinity

**Fuller's earth**: the primary cleaning agent
- A naturally occurring aluminum silicate clay with remarkable absorptive properties — pulling oils, lanolin, and dirt out of wool fibers during the fulling process
- Significant enough as a trade material to be subject to specific English export regulations at various points — the crown recognizing its importance to the domestic cloth industry and attempting to prevent export that might benefit foreign cloth producers
### What Happened After Fulling
Fulling was one stage in a multi-step finishing process:

**Tentering**: after fulling, cloth had shrunk and was potentially distorted — it needed to be stretched back to even dimensions and dried under tension
- **Tenter frames**: wooden frames with hooks (tenterhooks — giving us the phrase "on tenterhooks" for a state of tension) holding the cloth under tension while drying
- **Tenter fields**: in cloth-producing areas, fields of tenter frames were a distinctive landscape feature — stretches of cloth drying in rows, a visible marker of the textile industry's presence

**Napping/raising**: using dried teasel heads (the teasel plant's prickly seed heads having ideal properties for this purpose) or later metal cards to raise a surface nap from the fulled cloth
- Connecting to the farm entry's broader agricultural context — teasels cultivated specifically for this industrial textile purpose

**Shearing**: cutting the raised nap to an even length with large cloth shears — producing the smooth, uniform surface characteristic of high-quality finished broadcloth
- Shearmen (cloth shearers) sometimes a distinct trade from fullers

**Dyeing**: as covered in the dyer entry, dyeing might happen before or after fulling depending on the desired result — piece dyeing (after weaving and fulling) versus yarn dyeing (before weaving) producing different visual effects
### Guild Structure and Social Position
- Fullers organized into guilds in major cloth-producing towns — sometimes combined with other cloth-finishing trades, sometimes maintaining distinct guild organization
- **Significant political role in Flemish cloth towns**: as touched on in the weaver entry, the Flemish cloth towns' social conflicts often involved fullers specifically — fullers and weavers representing different stages of production with sometimes competing economic interests, with fullers being among the most politically active and sometimes radical elements of urban cloth-worker populations
- **The mill owner question**: the transition from foot fulling to mill fulling created a specific economic tension — mill owners (sometimes lords or wealthy merchants rather than fullers themselves) controlling capital-intensive infrastructure that fullers depended on, creating dependency relationships connecting to the broader mill monopoly issues covered in the mills entry
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A fulling mill as a specific, atmospheric industrial location — the thunderous noise of water-powered hammers pounding cloth, the smell of fuller's earth and wet wool, and the mechanical complexity of the water-driven mechanism creating a distinctive sensory environment quite different from craft workshops
- The tenter field as a specific landscape detail — rows of cloth stretched on frames across a field adjacent to a mill town, immediately communicating something about the local economy and the scale of cloth production
- Fuller's earth as a specific traded material with plot relevance — control of a specific fuller's earth deposit creating economic leverage over cloth production in a region, mirroring real medieval concerns about this material's strategic importance
- The fuller/mill owner tension as a small-scale economic conflict — a community of traditional foot fullers facing displacement by a wealthy merchant's new fulling mill, with genuine competing interests (the mill's efficiency versus the fullers' livelihoods) creating moral complexity without requiring cartoonish villainy
