### From Moneychangers to Merchant Bankers
Medieval banking didn't emerge as a single, designed institution — it grew organically out of several overlapping trades, and the word "bank" itself comes from _banco_, the bench Italian moneychangers sat at in marketplaces to conduct their trade. By the late medieval period, banking had become a genuinely sophisticated, Europe-spanning system, even without a formal banking license or central regulatory body in the modern sense.
### The Roots: Moneychangers
The most basic financial trade was **currency exchange**. Medieval Europe had no single currency — every city, kingdom, and even some individual lords might mint their own coins, with varying weights and purity of precious metal. A moneychanger:
- Assessed and exchanged coins from different mints, taking a fee or spread on the transaction
- Tested coin purity (debased or clipped coins were a constant problem)
- Often worked in or near marketplaces and fairs, especially the great international trade fairs (like those at **Champagne** in France) where merchants from many regions converged
### The Italian Banking Houses
By the 13th-14th centuries, banking had become dominated by powerful **Italian merchant banking families and houses**, especially from Florence, Siena, and later Genoa and Venice:
- **The Bardi and Peruzzi families** (Florence, 13th-14th century) ran banking operations across Europe with branches in multiple countries — genuinely international operations for the era, financing kings, including English royal wars, which famously bankrupted both houses when **Edward III defaulted** on enormous loans in the 1340s.
- **The Medici Bank** (15th century, just past the strictly medieval period but built on these foundations) became perhaps the most famous example, with the family using banking wealth to become the de facto rulers of Florence.
### What Bankers Actually Did
**Deposit-taking and safekeeping**: wealthy merchants, nobles, and even the Church deposited funds for safekeeping, sometimes earning a modest return (carefully structured to avoid the appearance of usury — more below).

**Lending**: the core profitable activity — lending to merchants for trade ventures, to nobles for wars and lavish spending, and to monarchs for state expenses. Royal lending was lucrative but genuinely risky, since a king who defaulted had no real legal consequence to fear (see the Bardi/Peruzzi collapse above).

**Bills of exchange**: this was the truly revolutionary innovation. Rather than physically transporting gold or silver across dangerous medieval roads, a merchant could deposit money with a banker in one city and receive a **bill of exchange** — essentially an early form of a check or letter of credit — redeemable for the equivalent sum (often in a different currency) at a partner branch or correspondent banker in another city. This made long-distance trade dramatically safer and more efficient.

**International currency conversion**: bills of exchange inherently involved converting between currencies, which let sophisticated bankers profit from exchange rate differences between cities.

**Financing trade ventures**: providing capital for merchant voyages and goods purchases, sometimes in exchange for a share of profits rather than fixed interest (a structure that helped sidestep usury concerns).

**Financing the Church and Crusades**: the Papacy itself used banking services extensively to move money collected from across Christendom back to Rome, and crusade financing involved serious banking infrastructure.
### The Usury Problem
This is the central tension that shaped medieval banking: the Church considered **usury** (charging interest on loans) sinful, based on biblical prohibitions and Aristotelian arguments that money shouldn't "breed" money unnaturally. This created real complications:
- **Workarounds**: bankers structured transactions to disguise interest as something else — exchange rate margins on bills of exchange, "gifts" expected in return for a loan, or profit-sharing arrangements that functioned like interest without being technically named as such.
- **Jewish moneylenders**: because Jewish religious law didn't prohibit lending at interest to non-Jews the way Christian doctrine prohibited Christian-to-Christian usury, Jewish communities filled a crucial economic niche as moneylenders in many regions — though this also made them targets for resentment, debt-driven violence, and periodic expulsions (England expelled its Jewish population in 1290, partly tangled up with these economic tensions).
- **The Lombards**: Italian Christian moneylenders (broadly called "Lombards" regardless of specific city origin) became so associated with banking that "Lombard Street" remains a financial district name in London today.
- **Pawnbroking and the Monte di Pietà**: toward the very late medieval period, the Church itself began sponsoring charitable pawnshops (offering small loans against collateral at low, charitable rates) partly as an alternative to predatory lending.
### Tools and Practices
- **Double-entry bookkeeping**: increasingly refined by Italian merchants and bankers through the medieval period, allowing more accurate tracking of complex, multi-branch financial operations — this accounting innovation was hugely important and is still the basis of modern accounting.
- **Ledgers and account books**, often in specialized merchant shorthand or code
- **Correspondent networks**: relationships between banking houses in different cities that made bills of exchange and long-distance finance possible
- **Coin scales and touchstones**: for assessing the metal content and purity of coins
### Risk and Reputation
Banking was a genuinely risky business — beyond royal defaults, there was no deposit insurance, no central bank backstop, and a bank's failure could trigger real cascading economic damage in a city, as it relied heavily on trust and reputation rather than formal regulatory guarantees. A banker's good name and connections were, in a very real sense, his primary asset.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A banking house secretly backed by a dragon's hoard, making them untouchable even when their human clients default
- Bills of exchange enchanted to be unforgeable/magically verified, making a particular banking guild the trusted backbone of regional trade
- A subplot where a kingdom's crown owes a catastrophic debt to a banking house, and the political fallout if they can't (or won't) pay
- Tension between a "Jewish moneylender" analog group (a minority group filling an economically necessary but socially resented niche) as a way to explore prejudice and economic dependency in your setting, if you want that kind of thematic weight
- A banker character who knows everyone's financial secrets — a natural hub for intrigue, blackmail, or information-brokering plotlines
