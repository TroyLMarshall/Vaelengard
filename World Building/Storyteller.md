### The Craft and Its Structure
In an overwhelmingly illiterate society with no printed books, no mass media, and limited manuscript access even for those who could read, **oral storytelling was a primary vehicle for entertainment, moral instruction, practical knowledge transmission, and cultural memory** — practiced at every level of society, from professional itinerant performers to a household's own grandmother telling stories by the hearth fire, meaning this "profession" existed on a real spectrum from genuinely universal folk practice to specialized, skilled professional performance.

**Folk tales, fables, and everyday narrative** — Most storytelling happened informally, within families and communities, passed down through generations — folk tales (many surviving into the modern era through much later written collection, like the Brothers Grimm's work centuries afterward, but genuinely ancient in oral practice), animal fables carrying explicit moral lessons (the **Aesopic tradition**, known and adapted throughout medieval Europe, and the genuinely popular medieval **Reynard the Fox** cycle — clever trickster-animal stories satirizing human folly, greed, and social hierarchy through beast-fable allegory), religious and saints' legend narratives (often blending genuine devotional content with fantastical, entertaining miracle stories), and practical/cautionary tales warning against real dangers (getting lost in the woods, trusting strangers, specific local hazards) wrapped in memorable narrative form. This everyday, non-professional storytelling tradition was the water every medieval person swam in, whether or not anyone made a living from it specifically.

**Professional and semi-professional storytellers** — Overlapping heavily with minstrels and jongleurs, itinerant performers who specialized more in spoken narrative than sung verse made a living reciting folk tales, fables, riddles, and shorter entertaining stories at markets, fairs, taverns, and noble households — using real performance skill (voice, timing, audience engagement, sometimes call-and-response or riddle-games with the crowd) to hold an audience's attention purely through spoken narrative craft, without necessarily relying on music or physical performance the way a minstrel or acrobat might. Household storytellers — sometimes elderly household members, sometimes retained servants specifically valued for the skill — provided regular evening entertainment for noble and even modest households in an age with genuinely limited alternatives for passing long winter evenings.

**Riddles and wordplay** — A genuinely significant and popular medieval form worth calling out specifically: **riddling** was both entertainment and a real test of wit, appearing in written collections (the Old English _Exeter Book_ preserves a substantial collection of Anglo-Saxon riddles, ranging from clever wordplay to genuinely startling, sometimes bawdy or philosophical content) and functioning as oral social entertainment — a skilled riddler or storyteller might weave riddle-contests directly into a broader performance, engaging an audience actively rather than simply reciting at them.

**Frame narratives and story collections** — By the later medieval period, skilled writers began compiling and structuring individual oral-tradition-style tales into larger literary works using a **frame narrative** device — a group of characters telling each other stories within an overarching narrative structure — Chaucer's _Canterbury Tales_ (pilgrims telling stories to pass their journey) and Boccaccio's _Decameron_ (young Florentines telling stories while sheltering from plague) being the towering examples, both genuinely drawing on and reflecting real oral storytelling culture and its social function (passing time, entertaining companions, revealing character through what stories people choose to tell) even as they became sophisticated written literary works in their own right.

**Storytelling and practical knowledge** — Beyond pure entertainment, oral narrative served genuinely important **practical and educational functions** in a largely illiterate society — passing down agricultural knowledge, moral instruction, local history, and cautionary wisdom in a memorable, transmissible form accessible to people who couldn't read a book even if one were available, making the skilled storyteller a genuine (if informal) educator and culture-keeper alongside their entertainment function.

**Social position** — Highly variable, much like musicians — informal household/family storytelling carried no particular status distinction (everyone did it, to some degree), while professional itinerant storytellers shared the same general precarity and social ambiguity as minstrels and jongleurs, and truly exceptional individual figures (a household's specifically beloved storyteller, or a later literary figure like Chaucer or Boccaccio compiling and elevating the tradition into acclaimed written work) could achieve real, individual reputation and reward.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Performance, Insight (reading an audience, knowing which story fits which moment)
- **Tool Proficiency:** None specific, or proficiency in an instrument if blending with minstrel tradition
- **Feat:** Skilled or Lucky
- **Equipment:** A collection of memorized tales, fables, and riddles, simple traveling or household clothes, 5 gp

This background is an excellent, flexible fit for a **Bard** (a slightly different flavor than the musician-Bard — pure narrative and wit rather than music), and works beautifully for a **Druid** given fable and folk-tale traditions' deep ties to nature, animals, and practical land-knowledge wrapped in story form. It's also a strong pick for any character wanting a grounded, humble origin built around genuine cleverness and social skill rather than physical or magical training — someone who learned early that a good story (or a good riddle) could get you fed, sheltered, or out of trouble.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Household Storyteller** — A beloved, longtime fixture of a noble or common household, valued specifically for filling long winter evenings with exactly the right tale for the mood — a good warm, grounded recurring NPC.
- **The Riddle-Master** — Specializes in riddle-contests as much as narrative, using wit-games to test, entertain, or subtly humble overconfident audiences (including, potentially, the party).
- **The Fable-Teller with a Point** — Uses beast-fables (Reynard-the-Fox style) to deliver pointed social or political commentary under cover of "just a story" — echoing the jester's protected license to speak truth, but through narrative rather than direct wit.
- **The Frame-Narrative Compiler** — A Chaucer/Boccaccio-style figure gathering and structuring many individual tales (possibly from the party themselves) into a larger written work — a good "the party's adventures become legend" narrative device.
- **The Keeper of Practical Lore** — An elderly storyteller whose seemingly simple tales encode real, useful practical or historical knowledge — dismissed as quaint by some, quietly invaluable to those who listen closely.
### Game Hooks
- **The Story With a Hidden Truth** — A well-known folk tale or fable turns out to encode real, literal historical or practical information (a warning, a location, a genuine event) that the party needs to correctly interpret.
- **The Riddle Contest** — The party needs something (passage, information, respect) from a figure who demands they win a riddle-contest first — a fun, low-combat social/puzzle challenge.
- **Becoming the Story** — The party's own deeds are being gathered and told as a story by a traveling storyteller, and they have to decide how they feel about (and possibly influence) how they're being remembered.
- **The Suppressed Tale** — A particular story or fable has been banned, discouraged, or actively suppressed by an authority who recognizes its subversive point — the party helps a storyteller keep telling it, or investigates why it's so threatening.
- **Passing the Long Night** — A framing device adventure — the party is stuck somewhere (weathered in, under siege, awaiting rescue) and passes the time telling stories, Decameron/Canterbury-Tales style, with each story potentially a smaller side-adventure or flashback in its own right.
