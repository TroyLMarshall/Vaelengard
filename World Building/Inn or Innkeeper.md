### Pulling Together Threads From Several Earlier Entries
We've referenced inns repeatedly throughout this list — alongside taverns in the bartender entry, as a boardinghouse equivalent for longer stays, connected to ferry crossings and trade routes — so let's give this its own full, dedicated treatment as a distinct institution from the simpler alehouse/tavern.
### Inn vs. Tavern vs. Alehouse — The Real Distinction
As touched on in the bartender entry, these weren't identical institutions:
- **Alehouses**: the most basic, often just a household selling surplus ale, predominantly run by women (alewives)
- **Taverns**: somewhat more formal, often specifically wine-focused, a notch up in price and status
- **Inns**: the most substantial of the three, specifically combining **lodging with food and drink service**, oriented toward travelers needing overnight accommodation rather than just local drinking custom

This distinction mattered enough that inns occupied a genuinely different economic and social niche, requiring more capital investment (rooms, stabling, larger premises) than a simple alehouse.
### What an Inn Provided
**Lodging**: private or shared rooms for overnight (or longer) stays — quality and privacy varied enormously by the inn's status and the traveler's ability to pay, with wealthier guests securing private rooms while poorer travelers might share communal sleeping space

**Food and drink**: meals provided to guests, similar to the chef entry's broader discussion of food service, though an inn's kitchen would need to handle variable numbers of travelers rather than a stable household's predictable needs

**Stabling**: critically important given how much medieval travel depended on horses — secure stabling, feed, and care for travelers' animals was as essential a service as housing the travelers themselves

**Storage and security**: safekeeping for travelers' goods and valuables, connecting to the legal liability concept mentioned in the boardinghouse entry — innkeepers bore some recognized responsibility for protecting guests' property from theft while under their roof
### Who Stayed at Inns
- **Merchants**: traveling for trade, as covered extensively in the freight company and caravan guild entries — inns were essential infrastructure for the long-distance commerce this whole list has touched on repeatedly
- **Pilgrims**: traveling to shrines and holy sites (connecting to the healers entry's discussion of pilgrimage for healing, and the broader medieval pilgrimage tradition), representing a major and reliable category of inn custom along established pilgrimage routes
- **Government officials and messengers**: those traveling on royal, noble, or ecclesiastical business
- **Students**: traveling to and from universities, sometimes for extended stays in university towns (connecting to the boardinghouse entry's discussion of medieval student lodging)
- **General travelers**: anyone moving between towns for personal, legal, or business reasons
### Innkeepers — The People Running These Establishments
**A position requiring real capital and trust**: running an inn required more substantial investment than simpler trades — securing and maintaining a large enough property, managing variable demand, handling cash and valuables for strangers — making innkeeping a position that could be genuinely prosperous but also carried real responsibility and risk.

**Mixed-gender involvement**: while formal ownership/proprietorship might often be associated with a male innkeeper (the "host," as in Chaucer's Harry Bailly, the innkeeper character in _The Canterbury Tales_), the actual day-to-day work of running an inn — managing servants, overseeing food preparation, handling guests — often substantially involved women in the household, similar to patterns seen elsewhere in this list with trades that blended formal male guild/ownership structure with substantial female labor.

**Reputation mattered enormously**: given the absence of any formal rating or regulatory system beyond basic licensing, an innkeeper's personal reputation for honesty, cleanliness, and fair dealing was their primary asset — word-of-mouth among the traveling merchant and pilgrim community could make or break a specific inn's business.

**Regulation**: similar to alehouses and taverns, inns were subject to various forms of civic oversight — licensing requirements, and in some places specific regulations about innkeeper liability for guest property, pricing, and conduct standards.
### Famous Inns in Literature and History
- **The Tabard Inn**: immortalized as the starting point for Chaucer's _Canterbury Tales_ pilgrims, giving us a vivid (if literary rather than strictly documentary) picture of the social mixing an inn could host — pilgrims of wildly different social classes and occupations gathering and traveling together from a shared starting point
- **Pilgrim route inns**: established pilgrimage routes (to Santiago de Compostela, Canterbury, Rome, and other major shrines) developed networks of inns and hostels specifically catering to the predictable, substantial traveler traffic these routes generated
### Inn Architecture
- **Courtyard layout**: many substantial inns were built around a central courtyard, with stabling and storage on the ground floor and guest rooms accessed via galleries on upper floors — a practical layout for managing the combination of human lodging, animal stabling, and goods storage an inn required
- **Common rooms**: shared spaces for eating, drinking, and socializing among guests, similar in social function to the tavern's role but specifically serving the inn's lodging guests
- **Signage**: inns developed recognizable signage (painted signs, often pictorial given limited literacy) to be identifiable to travelers — many traditional English pub/inn names (the Red Lion, the White Hart, and similar) have roots in this practical signage tradition, sometimes connected to local noble heraldry or other locally meaningful symbols
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A well-known inn along a major trade or pilgrimage route as a recurring campaign location — the classic "party meets at the tavern/inn" trope grounded in genuine historical function, since inns really were where diverse travelers from different walks of life would plausibly cross paths
- An innkeeper NPC with genuine reputation-based standing in the region — a trusted, neutral figure PCs might rely on for information, safe lodging, or as a recognized character witness given how much real social weight innkeeper reputation carried
- Inn courtyard architecture as a useful, grounded detail for action scenes — multi-level galleries, stabling, and storage areas provide natural verticality and complexity for a tavern brawl, chase, or stealth sequence
- A Canterbury-Tales-style diverse traveling group sharing lodging at an inn as a framing device for introducing multiple NPCs or even PCs to each other at a campaign's start, mirroring the genuine historical social-mixing function inns served
