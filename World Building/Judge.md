### Pulling Together a System We've Touched on Repeatedly
We've referenced judicial authority throughout this list — manorial courts in the farm entry, ecclesiastical courts in the government official entry, itinerant royal justices, the sheriff's judicial functions, ale-conners and market officials enforcing trade regulation — so let's bring the full judicial picture together, since medieval Europe genuinely had **multiple overlapping court systems** rather than one unified judiciary.
### The Overlapping Jurisdictions
This is the essential starting point: unlike a modern unified court system, medieval justice operated through several **parallel, sometimes competing** jurisdictions, and which court handled a given case depended on who was involved and what kind of matter it was:

**Royal/secular courts**: the crown's own justice system, increasingly significant as monarchs worked to extend and standardize royal legal authority across their realms

**Manorial courts**: as covered in the farm entry, lords held judicial authority over their own tenants for many local matters — labor obligations, minor disputes, agricultural regulation

**Ecclesiastical courts**: as covered in the government official entry, the Church maintained its own court system with jurisdiction over matters like marriage, wills, clergy discipline, and moral offenses

**Borough/town courts**: chartered towns often held their own judicial rights, handling commercial disputes, guild matters, and local civic offenses (connecting to the guild office entry's discussion of guild internal dispute resolution, which existed alongside formal town courts)

**Forest courts**: as covered in the hunters entry, forest law operated through its own specific judicial apparatus given how distinct and harsh this body of law was from ordinary common law
### Royal Judges and the Development of Common Law
This is genuinely one of medieval Europe's more significant legal developments, particularly well-documented in England:
- **Itinerant/circuit justices**: as mentioned in the government official entry, monarchs sent traveling judges out across the realm to hear cases, gradually establishing more uniform royal legal standards in place of purely local custom and manorial variation
- **The King's Bench and other royal courts**: as royal justice formalized, dedicated central royal courts developed, staffed by judges with real legal training and royal authority
- **Common law's development**: this itinerant justice system, combined with growing reliance on **precedent** (previous case decisions informing future rulings) rather than purely local custom, gradually built up what became **English common law** — a genuinely significant legal innovation with lasting influence well beyond the medieval period
- **Royal writs**: formal legal documents initiating specific types of legal action, representing increasing procedural sophistication in how royal justice actually operated
### Trial by Jury — A Developing Practice
- **Early forms of jury involvement**: rather than judges single-handedly determining facts and guilt the way a modern judge might in certain proceedings, medieval English law increasingly relied on **juries** — initially functioning more as a group of local people providing relevant facts/testimony based on their own knowledge, gradually evolving toward something closer to the modern fact-finding jury role
- **Trial by ordeal and trial by combat**: worth noting these older methods (covered partly because they're vivid and often referenced in fantasy media) — determining guilt or resolving disputes through physical ordeal (handling hot iron, submersion in water, with the outcome interpreted as divine judgment) or formal combat between parties — were genuinely practiced earlier in the medieval period but increasingly fell out of favor and were formally prohibited or abandoned as more procedurally developed jury-based and evidence-based justice systems took hold, particularly from the 13th century onward in many regions
- **Compurgation/oath-helpers**: another older practice — an accused person could sometimes clear themselves by having a set number of others swear oaths supporting their innocence or good character, reflecting a legal system that placed real weight on oath-taking and communal reputation rather than purely physical evidence
### Who Actually Served as Judges
- **University-trained legal scholars**: as legal education developed (Bologna was particularly significant for the study of Roman law specifically, influencing legal scholarship across much of continental Europe), a genuine professional legal class emerged, distinct from purely customary or feudal judicial authority
- **Clergy**: given literacy requirements and the Church's own substantial legal tradition (canon law), clergy frequently served judicial functions, both within Church courts specifically and sometimes in broader administrative/legal roles for secular authorities (echoing the Chancellor position discussed in the government official entry)
- **Nobility exercising manorial/feudal judicial right**: a lord didn't need specific legal training to exercise manorial court authority over their own tenants — this judicial function came with landholding and lordship itself, rather than requiring professional credentialing
- **Royal appointees**: increasingly, as royal justice formalized, monarchs appointed specifically designated judges to staff royal courts and circuit justice positions, sometimes drawing on the emerging professional legal class trained in universities or through practical legal apprenticeship
### Roman Law's Influence on the Continent
Worth distinguishing from the English common law tradition: many continental European legal systems drew more heavily and directly on **rediscovered Roman law** (particularly following renewed scholarly study, again significantly centered at Bologna from the 11th-12th centuries onward), creating a genuinely different legal tradition and judicial approach than the English common law system's reliance on accumulated case precedent and jury-based fact-finding.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- Trial by combat or ordeal as a dramatic, period-flavored alternative to a standard "go gather evidence" investigation plot — letting a PC physically fight for justice (or watch an NPC do so) rather than only ever solving disputes through detective work
- The overlapping jurisdiction system (church vs. secular vs. manorial courts) as a genuine worldbuilding tool for legal intrigue — a case that could go multiple directions depending on which court claims authority, giving PCs a procedural puzzle to navigate alongside any combat or investigation elements
- An itinerant royal justice's arrival in a settlement as a plot catalyst (echoing the government official entry's similar suggestion) — bringing outside legal scrutiny that disrupts local corruption or creates new stakes for an existing local conflict
- A university-trained legal scholar NPC navigating complex jurisdictional questions as a useful "this case needs a specialist" resource for PCs facing a legal puzzle beyond simple combat or social resolution
