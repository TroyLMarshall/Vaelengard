### Northern European Traditions
**The Longship (Viking Age, c. 800-1100s)** — The iconic Scandinavian vessel: long, narrow, shallow-drafted, clinker-built (overlapping planks riveted together, giving real flexibility and strength for open-water and river travel alike), powered by both a single square sail and oars, allowing genuine tactical flexibility — sail for open sea, oars for maneuvering, river navigation, and approaching a shore without needing a proper harbor. Shallow draft meant longships could beach directly or navigate far up rivers, a huge part of what made Viking raiding and trading reach as far as it did. Distinct sub-types existed for different purposes — the _knarr_ was a beamier, deeper cargo/trading variant, less suited to raiding but far better for hauling goods across open ocean (including the North Atlantic crossings to Iceland, Greenland, and beyond).

**The Cog (c. 1200s-1400s)** — The workhorse merchant vessel of Northern European and especially **Hanseatic League** trade — a broad-beamed, flat-bottomed, single-masted vessel with a single square sail, clinker-built like the longship but bulkier and deeper-hulled, prioritizing **cargo capacity over speed or agility**. Cogs featured a distinctive straight, high stern and bow with raised platforms (fore- and sterncastles) that served double duty as cargo/defense positions — genuinely important, since a cog might need to fight off pirates as readily as carry wool, timber, or salt fish. The cog was THE ship of Baltic and North Sea trade, moving the bulk goods (salt, wool, timber, grain, fish, beer) that fed the whole Hanseatic trading network connecting the salt routes, wool trade, and Baltic ports covered throughout this whole list.

**The Hulk** — A related, somewhat larger northern cargo vessel type existing alongside the cog through the medieval period, with a distinctive curved, banana-like hull shape — less well-documented and understood than the cog, but a genuine parallel tradition worth knowing exists if you want naval variety without needing Mediterranean-style vessels.
### Mediterranean Traditions
**The Galley** — The dominant Mediterranean warship for most of the medieval period: long, narrow, and shallow, powered primarily by **oars** (with auxiliary sail for cruising, but oars for combat maneuverability and reliable movement regardless of wind), crewed by large numbers of rowers, and armed for close combat and ramming rather than the standoff cannon warfare of much later naval history. Galleys required genuinely large crews (rowers plus fighting men) and, being oar-powered and low-freeboard, were poorly suited to rough open-ocean conditions, keeping them largely a Mediterranean and coastal phenomenon rather than an Atlantic/North Sea vessel type. The **Venetian Arsenal** — a genuinely massive, sophisticated state shipyard capable of producing galleys at remarkable speed through what amounted to real proto-industrial assembly-line methods (standardized parts, organized production stages) — represents one of medieval Europe's most impressive feats of organized manufacturing, worth knowing about specifically if you want a "surprisingly industrial" production center in your setting.

**The Cog's Mediterranean cousins and the Carrack** — Toward the later medieval period, Mediterranean and northern shipbuilding traditions increasingly cross-pollinated, producing vessels like the **carrack** — a larger, multi-masted vessel combining features from both traditions (multiple masts with a mix of square and triangular lateen sails, allowing better handling and sailing closer to the wind than a single-square-sail cog), representing the genuine technological lineage leading toward the exploration-age ships (caravels, galleons) that come after the strict medieval period ends. Carracks sit right at the late-medieval edge, similar in timing to several other "genuinely medieval but pushing toward the period's later boundary" entries already covered.
### Vessel Components and Terminology Worth Knowing
- **Clinker-built (lapstrake):** planks overlapping and riveted, the dominant Northern European hull construction method — flexible, strong, but harder to make watertight at scale than the alternative
- **Carvel-built:** planks laid edge-to-edge over an internal frame (smoother hull, better suited to larger vessels and eventually replacing clinker construction for bigger ships) — more associated with Mediterranean tradition and later medieval/early-modern shipbuilding generally
- **Fore/sterncastle:** raised structures at bow and stern, serving as fighting platforms and cargo/crew space — the literal origin of "forecastle," still used in modern nautical terminology
- **Lateen sail:** a triangular sail (Mediterranean/Arabic maritime tradition) allowing a vessel to sail more effectively against the wind than a single square sail — genuinely important technology, and its combination with square sails on carracks represents real innovation
- **Draft:** how deep a vessel sits in the water — shallow draft (longships, cogs) allows river/coastal access; deep draft trades that flexibility for cargo capacity or stability
### Worldbuilding Applications
- **Match vessel type to trade route:** a cog hauling salt or wool along the Hanseatic/North Sea routes covered earlier; a galley in Mediterranean or Italian city-state contexts (Venice, Genoa); a longship for a Scandinavian-flavored raiding/exploring culture.
- **Naval combat flavor:** galleys favor ramming and boarding actions with large crews; cogs and northern vessels favor missile fire and boarding from raised castle platforms; longships favor speed, shallow-water tactics, and hit-and-run raiding.
- **Shipbuilding centers as setting locations:** a Venetian Arsenal-style state shipyard is a fantastic, distinctive location — proto-industrial, heavily guarded, a target for espionage or sabotage given its strategic value.
- **Connects directly to trades already covered:** the ship's chandler provisioning these vessels, the harbor pilot guiding them into port, the salt/wool merchants whose cargo they carry, the shipwrights (a natural next entry) who build and repair them.
