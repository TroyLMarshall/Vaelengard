### The Craft and Its Structure
A **parchmenter** (or parchment maker) transformed raw animal skin — most commonly **sheep, goat, and calf** — into the smooth, durable writing surface that carried the overwhelming majority of medieval written culture before paper became widely available. The finest grade, made from young or unborn calfskin, is properly called **vellum**, prized for its exceptional smoothness and used for the most prestigious manuscripts, while ordinary parchment from sheep or goat skin served the vast bulk of everyday documents, books, and records. Understanding parchment's central, unglamorous importance is key: essentially every medieval book, charter, land deed, letter, and legal record before the later spread of paper existed on this material, making parchmenters foundational, invisible infrastructure to medieval literacy and record-keeping — much like the papermaker and ink maker covered earlier, but older, more established, and considerably more prestigious given its association with lasting, legally significant documents.

**The process** — Genuinely laborious and multi-stage: raw hides were first **soaked and dehaired**, typically using a **lime solution** (a caustic treatment loosening hair and flesh from the skin — connecting directly to the tanner's trade in technique if not in final product), then scraped clean of remaining hair and flesh with a curved, two-handled **lunellum** (a specialized parchmenter's knife). The cleaned skin was then **stretched taut on a wooden frame** and scraped repeatedly while drying under tension — this stretching-while-drying step is what fundamentally distinguishes parchment-making from leather tanning, since tension during drying is what gives parchment its characteristic smooth, flat, stiff quality rather than leather's flexible, worked texture. The stretched skin was scraped, dried, scraped again, and often treated with **chalk or pumice** to whiten and smooth the surface further, then cut to size. This entire process demanded real skill and judgment — scraping too aggressively could tear or thin the skin unevenly, ruining a valuable piece of material, while insufficient preparation left a surface too rough or greasy for ink to take properly.

**Smell and civic placement** — Like tanning, the lime-and-hide preparation stage of parchment-making was genuinely unpleasant and smelly, meaning parchmenters, like tanners and renderers, were sometimes subject to similar civic placement pressure — pushed toward town outskirts or riverside districts, though parchment-making generally carried somewhat less social stigma than tanning or rendering, since the _finished product_ was so closely and prestigiously associated with learning, law, and the Church, rather than with butchery-adjacent trades.

**Monastic and scholarly connections** — Because monasteries were such major producers and consumers of manuscripts, many had their own **in-house parchmenters** or close working relationships with local suppliers, and university towns similarly supported dedicated parchment workshops feeding the constant demand from scribes, stationers, and students. This puts the parchmenter in a similar economic/social position to the ink maker and papermaker — necessary supply-chain infrastructure for the whole world of medieval scholarship and record-keeping, personally unglamorous but functionally indispensable to it.

**Economic value and reuse** — Good parchment was genuinely expensive — an entire animal's skin produced only one usable sheet or a limited number of smaller leaves, meaning a substantial book (a full Bible, for instance) could require the skins of an entire flock, representing serious material cost before a single word was written. This expense drove real, documented practices of **reuse**: old, no-longer-wanted texts were sometimes scraped and washed to remove the original writing so the parchment could be reused for a new text — a **palimpsest** — with the ghost of the erased original sometimes still faintly visible beneath the new writing, recoverable by later scholars (and, in your setting, potentially by a sufficiently clever or magical investigator). This is a fantastic, ready-made worldbuilding device: valuable lost information hidden beneath newer, more mundane text, physically layered into the material itself.

**Grading and specialization** — Parchmenters produced graded qualities depending on source animal, age, and preparation care — the finest uterine vellum (from unborn or very young animals) commanded enormous prices and was reserved for the most prestigious commissions (royal charters, the finest illuminated manuscripts), while ordinary parchment served everyday legal and administrative documents. A skilled parchmenter's reputation rested on consistency and quality, similar to the currier's position in the leather trade — judged on reliable technical output rather than individual artistic flair.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Investigation, Nature (or History, given close proximity to written records and texts)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Leatherworker's Tools, or a custom "Parchmenter's Tools" (lunellum, stretching frame)
- **Feat:** Skilled or Observant
- **Equipment:** A lunellum and stretching frame components, several prepared parchment sheets, a small stock of raw hides, working clothes, 10 gp

This background is a strong, understated fit for a **Wizard** (a very grounded, practical reason to understand exactly what a spellbook's pages are made of and how they're prepared, plus real exposure to valuable/reused texts), or a **Rogue** with an interest in the palimpsest angle — someone who's learned to recognize, recover, or even deliberately create reused/scraped parchment, useful for forgery or for uncovering hidden older texts. It also suits a **Cleric** with monastic training, tied closely to a scriptorium's material production side rather than its scribal/artistic side.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Monastery's Parchmenter** — A lay worker or lower-ranked monk responsible for supplying the scriptorium, deeply knowledgeable about exactly which skins produce which quality, and quietly aware of every text that's passed through their hands before being written on.
- **The Palimpsest Reader** — Someone skilled at recovering faint, scraped-away original text beneath newer writing — a genuinely useful specialist for uncovering lost or deliberately erased information.
- **The Uterine Vellum Supplier** — Deals exclusively in the finest, most expensive grade of parchment for royal or ecclesiastical commissions — wealthy, particular, and tied closely to the most prestigious documents in the region.
- **The Reuse Profiteer** — Buys up old, "worthless" texts specifically to scrape and resell the parchment, indifferent to (or quietly profiting from) destroying whatever information was on the original — a good source of moral tension if the party realizes what's being erased.
- **The Overworked Apprentice** — Doing the genuinely grueling physical labor of scraping and stretching hides, resentful of how invisible and unglamorous their essential work is compared to the scribes and illuminators who get all the credit for the finished book.
### Game Hooks
- **The Hidden Palimpsest** — The party discovers (or is told of) an old document that was scraped and reused, and the original erased text — a map, a spell, a confession, a lost historical record — is exactly what they need, requiring a parchment specialist's help to recover it.
- **The Ruined Commission** — A parchmenter's mistake, sabotage, or bad batch of material ruins an extremely expensive commissioned work (a royal charter, a fine illuminated Bible) partway through, and the party gets pulled into the fallout — who pays, who's blamed, can it be salvaged.
- **Scraped Away On Purpose** — Someone deliberately had a document erased and reused specifically to destroy evidence — a will, a confession, a treaty — and the party has to prove the original content existed and recover what they can of it.
- **The Skin Shortage** — A local flock disease, war, or trade disruption creates a genuine parchment material shortage right when an important document urgently needs to be produced — a logistics-flavored ticking-clock hook.
- **Tracing the Grade** — A forged or suspect document can be partially exposed by an expert recognizing that its parchment grade or preparation doesn't match its claimed age or origin — a "the fraud undoes itself in the material" investigation thread, similar to the ink and watermark hooks from papermaking and ink-making.
