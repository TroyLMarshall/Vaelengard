### Justice Outside the Official System — But When Was There an "Official System"?
"Vigilante" implies taking justice into one's own hands outside a recognized legal system — but as covered throughout this list (constable, judge, government official entries particularly), medieval law enforcement was genuinely limited, inconsistent, and often absent. Understanding medieval vigilantism requires first understanding how thin the official justice apparatus actually was, since the line between legitimate community justice and vigilantism was considerably blurrier than modern frameworks suggest.
### The Thin Line Between Community Justice and Vigilantism
As covered in the constable entry, medieval law enforcement operated primarily through **community mechanisms** rather than professional police forces:
- **Hue and cry**: the legal obligation to join pursuit of a fleeing criminal — essentially mandated community vigilantism, legally required rather than extralegal
- **Frankpledge system**: mutual community responsibility for members' conduct — collective rather than individual justice responsibility
- **Posse comitatus**: the sheriff's power to call on able-bodied men to assist in law enforcement — organized community action in support of official authority

These mechanisms made **community-organized enforcement genuinely part of the recognized legal system** rather than extralegal action — complicating the clean modern distinction between official justice and vigilantism.
### Genuine Vigilantism — When Community Action Exceeded Official Sanction
That said, genuinely extralegal community justice action existed in recognizable forms:

**Lynch law and mob justice**: spontaneous community violence against perceived wrongdoers, bypassing any formal legal process
- **Jewish pogroms**: tragically, some of the most documented medieval mob violence involved attacks on Jewish communities — sometimes triggered by specific accusations (blood libel, well-poisoning during plague), sometimes by economic resentment (connecting to the banker entry's discussion of Jewish moneylending), sometimes simply by scapegoating during crisis. These represent a specific, horrifying form of vigilante mob violence with genuine medieval historical documentation.
- **Heretic burning by mobs**: before formal inquisitorial processes, mob violence against suspected heretics occurred — sometimes preceding or circumventing official ecclesiastical investigation
- **Grain riot and food violence**: as covered in the laborer and peasant entries, food crisis sometimes produced mob action against merchants, millers, or officials perceived as causing or profiting from scarcity — a form of vigilante economic justice with its own moral logic

**Night watch supplementation**: as covered in the guard and constable entries, official night watch was minimal — communities sometimes organized supplementary informal patrols beyond the official watch, particularly in response to specific crime waves or threats

**Guild enforcement**: as covered in the craft guilds entry, guilds actively policed their trade territories — sometimes going beyond simple reporting to authorities into direct action against those violating guild monopolies. A guild physically destroying an unlicensed competitor's tools or goods crossed from legitimate trade-association activity into something closer to organized vigilantism.

**Outlaw hunting**: as touched on in the hunters and forest law entries, declared outlaws were literally outside the law's protection — anyone could legally kill them without legal consequence, making outlaw hunting a legally sanctioned form of vigilantism where the community itself served as executioner
### Peasant Collective Action
The peasant entry covered collective peasant resistance, but it's worth examining specifically its vigilante dimensions:

**Stack burning and property destruction**: peasants sometimes targeted landlords' property (haystacks, mills, records of their obligations) in coordinated direct action — a form of collective justice against perceived oppressive arrangements that bypassed any official channel  
**Poaching**: as covered in the hunters entry, illegal hunting was itself a form of vigilante redistribution — taking back from the lord's forest what peasants felt should rightfully be available for common use  
**The Peasants' Revolt of 1381**: as covered in the laborer entry, this represents perhaps the most dramatic medieval English example of organized vigilante collective action — targeting specific individuals (the Archbishop of Canterbury, the Lord Chancellor) whose deaths were understood as popular justice for specific policies
### Specific Organized Vigilante Traditions
Some more organized, persistent vigilante traditions existed:

**Rural self-defense organizations**: communities in areas with insufficient official protection sometimes organized their own defense against bandits, raiders, or local disorder — particularly relevant in frontier regions, during periods of political instability, or where official authority was genuinely absent

**The Vehmic courts** (Vehmgerichte): a specifically Germanic phenomenon worth noting — secret tribunals operating in Westphalia and beyond, claiming ancient authority to judge and execute wrongdoers outside normal official channels. The Vehmic courts operated with genuine secrecy, their own ceremonies and hierarchy, and real willingness to carry out death sentences on those they judged guilty — representing perhaps medieval Europe's most developed organized vigilante justice institution.

**Charivari**: a specific, widespread European tradition of community-organized humiliating noise and mockery directed at those who violated community norms — particularly targeting remarrying widows, wife-beaters, cuckolds, or others whose behavior the community found objectionable. Not violent vigilantism but organized community social pressure outside official channels, using public shaming as enforcement.
### The Moral Framework
How medieval people understood vigilante justice:

**Divine justice as ultimate referent**: medieval moral thinking understood human justice as imperfect approximation of divine justice — which created both justification for extra-official action (when official channels failed, seeking justice directly connected to this higher framework) and criticism of it (human judgment was fallible and potentially sinful)

**The common good argument**: justifications for community action outside official channels often appealed to the common good — when official justice failed to protect the community, community self-protection became legitimate

**Church position**: the Church generally supported official justice channels and condemned private vengeance, while simultaneously providing moral frameworks (divine justice, the just cause) that could be interpreted as justifying extra-official action in extreme circumstances
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- The Vehmic court tradition as inspiration for a secret vigilante justice organization in your setting — a genuinely ancient, ceremonially organized group claiming authority to judge and execute wrongdoers that official channels can't or won't reach, with their own internal hierarchy, secret membership, and real moral ambiguity about whether their justice is actually just
- Charivari as a community social-pressure mechanism PCs might encounter — arriving in a village to find a cacophonous community noise-making directed at a specific household, requiring them to understand what norm-violation triggered it and what it means for their mission
- The outlaw's legal position as a gameplay mechanic — a declared outlaw NPC being legally killable by anyone, creating specific dynamics around protection, bounty-hunting, and the difference between legal and moral permissibility
- Peasant vigilante collective action as a moral complexity generator — a community's organized direct action against a genuinely oppressive lord that PCs might be asked to suppress (by the lord), support (by the peasants), or mediate (by someone who wants a less violent resolution), with no clean "right" answer
