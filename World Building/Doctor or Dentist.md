### The Physician — Top of the Medical Hierarchy
**University training**: physicians were the only genuinely university-educated medical practitioners, studying for years (medical degrees could take longer than other university faculties, sometimes a decade or more including preliminary arts training) at major medical schools — **Salerno** (Italy) was Europe's earliest and most renowned medieval medical school, followed by Bologna, Paris, Montpellier, and Padua as medicine developed as a serious university discipline.

**Theoretical foundation — humoral theory**: physicians diagnosed and treated based on the **four humors** (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile), inherited from Galen and Hippocrates via Greek and Arabic medical tradition. Illness was understood as humoral imbalance, and treatment aimed to restore balance through:
- **Diet**: foods were categorized by their humoral qualities (hot/cold, wet/dry) and prescribed accordingly
- **Bloodletting**: as covered with barber-surgeons, often prescribed by a physician but performed by a barber-surgeon, reflecting the diagnose/treat division of labor
- **Purging**: induced vomiting or laxatives to expel excess humors
- **Herbal remedies**: prescribed by the physician, compounded and sold by the apothecary

**Diagnostic methods**:
- **Uroscopy**: examining urine's color, clarity, and even smell/taste was a genuinely standard diagnostic technique, taken quite seriously as a window into internal humoral balance
- **Pulse-taking**: checking pulse rate and quality
- **Astrological consultation**: as covered with astrologers, physicians incorporated planetary timing into both diagnosis and treatment timing — this wasn't a fringe practice but standard, university-taught medical knowledge
- **Patient examination**: notably, physicians often examined patients relatively minimally by modern standards, sometimes relying heavily on verbal description of symptoms, urine samples, or even examining patients without extensive physical contact, since hands-on bodily examination carried some of the same status-conscious reluctance that kept physicians out of surgery

**Limited hands-on treatment**: as established with barber-surgeons, physicians generally avoided direct manual treatment, surgery, or bloodletting themselves — their role was diagnosis, prescription, and theoretical medical authority, delegating hands-on work to apothecaries (remedies) and barber-surgeons (surgery, bleeding).

**Who could access a physician**: physicians were expensive and relatively rare, concentrated in cities and serving primarily wealthy clients — nobility, prosperous merchants, and the Church's higher ranks. Most of the population never consulted a university-trained physician in their lives, relying instead on apothecaries, barber-surgeons, midwives, folk healers, or simply enduring illness without professional intervention.
### Folk Healers and Midwives — Medicine Outside the Formal System
Worth emphasizing since it's easy to overstate the formal physician's actual reach: **most medieval medical care happened entirely outside university medicine**:
- **Wise women/folk healers**: practiced herbal remedies and traditional treatments passed through informal, often oral knowledge transmission, serving the majority of the rural and poorer population who'd never see a physician
- **Midwives**: handled childbirth almost universally — this was essentially exclusively women's domain, with male physicians rarely directly involved in normal childbirth, called in only for serious complications in wealthier households
- **Religious healing**: prayer, pilgrimage to specific healing shrines, and saints' intercession (particular saints became associated with specific ailments) represented a genuinely significant parallel "treatment" pathway alongside or instead of physical medicine, reflecting how thoroughly medieval medicine and religious worldview intertwined
### Medieval Dentistry
Dentistry wasn't a distinct, university-trained profession — it existed almost entirely within the **barber-surgeon's** practical domain, as touched on earlier, plus some overlap with traveling tooth-pullers and itinerant practitioners of varying legitimacy.

**What dental "treatment" actually meant**:
- **Extraction**: by far the dominant treatment for problem teeth — without antibiotics or real understanding of dental disease progression, a painful or infected tooth was simply pulled rather than treated, using forceps-like tools, without anesthesia beyond perhaps alcohol or whatever the patient could mentally endure
- **Basic cleaning**: some awareness of cleaning teeth existed (various tooth powders, cloth rubbing), though nothing resembling modern preventive dentistry
- **Cauterization**: sometimes used for pain relief or addressing infection in a crude, direct way
- **Folk remedies**: clove oil and various herbal preparations were genuinely used for toothache pain relief, reflecting real (if limited) practical herbal knowledge

**Tooth-drawers/itinerant dentistry**: beyond settled barber-surgeons, traveling **tooth-drawers** operated at markets and fairs, often combining genuine (if crude) extraction service with a fair amount of showmanship or outright charlatanism — this is the historical root of the "snake oil salesman" archetype, where legitimate practical skill and outright scamming coexisted in the same loosely regulated space.

**Dental prosthetics**: limited but not entirely absent — there's some evidence of wealthy individuals using replacement teeth (sometimes animal teeth, or even teeth purchased from living donors/the poor) wired or otherwise attached to remaining teeth, though this was rare, expensive, and far from common practice.

**No real understanding of dental disease mechanisms**: medieval practitioners had no germ theory and limited understanding of what actually caused tooth decay — explanations sometimes involved "tooth worms," a folk belief attributing dental decay to literal worms boring into teeth, found across multiple medieval and even ancient medical traditions.
### Surgery Beyond Teeth
As covered with barber-surgeons, more invasive surgical procedures (setting bones, treating battlefield wounds, amputation, lancing infections) similarly fell to barber-surgeons rather than physicians, reflecting that same persistent professional divide between theoretical medical authority and hands-on manual treatment.
### Hospitals
Worth clarifying since the word appears throughout medieval sources but meant something different than today: medieval **hospitals** (as touched on with the beggar entry) were typically **religious institutions providing shelter, basic care, and charity** for the sick, poor, elderly, or traveling pilgrims, rather than specialized medical treatment centers in the modern sense. Actual medical treatment within these institutions was often quite limited, with the primary function being care, shelter, and spiritual support rather than active medical intervention.
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A university-trained physician NPC whose humoral theory diagnostic framework actually produces correct results in your setting because of how you've designed magic/health interaction — letting "wrong" medieval theory accidentally work right, or alternatively playing up the comedy/tragedy of confident wrong diagnosis
- A traveling tooth-drawer character blending real skill with showmanship and minor charlatanry — a fun, morally ambiguous NPC type for a market or fair scene
- The physician/barber-surgeon status divide as a believable source of tension if a PC's party includes a more "lowly" hands-on healer character looked down upon by an NPC physician, despite the PC's healer being more practically effective
- A magical hospital/temple of healing blending the real historical religious-charitable hospital model with actual divine magic — letting you ground a cleric-staffed healing institution in genuine medieval institutional logic rather than inventing it from scratch
