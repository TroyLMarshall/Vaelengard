### A Role That Splits Across Multiple Real Historical Positions

"Guard" in the generic D&D sense — someone standing watch, providing security, enforcing order — maps onto several distinct medieval roles we've actually already touched on pieces of (the constable, the night watch, the castle garrison) but is worth pulling together as its own dedicated entry, since security and protection needs varied enormously by context.
### Castle and Fortification Guards
- **Garrison soldiers**: castles maintained standing garrisons of varying size depending on the castle's strategic importance, the lord's wealth, and the current state of regional peace or conflict — ranging from a skeleton peacetime presence to substantial wartime garrisons
- **Gatekeepers**: specifically responsible for controlling access through castle or town gates, checking those entering and exiting, and managing the literal mechanics of raising/lowering gates, portcullises, and drawbridges
- **Watchmen on walls/towers**: stationed at elevated points for early warning of approaching threats, a genuinely critical function given how much advance warning mattered for organizing defense
- **The constable** (covered in detail earlier): commanded the castle's garrison and held real administrative/military authority over the fortification
### Town Walls and Gates
- **City gate guards**: as mentioned in the constable entry, controlled access to walled towns, particularly significant since many towns closed and locked their gates at night, requiring guards to manage both routine daytime traffic and the more significant security function of nighttime closure
- **Wall watch**: similar to castle wall-watching, town walls (where they existed — not every settlement was walled, but significant towns and cities frequently were) required ongoing watch for both military threat and basic fire/disorder monitoring
### Night Watch
- As covered in the constable entry: many towns required citizens to take turns on **night watch duty**, patrolling streets after dark watching for fires, disturbances, theft, or other disorder — this was often a rotated civic obligation rather than a standing paid professional force, at least earlier in the medieval period
- **Watchmen calling the hours**: some town watch traditions included calling out the time and "all's well" type announcements through the night, both for practical timekeeping (before reliable personal clocks) and as a visible/audible demonstration that the watch was active and alert
### Noble Household Guards
- **Personal retainers/men-at-arms**: wealthy nobles maintained household guards beyond just castle garrison troops — personal protection for the lord and family, particularly relevant during travel or in periods of political tension
- **Bodyguards for especially high-status individuals**: monarchs and the highest nobility maintained more specifically dedicated personal protection, sometimes drawn from elite or specially trusted military retainers
### Merchant and Trade Guards
- As covered extensively in the caravan guild and freight company entries: merchants traveling with valuable goods often hired armed guards or traveled with armed escort, particularly through dangerous or poorly-policed stretches of road
- **Guild-employed security**: wealthy guilds sometimes maintained guards for valuable goods storage (warehouses, as mentioned in the docks/wharfs entry) or for guildhall security
### Prison/Gaol Guards
- Medieval **gaols** (jails) required guards/keepers, though medieval imprisonment functioned somewhat differently than modern incarceration — often used for holding the accused before trial or punishment rather than as the primary punishment itself (which more often involved fines, corporal punishment, or execution for serious offenses), and conditions were frequently genuinely brutal and unregulated
- **Gaolers**: sometimes held their position as a kind of franchise or paid office, occasionally extracting fees from prisoners for better treatment or basic necessities — a system genuinely prone to corruption and abuse given minimal oversight
### Status and Recruitment
- **Professional soldiers**: castle garrisons and noble retinues increasingly included genuinely professional, paid soldiers, especially as the medieval period progressed and warfare grew more sophisticated — mercenary companies became particularly significant in various conflicts, offering trained military manpower for direct payment rather than feudal obligation
- **Feudal levy/obligation**: earlier and in less professionalized contexts, guard duty (like night watch) could be a rotated civic or feudal obligation rather than a paid profession — ordinary people fulfilling a duty rather than pursuing a career
- **Social status range**: guard duty spanned an enormous status range — from a humble town watchman fulfilling civic obligation to a knight serving as a noble's personal bodyguard, to a powerful constable commanding an entire castle garrison
### Equipment
- Varied enormously by status and context — a town watchman might carry simply a staff, lantern, and perhaps a basic blade, while a castle garrison soldier or noble's man-at-arms would have proper military equipment (connecting back to the armorer and blacksmith entries) appropriate to actual combat readiness
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks, particularly relevant since "guard" NPCs are such a D&D staple:
- Differentiating guard competence/equipment by context as a worldbuilding detail — a sleepy, underequipped town night watchman behaves very differently (and presents very different stakes) than a castle's professional garrison soldier, letting you signal danger/security level through guard quality rather than exposition
- A corrupt gaoler as a classic, grounded "talk your way past/bribe" obstacle for a captured or investigating PC, mirroring the real historical pattern of fee-extracting, minimally-supervised prison keepers
- A reluctant civic night-watch duty (echoing the constable entry's similar obligation-based recruitment) as a humble PC or NPC backstory element — someone pulled into adventure precisely because their unglamorous watch duty stumbled onto something far above their normal scope
- Mercenary company guards as a morally flexible, paid-rather-than-loyal security presence — useful for a noble or merchant patron whose guards might be bribed, intimidated, or simply unreliable compared to genuinely loyal household retainers
