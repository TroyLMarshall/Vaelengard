### A Trade That Straddles Folk Practice and Formal Knowledge
Unlike many entries on this list, "herbalist" doesn't map onto one single clean social position — it spans from the village wise-woman growing remedies in her garden to genuinely learned monastic scholars maintaining sophisticated physic gardens and copying out detailed herbal texts. The thread connecting them all is specialized plant knowledge applied to health and healing.
### Where Herbal Knowledge Lived
**Monastic herb gardens**: monasteries maintained dedicated **physic gardens** — cultivated plots specifically growing medicinal herbs, distinct from kitchen gardens, often laid out with real care and even documented in surviving plans (the famous St. Gall monastery plan, for instance, shows a dedicated herb garden layout). Monks studied and copied classical and Arabic medical/botanical texts, making monasteries genuine centers of preserved and transmitted herbal knowledge, connecting directly to the apothecary trade's reliance on these same plant materials.

**Village wise-women and cunning folk**: as covered in the healers entry, most ordinary herbal knowledge lived in oral, family-transmitted tradition among rural folk healers, predominantly women, who grew and gathered plants for their own community's everyday ailments.

**Root-gatherers and wild-harvesters**: some people specialized specifically in **gathering** wild medicinal plants from forests, meadows, and hedgerows rather than cultivating them — requiring real knowledge of where specific plants grew, when to harvest for maximum potency (timing mattered enormously and was often tied to specific seasons or even, per astrological medical theory covered earlier, specific lunar phases), and how to properly dry/store gathered material

**Apothecaries**: as covered in that entry, apothecaries sourced, stored, and compounded herbal materials into the actual remedies they sold, representing the more formalized, guild-organized commercial end of herbal practice
### Common Medicinal Plants
- **Sage**: widely used and considered genuinely valuable — the name itself derives from associations with wisdom/health
- **Rue**: used for various complaints, also having some genuine (if crude and risky) historical use related to women's reproductive health
- **Wormwood**: used against intestinal parasites among other applications
- **Chamomile**: for digestive complaints and as a calming preparation
- **Mint varieties**: digestive aid and general remedy
- **Comfrey**: used for wound healing and bone-related complaints, sometimes called "knitbone" reflecting its traditional use
- **Yarrow**: used for wound treatment, including on battlefields
- **Feverfew**: used for fevers and headaches, as the name suggests
- **Plantain**: a common, accessible plant used for wound treatment and various complaints
### Herbal Texts — Written Knowledge
- **Herbals**: illustrated reference texts cataloguing plants, their appearance, and their medicinal properties — copied and recopied (sometimes with declining accuracy as illustrations were copied from copies rather than fresh observation) through the medieval period, drawing heavily on classical sources (particularly **Dioscorides**, an ancient Greek physician whose herbal text remained hugely influential throughout the medieval period) as well as Arabic medical/botanical scholarship
- **Practical vs. theoretical herbals**: some texts were genuinely practical working references for identifying and using plants, while others incorporated more symbolic, astrological, or even allegorical content reflecting medieval natural philosophy's tendency to blend practical and theoretical/symbolic understanding
- **Hildegard of Bingen**: worth specifically naming as a notable medieval figure — a 12th-century abbess who wrote genuinely substantial medical and herbal texts (_Physica_ and _Causae et Curae_), representing a rare instance of a named, individually credited woman's contribution to formal medieval medical/herbal scholarship, in contrast to the largely anonymous, orally-transmitted nature of most folk herbal knowledge
### Methods of Preparation
Connecting to techniques covered with apothecaries and alchemists:
- **Drying**: preserving plant material for use outside its growing season
- **Infusions and decoctions**: steeping or boiling plant material in water to extract medicinal properties
- **Tinctures**: extracting properties using alcohol as a solvent
- **Poultices**: applying crushed or prepared plant material directly to wounds or afflicted areas
- **Ointments and salves**: combining herbal preparations with fat or wax bases for external application
### Genuine Effectiveness, Genuine Risk
Worth being honest about both sides here:
- **Real, empirically-discovered effectiveness existed**: many traditional herbal remedies had genuine pharmacological activity (willow bark's pain-relief properties, connecting to what would eventually lead to aspirin, is a commonly cited example, alongside various other plants with real, if crudely dosed, medicinal effect)
- **Real risk and limitation existed too**: without precise dosing knowledge, standardized preparation, or understanding of toxicity thresholds, herbal treatment carried genuine risk of ineffectiveness or even harm — some plants used medicinally were also genuinely toxic in incorrect doses, and folk knowledge, while often valuable, wasn't uniformly reliable or safe by modern standards
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A monastic herbalist maintaining a physic garden with both mundane medicinal plants and a few genuinely magical specimens — a natural, grounded way to introduce magical herbalism/alchemy ingredients into your setting's economy
- Hildegard-of-Bingen-style learned woman herbalist as an unusually credentialed, respected NPC — a compelling alternative to the more common "anonymous folk healer" archetype, especially if your setting has religious institutions that might similarly elevate a particularly gifted individual
- A root-gatherer NPC with specific, hard-won knowledge of where rare or magical plants grow — a natural guide/contact for PCs needing a specific alchemical ingredient found only in dangerous or remote terrain
- Herbal knowledge transmission (oral, family-based, gendered) as a worldbuilding texture detail — perhaps a PC's grandmother's herbal knowledge becomes relevant background, or a dying wise-woman NPC's knowledge needs to be recorded/passed on before it's lost, creating a small, human-scale quest
