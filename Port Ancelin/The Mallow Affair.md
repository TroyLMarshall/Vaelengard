## **What happened**
Three seasons ago, **Hugh Mallow**, the senior wool grader at [[La Foire]] and a man who'd held the post for nearly twenty years, was caught systematically downgrading fleece from smallholders while passing inferior wool from larger estate flocks as first quality. The pattern had likely been running for years before anyone noticed — smallholders grumbled about bad luck, never suspecting a conspiracy, while the larger flockmasters quietly profited.
## **How it unraveled**
It came apart almost by accident. A shearer named **Robin Attwood**, suspicious after three consecutive "second quality" judgments on fleece he swore was better than what his wealthier neighbor sold as "first," paid a Flemish merchant a coin to privately re-inspect his rejected wool before it left town. The merchant agreed — partly out of curiosity, partly because Flemish buyers had quietly started noticing that "first quality" Port Ancelin wool had grown inconsistent, and it was hurting the town's reputation in Bruges. The merchant's verdict: the wool was first quality, no question.

Word reached **Margery Thorn** within the week — smallholders and shearers had started comparing notes over ale at the Fleece & Anchor, as they always do, and the pattern became obvious once people started talking openly. It's said Margery let the conversation happen rather than steering it, then made sure the right wool guild master overheard it the following night.
## **The fallout**
Mallow claimed honest error at first, but when the guild examined his grading records against known buyer complaints stretching back years, the pattern was too consistent to be accident. He was stripped of his position and fined heavily — though notably not imprisoned, since the larger flockmasters who'd benefited (and likely paid him) were never formally implicated, a fact that still rankles smallholders.
## **Lingering effects**
- The wool guild instituted a **rotating grader system** — no single grader judges the same flocks two seasons running.
- Robin Attwood is now treated as something of a folk figure among smallholders, though the flockmasters' families have never quite forgiven him.
- Mallow still lives in town, reduced to working as a cart-loader at Warehouse Row — a visible, daily reminder of the scandal.
- Foreign merchants, especially the Flemish, now occasionally request independent re-weighing for large purchases — a small but real dent in the town's trading reputation.
