### Housing Varied Enormously by Wealth — More Than Almost Any Other Category We've Covered
We've referenced housing pieces throughout this whole list — timber-frame construction with carpenters, furniture scarcity with joiners, the absence of glass windows with glassblowers — so this is a good chance to pull the whole domestic picture together. Few aspects of medieval life show class division more starkly than the home itself.
### Peasant Housing — The Vast Majority Experience
**Construction**: simple, often single-room or minimally divided structures
- **Cruck construction**: a common rural building technique using paired curved timbers (crucks) meeting at the peak to form the basic structural frame, with wattle-and-daub infill (as covered in the carpenter entry) for walls
- **Earthen floors**: most peasant homes had simple packed-earth floors rather than any kind of paved or wooden flooring
- **Thatched roofs**: straw or reed thatching was the dominant roofing material for ordinary homes, requiring periodic maintenance/replacement and representing a genuine fire risk (connecting to fire concerns mentioned repeatedly throughout this list)
- **Single shared space**: many peasant homes were essentially **one room** housing the entire family, sometimes with animals sharing the same space, especially in colder regions where animal body heat genuinely contributed to keeping the house warm (the longhouse tradition, where humans and livestock shared one building under a single roof, divided only by a low partition, was common in various regions)

**Interior conditions**:
- **The central hearth**: an open fire, often without a proper chimney for much of the period (smoke simply rising and exiting through gaps in the roof or a smoke hole), serving as the only heat and light source, plus the cooking location covered extensively in the chef entry
- **Minimal furniture**: as established in the furniture maker entry, peasant households owned remarkably little — perhaps a table, stools or a bench, a chest, and simple bedding rather than constructed bedsteads
- **No glass windows**: as covered with the glassblower entry, ordinary homes used simple wooden shutters, sometimes with a stretched, treated animal skin or oiled cloth for limited light transmission, rather than the glass that remained a luxury throughout most of the period
- **Smoke, smell, and crowding**: by modern standards, conditions were genuinely cramped, smoky, and lacking in privacy — multiple generations of a family might share the single living space, along with whatever livestock the longhouse tradition incorporated
### Urban Housing — Townhouses and Tenements
**Timber-framed townhouses**: as covered in the carpenter entry, the dominant urban construction method, with the characteristic exposed-beam, wattle-and-daub infill appearance
- **Multiple stories**: urban land scarcity (within town walls especially) encouraged building upward rather than outward, sometimes with upper floors actually **jettying out** over the street below — a construction technique increasing usable floor space without requiring more ground footprint, also creating the visually distinctive overhanging-upper-floor appearance associated with medieval town streets
- **Shop and workshop integration**: as mentioned repeatedly throughout this list (bakeries, smithies, butcher shops), many urban craftsmen lived above or behind their actual workspace — the ground floor served as shop/workshop, with living quarters on upper floors, integrating work and domestic life in a way quite different from modern separated work/home arrangements
- **Shared walls and density**: urban buildings were frequently built immediately adjacent to neighbors, sharing walls, creating dense townscapes very different from more spread-out rural settlement patterns

**Poorer urban housing**: the urban poor, particularly in larger cities, often lived in genuinely cramped, subdivided housing or rented single rooms within larger buildings — connecting to the "boardinghouse"-adjacent lodging arrangements discussed earlier, since many city dwellers without resources for their own complete house rented rooms within someone else's property
### Wealthy and Noble Housing
**Manor houses**: as touched on in the farm entry, the lord's residence on a rural estate, ranging from relatively modest fortified houses to substantial complexes depending on the lord's wealth and status
- **The great hall**: as covered extensively in the dance hall and gentlemen/ladies entries, the central multi-purpose room for feasting, receiving guests, and general household life
- **Solar**: a private chamber, often on an upper floor, providing the lord's family some separation from the more public great hall — reflecting growing later medieval interest in domestic privacy beyond purely communal hall living
- **Multiple specialized rooms**: wealthy households increasingly featured distinct chambers for different functions (private bedchambers, dedicated kitchens separate from the hall, sometimes a private chapel) as the medieval period progressed, contrasting with the single-room reality of peasant housing

**Castles**: combined residential and defensive function, as covered in the armorer, guard, and government official entries — genuinely complex, multi-purpose structures serving military, administrative, and domestic needs simultaneously, with their own internal hierarchy of more and less private/prestigious spaces

**Urban merchant houses**: successful merchants built substantial townhouses reflecting their wealth, sometimes rivaling lesser nobility in scale and comfort, featuring glass windows (a genuine status marker as established earlier), proper fireplaces with chimneys, and considerably more furniture and decorative investment than ordinary urban housing
### Heating and Lighting — Connecting Back to Earlier Entries
- **Fireplaces and chimneys**: a genuinely significant technological development through the medieval period — earlier housing relied on simple central open hearths without proper chimneys, while wealthier and later medieval housing increasingly incorporated proper fireplaces with chimneys directing smoke outside more effectively, improving both air quality and heat efficiency
- **Candles and rushlights**: as covered extensively in the candlemaker entry, lighting was genuinely expensive and rationed according to means — wax candles for the wealthy, tallow for the middling sort, rushlights for the poorest
### Sanitation
- **Limited indoor plumbing**: virtually nonexistent by modern standards — chamber pots, simple garderobes (a small projecting toilet structure built into castle/wealthy house walls, draining outside or into a moat/pit) for wealthier housing, and communal or very basic arrangements for ordinary people
- **Waste disposal**: often simply thrown into streets or nearby waterways in urban contexts, contributing to the genuine sanitation and disease concerns that plagued medieval towns, connecting to themes touched on in the butcher and dyer entries about waste and smell management
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- Housing quality as an immediate, wordless way to signal NPC wealth/status when PCs enter a settlement — a glance at construction quality, glass windows versus shutters, and furniture density tells a visiting player character (and the actual player) everything they need to know about who lives there, without exposition
- A longhouse-style shared human/animal living space as an evocative, grounded detail for a rural settlement's poorest residents, useful for contrast against wealthier NPCs or simply for atmospheric texture
- Urban density and jettying upper floors as a useful detail for chase scenes, rooftop travel, or simply visually distinguishing your setting's town architecture in ways that feel authentic rather than generic-fantasy-medieval
- The solar/great hall privacy distinction as a worldbuilding tool for noble intrigue — a private conversation literally requires retreating to a different, more restricted space, giving you a natural in-world reason for scenes to physically relocate for secrecy
