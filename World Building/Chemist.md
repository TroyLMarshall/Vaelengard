### A Term That Doesn't Quite Apply Yet — But the Practice Existed
"Chemist" as a distinct professional identity is genuinely anachronistic for the medieval period — the word and the modern scientific discipline of chemistry both emerge later (chemistry as a recognized, distinct science is really a 17th-18th century development, growing out of and eventually superseding alchemy). But the _practices_ we'd now call chemistry absolutely existed in medieval Europe, just distributed across several trades rather than concentrated in one profession.
### Where "Chemistry" Actually Lived in Medieval Society
**The alchemist** is the closest conceptual match — alchemists performed genuine chemical experimentation (distillation, calcination, sublimation, and other processes covered in that entry), even though their theoretical framework was bound up with mysticism, the four elements, and the search for the Philosopher's Stone rather than anything resembling modern chemical theory. A lot of real chemical knowledge — how to produce acids, how to distill alcohol, how various minerals and metals behaved when heated or combined — emerged from alchemical practice, even wrapped in symbolic and spiritual language.

**The apothecary** did genuinely practical chemical work in compounding remedies — extracting, distilling, and combining substances, even if guided by humoral theory rather than modern pharmacology.

**Dyers**: dyeing cloth required real chemical knowledge (mordants to fix colors, specific mineral and plant-based processes) that constitutes genuine applied chemistry, even though dyers wouldn't have framed their work that way.

**Metalworkers and smiths**: smelting, alloying, and tempering all involve real chemical and metallurgical processes, learned through generations of empirical trial and error rather than theoretical understanding.

**Glassmakers**: producing glass requires precise control of mineral mixtures and heat — another genuinely chemical process embedded in a craft trade rather than a distinct "chemist" role.
### The Key Distinction From Modern Chemistry
What's genuinely missing in the medieval period, compared to a modern chemist, is:
- **Systematic theoretical framework**: medieval practitioners worked from the four-element/four-humor theoretical models (covered with alchemists and physicians), which, while internally coherent, don't map onto actual chemical reality the way post-Enlightenment atomic/molecular theory does
- **Quantitative, repeatable experimental method**: while alchemists and apothecaries certainly experimented and recorded results, the rigorous, controlled, quantitative experimental approach we associate with modern science developed later (often credited to the Scientific Revolution, with figures like Robert Boyle in the 17th century specifically marking a turn from alchemy toward recognizable chemistry)
- **Professional/institutional identity as "chemist"**: there was no university chair of chemistry, no guild of chemists — the practice was embedded within other trades and disciplines rather than recognized as its own thing
### Genuine Medieval Chemical Achievements
Worth noting that despite lacking modern theoretical framework, medieval and especially Islamic-world practitioners (whose knowledge significantly fed into later European practice, similar to the astronomy transmission discussed earlier) achieved real, lasting practical results:
- **Distillation refinement**: alchemists significantly advanced distillation techniques, including the production of increasingly pure alcohol and various acids
- **Mineral acid production**: substances like sulfuric acid, nitric acid, and others were discovered/refined through alchemical experimentation
- **Gunpowder**: arriving in Europe via transmission from China through intermediary contact, gunpowder's chemistry (saltpeter, sulfur, charcoal) was worked with empirically by those producing and refining it for military use, without anyone involved having a modern combustion theory
- **Pigment and dye chemistry**: genuine sophisticated knowledge of how to produce, fix, and combine colors for art, manuscript illumination, and textile dyeing
- **Metallurgy**: alloying knowledge (bronze, various steel treatments) represents real applied chemical sophistication, even without atomic theory to explain why it worked
### If You Want a "Chemist" Analog for Your Setting
Given there's no clean historical equivalent, you have some legitimate creative freedom:
- **Lean into the alchemist framing**: since alchemy is the closest real historical practice, you could simply treat your setting's "chemist" as a more secular, less mystically-inclined branch of alchemy — someone who's stripped away (or never adopted) the spiritual/Philosopher's Stone framework and focuses purely on practical material transformation, perhaps viewed as a curious, almost heretical departure from "real" alchemy by more traditional practitioners
- **An artificer-adjacent specialist**: connecting back to the D&D class discussion, an artificer-flavored "chemist" fits naturally as someone whose alchemical/chemical knowledge produces useful, semi-magical practical results (potions, explosive compounds, preservative treatments) without needing the full ceremonial alchemical trapping
- **A guild-adjacent specialist serving multiple trades**: perhaps your setting has someone who consults across dyers, apothecaries, and metalworkers — a "practical materials expert" whose knowledge cuts across traditional trade boundaries, which could be an interesting, slightly anachronistic but useful narrative device (a person ahead of their time, viewed with a mix of respect and suspicion by more traditionally siloed guild members)
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A character who's quietly doing what we'd recognize as real chemistry, but who has to _describe_ their work in alchemical/mystical language to be taken seriously or avoid suspicion — a fun "ahead of their time" character concept
- Gunpowder's mysterious foreign origins (from your setting's equivalent of the Silk Road/Chinese transmission) as a worldbuilding thread — who controls the knowledge, who's trying to monopolize or steal the formula, and how it's changing warfare
- A dyer or apothecary character whose "trade secret" techniques are actually a more systematic, proto-scientific approach than their guild rivals realize, giving them a genuine edge dressed up in normal trade-secrecy
- Cross-trade knowledge-sharing (a metalworker and an apothecary collaborating, sharing techniques across guild lines) as a small but interesting social dynamic, given how siloed real medieval guilds tended to be
