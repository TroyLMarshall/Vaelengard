### The Craft and Its Structure
**Bladesmith vs. swordsmith** — In practice these overlapped, but by the high medieval period there was often specialization. A **bladesmith** forged and shaped the blade itself: folding and welding steel, quenching, tempering. A separate **cutler** or **hilt-maker** might handle the crossguard, grip, and pommel, and a **swordsmith** or **swordslip** (in England, literally a guild trade name) assembled and finished the whole weapon. Larger production centers had this broken into a small assembly line — bladesmith, grinder/polisher, hilt-fitter, scabbard-maker (a leatherworker), and sometimes a separate engraver for inscriptions or maker's marks.

**Guilds** governed the trade in most cities from roughly the 1200s onward. Becoming a master smith meant years as an apprentice (often 7 years), then working as a journeyman under other masters, then submitting a "masterpiece" weapon to the guild for judgment before you could open your own forge and take apprentices of your own. Guilds controlled quality, pricing, and who could legally practice — partly craft pride, partly monopoly protection.

**Famous centers** worth stealing wholesale for a setting:
- **Passau, Germany** — famous for the "wolf" mark (a running wolf stamped into blades), so prestigious that smiths elsewhere counterfeited it constantly. This is a great seed for an NPC conflict — a smith who forges someone else's mark.
- **Solingen, Germany** — "the City of Blades," an entire town built around the industry, with water-powered grinding mills.
- **Toledo, Spain** — legendary for sword steel, partly myth (magic tempering water), partly real metallurgical skill blending Christian, Muslim, and Jewish smithing traditions under different rulers.
- **Damascus/the Islamic world** — wootz steel imported and reforged produced the banded "watering" pattern that Europeans mythologized heavily; European smiths who couldn't replicate it developed pattern-welding instead, twisting and folding different steels to fake a similar visual effect.
### Status and Superstition
Smiths occupied a strange social position — essential to war, wealthy in successful cases, but also viewed with unease. Working with fire and transforming raw ore into something that could kill a man gave smithing a whiff of the supernatural across many European folk traditions (echoes of Wayland/Völund the smith-god survive into medieval legend). Village smiths were sometimes suspected of dealings with spirits or the devil; master swordsmiths in cities, by contrast, could be minor celebrities, their blades signed and sought after by name.

**Pattern-welding and named blades** — Before uniform steel was reliable, smiths folded and twisted iron and steel together to remove impurities and create strength, which incidentally produced beautiful rippling patterns on the blade. This practice, combined with the sheer cost and lifespan of a good sword (generations, easily), meant blades were **individually named, inherited, and storied** — think less "a sword" and more "a specific object with a biography," which is gold for your table.
## Worldbuilding Angles for D&D 2024
### Turning This Into a Background/Profession
You could build this as a **custom Background** (2024 rules let you build one from any skill/tool proficiency combo) — something like:
- **Skill Proficiencies:** Investigation, Persuasion (or History)
- **Tool Proficiency:** Smith's Tools
- **Feat:** Crafter or Skilled
- **Equipment:** Smith's tools, a journeyman's letter of introduction, a half-finished blade, traveling clothes, 10 gp

Flavor the origin feat choice as their "masterpiece" — the piece they forged to earn full guild status, which they still carry or lost.
### NPC Hooks
- **The Marked Forger** — A smith caught illegally stamping their blades with a prestigious city's maker's mark (Passau's wolf, etc.) to sell at a markup. Are they a fraud, or did they train under a master from that city and feel entitled to the mark?
- **The Guild Gatekeeper** — A master smith who blackballs an obviously talented outsider (maybe a former adventurer, a half-orc, a foreigner) from guild membership out of prejudice or fear of competition. The PCs could be the disruption that forces change.
- **The Widow Smith** — A smith's widow who kept the forge running after her husband's death, fighting the guild's inheritance rules (many required a male heir or remarriage to a guild member to keep a shop).
- **The Steel-Whisperer** — A reclusive smith rumored to have rediscovered a "lost" technique (echoing the real Damascus steel mystery) — their blades hold an edge unnaturally well, and everyone from rival smiths to a wizard's guild wants to know their secret.
- **The Nameblade Broker** — A dealer who specializes in identifying and authenticating storied/named blades, since forged provenance is a real problem — a "trueseer" of swords who can tell you which battles a blade has actually seen versus which stories were invented to inflate its price.
### Game Hooks
- **The Masterpiece Quest** — A PC or NPC apprentice needs rare materials (a dragon's tooth, meteoric iron, a fire elemental's ember) to forge their guild masterpiece, kicking off a fetch-quest-with-teeth.
- **Counterfeit War** — Two cities' guilds are in an economic cold war over knockoff maker's marks; the PCs get pulled in as either smugglers, investigators, or hired muscle.
- **The Broken Legacy Blade** — A PC inherits or finds a named ancestral sword, shattered. Reforging it requires finding the smith's line, lost techniques, or a magical forge — a whole side-quest arc built around a single object's biography, very on-theme for the "blades as characters" tradition above.
- **Guild vs. Magic** — Tension between traditional smiths and arcane weaponsmiths (enchanters who can produce "good enough" magic blades faster/cheaper) — economic displacement anxiety as a subplot, mirroring real guild protectionism.
- **The Wolf Mark Heist** — Someone's stealing genuine maker's-mark stamps (physical tools) from master smiths to counterfeit blades at scale — a mystery/heist hook.
