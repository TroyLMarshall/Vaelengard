### A Trade We've Referenced Repeatedly, Now Given Full Treatment
We've mentioned tanners throughout this list — the boot maker/cordwainer entry noted their essential supply role, the leatherworker entry covered the broader leather trade family and touched on tanning specifically, the butcher entry connected hide production to the supply chain — so let's give tanning its own dedicated, full treatment, since it's genuinely one of the more technically complex and socially interesting trades we've encountered.
### Why Tanning Was Necessary
Raw animal hide, if simply dried without treatment, becomes **rawhide** — stiff, brittle, and prone to rotting when wet, unusable for most leather applications. Tanning converted this perishable raw material into genuine leather — flexible, durable, and rot-resistant — through a chemical process that permanently altered the hide's protein structure. Without tanners, the entire leather economy covered in the leatherworker entry (footwear, armor, harness, bookbinding, and countless other applications) simply couldn't function.
### The Raw Material Supply Chain
- **Hides from butchers**: as covered in the butcher entry, hides were a significant byproduct of slaughter — cattle hides particularly valuable for heavy leather, sheep and goat for lighter work
- **Pelts from hunters and trappers**: connecting to the hunters and furrier entries, animals killed for food or fur also generated hides potentially useful for leather
- **Quality variation**: hide quality varied with the animal's age, health, feeding, and how carefully it was skinned — careless skinning leaving cuts or holes in the hide significantly reduced its value for leather production
### The Tanning Process — Genuinely Complex Chemistry
Medieval tanners didn't understand the underlying chemistry in modern terms, but they'd developed empirically sophisticated processes achieving genuine chemical transformation:

**Preparation phase**:
- **Soaking**: fresh hides were soaked in water to clean and soften them — sometimes for extended periods
- **Liming**: soaking in a lime solution (connecting to the stoneworker entry's lime-burning discussion) loosened and loosened hair, epidermis, and remaining flesh from the hide — an alkali treatment producing real chemical changes in the hide structure
- **Unhairing**: physically removing the loosened hair using a fleshing beam (a curved wooden beam over which the hide was draped) and a two-handled scraping blade — physically demanding work
- **Fleshing**: similar scraping process removing remaining flesh and fat from the hide's inner surface
- **Scudding**: further cleaning to remove remaining lime and loosened material before actual tanning

**Tanning phase** — two main approaches producing different leather types:

**Vegetable tanning** (the dominant process for most applications):
- **Tannin source**: oak bark was the primary tannin source across much of medieval Europe — bark stripped from oak trees (sometimes the same coppiced woodland covered in the logger entry, since tanbark and timber production could come from the same managed woodland) and ground into a paste or infused into water
- **Tan pits**: hides were submerged in a series of progressively stronger tannin solutions in dedicated pits — typically starting in weaker solutions and gradually moving to stronger ones over a period of **months to years** for the heaviest leathers
- **Slow process**: quality vegetable tanning was genuinely slow — sole leather for shoe construction might require a year or more of pit tanning to achieve full penetration and proper conversion throughout the hide's thickness
- **Regular handling**: hides needed periodic handling, turning, and moving between pits throughout the process — ongoing labor investment across an extended production timeline

**Alum tawing** (producing "white leather" or tawed leather):
- Using alum (a mineral salt) rather than plant tannin produced a distinctive white or pale leather with different properties — softer, more supple, suitable for different applications than vegetable-tanned leather
- This process was associated with **whittawyers** — a distinct specialization as covered in the leatherworker entry
- Faster than vegetable tanning but producing leather with different characteristics

**Post-tanning finishing**:
- **Currying**: as covered in the leatherworker entry, further treatment with oils and fats to improve leather's flexibility, water resistance, and working properties — sometimes a distinct trade, sometimes done by the tanner
- **Dyeing**: connecting to the dyer entry, leather could be dyed using similar mordant-based techniques to cloth dyeing
### The Notorious Smell — Social Consequences
This is genuinely one of the most significant aspects of tanning as a medieval trade:

**Multiple sources of foul odor**:
- **Rotting organic matter**: the early stages of processing involved significant organic decomposition
- **Lime**: the chemical smell of lime solutions used in unhairing
- **Urine**: dog feces (collected by specialized **pure finders** or **gong farmers** — connecting to the outhouses entry's gong farmer discussion) and sometimes urine were used in some preparation stages, particularly for softening certain leather types — producing genuinely eye-watering smells
- **Rotting bark and tannin solutions**: the tan pits themselves had a distinctive heavy, organic smell

**Social and geographic consequences**:
- **Mandatory marginal location**: as mentioned in the boot maker and leatherworker entries, tanneries were typically required by civic regulation to locate at town edges, downwind, and near water sources — protecting the rest of the urban population from the smell while ensuring the tanner had the water access the process required
- **Low social prestige despite essential function**: similar to gong farmers, cesspit cleaners, and butchers, tanners occupied a somewhat socially marginal position despite the absolute necessity of their work — the physical unpleasantness marking them as lower-status even when reasonably prosperous
- **Occupational identity**: tanning was so strongly associated with specific locations that "Tanner Street," "Tanner's Lane," and similar place names survive in various English towns, marking where this trade once operated
### Capital Requirements and Economics
- **Significant upfront investment**: establishing a tannery required digging and maintaining tan pits, sourcing and storing substantial quantities of tanbark, managing extended work-in-progress across long production timelines, and maintaining the physical infrastructure — a more capital-intensive operation than many craft trades
- **Tanbark supply**: connecting to the logger entry's discussion of coppice woodland management, oak bark for tanning represented a significant, ongoing raw material cost — tanners needed reliable tanbark supply chains, and the competition between tanning and other woodland uses could create real resource tension
- **Extended production timeline**: with quality leather taking months or years to produce, tanners had significant capital tied up in work-in-progress — requiring either substantial financial reserves or credit arrangements connecting to the banker entry's discussion of merchant credit
### Guild Structure
- Tanners organized into guilds in major towns, following standard progression
- **Separation from finishing trades**: as noted in the leatherworker entry, tanners (producing the raw leather) and curriers/finishers (preparing it for use) were sometimes distinct guilds in larger towns, reflecting genuine technical specialization even within the leather trade family
- **Tanbark rights**: access to oak bark was sometimes regulated — forest owners controlling tanbark access as a genuine commercial resource, creating supply-chain dependencies connecting the urban tanning trade to rural forest management
### Worldbuilding Angle
A few fantasy-flavored hooks:
- A tannery district as an atmospheric, sensory-rich urban location — the smell alone making it immediately distinctive, while the combination of pits, scraped hides, and working conditions creates a visually striking environment quite different from cleaner craft workshops
- A tanner's marginal social position and marginal urban location as a natural setting for criminal or underground activity — the same isolation and low visibility that pushed tanneries to town edges makes them natural locations for activities that benefit from being overlooked
- Tanbark supply as a worldbuilding economic detail — a tanner's workshop that's been forced to reduce production because a new lord has restricted access to their traditional woodland tanbark source, creating a believable small-scale economic grievance with genuine historical grounding
- The chemical knowledge embedded in tanning (alkali treatments, acid-base interactions in the tanning process, understanding of organic decomposition) as a natural bridge to alchemical interest — a tanner NPC who's begun applying their practical chemistry knowledge to less mundane experiments, connecting a humble, smelly trade to your setting's magical/alchemical tradition
