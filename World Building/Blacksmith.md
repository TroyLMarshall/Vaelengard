### The Backbone Trade of Medieval Craft
If there's one medieval profession that's already deeply embedded in fantasy and D&D imagination, it's the blacksmith — and for good reason. Smithing was genuinely foundational to medieval material culture: almost everything requiring metal, from a horseshoe to a plowshare to a sword, passed through a smith's hands at some point. Unlike the more specialized [[Armorer and Armory]], the general blacksmith was the village/town generalist of metalworking.
### Blacksmith vs. Armorer vs. Other Metal Specialists
Worth clarifying since we covered armorers already: a **blacksmith** was the broad, general-purpose iron and steel worker, while specialists branched off for specific high-skill needs:
- **Armorers**: specialized in armor (as covered)
- **Bladesmiths/swordsmiths**: sometimes a distinct specialization for fine weapon-making, though many blacksmiths made basic blades too
- **Farriers**: specialized in horseshoeing specifically (sometimes overlapping with general blacksmithing, sometimes a distinct trade)
- **Locksmiths**: specialized in locks, keys, and fine mechanical metalwork
- **Whitesmiths**: worked with finished, finer metal (tin, pewter) rather than the heavier ironwork of "blacksmith" (named for the black firescale/oxide coating on worked iron)

A village blacksmith, though, typically did a bit of everything iron-related, since most communities couldn't support multiple narrow specialists.
### What a Blacksmith Actually Made
**Agricultural tools** — probably the bulk of routine work in a rural setting:
- Plowshares, scythes, sickles, hoes, axes
- Horseshoes and harness fittings
- Cart and wagon fittings (wheel rims, axles)

**Household items**:
- Nails (an enormous, constant demand — nail-making was sometimes its own specialized sub-trade given the sheer volume needed)
- Hinges, locks, latches
- Cooking equipment: pots, trivets, fire tools
- Candle holders and lighting fixtures

**Weapons and military equipment**:
- Basic blades, spearheads, arrowheads
- Some armor pieces, especially simpler items (helping explain the overlap with armorers in smaller towns lacking dedicated armor specialists)

**Building hardware**:
- Hinges, bolts, gates, grilles, and structural ironwork
### The Forge and Tools
- **The forge/hearth**: the central feature, burning charcoal (preferred over raw coal for more consistent, cleaner-burning heat without certain impurities) to reach the high temperatures needed to soften iron for working
- **Bellows**: pumped to force air into the forge, raising temperatures — often worked by an apprentice when the smith's hands were occupied
- **Anvil**: the iconic tool, a heavy iron block (sometimes with a steel face) providing a stable surface for hammering hot metal into shape
- **Hammers**: various sizes and shapes for different tasks — heavier sledgehammers for rough shaping (sometimes wielded by an assistant while the smith held and guided the workpiece with tongs), lighter hammers for finer work
- **Tongs**: essential for holding hot metal safely
- **Quenching trough**: water or oil for rapid cooling, part of the hardening process
- **Files and grinding stones**: for finishing work after shaping
### The Process: Working Iron
1. **Heating**: iron needed to reach a glowing, malleable temperature in the forge before it could be worked
2. **Hammering/shaping**: repeated heating-and-hammering cycles to shape the metal, since it cools and stiffens quickly and needs reheating to stay workable
3. **Welding (forge welding)**: joining pieces of iron by heating both to near-melting and hammering them together — true fusion welding, distinct from modern electric welding but achieving similar results
4. **Quenching**: rapid cooling in water or oil to harden steel
5. **Tempering**: careful reheating after quenching to reduce brittleness while retaining hardness — balancing strength against the risk of the metal shattering under stress
6. **Finishing**: filing, grinding, and polishing as needed
### Raw Materials
- **Bloomery iron**: medieval Europe primarily used the **bloomery process** to produce usable iron from ore — a furnace technique that produced a spongy mass ("bloom") of iron mixed with slag, which then needed to be repeatedly heated and hammered to drive out impurities and consolidate it into usable wrought iron
- **Blast furnaces**: began appearing in parts of Europe later in the medieval period (especially associated with increasing use in the 14th-15th centuries), allowing higher temperatures and the production of cast iron, though wrought iron from bloomeries remained extremely important throughout
- **Charcoal**: the essential fuel, requiring its own dedicated trade (charcoal burners working in forests) to supply smiths with consistent fuel
- **Scrap and recycled iron**: smiths often reworked old, broken, or worn-out iron items rather than always starting from freshly smelted ore, since iron was valuable enough to be worth recycling
### Social Position
Blacksmiths held a genuinely respected position in medieval communities:
- **Essential and visible**: every village or town needed a smith, and the trade's importance to agriculture, transport, and basic infrastructure gave smiths real social standing despite being craftsmen rather than nobility
- **Often prosperous**: a skilled smith with steady custom could do quite well economically, especially compared to less essential trades
- **Sometimes quasi-mystical reputation**: working with fire and transforming raw ore/scrap into useful, even beautiful objects gave smithing a certain folkloric weight across many cultures — smith-deities and smith-heroes appear across numerous mythologies (Wayland/Völundr in Germanic tradition, for instance), and this mystique persisted in folk attitudes even in Christian medieval Europe
- **Guild organization**: in towns large enough to support formal guild structures, smiths organized like other craftsmen, with apprentice-journeyman-master progression and guild-regulated standards
### Apprenticeship
Standard craft apprenticeship pattern: a boy (smithing was essentially exclusively male, given the physical demands and trade exclusivity) apprenticed around age 12-14 for roughly 7 years, starting with menial tasks (working the bellows, fetching materials, basic cleanup) before progressing to actual metalworking under supervision, eventually becoming a journeyman who might travel and work for various masters before potentially establishing his own smithy as a master.
### Worldbuilding Angle
Given how thoroughly fantasy already mines this trade, a few hooks that might add some freshness:
- A smith who works with non-standard materials — meteoric iron, monster bone/scale, or magically-infused ore — making them a sought-after specialist rather than a generic shopkeeper
- The mystical/folkloric angle played up directly: a smith with genuine minor magical aptitude (smithing and minor enchantment going hand in hand in your setting's tradition), without necessarily being a full wizard
- A village smith as a quietly important community pillar/mentor figure — someone PCs return to for repairs and grounding between adventures, rather than a one-off shop
- Guild tension between traditional smiths and a rival magical/alchemical metalworking tradition, mirroring the real friction between trade-trained and university-trained practitioners seen elsewhere in this list
